Terroir · Jura, France

Lons-le-Saunier

Salt, Comté, Vin Jaune — slow France

Lons-le-Saunier doesn't show off. A 17,000-soul prefecture perched on the Revermont, where the Bresse plain dies into the first Jura plateau. The ground has been weeping salt since the Bronze Age — hence the name (saunier = salt-worker) — and that salt fixed everything else: the Montbéliarde cows drinking brine to make rich milk, the Comté ripening in cool caves, the Morteau sausage smoked over the tuyé. Add the Savagnin grape growing 30 km east on Château-Chalon's blue marls, and you have the formula: a terroir built on patience.

The neighborhoods

Where it lives

Lons is small — fifteen minutes on foot covers everything. The real addresses cluster on three axes; the rest is the periphery, or out in the vineyard.

  • Place de la Liberté & rue du Commerce — the historic core, Rouget de Lisle's statue (Bartholdi, 1882), and 146 metres of medieval golden-stone arcades — the only arcaded street in Franche-Comté. Pâtisserie Pelen, Fromagerie Vagne, Caveau des Jacobins.
  • Place de la Comédie / Halles — the market (Thursday, Saturday), Le Bistrot des Halles, Le Café de la Comédie. This is where you have lunch on market day.
  • Quartier de la Chevalerie & Source Salée — plane-tree park, 1900 bandstand, and the basin of the historic salt springs (the water still runs at 30 g/L). The town's thermal past.
  • Quartier du Stade / Pavigny — south-east, on the edge of the centre. Le Comtois and Le Bouchon du Stade — non-touristy bistro addresses.
  • Montciel & Chille — residential quarters to the north-east. Le Parenthèse sits 5 minutes by car, in the Chille countryside.
  • Out of town — Arbois (15 km, capital of Jura wine), Château-Chalon (15 km, Vin Jaune), Poligny (25 km, capital of Comté), Montigny-lès-Arsures, Pontarlier (absinthe). The terroir is never centralised — you have to drive.
Why now
May–June: summer-pasture Comté starts arriving at the affineurs', morels are still around, and the just-bottled Vin Jaune (six years older than the vintage, released in March) is finding its rhythm. The season the Jura unfolds.
History & terroir

Where it comes from

The brine springs here were already worked 5 000 BC. The Romans built a saltworks. Medieval growth came from salt and cheese fairs. Pasteur was born in Dole (1822), set up his lab in Arbois (15 km north), and it was the Vin Jaune of Pupillin that gave him the data for his 1857 doctoral thesis on alcoholic fermentation. Rouget de Lisle, the city's son (born rue du Commerce, 1760), composed La Marseillaise in Strasbourg in 1792 — his statue by Bartholdi (the man who carved Liberty) has stood on Place de la Liberté since 1882. In the 19th century, the Bel family invented La Vache qui rit here (1921). The thermal baths (saline springs, the Source de l'Étoile) still run.

What you eat

At the table

Three uncontested truths. Comté — not one cheese but a family: 18 months in cave, fatty and hazelnut ; 24 months, more saline and granular ; 36 months, dry and caramel. Tasting three ages side by side is the basic education, and any serious cheesemonger sets it up. Saucisse de Morteau (a wood-pegged ring) and the slimmer, more smoked Montbéliard, served with cancoillotte melted with a thread of garlic and white wine — peasant Haut-Jura cooking is built on this. Poularde de Bresse poached in Vin Jaune with morels is the cathedral dish, the one you order to understand why the region exists: the sotolon in Vin Jaune (4,5-dimethyl-2-furanone) bonding with the glutamate of dried morels and the blue-foot Bresse hen's fat — pure chemistry.

What you drink

By the glass

The Jura grows seven grapes, not the Bordeaux five. Savagnin (the great white, either oxidative under voile or topped-up ouillé), Chardonnay, Trousseau and Poulsard for the slim reds, Pinot Noir, plus Vin Jaune (Savagnin aged 6 years 3 months under flor in a non-topped barrel, bottled in the 62 cl clavelin) and Vin de Paille (grapes raisined three months, sweet). Drink Macvin (marc-and-must mistelle) as aperitif, Crémant du Jura at any hour, and keep Vin Jaune for the table — Domaine Macle (Château-Chalon), Stéphane Tissot (Arbois) and Jean-François Ganevat (Rotalier) are the three names you learn first. For digestif: pine-bud liqueur (Distillerie Les Fils d'Émile Pernot, Pontarlier) and absinthe — banned in France until 2011, distilled again 30 km away in Pontarlier.

A walk

The path

Start at Place de la Liberté at dawn, under Rouget de Lisle pointing his violin. Walk down rue du Commerce — the only medieval arcaded street in Franche-Comté, 146 metres of golden stone — past the house where he was born (n°24, plaque). Cut left into rue Saint-Désiré: at the 11th-century Romanesque church, ask to see the crypt — Merovingian sarcophagus of Saint Désiré, 6th century. Out again, cross the parc de la Chevalerie (old plane trees, 1900 bandstand), down past the baths, along the Source Salée basin (the water still runs at 30 g/L salt), end at the market on Place de la Comédie. Forty minutes. The city reads through smells: wet stone, salt, sourdough, Morteau smoke from the charcutier's tuyé.

What to skip

What we leave behind

Rue du Commerce at Saturday lunchtime is a trap — two tourist pizzerias pass off industrial Comté as a long-aged cheese. Walk past, climb to the covered market or book Le Comtois. Skip the "Vin Jaune tasting cellars" downtown that pour topped-up Savagnin and call it jaune: the real thing is tasted at the vigneron's, in Château-Chalon (15 km).

The right moment
May–June for morels, summer-pasture Comté and cold Crémant. September for the harvest and — though the Percée du Vin Jaune itself falls on the first weekend of February — the month when the cellars actually let you taste the new Savagnin.
