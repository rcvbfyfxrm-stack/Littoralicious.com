Nafplio

Ναύπλιο · Peloponnese, Greece
“A walled limestone peninsula on the Argolic Gulf where the stairs count to 999, the stones are Venetian, and the soup on every waterfront table is whatever the gulf gave up at dawn.”
Field brief · July 2026
The name Nafplio takes its name from Nauplius, the mythical Danaid king who founded the harbour in Greek legend. Strabo’s Geographica records it as Nauplia; the Venetians called it Napoli di Romania — “Napoli of the Greeks” — distinguishing it from their Adriatic Napoli. The Ottoman form was Anabolu. All three compress the same ancient root into a harbour name that the English word “naval” shares, distantly, via the Greek naus, ship.

Nafplio is a peninsula city on the Argolic Gulf — a limestone spur ringed by three Venetian-era fortresses, the preserved neoclassical fabric of Greece’s first capital, and a seafood table fed daily by the gulf’s boats. From 1823 to 1834 this was the seat of the Greek state; the streets where Kapodistrias governed — Venetian house-fronts on the waterfront, neoclassical public buildings on Syntagma Square, the old fishermen’s lanes of Psaromachalas — were laid down in those eleven years and have changed little since. The Palamidi fortress looks down on all of it from 216 metres; the Bourtzi sits alone on its harbour island. The table is as good as the architecture.

The only European capital where the head of state was assassinated at a church door. Ioannis Kapodistrias, Greece’s first Governor, was shot on 9 October 1831 at the entrance of St. Spyridon church in the old town — a building you pass on the standard old-town walk today, still with its bullet-hole legend in the doorframe.

When to come July and August are peak season — full hotels, crowded Palamidi steps, waterfront tables at a premium. The better windows are April–May (warm, uncrowded, wildflowers on the Palamidi slopes, the market full of spring greens and asparagus) and September–October (the heat breaks, Nemea grapes arrive in town, the day-trip crowds thin and the light goes amber). The town is good year-round; these are the best two moments.
THE SOUL OF THE PLACE
What the place is, underneath

Three fortresses on three elevations define Nafplio before any other claim. The Byzantine Acronauplia clings to the seaward cliff; the Venetian Bourtzi sits alone on its harbour island, reachable by small ferry; and Palamidi’s great limestone bluff rises 216 metres above the old town, its 999 steps the one physical fact everyone who visits remembers. Every European power that mattered once held this peninsula: Franks, Venetians, Ottomans, and finally the new Greek state, which chose it as its first capital in 1823 when the country it governed was barely an idea.

The quintessenceNafplio is the only town in Greece where you can eat kakavia looking across the harbour at an island fortress while the first capital’s political history unfolds in every neoclassical façade around you. The three fortresses are not ruins on a tourist map; they are the reason the street grid of the old town exists in the shape it does, and the reason the fish tavernas ended up on the waterfront rather than the hill. The bowl is Venetian; the soup is Greek; the light at dusk, when the Bourtzi goes amber on the water, belongs to neither and to both.

HISTORY & TERROIR
Where it comes from

Named for the mythical Danaid king Nauplius, the site was occupied continuously from the Neolithic through Mycenaean, Classical and Hellenistic periods before the Franks built the first castle on Acronauplia in the early 13th century.

The Venetians took Nafplio in 1388 and held it until 1540 (First Venetian period), losing it to the Ottomans after a long siege. The Second Venetian period (1686–1715), won in the Morean War, saw the construction of Palamidi (1711–14) and the expansion of the Bourtzi sea-castle under the Provveditore Agostino Sagredo. The Ottomans returned in 1715; the city fell to the Greek revolutionary forces on 30 November 1822 after a prolonged siege. In January 1823 the National Assembly named it Greece’s first capital. The decade that followed — Kapodistrias’s governorship, his assassination at St. Spyridon’s door in 1831, the regency of King Otto — left the neoclassical building programme that the old town is today. Athens replaced it as capital in 1834, and Nafplio, spared the Athenian building pressure, kept its 19th-century fabric largely intact.

WHY NOW
The right moment

Come in April or May — the Peloponnese spring light is better than anywhere else in Europe at that hour, the Palamidi slopes have wildflowers, the market has asparagus and wild greens, and the town is under-visited enough that you can still eat at Savouras without a wait. Or come in September or October for the Nemea grape harvest, when the valley 30 km away goes amber and the wine shops fill with that year’s Agiorgitiko. Nafplio is 140 km from Athens — two hours by car, close enough to draw the Athenian weekend crowd in all seasons, which is part of why the town’s restaurant culture is better than its size would suggest.

WHAT TO EAT
The Argolic table

The table is the Argolic Gulf and the Peloponnese plain behind it — anchovy, sea bream, scorpionfish and squid from the gulf; olive oil from the coastal groves; feta and graviera from Argolid goats; lamb from the interior valleys; wild thyme honey from the Arcadian hills.

The canonical opening is kakavia — the rough fisherman’s soup, bones and all, a broth of whatever the net brought in, brightened with celery and lemon, and found properly only at the plain waterfront tavernas like Savouras, not the tablecloth restaurants. Alongside the fish table, the back-street tavernas — Omorfo Tavernaki being the one to seek — serve a meze card rooted in the Peloponnese plateau: dolmades with avgolemono, braised lamb, slow-cooked greens with olive oil. And the one sweet the town has made its own is loukoumades drizzled with dark Peloponnesian thyme honey — still warm from the fryer, eaten standing, the correct way to finish a Nafplio morning.

WHAT TO DRINK
Nemea and the local cellar

The wine is Nemea — the valley 30 km inland where the Agiorgitiko (St. George) grape makes reds from light-cherry to deep and structured. It is the only appellation close enough to Nafplio to count as the local pour.

The producers to know: Gaia Estate, Semeli, and Skouras are the names most wine lists in town build around. In Karonis, the old family shop near Syntagma, you can taste before you buy and leave with a fair-priced bottle of the current vintage. For a deeper session by the glass, Alkioni Wine Bar runs a Peloponnese-focused list and can walk you through the producers. Outside wine: tsipouro poured neat is the spirit of the Peloponnese interior; you will find it at Karonis and by the carafe in the tavernas.

WHERE THE MONEY SITS
What funds the table

Tourism is overwhelmingly the economic engine — weekend Athenian visitors, growing international traffic, the Mycenae and Epidaurus day-trip circuit — and the restaurant culture is as good as it is largely because of Athens, not despite it.

The food money is best spent at the plain waterfront tavernas on the fresh-catch plates, at the back-street family kitchens for the meze, at Karonis for bottles to take home, and at the Saturday farmers market for Argolid oil and thyme honey. Skip the Syntagma Square café menus — tourist premium in a beautiful room — and walk thirty seconds into the side streets. The Antica Gelateria is the only thing on the Aslipiou arcade worth paying the arcade price for; everything else on that street is at tourist-strip markup.

Beaches & coves
The peninsula, the sandy south, the resort bay
3 beaches

Nafplio is not a beach town in the Aegean island sense — it is a fortress town with beaches at the foot of its walls. The swimming is organised by proximity to the old town: Arvanitia is ten minutes on foot; Karathona is five minutes by car; Tolo is the full sandy resort, 11 km south.

#1
BEACH

Arvanitia Beach & Promenade

The town beach under the fortress walls — a small pebble-and-rock cove at the end of the most beautiful flat walk in Nafplio, the paved cliff promenade around the base of Acronauplia.

Rocky entry but clear water; the sea is protected by the Acronauplia mass and stays calm when the Gulf is rough. The promenade walk from the Boumpoulinas waterfront (1.5 km, 30 min, flat) is itself the point — you walk below the Venetian walls with the Argolic Gulf to your left. A seasonal beach bar, no loungers. Best in May, June and September when the summer heat is off.

#2
BEACH

Karathona Beach

The town’s main sandy beach — a 2 km arc of pale sand south of Palamidi, backed by pine and tamarisk, with the fortress profile above it and the full width of the gulf ahead.

Shallow entry, organised lounger area and a beach restaurant, quieter than you expect given the proximity to the old town. Walk around the base of Palamidi (about 3 km), or take the short road that cuts below the fortress. The afternoon has better shade from the pines; morning light on the fortress above is the reason to come early.

5 min drive; 3 km walkable · Open in Google Maps →
#3
BEACH

Tolo (Tolon) Beach

The resort option — 11 km south of Nafplio by car, a wide sandy bay with shallow water, full facilities and the island of Romvi offshore. The Peloponnese family beach.

Tolo is the beach for a full day in the water: long sandy shore, shallow and calm, organised loungers and several taverna rows along the front. Not a wilderness but genuinely good swimming, and the ferry out to the small island of Romvi is a half-hour trip worth taking before the afternoon crowd. Best avoided in peak August when it fills with package holidays from the surrounding hotels.

Walks & climbs
The promenade, the 999 steps, the old quarter
3 walks
#1
WALK

Arvanitia Promenade

The flat sea-cliff walk around the base of Acronauplia — paved, 1.5 km, 30 minutes, the most beautiful easy walk in Nafplio.

Start from the Boumpoulinas waterfront (near the cannons) and follow the paved path around the seaward face of Acronauplia to Arvanitia beach. Entirely below the Venetian walls, with the Argolic Gulf to the left throughout. Flat, no stairs, accessible; the return is the same path or through the town. Best in the early morning before the heat, or late afternoon for the light on the water.

#2
WALK

Palamidi Steps Climb

The canonical hard walk — 999 hewn steps from Polyzoidou street rising 216 metres to the Venetian gate; the single best view in the Peloponnese at the top.

Count them if you like: the steps vary in accounts from 857 to over 1,000 depending on where you start, but the popular figure is 999 and it captures the effort. The ascent takes 30–45 minutes at a steady pace; wear proper shoes and carry water. At the summit, the fortifications spread across eight linked bastions — Miltiades, Themistoklis, Achilles and the others — and the panorama takes in the old town directly below, the Bourtzi on its island, the Argolic Gulf, and on clear days the mountains behind Epidaurus. Go at sunrise or in the last hour before closing to avoid the organised tour groups.

999 steps · 30–45 min up · wear shoes · Open in Google Maps →
#3
WALK

Psaromachalas Old Quarter Loop

Through the fishermen’s quarter and back via Kapodistrias Street — the walk for reading the first capital’s neoclassical layers on foot.

Start from St. Spyridon’s door on Staikopoulou, walk north-east through the narrow lanes of Psaromachalas (laundry overhead, cats on the steps, the oldest domestic fabric in the town), loop back south past the old Parliament building on Kapodistrias Street and end on Syntagma Square for a coffee. About 2 km, 45 minutes at reading pace. The quarter is small enough that getting lost is part of the route.

Rooms to linger
The square morning, the pedestrian street evening
2 spots
#1
MORNING

Syntagma Square Morning

The mandatory first two hours — an outdoor table on the best-preserved neoclassical square in Greece, Greek coffee or frappé, watching the town wake up around the Venetian Arsenal façade.

The cafés that ring Syntagma charge tourist premium; this is not the revelation, the view is. The square’s eastern side catches the morning light first — the Archaeological Museum in the old Venetian Arsenal to your left, the neoclassical Parliament building ahead, St. Spyridon’s bell tower to the right. Frappé (iced instant, long, with sugar and milk) is the correct order; Greek coffee (ellenikos kafes) in a small copper pot is the local one. A pastry from the nearest bakery completes the four-part Nafplio morning. The same coffee two streets in is half the price.

#2
EVENING

Staikopoulou Evening Terrace

The pedestrian restaurant street for evening — the tables come out at dusk and the street does what it has done since the 19th century: it becomes an outdoor dining room under a shared sky.

Staikopoulou runs south-east from the old town and concentrates most of the old town’s outdoor restaurant tables. By 8pm in summer it is all but car-free and the tables from a dozen establishments blur into one long room. Not the place for a quiet corner; the place for the collective Nafplio evening: a carafe of local wine, a shared meze board, the sound of the street. If you want quiet, choose one of the back-street tavernas; if you want the town as a whole, come here.

Culture & landmarks
Three fortresses, two museums, one assassination
6 stops
#1
CULTURE

Palamidi Fortress

The Venetian masterwork of 1711–14, eight linked bastions at 216 metres, with the finest fortress panorama in the Peloponnese.

Built by the Provveditore Agostino Sagredo in three years (1711–14), the complex comprises eight bastions connected by a ring wall around the summit of the Palamidi rock — each named for a Greek military hero (Miltiades, Themistoklis, Achilles and others). The approach is either the 999 steps from below or the road from the east; the steps are the experience. The panorama from the top’s highest point takes in the old town, the Bourtzi, the gulf, and on clear days the mountains behind Epidaurus. The Greek revolutionary Theodoros Kolokotronis was imprisoned here by the Bavarian regency in 1833–34 — the very fortress his forces had stormed a decade earlier.

#2
CULTURE

Bourtzi Castle Island

The sea-fortress on its own island in the harbour, 300 m offshore — the guardian of the channel between itself and the mainland that once carried an iron chain across the water to control entry.

Originally built by the Venetians in 1473 over the islet of Agioi Theodoroi, expanded during subsequent periods, used as the public executioner’s residence in Ottoman and early Modern Greek times. Reached by a short boat trip from the Nafplio quay; when open, the interior offers a view back to the old town and Palamidi from the water’s level. Check opening status before crossing — restoration works have periodically closed the interior in recent years.

#3
CULTURE

Acronauplia (Its Kale)

The original fortified city on the seaward cliff — the oldest part of Nafplio, with Byzantine, Frankish and Venetian layers compressed into one peninsula promontory.

Known in Ottoman times as Its Kale (inner castle), the Acronauplia is the raised rocky promontory above the Boumpoulinas waterfront, enclosed within its own fortification walls that now contain a hotel complex. The Frankish gate at the base (13th century), the Byzantine and later Venetian walls on the seaward side, and the views back across the harbour to the Bourtzi are the draw. The Arvanitia promenade circles its base at sea level; the summit is accessible via the old town side or by lift from the hotel.

Free to walk around; upper terrace via hotel · Open in Google Maps →
#4
CULTURE

St. Spyridon Church

The church where Greece’s first Governor was assassinated — and the one building in Nafplio that condenses the whole drama of the early Greek state into a doorframe.

Ioannis Kapodistrias was shot at the church entrance on 9 October 1831 by the brothers Konstantinos and Georgios Mavromichalis, in a political assassination that plunged the new state into civil war. The church is small and simple, still active. The bullet-hole in the stonework is marked by a metal plate — a piece of European political history at street level, with no ticket and no queue. On the Staikopoulou side of the old town, easy to pass on the way to a meal.

Free; active church · Open in Google Maps →
#5
CULTURE

Archaeological Museum (Venetian Arsenal)

The national collection for the Argolid in a three-storey Venetian building on Syntagma Square — the best single room for the Mycenaean and Neolithic layers under the region you are walking through.

The building itself (1713, the Venetian powder magazine and arms store) is one of the finest examples of Venetian civic architecture in Greece. The collection covers the Argolid from the Neolithic to the late Roman period, with a strong Mycenaean section that contextualises the Mycenae site 28 km north. Best to visit in the middle heat of the day; allow ninety minutes for the collection and the building.

Ticket required; Syntagma Square · Open in Google Maps →
#6
CULTURE

Kolokotronis Square & Statue

The equestrian statue of Theodoros Kolokotronis, the kleft general who led the War of Independence — on the square named for him, a ten-minute walk north-east from Syntagma.

Kolokotronis (1770–1843) remains the most recognisable figure of the Greek War of Independence, and this is where Nafplio placed his monument. The square is quieter than Syntagma and more local — the morning coffee crowd, the plateia trees, the statue in profile against the Palamidi rock. The irony embedded in the placement: the Bavarian regency imprisoned Kolokotronis in the Palamidi fortress in 1833 — the very fortress behind the statue, which he had helped to capture in 1822.

THE QUARTERS
Four neighbourhoods, one peninsula

Nafplio’s old town is small enough to walk in forty minutes but layered enough to take a day.

The Psaromachalas quarter, at the north-east tip of the peninsula, is the oldest continuously inhabited part of the town — narrow lanes, laundry overhead, cats on the steps, the kind of quarter that has stayed residential because there was nothing grand enough to convert it into anything else. Syntagma Square is the civic heart, with the Venetian Arsenal, the old Parliament, and the café ring that sets the morning tone. Running south-east from Syntagma, Staikopoulou is the pedestrian restaurant street — where the tables come out at dusk and merge into a single outdoor room. The Boumpoulinas waterfront is where the fishing boats still tie up between the fish tavernas, and where the ferry to Bourtzi leaves from the quay. Walking from one quarter to the next, the chronological layers of the town shift — Venetian façades on the waterfront, neoclassical public buildings on the square, the older irregular fabric in Psaromachalas — and reading them against each other is the best way to understand what the ten years of the first capital left behind.

Market & the Argolid
The weekly market, the Nemea valley, the gulf produce
3 stops
#1
MARKET

Nafplio Farmers Market (Laïki Agorá)

The Wednesday and Saturday market — the Argolid plain and the Arcadian hills at a price the restaurant card never offers.

The laïki agorá is a working provisioning market, not a tourist one: small Argolid farmers alongside hill-village producers from the Arcadian highlands behind the coast. Nemea grapes in September, Argolid citrus in winter, wild greens and Peloponnesian asparagus in spring, thyme honey, dried herbs and early-harvest olive oil year-round. The key buys: Argolid extra-virgin in the green (peppery, bright, raw press), Peloponnesian thyme honey at the beekeepers’ stalls, and Kalamata-area olives at source. Arrive before 11am; it is done by noon.

#2
WINE COUNTRY

Nemea Wine Valley

The Agiorgitiko country, 30 km inland — one grape, one valley, a range from pale-cherry summer drinking to structured ten-year reds. The local wine for the whole Nafplio table.

The Nemea PDO zone centres on the valley town of Nemea (ancient sanctuary of Zeus, and home of the Nemean Games) and the surrounding villages. The Agiorgitiko (St. George) grape is one of Greece’s two or three most significant indigenous reds, capable of producing wines as different as a light rosé and a structured age-worthy red from the same appellation. Producers to visit (call ahead): Gaia Estate at Koutsi (the single-vineyard label that established Nemea’s modern reputation), Semeli (large producer, good cellar-door range), Skouras (consistently serious, the Megas Oenos label). Day trip from Nafplio; budget half a day for two cellar doors.

#3
PRODUCE

The Argolic Gulf Catch

The fishermen’s boats tie up on the Boumpoulinas promenade — the daily catch that goes directly from net to the waterfront tavernas before noon.

The Argolic Gulf is a semi-enclosed sea that produces anchovy, sea bream, red mullet, scorpionfish, squid, octopus and mussels. The catch is modest by industrial standards and mostly consumed locally — which is why the fish at Savouras is served the same morning it was caught, and why the kakavia is not always available by 2pm. The fish landed here does not travel to Athens; it stays in the tavernas within 300 metres of where the boats moor. Buy it there, not from a tourist fish display with a price card.

BEFORE YOU GO
Getting there, and two honest warnings

Nafplio is 140 km south-west of Athens by road (the E65 motorway to Corinth, then south; allow 2 hours). By bus: the KTEL Argolidas service from the Athens Kifissos terminal runs regularly and reaches Nafplio in about 2.5 hours. There is no train. By sea, the town is a stopover on bareboat-charter routes through the Argolic Gulf; the harbour has visitor berths on the Boumpoulinas quay.

The two honest warnings: the Palamidi steps in July at noon will beat you. And the Syntagma Square cafés will charge you for the view. Both are worth it; just be ready for them.
