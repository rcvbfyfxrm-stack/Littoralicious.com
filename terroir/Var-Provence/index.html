THE SOUL OF THE PLACE
What the place is, underneath

The Var is the Cote d'Azur without the sunglasses — or rather, the half that never needed them. Wedged between glittering Nice and Monaco to the east and the Marseille conurbation to the west, it keeps the biggest forests in the region: the cork oak and chestnut of the Maures, the red porphyry of the Esterel. More than half the department is wooded, second in France only to the Landes. This is a coast that still works for a living.

Its capital is not a resort but a warship town. Toulon is the largest naval base in the Mediterranean, home port of the carrier Charles de Gaulle and of most of the French fleet's tonnage. Offshore lie the Iles d'Hyeres — Porquerolles, Port-Cros, Le Levant — the "golden islands," much of them national park. Hyeres itself was the Riviera's first winter resort, all palms and Belle Epoque villas, decades before Cannes learned the trick.

And yes, Saint-Tropez is here too, with its superyachts and its Club 55. But drive twenty minutes and you are in a vigneron's cellar or a fishing port where the cade is still baked in a copper pan. The Var wears both without apology. It also remembers August 1944, when the "second D-Day," Operation Dragoon, put half a million Allied troops ashore on these very beaches and freed Provence in under a fortnight.

The quintessenceThe true ritual is the rose apero: late afternoon, the light going amber, a chilled bottle of Provence pink (or a serious Bandol) on a cafe table by the port at Sanary-sur-Mer or Le Brusc, a saucer of tapenade and olives, pastis for the older men, the pentaque balls clicking on the gravel. The morning version is the Provencal market — Sanary's is among the best on the coast — heaped with tomatoes, tapenade, cade and chickpea galettes. And to walk it off, the sentier du littoral, the old customs path first cut in 1791 against smugglers, threading the rocks cove by cove from Gigaro toward Cap Taillat.

THE STORY
How it came to be

The vine came by sea. Greek sailors from Phocaea, founders of Massalia (Marseille), brought viticulture to this coast in the 6th century BC, and the Romans later terraced the slopes above what is now Bandol and shipped the wine from its port. At Frejus they raised Forum Julii, founded around 49 BC by Julius Caesar as a counterweight to Greek Massalia; it was here that Octavian brought home the galleys captured from Mark Antony at Actium in 31 BC, and Augustus made it one of the three great naval bases of the western Mediterranean, then capital of Gallia Narbonensis in 22 BC.

Toulon's destiny was sealed by its deep, sheltered roadstead. Henri IV founded the arsenal (a shipyard from 1599); Richelieu, then Colbert and Vauban, enlarged it into France's Mediterranean naval capital. Inland, the Maures lived for centuries on cork and chestnuts — the cork oak of the massif corked the bottles of the south — and on wine. Bandol was granted its own appellation in 1941, largely through the work of Lucien Peyraud of Domaine Tempier (a farm in his wife Lulu's family since 1834), who rebuilt the region around its old, tannic Mourvedre grape.

Two later chapters define the modern Var. Hyeres and the Villa Noailles drew the interwar avant-garde — Man Ray, Bunuel, Giacometti — while nearby Sanary-sur-Mer became, after Hitler's rise in 1933, the self-styled "capital of German literature in exile": Thomas Mann, Brecht, Feuchtwanger, Werfel and Alma Mahler; Aldous Huxley had already written Brave New World there. Then, on 15 August 1944, Operation Dragoon landed between Cavalaire and Saint-Raphael and liberated Toulon and Marseille within two weeks — the invasion Provence, not Normandy, remembers as its own.

WHY NOW
When to come

It is high summer — the season the Var was built for and the one it can barely hold. The rose is being poured across a thousand terraces and the markets are at full tilt, while the great autumn set-piece, Les Voiles de Saint-Tropez (300 classic and modern yachts racing the bay), is still months off in late September. Come for the light and the sea, but know that the Saint-Tropez peninsula chokes in August; the reward goes to whoever slips to a working port like Sanary or Le Brusc, inland to the cellars, or onto a ferry to a car-free island.

WHERE THE MONEY SITS
What funds the table

Three economies share this coast. The oldest and steadiest is the Navy: roughly 20,000 military and civilian staff work the Toulon arsenal, which holds more than 60% of the French fleet's tonnage — unglamorous, permanent money that has nothing to do with the season. The loudest is summer tourism and the superyacht trade: the Port de Saint-Tropez takes boats up to about 70 metres, a week's charter runs from roughly 30,000 euros to a million, and France drew over 100 million visitors in 2025, with the Mediterranean coast absorbing much of the August crush.

The quiet fortune is wine. Provence makes roughly 40 to 45% of France's AOP rose and around 5% of the world's rose; Cotes de Provence alone is about 20,000 hectares, some 90% of it pink, and Sacha Lichine's Whispering Angel turned pale rose into a global luxury category now worth over a billion euros a year (Provence rose export value rose more than 1,000% between 2008 and 2017). That is where the markup lives — a fashionable pink can cost more than a red of real substance. The genuine value sits just to the west in Bandol: age-worthy Mourvedre reds, at least 18 months in old oak foudres, from Tempier or Pibarnon, still a fraction of a comparable Burgundy or Bordeaux. And Toulon, for all its lack of glamour, is where you eat and drink honestly for a third of a Saint-Tropez bill.

The dishes & the drink
The typical plates and the local product
10 dishes
DISH

Bouillabaisse

The famous rockfish stew, born from fishermen's unsellable catch: rascasse (scorpionfish) is essential, and since the 1980 Marseille charter the ritual is fixed — the saffron-orange broth first, with rouille and croutons, then the fish on a platter after. Eaten all along the Var, but be honest about its passport.

Origin Marseille (Bouches-du-Rhone) — not really a Var dish

DISH

Soupe de poissons

Bouillabaisse's humble, everyday cousin and what most Var bistros actually pour: rockfish simmered and then strained into a rust-coloured broth with no whole fish, served with rouille, garlic croutons and grated gruyere to raft on top.

Origin Provencal coast, generally

DISH

Cade toulonnaise

Toulon's own chickpea-flour galette, baked thin in wide copper pans over a wood fire — a Ligurian cousin of Nice's socca. Legend has it Napoleonic soldiers brought it and 'caldo' (hot) became 'cade.' The true Toulon apero, and a star of the Sanary market.

Origin Toulon

DISH

Chichi fregi

A sugared fried doughnut of wheat and chickpea flour, sold from seafront kiosks — with the cade, one of only two dishes claimed as 100% Toulonnais. Beach food, defended locally with near-religious fervour.

Origin Toulon

DISH

Daube provencale

Beef simmered for hours in red wine with orange peel and herbs, traditionally in a bulbous clay or copper daubiere that rains the steam back over the meat. Peasant and carters' cooking; the name descends from Provencal 'adobar,' to prepare.

Origin Provence (inland origins, carters between Haute-Provence and Aix/Marseille)

DISH

Grand aioli

Less a sauce than an occasion: a stiff, pungent emulsion of raw garlic, egg yolk and olive oil, whipped by hand and served with a mountain of poached salt cod, boiled vegetables, eggs and sea snails, dipped communally. A pillar of Provencal identity, usually a Friday feast.

Origin Provence

DISH

Pieds et paquets

Sheep's trotters and little parcels of stuffed tripe stewed for most of a day in white wine and tomato — poverty cooking that wasted nothing, credited to Louis Ginouves around 1880. The Var eats it, but Marseille and Sisteron own it.

Origin Marseille / Sisteron

DISH

Tarte tropezienne

A sugar-crusted brioche split and filled with two creams (pastry cream and buttercream), created in 1955 by Polish patissier Alexandre Micka in Saint-Tropez. Brigitte Bardot, filming 'And God Created Woman,' suggested the name; Micka trademarked it in 1973.

Origin Saint-Tropez

DISH

Tapenade

The definitive rose-apero smear: black olives, capers (Provencal 'tapena,' hence the name) and anchovies pounded with olive oil. Invented in 1880 by chef Meynier at the restaurant La Maison Doree in Marseille — his original even carried tuna.

Origin Marseille

DISH

The tuna (thon)

Mediterranean bluefin was once caught in madragues — vast net-mazes — off the Var coast from the 17th century; Brigitte Bardot's Saint-Tropez house, La Madrague, is named for one. The traps are all but gone now, but grilled tuna and thon a la provencale remain the coast's great fish.

Origin Golfe de Saint-Tropez / Mediterranean tuna-trap heritage

The wines
The appellations that define the coast
4 wines
WINE

Bandol

The Var's glory and the reason to take the region seriously. Mourvedre-led (minimum 50%), the reds are aged at least 18 months in large old oak foudres — tannic and closed young, built to run for decades. AOC since 1941. Domaine Tempier (the Peyraud family; farm since 1834) and Chateau de Pibarnon (vines on hand-built restanque terraces in a natural amphitheatre of blue marl) are the references. The rose is serious too — the one Provence pink genuinely worth cellaring a year.

WINE

Cotes de Provence

The workhorse and the volume: the largest Provencal appellation, roughly 20,000 hectares, about 90% of it rose (Grenache, Cinsault, Syrah, Rolle). Quality runs from industrial to genuinely fine. Its cooler inland sibling, Coteaux Varois en Provence around Brignoles, is often the better value.

WINE

Cassis

An honest edge case — just over the Var line in Bouches-du-Rhone, east of Marseille, entirely inside the Calanques National Park. One of France's first AOCs (1936) and, rare for Provence, a white-wine appellation (Marsanne, Clairette): saline and mineral, made for the sea urchins and grilled fish under the cliffs. Included because the terroir belongs to this coast even if the postcode doesn't.

WINE

Provence rose (the machine)

The caveat, told straight: Provence is the world's rose engine (about 40-45% of France's AOP rose, ~5% of global rose), and Sacha Lichine's Whispering Angel made pale pink a luxury category worth over a billion euros a year. Much of it is fashion, priced above reds of more substance. Drink it young, cold and local — but don't mistake the colour for the region's real depth, which is in Bandol.

Beaches & the coast
Where to swim and walk
5 spots
#1
BEACH

Plage Notre-Dame, Porquerolles (Hyeres)

An 800-metre crescent of pale sand and turquoise water on the largest of the Iles d'Hyeres, backed by pine and oak and protected within the Parc national de Port-Cros; voted 'most beautiful beach in Europe' by European Best Destinations in 2015.

Heads up — Cars are banned on the island: reach it by ferry from La Tour Fondue (Giens), then a 45-minute walk or 25-minute bike ride from the village. No shade beyond the trees, and it fills in July-August.

#2
BEACH

Pampelonne (Ramatuelle, Saint-Tropez)

The Riviera's most storied beach, 4.5 km of sand where the beach-club era began in the 1950s — Club 55 dates to 1955, when it fed Bardot's film crew.

Heads up — Much of the sand is carved into private beach-club concessions with matching prices; the public stretches are real but you must hunt for them, and access and parking are a summer ordeal.

#3
BEACH

Cap Lardier / Gigaro (La Croix-Valmer)

Wild, protected coves at the tip of the Saint-Tropez peninsula, held by the Conservatoire du Littoral; the finest walk on the coast, the sentier du littoral, runs from Gigaro beach around Cap Lardier to Cap Taillat.

Heads up — Largely undeveloped — little or no shade, few facilities, you carry in what you need and walk to the best coves; some cliff sections are genuinely exposed.

#4
BEACH

Le Brusc / Petit Gaou (Six-Fours-les-Plages)

A quieter, family and nature end of the coast: the basalt-black Gaou peninsula, reached by footbridge, and a 40-hectare lagoon sheltered by a posidonia reef and the Embiez archipelago (Paul Ricard's island, a 12-minute shuttle from the port of Le Brusc).

Heads up — Not the postcard blond sand — much of it is rock, basalt and lagoon shallows; charming but modest, and the water is calm rather than dramatic.

Open in Google Maps →
#5
BEACH

Calanque de Figuerolles / du Mugel (La Ciotat)

Dramatic pudding-stone cliffs and clear green water at the western gate of the Calanques National Park; Figuerolles is a tiny wild inlet under the Rocher du Capucin, Mugel a sheltered cove below a botanical garden.

Heads up — Strictly this is Bouches-du-Rhone, just over the Var border. Mugel is pebble, not sand; Figuerolles is reached down a steep flight of steps with rocky footing — bring water shoes, and mind national-park rules.
