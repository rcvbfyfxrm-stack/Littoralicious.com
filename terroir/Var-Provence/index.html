The name

The Var takes its name from the river Varus (Greek Ouaros) — an ancient hydronym for the water, not the land. It is the one case in France of a departement named after a river that no longer flows through it: when Nice was annexed in 1860 and the new departement of Alpes-Maritimes was formed, absorbing the Var's eastern arrondissement of Grasse, the river itself passed into it and left its namesake behind.

The reading most philologists accept — Indo-European, and probably Celtic: a very old water-word built on the root *uor-/*uer-, simply 'water, river' (cognate with Sanskrit var and Old Norse vari), and echoed in a scatter of European stream names. It would be one of the deepest river-naming elements in the region.

An older, weaker reading reaches for a pre-Latin, possibly Ligurian substrate — sometimes tied to a word for 'valley' (compare Basque ibar). The popular link to Latin varus, 'bent, crooked,' as a supposed portrait of the winding torrent, is a folk etymology scholars do not accept. Which layer the name truly descends from has never been fully settled.

Attested in classical antiquity — Greek Ouaros in Strabo, Latin Varus in Pliny — with the river long marking the eastern boundary of Roman Italy. The departement name is a Revolutionary coinage: France's departments took effect on 4 March 1790, when the Var still ran along the new departement's eastern edge.

The working half of the Riviera. The Var is the stretch of the Cote d'Azur that never took its working clothes off — roughly 120 kilometres of coast from the Saint-Tropez peninsula in the east to the Bandol vineyards and the great Toulon roadstead in the west, with the car-free Iles d'Hyeres anchored offshore and the forested massif of the Maures rising behind. More than 60% of the departement is wooded — the highest forest cover of any departement in mainland France, behind only French Guiana and Corsica nationally: cork oak and sweet chestnut on the Maures, red porphyry on the Esterel. For every superyacht in Saint-Tropez there is a vigneron's cellar twenty minutes inland and a fishing pointu still landing daurade at dawn.

A warship town, not a resort. The hinge here is the sea, and the sea means the Navy. The departement's capital is not a beach but Toulon, the largest naval base in the Mediterranean — its deep, sheltered roadstead an arsenal since Henri IV in 1599, enlarged by Richelieu, Colbert and Vauban into France's Mediterranean naval capital. Some 20,000 people still work it, unglamorous permanent money that owes nothing to the season. Just west, the limestone amphitheatre of Bandol holds the coast's other working tradition: a farm couple, Lucien and Lulu Peyraud, who from the late 1930s rebuilt an entire appellation around Mourvedre, the tannic black grape that likes its feet in the sea and its head in the sun.

The islands, the exiles, the starlet. Hyeres was the Riviera's first winter resort, all palms and Belle Epoque villas decades before Cannes learned the trick; its Villa Noailles drew the interwar avant-garde — Man Ray, Bunuel, Giacometti — while a few coves west, Sanary-sur-Mer became after 1933 what came to be called the 'capital of German literature in exile' (Thomas Mann, Brecht, Feuchtwanger; Aldous Huxley had already written Brave New World there). Offshore lie the Iles d'Hyeres — Porquerolles, Port-Cros, Le Levant, the 'golden islands,' much of them national park and closed to cars. And in 1956 a starlet named Brigitte Bardot filmed And God Created Woman across the bay in Saint-Tropez, and the fishing village never slept again.

When to come. The terroir reaches the table as a ritual: the rose apero at a working port like Sanary or Le Brusc — a chilled Provence pink or a serious Bandol, tapenade and olives, the petanque clicking on the gravel — then the inland sweetness of Collobrieres chestnut and Sollies fig. High summer is the season the Var was built for and can barely hold: come for the light and the sea, but know the Saint-Tropez peninsula chokes in August, so slip to a working port, up to the cellars, or onto a ferry to a car-free island (Porquerolles from La Tour Fondue on the Giens peninsula). Fly into Toulon-Hyeres, take the TGV to Toulon, or arrive by boat to skip the peninsula road; the great autumn set-piece, Les Voiles de Saint-Tropez, races the bay in late September into early October, once the crowds have thinned.

Getting there. From late June to early September the Var funnels through two chokepoints. The best way: Take the first Porquerolles ferry of the day from La Tour Fondue and pre-book a bike in the village: the island's roughly 50 km of car-free trails empty out by mid-morning once the day-trippers cluster at the port and Plage Notre-Dame.

A department that lost its own river

The Var is the only French department whose namesake river no longer flows through it: when Alpes-Maritimes was created in 1860 after Nice joined France, the new border left the Var river running through the neighbouring department, and it has not touched the Var since.

Porquerolles was bought as a wedding gift

In February 1912 the Belgian adventurer Francois Joseph Fournier, who had struck it rich in Mexican gold mines, bought the entire island of Porquerolles at auction, reportedly for about 1.1 million francs, as a present for his wife, then planted some 200 hectares of vines on it.

THE SOUL OF THE PLACE
What the place is, underneath

The Var is the Cote d'Azur without the sunglasses — or rather, the half that never needed them. Wedged between glittering Nice and Monaco to the east and the Marseille conurbation to the west, it keeps the biggest forests in the region: the cork oak and chestnut of the Maures, the red porphyry of the Esterel. More than half the department is wooded, second in France only to the Landes. This is a coast that still works for a living.

Its capital is not a resort but a warship town. Toulon is the largest naval base in the Mediterranean, home port of the carrier Charles de Gaulle and of most of the French fleet's tonnage. Offshore lie the Iles d'Hyeres — Porquerolles, Port-Cros, Le Levant — the "golden islands," much of them national park. Hyeres itself was the Riviera's first winter resort, all palms and Belle Epoque villas, decades before Cannes learned the trick.

And yes, Saint-Tropez is here too, with its superyachts and its Club 55. But drive twenty minutes and you are in a vigneron's cellar or a fishing port where the cade is still baked in a copper pan. The Var wears both without apology. It also remembers August 1944, when the "second D-Day," Operation Dragoon, put half a million Allied troops ashore on these very beaches and freed Provence in under a fortnight.

The quintessenceThe true ritual is the rose apero: late afternoon, the light going amber, a chilled bottle of Provence pink (or a serious Bandol) on a cafe table by the port at Sanary-sur-Mer or Le Brusc, a saucer of tapenade and olives, pastis for the older men, the pentaque balls clicking on the gravel. The morning version is the Provencal market — Sanary's is among the best on the coast — heaped with tomatoes, tapenade, cade and chickpea galettes. And to walk it off, the sentier du littoral, the old customs path first cut in 1791 against smugglers, threading the rocks cove by cove from Gigaro toward Cap Taillat.

THE STORY
How it came to be

The vine came by sea. Greek sailors from Phocaea, founders of Massalia (Marseille), brought viticulture to this coast in the 6th century BC, and the Romans later terraced the slopes above what is now Bandol and shipped the wine from its port. At Frejus they raised Forum Julii, founded around 49 BC by Julius Caesar as a counterweight to Greek Massalia; it was here that Octavian brought home the galleys captured from Mark Antony at Actium in 31 BC, and Augustus made it one of the three great naval bases of the western Mediterranean, then capital of Gallia Narbonensis in 22 BC.

Toulon's destiny was sealed by its deep, sheltered roadstead. Henri IV founded the arsenal (a shipyard from 1599); Richelieu, then Colbert and Vauban, enlarged it into France's Mediterranean naval capital. Inland, the Maures lived for centuries on cork and chestnuts — the cork oak of the massif corked the bottles of the south — and on wine. Bandol was granted its own appellation in 1941, largely through the work of Lucien Peyraud of Domaine Tempier (a farm in his wife Lulu's family since 1834), who rebuilt the region around its old, tannic Mourvedre grape.

Two later chapters define the modern Var. Hyeres and the Villa Noailles drew the interwar avant-garde — Man Ray, Bunuel, Giacometti — while nearby Sanary-sur-Mer became, after Hitler's rise in 1933, the self-styled "capital of German literature in exile": Thomas Mann, Brecht, Feuchtwanger, Werfel and Alma Mahler; Aldous Huxley had already written Brave New World there. Then, on 15 August 1944, Operation Dragoon landed between Cavalaire and Saint-Raphael and liberated Toulon and Marseille within two weeks — the invasion Provence, not Normandy, remembers as its own.

WHY NOW
When to come

It is high summer — the season the Var was built for and the one it can barely hold. The rose is being poured across a thousand terraces and the markets are at full tilt, while the great autumn set-piece, Les Voiles de Saint-Tropez (300 classic and modern yachts racing the bay), is still months off in late September. Come for the light and the sea, but know that the Saint-Tropez peninsula chokes in August; the reward goes to whoever slips to a working port like Sanary or Le Brusc, inland to the cellars, or onto a ferry to a car-free island.

WHERE THE MONEY SITS
What funds the table

Three economies share this coast. The oldest and steadiest is the Navy: roughly 20,000 military and civilian staff work the Toulon arsenal, which holds more than 60% of the French fleet's tonnage — unglamorous, permanent money that has nothing to do with the season. The loudest is summer tourism and the superyacht trade: the Port de Saint-Tropez takes boats up to about 70 metres, a week's charter runs from roughly 30,000 euros to a million, and France drew over 100 million visitors in 2025, with the Mediterranean coast absorbing much of the August crush.

The quiet fortune is wine. Provence makes roughly 40 to 45% of France's AOP rose and around 5% of the world's rose; Cotes de Provence alone is about 20,000 hectares, some 90% of it pink, and Sacha Lichine's Whispering Angel turned pale rose into a global luxury category now worth over a billion euros a year (Provence rose export value rose more than 1,000% between 2008 and 2017). That is where the markup lives — a fashionable pink can cost more than a red of real substance. The genuine value sits just to the west in Bandol: age-worthy Mourvedre reds, at least 18 months in old oak foudres, from Tempier or Pibarnon, still a fraction of a comparable Burgundy or Bordeaux. And Toulon, for all its lack of glamour, is where you eat and drink honestly for a third of a Saint-Tropez bill.

DISH

Bouillabaisse

The famous rockfish stew, born from fishermen's unsellable catch: rascasse (scorpionfish) is essential, and since the 1980 Marseille charter the ritual is fixed — the saffron-orange broth first, with rouille and croutons, then the fish on a platter after. Eaten all along the Var, but be honest about its passport.

Origin Marseille (Bouches-du-Rhone) — not really a Var dish

DISH

Soupe de poissons

Bouillabaisse's humble, everyday cousin and what most Var bistros actually pour: rockfish simmered and then strained into a rust-coloured broth with no whole fish, served with rouille, garlic croutons and grated gruyere to raft on top.

Origin Provencal coast, generally

DISH

Cade toulonnaise

Toulon's own chickpea-flour galette, baked thin in wide copper pans over a wood fire — a Ligurian cousin of Nice's socca. Legend has it Napoleonic soldiers brought it and 'caldo' (hot) became 'cade.' The true Toulon apero, and a star of the Sanary market.

Origin Toulon

DISH

Chichi fregi

A sugared fried doughnut of wheat and chickpea flour, sold from seafront kiosks — with the cade, one of only two dishes claimed as 100% Toulonnais. Beach food, defended locally with near-religious fervour.

Origin Toulon

DISH

Daube provencale

Beef simmered for hours in red wine with orange peel and herbs, traditionally in a bulbous clay or copper daubiere that rains the steam back over the meat. Peasant and carters' cooking; the name descends from Provencal 'adobar,' to prepare.

Origin Provence (inland origins, carters between Haute-Provence and Aix/Marseille)

DISH

Grand aioli

Less a sauce than an occasion: a stiff, pungent emulsion of raw garlic, egg yolk and olive oil, whipped by hand and served with a mountain of poached salt cod, boiled vegetables, eggs and sea snails, dipped communally. A pillar of Provencal identity, usually a Friday feast.

Origin Provence

DISH

Pieds et paquets

Sheep's trotters and little parcels of stuffed tripe stewed for most of a day in white wine and tomato — poverty cooking that wasted nothing, credited to Louis Ginouves around 1880. The Var eats it, but Marseille and Sisteron own it.

Origin Marseille / Sisteron

DISH

Tarte tropezienne

A sugar-crusted brioche split and filled with two creams (pastry cream and buttercream), created in 1955 by Polish patissier Alexandre Micka in Saint-Tropez. Brigitte Bardot, filming 'And God Created Woman,' suggested the name; Micka trademarked it in 1973.

Origin Saint-Tropez

DISH

Tapenade

The definitive rose-apero smear: black olives, capers (Provencal 'tapena,' hence the name) and anchovies pounded with olive oil. Invented in 1880 by chef Meynier at the restaurant La Maison Doree in Marseille — his original even carried tuna.

Origin Marseille

DISH

The tuna (thon)

Mediterranean bluefin was once caught in madragues — vast net-mazes — off the Var coast from the 17th century; Brigitte Bardot's Saint-Tropez house, La Madrague, is named for one. The traps are all but gone now, but grilled tuna and thon a la provencale remain the coast's great fish.

Origin Golfe de Saint-Tropez / Mediterranean tuna-trap heritage

Saint-Tropez — the scene & the legend
How a fishing village became the glossiest summer address — and where the money parties now
10 places

Saint-Tropez spent most of its life with its back to the world: a fortified fishing and naval village on the southern shore of its own gulf, hard to reach by land, named for an early Christian martyr, Torpes of Pisa, whose decapitated body legend says drifted ashore here in a boat. What broke the isolation was, first, a book and then a boat of a different kind. Guy de Maupassant's 1888 sailing memoir "Sur l'eau" lodged the place in the imagination of the Pointillist painter Paul Signac, who first sailed into the bay in 1892, fell for the light off the water, and settled — buying a house he called La Hune (a few years later, in 1897) and building a vast studio. He drew the others down: Henri Matisse spent the summer of 1904 working beside Signac and Henri-Edmond Cross and painted "Luxe, calme et volupté" here, its title lifted from Baudelaire. Signac bought the canvas; it is widely called the starting point of Fauvism, which makes this small port something like the birthplace of modern color. Charles Camoin and the avant-garde followed, and in the 1920s Colette bought a villa above the Baie des Canoubiers she named La Treille Muscate and wrote through the next decade there.

The second rupture was cinema. In 1956 Roger Vadim shot "Et Dieu créa la femme" on these streets and beaches with his young wife Brigitte Bardot, filming in real locations rather than a studio. Overnight Bardot became a sex symbol and Saint-Tropez became shorthand for sun, sex and freedom; she bought the villa La Madrague in 1958 and the jet set came after her. The paparazzi came after them. By the time Louis de Funès was playing a hapless local gendarme in the 1960s, the myth was fully built.

What was a canteen for a film crew is now one of the most expensive stretches of coast in France. In summer the gulf fills with superyachts moored stern-to along the old quay, the beach clubs of Pampelonne run daybed minimums and magnum service, and a village of a few thousand year-round residents swells with a crowd that comes to be seen. The light Signac chased is still free; almost nothing else in Saint-Tropez in August is.

BEACH CLUB

Le Club 55

The founding legend of Pampelonne and the most famous beach club in the world. Bernard and Geneviève de Colmont built wooden cabins on the Ramatuelle sand in 1954; in 1955, seeing the family eating on the beach, a young Brigitte Bardot asked Geneviève to feed the crew of 'Et Dieu créa la femme.' The family's beach hut fed 80 people over the shoot — with no electricity, Geneviève borrowed a Saint-Tropez baker's oven to roast the beef — and 'Le Club 55' was born, named for the year. Their son Patrice de Colmont ran it from 1972 until his death in October 2025 at 77; his daughter Camille now runs it.

The honest truth Not a party club — a lunch institution, and it trades hard on the legend. Book far ahead in season, expect Provençal classics at collector prices, and a paid sunbed on top; the scene is the point as much as the food.

Signal Founded 1955 by Bernard and Geneviève de Colmont as the canteen for the Bardot film crew. Son Patrice ran it 1972–2025; now run by his daughter Camille, still family-owned and open.

Sénéquier

The red-awninged café-institution on the old port. The Sénéquier name dates to 1887, when Marie and Martin Sénéquier opened a pâtisserie (famous for a nougat recipe of the same year) at the Place aux Herbes; the business expanded to the quayside, where it became a café in 1930. Saint-Tropez, in facts
The painters and Fauvism
Saint-Tropez is arguably where modern color was born. Paul Signac first sailed in during 1892 after reading Maupassant's sailing book 'Sur l'eau,' fell for the light and drew a colony of painters south (he bought his house La Hune a few years later, in 1897). In the summer of 1904 Matisse worked beside Signac and Henri-Edmond Cross and painted 'Luxe, calme et volupté' (title from Baudelaire) — the canvas Signac bought and that is widely called the starting point of Fauvism.
The 1956 Bardot film
Roger Vadim's 'Et Dieu créa la femme' (1956), shot on location in the village with his young wife Brigitte Bardot, turned a quiet port into the capital of jet-set glamour. Bardot became an international sex symbol and bought the villa La Madrague in 1958; the stars and the paparazzi followed her south.
Les Voiles de Saint-Tropez regatta
The great late-September/early-October classic-yacht regatta traces to 1981, when a friendly challenge — associated with Patrice de Colmont of Club 55 — pitted two boats, Ikra and Pride, in a race around the Nioulargo shoal to a finish at Pampelonne. Ikra won and took a silver platter and a free lunch at Club 55. Run from 1982 as 'La Nioulargue' until a fatal 1995 collision halted it, it was revived in 1999 as Les Voiles de Saint-Tropez.
Place des Lices and pétanque
The town's social heart is a sandy square shaded by rows of plane trees. It hosts the Tuesday and Saturday market, then turns back into a pétanque court where boules are still thrown in the afternoon — a scene painted by Signac, Matisse and Camoin from the terrace of the old Café des Arts.
The gendarmes and Louis de Funès
Louis de Funès played bumbling gendarme Ludovic Cruchot in 'Le Gendarme de Saint-Tropez' (1964), the top-grossing French film of that year (about 7.8 million admissions) and the first of six films in the saga. The town's real gendarmerie, in service from 1879 to 2003, reopened in 2016 as the Musée de la Gendarmerie et du Cinéma.
The Tarte Tropézienne
The town's signature cake — a sugar-crusted brioche split and filled with two creams — was created in 1955 by Polish-born pâtissier Alexandre Micka, who catered the 'Et Dieu créa la femme' shoot. Brigitte Bardot, eating them on set, is credited with suggesting the name 'tarte tropézienne'; Micka registered it as a trademark in 1973.
A working fish market survives
Beneath the glamour the port keeps a genuine covered fish market in the old town, tucked behind the Quai Jean Jaurès at the Place aux Herbes, where fishermen still sell the day's catch — sea bream, bass, red mullet, scorpion fish, octopus — laid out on marble slabs, and where the chefs of the expensive restaurants do their morning rounds. It trades mornings (roughly 8am–1pm); the tourist office lists Thursday to Saturday, with near-daily selling through high summer.
La Ponche, the old fishing quarter
Below the citadel, the original fishermen's quarter, La Ponche — its name from the Provençal 'la pouncho,' 'the point' — keeps cobbled lanes and colored facades far from the yacht crowd. Its Hôtel La Ponche grew from a 1930s fisherman's bar (where Bardot, Vadim and Romy Schneider once stayed) into a five-star hotel, restored in a 2021 renovation.
The wines
The appellations that define the coast

WINE

Bandol

The Var's glory and the reason to take the region seriously. Mourvedre-led (minimum 50%), the reds are aged at least 18 months in large old oak foudres — tannic and closed young, built to run for decades. AOC since 1941. Domaine Tempier (the Peyraud family; farm since 1834) and Chateau de Pibarnon (vines on hand-built restanque terraces in a natural amphitheatre of blue marl) are the references. The rose is serious too — the one Provence pink genuinely worth cellaring a year.

WINE

Cotes de Provence

The workhorse and the volume: the largest Provencal appellation, roughly 20,000 hectares, about 90% of it rose (Grenache, Cinsault, Syrah, Rolle). Quality runs from industrial to genuinely fine. Its cooler inland sibling, Coteaux Varois en Provence around Brignoles, is often the better value.

WINE

Cassis

An honest edge case — just over the Var line in Bouches-du-Rhone, east of Marseille, entirely inside the Calanques National Park. One of France's first AOCs (1936) and, rare for Provence, a white-wine appellation (Marsanne, Clairette): saline and mineral, made for the sea urchins and grilled fish under the cliffs. Included because the terroir belongs to this coast even if the postcode doesn't.

WINE

Provence rose (the machine)

The caveat, told straight: Provence is the world's rose engine (about 40-45% of France's AOP rose, ~5% of global rose), and Sacha Lichine's Whispering Angel made pale pink a luxury category worth over a billion euros a year. Much of it is fashion, priced above reds of more substance. Drink it young, cold and local — but don't mistake the colour for the region's real depth, which is in Bandol.

What to Eat
aioli → chestnut
the canon

Raw garlic pounded to a paste at noon by the sea; sweet chestnut roasting in the hills by October — the Var eats from the coast to the forest in a single day's drive.

The coast's two pillars are garlic and slow fire. The grand aioli is less a sauce than a Friday occasion: a stiff, pungent emulsion of raw garlic, egg yolk and olive oil whipped by hand, served with a mountain of poached salt cod, boiled vegetables, eggs and little sea snails to dip communally. Its cold-weather twin is the daube provencale — beef braised for hours in red wine with orange peel and herbs, traditionally in a bulbous clay daubiere that rains the condensed steam back down onto the meat. Both are peasant cooking the coast simply never abandoned, and Toulon adds two of its own: the cade, a chickpea-flour galette baked thin over a wood fire, and the sugared, fried chichi fregi.

The great beach sandwich, the pan bagnat, is worth ordering and worth one honest footnote: it is Nicois, not Var — pan banhat, 'bathed bread' in the old Nissart tongue, a whole salade nicoise of tuna, egg, anchovy, tomato and olive oil crushed into a round loaf until the bread drinks the oil. The catch that lets it belong on this coast at all is a matter of borders: the very line splitting the shore into the Var and the Alpes-Maritimes was only drawn in 1860, when Nice was annexed to France and the Var shed its eastern arrondissement — two neighbouring kitchens whose frontier is barely a century and a half old.

Turn inland and the register goes sweet. Collobrieres, deep in the Maures, is the chestnut capital of Provence — some 900 hectares of groves around the village, part of a wider chestnut belt across the massif (La Garde-Freinet, Gonfaron, Les Mayons, Pignans) — feeding its marrons glaces (the Confiserie Azureenne has candied them there for over half a century) and a chestnut festival across three weekends each October. East of Toulon, Sollies-Pont calls itself the capital of the fig: the purple-black Bourjassotte Noire, granted protected origin as the Figue de Sollies AOP in 2006, with the Sollies basin alone growing well over half of France's fresh figs, sold dead-ripe from the vergers from late summer into October.

What to Drink
rose → Mourvedre
in the glass

The colour of a Var afternoon is pale pink; its real depth is almost black.

Provence is the world's rose engine — roughly 40 to 45% of France's AOP rose — and the Var is its heartland. Cotes de Provence, the largest of the region's appellations at some 20,000 hectares and about 90% pink (Grenache, Cinsault, Syrah, Rolle), an AOC since 1977, is the wine of the apero: poured cold at sundown from a thousand terraces. Sacha Lichine's Whispering Angel, made at Chateau d'Esclans in the Var hills, turned pale pink into a global luxury category — drink it young, cold and local, but know that much of it is fashion priced above reds of more substance. The cooler inland Coteaux Varois around Brignoles is often the better value.

The region's true glory is Bandol, granted its own AOC on 11 November 1941. The reds are led by Mourvedre — a legal minimum of 50%, usually far more — and aged at least eighteen months in large old oak foudres: tannic and closed young, built to run for decades. This is one of France's great age-worthy reds still selling for a fraction of a comparable Burgundy or Bordeaux, and the one Provence pink genuinely worth cellaring a year is a serious Bandol rose.

The references sit in a tight arc of hills behind the bay: Domaine Tempier (the Peyraud family, farm since 1834), Chateau de Pibarnon (Mourvedre on limestone and marl above 300 metres, staring straight down at the bay of La Ciotat), and the organic Domaine de Terrebrune at Ollioules, whose reds are prized for their salinity. The ritual is patience: buy the flagship red and forget it for a decade, or pour the Bandol rose beside a whole grilled fish and drink the coast at its most serious.

1941 · the couple who saved a black grape
The one story worth the detour
story

Before Lucien and Lulu Peyraud, Mourvedre was being torn out of Bandol for grapes that yielded more; after them, it became one of the great red wines of France.

The bastide at Domaine Tempier had stood in the Tempier family since 1834, but the wine was ordinary and the future looked pinker. Across Bandol, growers were pulling up their old Mourvedre — low-yielding, slow, stubborn to ripen — and replanting with varieties that gave more juice for less trouble. When Lucie 'Lulu' Tempier married Lucien Peyraud in 1936 and her father handed the young couple the estate, Lucien made exactly the opposite bet.

He was convinced Mourvedre was the historic grape of this coast and its salvation: slow to ripen but resistant to oxidation, capable of wines that could age for decades. He championed a protected appellation, and on 11 November 1941 Bandol was granted its AOC — with Mourvedre written into the rules. Lucien Peyraud is remembered as the father of the modern Bandol appellation; the appellation's insistence on Mourvedre, and its long rest in old oak foudres, is essentially his doing.

The second chapter is a kitchen. The American food writer Richard Olney lived nearby and, in the 1970s, introduced the Peyrauds to a young California importer, Kermit Lynch — who has shipped Tempier to the United States since 1976 — and to Alice Waters of Chez Panisse. Lulu's unfussy Provencal cooking, laid out under the plane trees, became a touchstone of the American farm-to-table movement; Olney devoted a whole book to it, Lulu's Provencal Table, in 1994. Lulu Peyraud died in 2020, aged 102, having watched a grape she and her husband refused to give up become a benchmark of France.

Go here Domaine Tempier — cellar-door tastings at the family estate at Le Plan-du-Castellet (commune of Le Castellet), in the Bandol AOC; visits by appointment, book ahead.

POUR

Bandol Rouge (Mourvèdre)

The one Var wine built to age like a serious red, because the appellation forces it to: at least 50% Mourvèdre, and eighteen months' ageing - much of it in old-oak foudre - before release. Late-ripening Mourvèdre only finishes fully in this sea-facing amphitheatre of limestone terraces (Pibarnon climbs to 300m), which is why no other French appellation is built around it. Young, it drinks of leather, garrigue and black olive more than fruit; decant it.

Look for Domaine Tempier (Le Plan-du-Castellet), Château de Pibarnon (La Cadière-d'Azur), Château Pradeaux (Saint-Cyr-sur-Mer), Domaine du Gros'Noré (Le Castellet)

POUR

Bandol Rosé

Not the pale poolside kind. Tempier's rosé runs roughly 55% Mourvèdre - darker and structured, built for bouillabaisse rather than sunbathing. It was Domaine Tempier, carried to America by importer Kermit Lynch from the late 1970s, that taught drinkers a Provençal rosé could be a serious wine. Treat it like a light red: it takes fish soup, aïoli, even lamb.

Look for Domaine Tempier, Château de Pibarnon, Domaine de la Bégude

POUR

Côtes de Provence Rosé

The everyday Var pour, and an industry: rosé is around 80% of the appellation's output, mostly Grenache-Cinsault-Syrah pressed pale and bone-dry. Drink the most recent vintage, cold and young - it is not built to keep, and last year's bottle is already tiring.

Look for Château Minuty (Gassin), Domaines Ott – Clos Mireille (La Londe-les-Maures), Château Léoube (Bormes-les-Mimosas), Château de Berne (Lorgues)

POUR

Bellet (the Riviera neighbour)

Be honest - Bellet is not in the Var. It sits inside the city limits of Nice (Alpes-Maritimes), one of France's smallest AOCs at about 55 hectares, and it earns a place here because nothing else on the coast tastes like it. Whites are Rolle (Vermentino); reds and rosés lean on two near-endemic grapes, Braquet and Folle Noire (Fuella). Production is tiny and rarely leaves the region - a bottle to hunt, not to expect.

Look for Château de Bellet, Clos Saint-Vincent, Château de Crémat

POUR

Pastis

The 6pm ritual, not a wine. Star anise, liquorice and garrigue herbs, cut roughly five parts cold water to one until it louches milky - water first, then ice, so the anethole doesn't clump. It is not made in the Var: Ricard is Marseille's child, created there in 1932 amid the wave of pastis that filled the void left by the 1915 absinthe ban. Reach for Henri Bardouin, built on more than sixty plants and spices, if you want to taste the herbs past the liquorice.

Look for Ricard and Pastis 51 (Marseille), Henri Bardouin – Distilleries et Domaines de Provence (Forcalquier)

POUR

Fleur de Figue (fig liqueur)

The Var's other obsession is the fig - the violet Bourjassotte Noire of Solliès-Pont, France's only AOP fig. This liqueur, macerated and bottled in the Var at Taradeau, is September in a glass: a digestif, or splashed into cold rosé or sparkling wine as an apéritif. Real fruit, none of the synthetic fig-candy note.

Look for Liquoristerie de Provence (Taradeau)

POUR

Liqueur de châtaigne / Crème de marrons (Collobrières)

Collobrières, deep in the Massif des Maures, is the chestnut capital of the coast, its wooded slopes long planted with châtaigneraies. The Confiserie Azuréenne - making marrons glacés since 1945 - turns the chestnut into everything, alcohol included, alongside its celebrated crème de marrons. Autumn drinking; the village chestnut festival fills the last three Sundays of October.

Look for Confiserie Azuréenne (Collobrières)

MUSEUM

Villa Noailles

Robert Mallet-Stevens' 1920s modernist villa above Hyères, now an art centre, with a triangular Cubist garden and two summer festivals born inside it.

Charles and Marie-Laure de Noailles, the most reckless art patrons of their day, handed the young Robert Mallet-Stevens a hillside and asked for 'an interesting little house to live in.' In successive campaigns from 1923 to 1933 he answered with a white, cubic manifesto of the Modern Movement: indoor pool, gymnasium, squash court, and a triangular Cubist garden built in 1926-27 by Gabriel Guévrékian, once crowned by Jacques Lipchitz's bronze 'La Joie de Vivre,' which rotated on its base every four minutes. The couple bankrolled the avant-garde with the same open hand: Buñuel's scandalous L'Age d'Or and Man Ray's 'Les Mystères du Château du Dé' (shot here in 1929) were their money. Hyères took the house over in 1973; today it hosts the two events it spawned, the Hyères International Festival of Fashion & Photography (founded 1985 by Jean-Pierre Blanc, then 21, and now the oldest young-designer fashion competition in the world) and Design Parade. The one real thing to carry away: this is where modernism learned it was allowed to be joyful, not just severe.

Signal Built in campaigns 1923-1933 by Robert Mallet-Stevens; municipal art centre since 1973; Design Parade held 25-28 June 2026

Fondation Carmignac (Villa Carmignac)

A contemporary-art foundation buried under a restored Provençal farmhouse on a car-free national-park island, walked barefoot.

Financier and collector Édouard Carmignac sank a 2,000 m² gallery beneath an old mas and a ceiling of water on the protected island of Porquerolles, opening it in 2018. You leave your shoes at the door and cross the galleries barefoot before spilling into a fifteen-hectare garden studded with sculpture. Because Porquerolles is a national park, you arrive only by boat and there are no cars, so the art is framed by umbrella pines, salt air and heat-shimmer instead of white walls. Verified: the 2026 show 'Sea, Pop & Sun' (curated by Dieter Buchhart and Anna Karina Hofbauer, setting Warhol, Lichtenstein and Martial Raysse against living artists) runs 25 April to 1 November. The real thing to know: it borrows its title from a summer mood and is engineered to feel like a daydream rather than a museum.

SECRET GEM

Domaine Tempier

The estate that made Bandol serious wine, and whose rosé is one of the Mediterranean's genuinely beautiful drinks.

This is the terroir story of the Var itself. The Tempier family had held the domaine since 1834; in 1936 Lucie Tempier married Lucien Peyraud and her father gave the couple the estate, which they took over around 1940. Together they fought to win Bandol its own appellation (granted 11 November 1941) and, on 27 October 1943, bottled the estate's first wine, a rosé, instead of selling in bulk. Bandol is mourvèdre country, sun-baked, saline, structured, and Tempier proved it could age for decades. Lucie 'Lulu' Peyraud (1917-2020) was also a self-taught cook whose long feasts became legend; the American writer Richard Olney gave her a whole book, 'Lulu's Provençal Table' (1994), and her disciples Alice Waters of Chez Panisse and importer Kermit Lynch carried her name across the world. The real thing: the pale, mineral Bandol rosé you drink up and down this coast exists in its modern form largely because of one family in the hills above Bandol.

Signal Estate in the Tempier family since 1834; Peyrauds drove Bandol AOC (1941); first bottled wine (rosé) 1943; Lulu Peyraud 1917-2020

SECRET GEM

La Tarte Tropézienne (Alexandre Micka)

The cream-filled brioche invented and still sold here, the pastry Bardot named.

In 1945 a Polish confectioner named Alexandre Micka settled in Saint-Tropez, bringing a family recipe for a sugar-crusted brioche split and filled with two creams. In 1955, catering the shoot of 'And God Created Woman,' he served it to Brigitte Bardot, who suggested he call it 'la tarte de Saint-Tropez'; Micka kept the idea and trademarked the name Tarte Tropézienne in 1973. His original shop still trades under that name. Not a drink, but the definitive edible souvenir of the peninsula and the perfect foil to a glass of cold rosé. The real thing: the town's most famous dessert was christened by an accident of cinema, and the woman who named it never took a centime for it.

Signal Micka settled 1945; tarte created 1955; named by Brigitte Bardot; trademarked 1973; original shop still open

HOTEL BAR

Les Caves du Roy — Hôtel Byblos

The legendary cellar nightclub-bar inside the Byblos, open only in season.

The whole hotel is a love letter to a muse: Lebanese businessman Jean-Prosper Gay-Para built the Byblos as a 'palace of a thousand and one nights' inspired by Brigitte Bardot, a faux-Provençal hamlet in apricot and salmon stucco inaugurated on 27 May 1967 with godmothers Bardot and Mireille Darc and three days of festivities for more than 700 guests. Its cellar-bar, Les Caves du Roy, has been the 'temple of Tropezian nights' since July 1967, with Juliette Gréco, Françoise Sagan and Jacques Chazot at the opening and Mick and Bianca Jagger's wedding celebrated here in 1971. Gay-Para lost the hotel within months, selling to Sylvain Floirat on 19 September 1967 after the Six-Day War pulled him back to Beirut; the Floirat family has kept the flame for over fifty years. The real thing: the legend is deliberately rationed, weekends only in spring and autumn and nightly only across high summer, roughly late June to end of August.

Signal Byblos inaugurated 27 May 1967; Caves du Roy open since July 1967; seasonal, nightly late Jun-late Aug (2026)

Bar de La Ponche

The bar of a discreet 5-star hotel in the old fishermen's quarter where the Tropezian myth actually began.

Before Saint-Tropez was Saint-Tropez, La Ponche was a fishermen's inn in the eponymous old quarter, run for generations by the Duckstein family. In 1949 Boris Vian annexed the neighbouring barn to make a bar, and through the 1950s it filled with the New Wave: Bardot's laughter, Vian's trumpet, Vadim shooting scenes for 'And God Created Woman' a few metres down the lane, Sagan, Sartre and Simone de Beauvoir drifting through. Reopened as a year-round five-star in 2021 after an eight-month renovation redesigned by Fabrizio Casiraghi, it kept its low-key soul. The real thing: this shadowed corner above the little port, not the superyacht quay, is where the whole Tropezian legend was actually lit.

Sénéquier

The red-chaired café on the quay, an open-air observatory of the port since the age of the New Wave.

HIKE

Sentier Blanc-Martel

The Verdon's flagship hike: a roughly 16 km one-way descent into Europe's deepest river canyon, with steel ladders and a long unlit tunnel of around 670 m (bring a torch), from La Maline down to the Couloir Samson. A navette shuttle links the two trailheads so you don't double back.

Named for the two men behind the first full traverse of the canyon in August 1905: Paris speleologist Edouard-Alfred Martel, sent by the Ministry of Agriculture to survey drinking water for Provence (the Fontaine-l'Eveque spring), and Isidore Blanc, the naturalist schoolmaster of Rougon who guided him. The Touring Club de France engineered the trail proper, ladders at the Breche Imbert and all, between 1926 and 1928; Blanc, made a TCF delegate, worked it up until his accidental death in 1933, and the path was renamed Blanc-Martel in his honour in 2005. Honesty by negation: the path itself runs on the Alpes-de-Haute-Provence bank, not the Var; the Var owns the south rim (the Corniche Sublime belvederes above Aiguines). The wilder Imbut/Styx trail on the Var bank has been closed by municipal order since July 2022 for rockfall, so this is the canyon walk that is actually open.

Signal First full canyon traverse by Martel and Blanc, August 1905; trail engineered by the Touring Club de France 1926-1928; renamed Blanc-Martel in 2005.

HIKE

Presqu'ile de Giens, double tombolo & Salins des Pesquiers

A peninsula lashed to the coast by two parallel sand bars, one of only a handful of double tombolos on Earth (three to five worldwide), enclosing old salt pans; the sentier du littoral rounds the wild, wind-hammered west and south shores.

The salt works, run by the Compagnie des Salins du Midi, closed in 1995; the Conservatoire du littoral now owns the marsh (since 2001), a roost for pink flamingos year-round that peaks around 2,000 birds in late summer. The coast path you walk is the old sentier des douaniers, the early-19th-century customs path along which patrols once watched the shore for contraband, and it is the very right-of-way that a 1976 French law fixed nationwide as a public easement (a 3 m passage across private coastal land), later reinforced by the 1986 loi Littoral.

Signal Salt production ceased 1995; marsh Conservatoire-owned since 2001, flamingos year-round; path is the old sentier des douaniers, and the 3 m coastal easement was set by a 1976 law (loi Littoral 1986).

CALA

Plage Notre-Dame, Porquerolles

800 m of pale sand backed by Aleppo pines that run almost to the water, a 30-min walk or bike from the car-free village, and angled so it shelters from both the Mistral and the east wind.

The whole island was a wedding gift. On 22 February 1912 Francois-Joseph Fournier, a Belgian boatman's son who made a gold fortune on the Veta Verde lode in Mexico, bought nearly all of the island's 1,254 hectares at auction for his wife Sylvia, then planted the eucalyptus, mimosa and umbrella pines that still shade the paths. European Best Destinations voted Notre-Dame the most beautiful beach in Europe in 2015, and it has paid for the fame: a 6,000-visitor daily summer cap (in place since 2021) and compulsory ferry booking now guard the freshwater table and the island's rich flora.

Signal Island bought 22 Feb 1912 by Fournier; named Europe's most beautiful beach 2015; 6,000/day summer quota since 2021.

Cap Taillat

SECRET GEM

Sentier sous-marin de la Palud, Port-Cros

A 40-45 min walk from the harbour to the Plage de la Palud, then a free, marked snorkel loop (mask, fins, tuba) past six submerged interpretation buoys in a few metres of water, among grouper, wrasse and posidonia meadow.

Port-Cros became the first marine national park in Europe by decree of 14 December 1963, born of a donation rather than a purchase: the Henrys had run the island since 1921, and Marceline Henry gave a third of it (213 hectares of the western island) to the State on condition it be protected, with Andre Malraux carrying it through. The underwater trail here, laid in 1979, was the first in France. The real lesson: on this island you read the sea on foot, not just the land, and the fish are unafraid because nobody has speared them in over 60 years.

Signal National park by decree of 14 Dec 1963 (first marine park in Europe); underwater trail opened 1979 (first in France).

Chartreuse de la Verne

A huge, near-black monastery built of green Maures serpentine, hidden on a forested ridge above the chestnut woods and reached only by a rough road; a working silent cloister with a partial visitor route.

Founded around 1170 by the bishops of Toulon (Pierre Isnard) and Frejus (Fredol d'Anduze); the first Romanesque church was consecrated on 3 October 1174, pillaged by the lords of Bormes in 1416, and emptied when the Carthusians left for Italy at the Revolution (its property confiscated by decree of 2 November 1789). The green serpentine framing its portals was quarried nearby in the Maures and hauled up by mule, the local stone made monumental. Silent again since 1983, when the monastic Family of Bethlehem moved in. Honesty: much is enclosed, so you visit the edges of a living silence, not a museum.

Signal Founded c.1170, church consecrated 1174, pillaged by the lords of Bormes 1416; monastic Family of Bethlehem in residence since 1983.

Village des Tortues, Carnoules

A 2-hectare sanctuary and veterinary clinic at the foot of the massif where you walk among Hermann's tortoises: rescue, breeding pens, incubation and a release programme.

The Maures and the neighbouring Plaine des Maures hold the last wild population of Hermann's tortoise on the French mainland. Naturalists Bernard Devaux and David Stubbs founded the SOPTOM association in 1986 and opened the first village at Gonfaron on 28 May 1988; it moved to its current Carnoules site in June 2017. The one real thing it teaches: France's only native land tortoise survives essentially here, and its two great killers are summer wildfire and the roads that cut its range.

Signal SOPTOM founded 1986; village opened 28 May 1988 (Gonfaron); relocated to Carnoules June 2017.

Domaine du Rayol, Le Jardin des Mediterranees

20 hectares of protected coast (7 of garden, 13 of maquis) facing the Port-Cros islands, with a grand antique-style pergola and cypress-lined stair down to the sea and, in summer, a guided snorkel trail in the bay.

The Conservatoire du littoral rescued the abandoned estate in 1989 and handed it to landscape gardener Gilles Clement, who refused a manicured garden and instead planted a mosaic of the world's other Mediterranean climates: South African fynbos, Chilean matorral, Californian chaparral, Australian kwongan and mallee, all thriving under the same Var sun. The monumental pergola was raised around 1910 for the estate's first owner, the Parisian businessman Alfred Courmes; the aviator Henri Potez, who bought the domain in 1940, later drove the long cypress-lined stair down through it. Terroir made literal, and gardened without irrigation or pesticides from the start.

Signal Pergola built c.1910 for Alfred Courmes; estate bought by the Conservatoire du littoral 1989; garden conceived by Gilles Clement.
