Girona

Catalunya · Spain
"Four rivers, a Roman spine and the widest Gothic vault ever raised — a stone city that reflects itself in the Onyar and eats off the Empordà plain."

History·Eat·Tables·Dishes·Walks·Culture·Crafts·Producers·Drink·Music·Sources

Girona stands on a rock where four rivers meet. The Romans founded it about 77 BCE as Gerunda, an oppidum on the Via Augusta; the Onyar splits it in two and the Ter, Güell and Galligants close the ring — a moat on one side, height on the others. That is why the city was besieged some twenty-five times and taken only seven, and why it is called the Immortal.

Across the water hang the ochre and rust cases de l'Onyar, and behind them the Cathedral lifts the widest Gothic nave in the world — 22.98 m of unbroken vault — over a 90-step Baroque staircase. Below it the Call, one of Europe's best-preserved medieval Jewish quarters, folds into black-stone lanes where Nachmanides taught Kabbalah in the 13th century.

The table answers the stone. Girona eats off the Empordà plain and a Costa Brava an hour east: mar i muntanya, suquet from the ports, the xuixo invented three streets from the Cathedral — and El Celler de Can Roca, twice named the best restaurant in the world.

Come in September for the harvest and the quiet, or for Temps de Flors (9–17 May 2026) when the patios open; book El Celler seasons ahead, and walk the old town before ten.

Girona is defended by flies. In 1286, when French troops broke open the tomb of Sant Narcís in the Basílica de Sant Feliu, a swarm of flies is said to have burst out and routed the army — the mosques de Sant Narcís, retold in the sieges that followed. The fly became a city emblem; a lane in the Barri Vell is still called Carrer de les Mosques and carries the sculptures. A city besieged some twenty-five times keeps the miracle that saved it.

THE SOUL OF THE PLACE
What the place is, underneath

Girona's soul lives where stone meets water. The ochre, rust and pink houses hang over the Onyar and double themselves in it, a reflection so complete that Josep Pla wrote of the city's weight settling into every fibre of his life. The light comes off the Ter valley low and warm, catching the Cathedral's Baroque staircase and the ivy on the bastions. It is a city built to withstand — twenty-five sieges, a saint whose flies still guard the walls in legend — and yet its texture is soft: worn steps, weathered Romanesque stone, a slow river doing all the framing. To read Girona is to learn that the most defended places are often the gentlest to be in.

He sentit el pes de les seves pedres en totes les fibres de la meva vida.
"I have felt the weight of its stones in every fibre of my life."Josep Pla · Girona, un llibre de records (1952)

The quintessenceEach spring Girona hangs the Tarlà — an acrobat puppet, wooden-headed and sawdust-filled until this spring's fibreglass-and-aluminium rebuild — on a bar across Carrer de l'Argenteria, where it turns somersaults over the crowds from mid-April to mid-May. Legend has it the original Tarlà, caught hiding in a flour sack from a pastry chef whose daughter he loved, sneezed "xui-xui" — and the xuixo, Girona's fried, sugar-crusted, crema-filled pastry, was named for the sound. The tale is apocryphal — folklore given its modern shape by the Girona writer Carles Vivó; the documented version is that Emili Puig created the pastry at his shop on Carrer de la Cort Reial around 1918, as the Great War ended, after a French pâtissier showed him choux à la crème and Puig sent the idea to the fryer. Eat one warm at mid-morning, sugar on your fingers in a medieval lane, and you know exactly which city you are standing in — one that keeps the legend and the ledger side by side.

HISTORY & TERROIR
Where it comes from

The oldest facts in Girona are the rock and the water. Founded about 77 BCE as the Roman Gerunda, an oppidum on the Via Augusta, the city grew on a promontory where four rivers meet — and that geography made it a fortress for two thousand years.

The rock and the rivers made a fortress. The Ter, the largest, runs the northern edge; the Onyar splits the city in two and gives it the reflected houses; the small Güell and Galligants feed in near Pedret. Height on three sides, water on the fourth — which is why Girona was besieged something like twenty-five times and taken only seven, and why it wears the epithet the Immortal.

The Call made it luminous. Along Carrer de la Força, below the Cathedral, one of Europe's best-preserved medieval Jewish quarters folds into stepped black-stone lanes. Nachmanides — Bonastruc ça Porta — born here in 1194, made Girona the first centre of Kabbalah in Spain; excavation under the quarter reached the medieval yeshiva. The Carolingian walls above it, 9th–10th century and laid over Roman lines, are among the longest early-medieval circuits in Europe.

1809 fixed the legend. From 6 May to 12 December, some thirty-five thousand French and Westphalian troops besieged a garrison of about five thousand seven hundred; the governor, Mariano Álvarez de Castro, decreed death for anyone who so much as said the word surrender. The city fell only to famine and typhus, and earned its name — Girona Immortal.

What you taste in Girona is the frontier. A plain that had to feed a besieged city — the Poma de Girona apples, the Santa Pau beans on volcanic Garrotxa soil, the Empordà wine on slate — a coast an hour east for the prawns of Palamós and the anchovies of L'Escala, and a French border close enough to have handed the city its own pastry. Stone, water, wind and a border: the whole terroir is a defended crossing.

WHY NOW
The right moment

September is the year's best week: the DO Empordà harvest is on inland, the sea is still warm, the Costa Brava crowds have gone home, and the Poma de Girona apples are coming in. The two dates worth planning around are Temps de Flors (9–17 May 2026), when the Barri Vell's private patios open for the flower festival, and the Fires de Sant Narcís at the end of October, the city's own week. Come for either, but book tables and, for El Celler de Can Roca, book seasons ahead.

WHAT TO EAT
The canon

Sea-and-mountain in one pot, feast-day dishes kept to the calendar, and a fried pastry the whole city argues about.

Girona eats off two landscapes at once — the Empordà plain behind it and the Costa Brava an hour east — and its canon is built on that seam. The cathedral dish is mar i muntanya, sea-and-mountain in one pot: chicken with langoustines, or rabbit with prawns, bound at the end with a picada of fried bread, almonds and garlic. Down at the ports it becomes suquet de peix, the fishermen's stew thickened with potato and almond, never flour; in deep winter, in Palafrugell alone, it becomes the baroque niu of cod tripe and stockfish, cooked five hours and served for a few weeks a year.

The province keeps its feast days on the plate. Canelons are the 26 December dish, built from Christmas Day's leftover meat; botifarra amb fesols de Santa Pau pairs a grilled pork sausage with the small beans grown on volcanic soil and PDO-protected since 2015. From the coast come the barrel-cured anxoves de l'Escala and the auctioned red gamba de Palamós, seared seconds a side.

And then there is the xuixo — the fried, crema-filled pastry invented on Carrer de la Cort Reial around 1918, three streets from the Cathedral, and now the thing every gironí argues about. Eat it warm, from a Barri Vell bakery, before you do anything else.

Food gem · Sweet
Botifarra dolça

Europe's charcuterie map holds one genuine anomaly — a pork sausage cured with sugar, lemon peel and sometimes cinnamon — and it exists only in the Girona region. Barcelona assumes it is an Empordà speciality; the food historian Jaume Fàbrega insists the epicentre is Girona city itself, with La Selva, the Pla de l'Estany and part of the Garrotxa in on the secret. It reached print late — Ferran Agulló first described it in the 1930s — and Josep Pla favoured a monastic origin for which, Fàbrega notes, no proof exists. Salvador Dalí made his Madrid friends eat it at Ca la Teta in Figueres. Born of the farmhouse matança, it now carries the Marca de Garantia Productes de l'Empordà (2003), and is best caramelised with apple — as pudding.

Try it — the charcuterie stalls of the Mercat del Lleó in Girona; in Empordà country restaurants it arrives caramelised with apple, at the end of the meal.
Food gem · Fish
Anxova de l'Escala

L'Escala began in the sixteenth century as a fishermen's quarter dependent on Empúries — whose ruins hold a first-century-AD salting factory, among the oldest on the Iberian peninsula. The village's fortune was salt: once the Alfolí, its salt store, was completed in 1697, cabotage ships landed salt on the old port beach, barrels of anchovy sailed out, and the population quadrupled through the eighteenth century. More than a hundred lateen-rigged sardinal boats once worked this bay; motorised teranyines replaced them in the early twentieth century. L'Escala's municipal archivist, Lurdes Boix, documents an unbroken salting tradition since the 1700s — certified, since 1987, by the Denominació de Qualitat Anxova de l'Escala. The fillets spend roughly eight months under salt before they ever see olive oil.

Try it — the salting houses of l'Escala, forty minutes north-east of Girona — Anxoves Solés has salted since 1888 — with the Museu de l'Anxova i de la Sal (2006) to see the barrels and brine first.
Food gem · Cheese
Formatge Garrotxa

Garrotxa the cheese very nearly outlived the habit of making it. The old farmhouse goat's cheese of these hills had all but disappeared when a recovery project in 1981 — credited by the Generalitat's own product inventory to the neorurals, the back-to-the-land generation — typified it: pasteurised goat's milk, three weeks' minimum maturing, one-kilo wheels sized after the district's old clay moulds. Around Sant Miquel de Campmajor, the young cheesemakers learned from the adviser Enric Canut to coax the pell florida — the grey-blue penicillium bloom that gives the rind its ash colour and the paste its hazelnut finish. The revival's quiet irony: of roughly ten Catalan producers today, exactly one, Mas Claperol, still makes it inside the Garrotxa comarca.

Try it — Cal Formatger, Cort Reial 12B, in Girona's old town — Marc Masó's shop (2019) leans hard on Girona-province cheeses; ask what came in from the Garrotxa or the Pla de l'Estany that week.
WHAT TO DRINK
Empordà in the glass

Garnatxa and carinyena on slate, a sun-dried sweet for after, ratafia and cremat around the edges.

The glass is Empordà. The DO — Spain's north-easternmost, founded in 1975 — grows garnatxa and carinyena low to the ground against the tramuntana, on slate and clay, across roughly 1,768 hectares. The reds are dark and wind-dried; the whites, increasingly, are serious. After dinner comes Garnatxa de l'Empordà, the sun-dried sweet garnatxa concentrated on the vine to 14–15% before the ferment is stopped — a protected style, not a separate appellation.

Around the wine sits a ring of things the province distils and presses. Ratafia, the green-walnut-and-herb liqueur, is an EU-protected spirit with its festival at Santa Coloma de Farners; cremat, rum set alight with coffee and lemon, belongs to the havanera nights down the coast. And the plain's other liquid is Oli de l'Empordà, a PDO oil pressed mainly from the local Argudell olive — the oil under every pa amb tomàquet.

Food gem · Drink
Ratafia

Every Catalan house that makes ratafia guards its own recipe; the paper trail's oldest, from 1842, comes from La Selva — precisely the comarca whose capital now hosts the liqueur's great festival. The method is fixed by its geographical indication: walnuts picked green around Sant Joan in late June, before the shell hardens, macerated in spirit with — at minimum — lemon verbena, cinnamon, clove and nutmeg, then three months' steeping and three more in wood. Santa Coloma de Farners has run its ratafia competition since 1982, some two hundred homemade bottles entered each November. A Catalan quality denomination since 1985 and today the IGP Ratafia Catalana, it remains at heart a farmhouse solstice ritual — bottled at midsummer, ready by winter.

Try it — Santa Coloma de Farners, half an hour south-west of Girona — the Festa de la Ratafia, second weekend of November; the rest of the year, look for the IGP Ratafia Catalana seal on the bottle.
Where the money sits
What funds the table — and where a chef’s money is well spent

One family's restaurant turned a working provincial city of 109,000 into a pilgrimage site — and the pilgrims' money and the locals' money almost never sit at the same table.

Start at the top, because everyone else does. El Celler de Can Roca — three Michelin stars, twice the World's Best Restaurant, 2013 and 2015 — takes bookings a strict eleven months out, releasing each new month at midnight on the first; the Festival menu now runs €315 before the €155 pairing, and the room is shut Sundays and Mondays regardless of who you are. What the fame built beneath it is a complete price ladder, most of it still in family hands: Esperit Roca, ten kilometres out in the castle at Sant Julià de Ramis, serves the Celler's back catalogue — chef Raül Sillero calls it "the library of El Celler's dishes" — at €170, and holds a star of its own; Massana, the city's quieter star since 2007 — Pere Massana's own house since 1986 — sits in the same band; Normal, the brothers' old-town bistro, at €75; and at the bottom, the truest number in Girona — the €18 menú at Can Roca, the Taialà neighbourhood bar their parents opened in 1967, where Joan Roca says he still eats every day. Three hundred and fifteen euros to eighteen, inside one family: the whole economy in miniature.

Three separate currencies fund the tables. The day-trip euro arrives on the AVE from Barcelona in thirty-eight minutes and in coaches up from the Costa Brava — the Costa Brava Girona tourism board logged 8.5 million visitors and 28.3 million overnight stays in 2024 — and most of it is spent within sight of the cathedral steps by people who will never come back. The cycling euro stays longer: a University of Girona study counted some 40,000 cycling tourists a year by 2018, averaging ten days and roughly €162 a day — €89 million — and the peloton itself lives here, 100–150 professionals by the sober estimates; when an ex-pro like Christian Meier, who rode the 2014 Tour, settles in and opens La Fabrica and the Espresso Mafia roastery, the flat-white economy stops being a fad and becomes a settlement. Underneath both, the resident euro — 15,000 university students, the courts and clinics of a provincial capital — is what keeps a proper menú del dia honest at €15–20. Every restaurant in town has quietly chosen which of the three it courts, and the plate tells you within two bites.

The rent tells you who is winning. Of the city's 763 licensed tourist flats, some 480 crowd into the Barri Vell and Mercadal — roughly one home in seven in the old quarter, which had already hit the old 15% neighbourhood ceiling — so in 2023 the council capped tourist flats at 4% of homes citywide; by March 2024 Girona was on Catalonia's stressed-market list, residential rents capped through March 2027. Ground floors on the tourist spine are priced for turnover, not for regulars — which is precisely why the laminated photo menus exist. A terrace paying visitor-economy rent must cook for people it will never see twice, and the food says so.

So spend where repeat custom still disciplines the cooking. The Mercat del Lleó opens at seven — weekdays until two, Saturdays half past — and its fish stalls buy off the same auctions the starred kitchens do: Peixos Serra out of the Roses llotja, Isabel i Maria out of Llançà and El Port de la Selva. An hour east, DO Empordà stays deliberately small — under 2,000 hectares, forty-seven cellars on the DO's own books, the tramuntana scouring the vines clean of mildew — and at the cellar door the person pouring has usually signed the label. The province's larder does the rest: salt-cured anchovies from L'Escala, rice from the paddies at Pals, the red prawn landed at Palamós. What is wasted here is easy to name — paella on the Rambla for the 38-minute visitor. Girona's rule is unusually clean: money spent where people return buys food; money spent where they don't buys rent.

The Best

El Celler de Can Roca is the ceiling — three stars, twice the world's best, booked eleven months out. Below it Massana (duck since 1986) and Divinum hold a star each; Nu, Massana's looser sister, gives you the same hands for half the bill. This is one of the deepest small-city fine-dining benches in Europe.

Recognized

The Roca constellation makes the city legible: Normal for straight Catalan stew, Casa Cacao for chocolate over the river, Rocambolesc for the dessert language as street ice cream. Fonda Cal Ros, relaunched by the Els Tinars team, is the seasonal-market table under stone vaults.

Stories

Eat where the city keeps its memory. Can Marquès has fed Girona since 1920, Casa Marieta since 1892, both handed down four and five generations on the arcaded squares. Can Castelló guards the early xuixo recipe — from Carrer de l’Argenteria now, the old counters gone. Girona has no listening-bar / Hi-Fi scene of note — the city's living soundtrack is the cobla in the square and the havanera down the coast, not a turntable behind a bar.

The Authentic

The everyday province is on the plate: botifarra with Santa Pau's volcanic beans, mar i muntanya bound with a picada, suquet from the Costa Brava ports, a xuixo for the afternoon. Shop it yourself at the Mercat del Lleó and at Cal Formatger, and drink DO Empordà from slate-grown garnatxa.

Local bistro

For a normal lunch: Arròs 9 for a proper rice, Nexe for the short carte at the open bar, or Can Marquès off the market. None will change your life; all will feed you the real Girona at a fair price.

The Dish
The canon, with the technique that matters
8 dishes
#1
DISH

Xuixo AT SOURCE

Girona's fried cream horn — a real pastry with a documented address, not a folk tale.
EUR 3–4Breakfast / berenar

Created in the 1920s (family accounts say from about 1918) by the confectioner Emili Puig on Carrer de la Cort Reial, reputedly after a French pastry worker showed him a cream-filled technique. Technique: a yeasted, laminated dough is shaped into a cylinder, deep-fried, then filled with crema catalana and rolled in granulated sugar while still warm so the sugar catches. Eat it hot; the sugar has to crackle. A carnival legend of the acrobat 'el Tarlà' sneezing 'xui-xui' runs alongside the Puig story — that part is folklore. The pastry carries the Catalan 'Producte de la Terra' mark, and the 'best in Girona' title is a live rivalry, not a settled fact.

#2
DISH

Mar i muntanya AT SOURCE

Sea-and-mountain in one pot — pollastre amb escamarlans, chicken with langoustines, the Empordà's signature paradox.
EUR 18–28Lunch / dinner

Born of fishing-family economy — chicken as the cheap protein, shellfish to stretch the pot. Technique: a slow sofregit base, then a picada stirred in at the very end — coarse salt, fried bread, almonds, hazelnuts, garlic, parsley, sometimes the fried chicken liver or a square of dark chocolate — to thicken and perfume the sauce. The picada is the whole trick; add it too early and it splits. A festive main across the Empordà and Costa Brava.

#3
DISH

Suquet de peix AT SOURCE

The fishermen's stew, thickened by potato starch and an almond picada — never flour.
EUR 20–35Lunch

Cooked originally aboard from unsellable catch (rockfish classically, then monkfish or hake); the name comes from 'suquejar', to release juice. Technique: fish and potatoes simmered in a garlic-tomato sofregit, finished with a picada of toasted bread, garlic, parsley and almonds — the almond picada plus the potato starch is the textural signature. Roses is the reference port; eat it in the harbour towns.

#4
DISH

Niu de Palafrugell FEAST-DAY

A baroque winter stew of cod tripe and stockfish that only surfaces for a few weeks a year.
EUR 30–45Winter lunch

A Palafrugell-specific dish, originally a meatless Lenten pot of cod tripe, peixopalo (dried stockfish), potato and egg; cork-factory workers later enriched it with game birds and sausage, fishermen with cuttlefish. Technique: about five hours total — roughly an hour on the sofregit, four hours slow — finished with allioli. Today it appears only during the organised 'Es Niu' campaign each winter (Jan–Feb) at a handful of Palafrugell tables.

#5
DISH

Canelons de Sant Esteve FEAST-DAY

The 26 December cannelloni built from Christmas Day's leftover meat.
EUR 10–1626 December lunch

Canelons reached Catalonia through Italian cooks in late-18th-century Barcelona fondes; the Sant Esteve tradition is a 20th-century habit, not medieval. Technique: pasta sheets rolled around the shredded beef, chicken and pork left over from Christmas Day's escudella / carn d'olla, layered under béchamel and grated cheese and gratinéed. Eaten on 26 December, Catalonia's second Christmas Day, at the family table.

#6
DISH

Botifarra amb fesols de Santa Pau MARKET

Grilled pork sausage with the small beans grown on Garrotxa volcanic soil — a DOP since 2015.
EUR 12–18Lunch / dinner

The sausage-and-beans pairing first appears in print in the 1830 cookbook 'Nou manual per cuinar amb tota la perfecció'; in Girona province the defining beans are the Fesols de Santa Pau (Tavella Brisa, Setsetmanera and Gra Petit varieties), grown on the volcanic soils of the Garrotxa and PDO-protected since 2015. Technique: a raw pork botifarra grilled over coals, the beans slow-simmered until they turn creamy, dressed with the sausage fat. Best in Garrotxa village kitchens.

#7
DISH

Anxova de l'Escala MARKET

Hand-salted anchovies barrel-cured up to a year, from a single fishing town down the coast.
EUR 12–30 by weightAperitif

L'Escala's curing houses go back to firms like Solés (1888) and Callol Serrats. Technique: fresh anchovies are hand-gutted, layered in salt in wooden barrels, pressed and left to mature 10–12 months at ambient temperature — the wood barrel, not plastic, is held essential to a correct cure. Eaten as a tapa on bread with oil; sold straight from the L'Escala factories. Strong local identity, but note: it carries no EU protection — a regional 'Producte de la Terra', not an IGP.

#8
DISH

Gamba de Palamós MARKET

The red prawn sold live off the boat at auction, under its own guarantee mark.
EUR 60–120/kg (seasonal)Lunch / dinner

Aristeus antennatus, landed by a specialised trawler fleet and auctioned twice daily at the Confraria de Pescadors de Palamós; the 'Marca de Garantia Gamba de Palamós' checks fishing ground, texture, count-per-kilo and landing temperature before any lot may carry the name. Technique: eat it barely seared a la planxa — seconds a side — to keep the creamy head juices; overcooking it is the local sin. A guarantee mark, not an EU PDO — don't conflate the two. Prices run high and volatile in season.

Universo Roca — the family that runs the city
Three brothers, one three-star table, and a whole world you can walk into
3★ · twice World's Best

Girona's gravity well is a family of three brothers who never left. Around their three-star table sits a whole small constellation you can actually walk into.

Joan, Josep and Jordi Roca grew up over their parents' workers' restaurant, Can Roca, in the Taialà district on the edge of town — and never moved far. El Celler de Can Roca holds three Michelin stars and was named the best restaurant in the world twice, in 2013 and 2015, before the World's 50 Best list retired it to its 'Best of the Best' hall so others could have a turn. Joan cooks, Josep runs the cellar, Jordi builds the desserts.

What makes Girona unusual is that the rest of the family's world is open to anyone who books nothing. Rocambolesc, Jordi's gelateria on Carrer Santa Clara, sells the Celler's dessert language as a soft-serve you eat on the street. Casa Cacao, his bean-to-bar chocolate house and a fifteen-room hotel, sits over the Onyar on Plaça Catalunya. And Normal, on Plaça de l'Oli, is the brothers' deliberately plain room — Catalan stews done straight, the anti-Celler, a few days' notice instead of eleven months.

The point is the ladder. You can taste the same intelligence at a EUR 4 ice cream, a EUR 40 stew, or the EUR 300 table — the whole city is graded by one family's hand.

Walks & the old town
A turn-by-turn route, the walls and rivers, and the corners worth finding
7 routes
The one walk to take

Ninety minutes at a slow read, from the Eiffel bridge to the walls.

Start on the Mercadal (new-town) bank at the Pont de les Peixateries Velles, the riveted-iron footbridge put up in 1877 by Gustave Eiffel's Paris company — the personal-design attribution is disputed, but the firm and the date are not. From its middle you get the postcard: the ochre, rust and pink cases de l'Onyar hanging over the water, the Cathedral's grey mass behind them.

Cross into the Barri Vell and climb the Cathedral's 90-step Baroque staircase (built 1607) to stand under the widest Gothic nave in the world — 22.98 m of unbroken vault. Drop down the far side into the Call, the medieval Jewish quarter on Carrer de la Força, and let yourself get lost: this is one of Europe's best-preserved, a black-stone labyrinth where Nachmanides taught in the 13th century.

Come out at Sant Feliu, then climb onto the Passeig de la Muralla — the wall-top walk along a Carolingian circuit built over Roman lines. Follow it south with the whole plain on your left and the towers on your right; drop off near the Jardins dels Alemanys and finish with a xuixo three streets from where it was invented. Ninety minutes at a slow read.

Culture & the stone
Cathedral, Call, walls, baths, the reflected houses
8 stops
The widest Gothic nave in the world is here. After roughly fifty years of argument, the architect Guillem Bofill won the gamble in 1417 to roof Girona Cathedral in a single span rather than three — 22.98 m of unbroken vault, the widest Gothic nave anywhere and, among all church naves, second only to St Peter's in Rome.
#1
CULTURE

Girona Cathedral (Santa Maria)

The widest doubt in Gothic engineering, resolved in stone.

A single Gothic nave 22.98 m wide, 35 m high — the widest Gothic nave in the world, second among all church naves only to St Peter's in Rome. After ~50 years of argument, the architect Guillem Bofill won the single-nave gamble in 1417. The exterior staircase is 90 steps, Baroque, built 1607. In Game of Thrones (filmed here September 2015) Jaime Lannister rides his horse up those steps to the Great Sept of Baelor.

~EUR 7 (with Sant Feliu) · Daily; check catedraldegirona.cat · Open in Google Maps →
#2
CULTURE

Museu d'Història dels Jueus (Call)

Eleven rooms of a medieval Jewish world, on its own ground.

In the Centre Bonastruc ça Porta on Carrer de la Força, at the heart of the Call. Eleven rooms trace the daily life, learning and expulsion of Girona's Jewish community — the first Kabbalah school in Spain, founded around Nachmanides in the 13th century. Excavations here reached the medieval yeshiva.

EUR 4 · Tue–Sat 10–18h; Sun/Mon 10–14h (longer in summer) · Open in Google Maps →
#3
CULTURE

Basílica de Sant Feliu

The pre-cathedral church with eight Roman tombs in its walls.

Girona's principal church before the Cathedral, over the martyr St Felix (d. 304). A Romanesque core under a Gothic nave and Baroque façade, raised to minor basilica by Benedict XVI in 2011. Set into the presbytery are eight 3rd–4th-c. Roman and early-Christian sarcophagi, a run unique on the Iberian Peninsula.

Joint ticket with the Cathedral · Check catedraldegirona.cat · Open in Google Maps →
#4
CULTURE

Banys Àrabs

'Arab baths' that are actually 12th-century Christian Romanesque.

Despite the name, a Romanesque bathhouse of about 1194 modelled on Roman and Islamic precedents, not built by Muslim hands. The domed, columned central pool room (apodyterium) is the photograph everyone takes. A rare civil building of its age.

EUR 3 (EUR 2 reduced) · Varies by season; closed Sun/holiday afternoons · Open in Google Maps →
#5
CULTURE

Sant Pere de Galligants

A Romanesque monastery that is now Catalonia's archaeology museum in Girona.

First documented 988, the present Benedictine church built in the first half of the 12th century, with a Lombard-banded octagonal tower. Since 1857 a museum; today the Girona seat of the Museu d'Arqueologia de Catalunya, holding the 4th-c. Sepulcre de les Estacions from Empúries. Its cloister played Oldtown's Citadel in Game of Thrones.

~EUR 6 · Tue–Sat; check visitmuseum.gencat.cat · Open in Google Maps →
#6
CULTURE

Cases de l'Onyar & the Eiffel bridge

The reflected houses and the iron bridge that frames them.

The ochre-to-rust river façades are the city's signature; their palette was fixed by a 1980s restoration led by the painter Enric Ansesa. The Pont de les Peixateries Velles that crosses between them was built in 1877 by Gustave Eiffel's company — two years before he built Girona nothing else, and two decades before Paris got its tower. The personal-design credit is disputed; the firm and the year are documented.

Free · Any · Open in Google Maps →
#7
CULTURE

Casa Masó

The only Onyar riverfront house you can walk into.

Birthplace of the Noucentista architect Rafael Masó (1880–1935) and the single riverside house open to visitors — four dwellings merged, the façade reworked by Masó between 1911 and 1919. Guided tours only, small groups, booking required; city-owned since 2006 and run by the Fundació Rafael Masó.

~EUR 9 (guided) · Tue–Sat, booking required · Open in Google Maps →
#8
CULTURE

Passeig de la Muralla

The wall-top circuit — the city's high edge.

A walkable rampart on one of Europe's longest Carolingian wall circuits (9th–10th c.), laid over Roman fortifications because Girona guarded the frontier. Free, open dawn to night; the towers are the best free viewpoint over the Cathedral, the Barri Vell roofs and the plain of the Ter.

Crafts to learn
The skills still alive here — and where you can enrol
clay & green walnuts

Not what to watch — what you can actually learn. Two living crafts of the Girona region where a named master, a real teaching atelier and an open, bookable door all genuinely exist — the terroir kept as a skill, not a museum.

#1
CRAFT

Ceramics — La Bisbal d'Empordà AT SOURCE · Baix Empordà

Catalonia's pottery capital, where you can still take the wheel from a named master.
OPEN ENROLMENT

La Bisbal has thrown terracotta since the Middle Ages — the clay is in the ground and the town is built on it, with a working Terracotta museum and a live ceramicists' association. The Escola de Ceràmica de la Bisbal (founded ~1972) teaches on real kilns; its summer courses run weekly from late June to early September under named masters — Dolors Ros on wheel technique, Anna Martínez on the introduction to throwing, María Torné on porcelain — at around EUR 170 a course, booked online and open to anyone. Independent ateliers in town and nearby Peratallada run shorter bookable wheel sessions too.

Enrol: summer courses ~EUR 170 · esceramicbisbal.com · Map →
#2
CRAFT

Ratafia — Santa Coloma de Farners La Selva

Macerate green walnuts into the Empordà's herb liqueur, with the cooperative that revived it.
OPEN ENROLMENT

Ratafia — the EU-protected green-walnut-and-herb liqueur — has its cultural capital at Santa Coloma de Farners, and since 2021 its first dedicated obrador: La Sobirana, a young cooperative (Ernest Morell, Pau Pujol, Pau Torra) working from an 1842 tavern-keeper's recipe. Their taller d'elaboració de ratafia is a public, bookable three-hour workshop, about EUR 50 a head — the green walnuts, herbs and alcohol for two litres included — and you leave with your own maceration to age.

THE NEIGHBORHOODS
Where it lives

Girona reads as two cities across one river. On the east bank the Barri Vell climbs the promontory in stone stairs; on the west the flatter Mercadal holds the market, the park and the shops. The Onyar between them, with its reflected houses, is the seam — and everything worth seeing is within a twenty-minute walk of it.

  • Barri Vell — The old town: Roman-medieval core, stone stairs, the Cathedral and the walls.
  • El Call — The medieval Jewish quarter within the Barri Vell, folded off Carrer de la Força.
  • Mercadal — The medieval 'new town' across the Onyar — market, Devesa park, pedestrian shopping.
  • Sant Pere de Galligants — The riverside district around the Romanesque monastery, at the Barri Vell's north edge.
  • Sant Feliu — The steep lanes below the cathedral hill, around the basilica.
  • Onyar riverfront — The strip of coloured cases de l'Onyar along both banks between old town and new.
  • Pedret — The northern gateway where the Onyar meets the Ter — El Celler de Can Roca's edge of town.
  • Devesa / Parc — The west bank around Catalonia's largest urban park, the Fires fairground each autumn.
  • Montjuïc de Girona — The wooded hill over the city, historic high ground, path to the Castell de Sant Miquel.
  • Vall de Sant Daniel — The green valley of springs behind Montjuïc, up to the Romanesque monastery.
  • Santa Eugènia — The western district, an independent town until it was annexed in 1963.

Producers & the land
#1
PRODUCER

DO Empordà cellars

Slate-grown garnatxa & carinyena

The Alt/Baix Empordà DO, founded 1975, ~1,768 ha of low bush vines held down by the tramuntana. Visit Espelt (Vilajuïga), La Vinyeta (Mollet de Peralada), Masia Serra (Cantallops) or Terra Remota (Sant Climent Sescebes) — most take booked tastings.

#2
PRODUCER

Fesols de Santa Pau (DOP)

Volcanic-soil beans, PDO since 2015

Small beans of the Tavella Brisa, Setsetmanera and Gra Petit varieties, grown only on the volcanic soils of Santa Pau and six Garrotxa villages — EU-protected (Reg. 2015/1312). The reason a plate of botifarra amb seques tastes of the Garrotxa and not just of beans.

#3
PRODUCER

Poma de Girona (IGP)

The plain's PGI apples

Five varieties (Golden, Gala, Granny Smith, Fuji among them) grown on the Gironès–Empordà plain, PGI-protected since 2003 (Reg. 1491/2003). The Ter's water and cold nights give a firm, high-acid apple — the everyday fruit of the province.

#4
PRODUCER

Formatge Garrotxa (IGP)

Bloomy-rind goat's cheese

A grey-moulded goat's cheese from the Garrotxa, matured at least 21 days — EU-registered as a PGI only in December 2023 (Reg. 2023/2872), so recent. Nutty, faintly mushroomy from the rind; the backbone of any Girona cheese board.

#5
PRODUCER

Anxova de l'Escala

Barrel-cured anchovies

Hand-salted, wood-barrel-matured 10–12 months by houses like Solés (1888) and Callol Serrats down the coast at L'Escala. Deep local identity but no EU protection — a 'Producte de la Terra', bought straight from the factories.

#6
PRODUCER

Gamba de Palamós

The auctioned red prawn

Aristeus antennatus, landed and auctioned twice daily at the Confraria de Pescadors de Palamós under a guarantee mark that checks ground, texture and count. Seared seconds a side; a guarantee mark, not a PDO.

Food gem · Tradition
Esmorzar de forquilla

The esmorzar de forquilla — the fork breakfast — is working Catalunya's second breakfast: a bite before dawn, then, mid-morning, cap i pota, tripe, pig's trotters, stewed botifarra, a glass of red. Food writers trace its consolidation to the mid-eighteenth century, with markets as its epicentres — which is why Girona still does it properly. Can Marquès opened in 1920 facing what would later become the Mercat del Lleó, still opens at nine to feed the market opposite, and remains one of the longest-running restaurants in the city. In 2024 the tradition finally got its own book, Laia Freixinet's Esmorzars de forquilla. Around Girona it never needed reviving — in villages like Canet d'Adri, it simply never stopped.

Try it — Can Marquès, Plaça Calvet i Rubalcaba 3, opposite the Mercat del Lleó — from 9am, Monday to Saturday; or the twenty-minute drive to Cal Sabater in Canet d'Adri.
At the glass
Empordà DO, the sweet garnatxa, ratafia and cremat
7 pours
#1
DRINK

DO Empordà reds Wine

Garnatxa and carinyena grown on slate and tramuntana wind, in Spain's north-easternmost DO.
EUR 10–35

The DO was founded by Spanish order of 19 May 1975 (originally 'Empordà–Costa Brava'; the coast name was dropped and the zone widened into the Baix Empordà in 2006). Black grapes run to carinyena negra, garnatxa negra — locally lledoner — plus cabernet, merlot, syrah and ull de llebre; garnatxa alone covers roughly a third of the DO's ~1,768 ha. The wind keeps the vines low and the yields small.

Producers: Espelt · Perelada · La Vinyeta · Masia Serra · Terra Remota
#2
DRINK

Garnatxa de l'Empordà Dessert wine

The sun-dried sweet garnatxa you drink after dinner — a protected style, not a separate DO.
EUR 8–20 / half

A traditional style inside the DO Empordà regulation, at least ~90% garnatxa. Technique: grapes are left to over-ripen and dry (the raïm assolellat / sun-dried method), harvested late — traditionally the third week of September — to concentrate sugar to around 14–15% potential alcohol before the ferment is arrested. Warm, raisined, generous. The Espolla cooperative is the classic source.

Producers: Celler Cooperatiu d'Espolla · Empordàlia · Espelt
#3
DRINK

Ratafia catalana Liqueur

Green-walnut-and-herb liqueur — an EU-protected spirit GI, with its festival at Santa Coloma de Farners.
EUR 10–20

Registered as a geographical indication under Regulation (EC) 110/2008. The spec: a hydroalcoholic maceration of green walnuts (min. 10 g/l) for at least three months with aromatic herbs, 24–30% ABV, aged a minimum three months in wood. Home practice picks the walnuts green in June–July and macerates them in the sun for about forty days. Santa Coloma de Farners, in Girona province, is its cultural capital — the Festa de la Ratafia ran its 44th edition in 2025.

Producers: Ratafia Russet · local Santa Coloma makers
#4
DRINK

Cremat Hot cocktail

Rum set alight with coffee, cinnamon and lemon — the drink of the havanera nights.
EUR 4–7

A flambéed mix of rum, sugar, cinnamon stick, lemon peel and coffee, burned off before serving; the Cuban rum and the havanera songs both came home with sailors from the Antilles trade. Technique: it is stirred in its own flames for several minutes before being doused with coffee. Calella de Palafrugell's Cantada d'Havaneres, the first Saturday of July, is where it is drunk communally and hot.

Producers: Served at Costa Brava taverns; the Calella cantada
#5
DRINK

Oli de l'Empordà Olive oil

A PDO oil pressed mainly from the local Argudell olive — the plain's other liquid.
EUR 12–25

Protected as a PDO by EU Implementing Regulation 2015/385. The zone covers the Alt and Baix Empordà with parts of the Pla de l'Estany and the Gironès; the oil is at least 51% Argudell, with Curivell, Llei de Cadaqués and Arbequina alongside. Green, faintly bitter, cut for pa amb tomàquet and for finishing suquet.

Producers: Empordàlia (Oli de Pau) · Olis Bàrbara
#6
DRINK

Vi novell Young wine

The season's first wine, tapped around Sant Martí on 11 November.
EUR 5–10

'Per Sant Martí, mata el porc i enceta el vi' — for Saint Martin, kill the pig and broach the wine. Technique: a few weeks of aging, no more, bottled to keep the fruit and a faint natural spritz from the ferment. Drunk young and festively in the weeks after the November release; the Espolla cooperative bottles a well-known one within the DO Empordà.

Producers: Celler Cooperatiu d'Espolla
#7
DRINK

Vermut de l'Empordà Vermouth

An Empordà vermouth built on the native garnatxa roja, not the Reus tradition.
EUR 10–18

Celler Gerisena in Garriguella makes 'Obreboques', a vermouth on garnatxa roja — a native Empordà variety — aged two years in oak, framed by its makers as an Empordà terroir drink rather than a copy of the Reus/Barcelona style. The Sunday-noon vermut before lunch is the ritual it belongs to.

Producers: Celler Gerisena (Obreboques)
Music & the night
The sardana, the havanera, chamber music under the nave
5 notes
#1
MUSIC

'La Santa Espina' — Enric Morera

The sardana that became a banned anthem.

Composed by Enric Morera to a text by Àngel Guimerà, premiered 1907; the most emblematic Catalan sardana, banned under both the Primo de Rivera and Franco dictatorships. If a cobla strikes it up in Plaça de la Independència, the older dancers will rise first.

#2
MUSIC

'El meu avi' — the havanera

The 1968 song that ends every Costa Brava night.

Written in 1968 and the best-known havanera, it has closed the Cantada d'Havaneres de Calella de Palafrugell every year since 1977 — sung over a cremat, the whole square joining the last verse about a grandfather's boat lost off Cuba.

#3
MUSIC

Cobla La Principal de la Bisbal

The Empordà's own sardana orchestra.

An active cobla from La Bisbal d'Empordà, in Girona province, carrying the eleven-instrument sardana tradition — the tenora's nasal reed cutting over the brass. The live soundtrack to any Girona festa where the circle-dance forms.

#4
MUSIC

Havaneres & cremat

Cuban song brought home by returning sailors.

The habanera crossed back to the Costa Brava with the men who worked the 19th-century Cuba trade — slow, salt-worn songs sung to guitar and accordion, always with a pot of flaming rum-and-coffee cremat going. The genre the coast made its own.

#5
MUSIC

Nits de Clàssica in the nave

Chamber music under the widest Gothic vault.

The city's festival puts violinists and pianists inside the Cathedral, its cloister and Sant Pere de Galligants — stone that turns a solo line vast. Hearing Bach under the 22.98 m vault is the closest thing Girona has to a house sound.

WHAT TO SKIP
What to leave alone

The Rambla de la Llibertat and Plaça de la Independència terraces are set up for the day-tripper: fine for a vermut and the arcades, thin value for a full meal. Skip the 'authentic paella' boards near the Pont de Pedra — paella is Valencian, not Gironí; if you want rice, book an arròs at Arròs 9. Don't drive to the Cantada d'Havaneres in Calella, and don't expect to walk into El Celler de Can Roca — the booking window opens eleven months out. And treat the 'best xuixo in Girona' as an argument to enjoy, not a fact to settle.

  • Plaça de la Independència for dinner — The scenic square is priced for the view, not the plate. Instead: Have the vermut there, then eat at Can Marquès, Fonda Cal Ros or Divinum.
  • 'Authentic paella' boards — Paella is Valencian; the photo-menu versions near the bridges are for tourists. Instead: Book a dry or black arròs at Arròs 9, or a fideuà, both genuinely Catalan.
  • Driving to the Calella havaneres — Parking on the Costa Brava in July is a lost evening. Instead: Take the bus to Calella de Palafrugell and let the cremat do the rest.
  • Turning up at El Celler unbooked — There is no walk-in; the list is closed months ahead. Instead: If the Celler is full, Nu gives you the same kitchen family for a third of the price.
Keep one thing: walk the old town before ten, come in September, and book the one table that needs a season's notice.
