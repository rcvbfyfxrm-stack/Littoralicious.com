Beaune

Bourgogne · France
“A terroir of patience, kept in the dark”
Terroir Briefing · 25 May 2026
Districts· Why Now· History· Eat· Drink· Tables· Walk· Avoid

Beaune is the only town in France where the premier crus begin at the last street. A walled prefecture of 22,000 on the eastern lip of the Côte d'Or, where the Bressan rift collapsed about 30 million years ago under the rising Alps — tilting the Jurassic limestone of the Côte to the morning sun and dropping the Saône plain east into a flat alluvial trough. The slope west of the ramparts was read parcel by parcel by Cistercian monks from the Abbey of Cîteaux (founded 1098, 30 km north), who tasted each square of vine for four hundred years and recorded the differences on parchment. That mapping survives: more than 1,200 named climats along the Côte (UNESCO Cultural Landscape, 4 July 2015), 42 of them inside Beaune's appellation alone — and not one grand cru, the singular fact of the place. Since 1443 the Hôtel-Dieu has been a free hospital for the poor, paid for since the first gift of vines in 1457 by the wine of the Côte itself, and treating patients without charge for 528 years until 1971. Such is Beaune's condition: a wine town that built a charity, and the charity that still farms the wine.

Three things to lock in first: (1) the Hôtel-Dieu at 09:00 sharp, before the tour groups — the polychrome roof of glazed terracotta tiles, laid in interlaced diamonds of red, brown, yellow and green (the current panels are the 1902–1907 reconstruction, made by Tuilerie Laurent at Nan-sous-Thil after the 15th-century originals perished), and Rogier van der Weyden's Last Judgment polyptych (c. 1443–1451), 215 × 560 cm across 15 panels on 9 oak boards, hung so the dying in the Grande Salle could see the Archangel Michael weighing souls; (2) taste the slope across three glasses at the Marché aux Vins inside the deconsecrated Cordeliers church — Découverte circuit, 5 wines, 25 €, walked through three intact Gothic chapels; (3) walk out of the ramparts from the Parc de la Bouzaise — two hundred metres past the last street and you are inside Clos des Mouches and Les Grèves, upper-slope premier crus rising eighty metres to the Montagne de Beaune.

The warning: Burgundy by the glass on a Place Carnot café terrace is the négoce's most anonymous regional cuvée poured at a markup — the wine that explains the slope is tasted in a cellar with a producer or at a serious counter (Enstase for the natural-wine programme, Loiseau des Vignes for 32 Burgundies poured by the glass). And do not arrive expecting Beaune quiet on the third Sunday of November: that is the Vente des Vins weekend, the loudest in the town's year.

The Hospices wine auction is decided by a candle. Since the first sale in 1859, the charity barrel — the Pièce des Présidents — is sold à la bougie: two short candles are lit in succession, and the last bid before the second flame gutters out takes the lot. The wine that pays for a hospital is timed by a wick.

THE NEIGHBORHOODS
Where it lives

Beaune is a ring. The ramparts draw an almost unbroken circle barely 700 metres across, and everything of consequence — every cellar, every table, the market, the Hôtel-Dieu — sits inside it or in a faubourg just outside a gate. You walk it in fifteen minutes; you read it in three.

  • Place Carnot & Place de la Halle — The commercial heart, two linked squares: Place Carnot for the cafés, the brocante and Le Carmin; Place de la Halle for the covered market and the Hôtel-Dieu one block on. Mulot & Petitjean's pain d'épices and the Marché aux Vins both open onto this space.
  • Rue Maufoux & the cellar streets — The wine-trade spine running south-west — rue Maufoux, rue Paradis, rue d'Enfer — lined with négociant doorways and the vaults beneath. Loiseau des Vignes, Bissoh and the Bistro de l'Hôtel are all on or off Maufoux.
  • Place Madeleine & Faubourg Madeleine — The eastern square and its faubourg — the after-dark side of town, wine bars and the natural-wine bistros. Caves Madeleine and Le Comptoir des Tontons sit here, away from the monument crowds.
  • Place Monge — A quieter square that is, quietly, the charcutier quarter: Raillard and Franck Bourgeon within a few doors, the jambon persillé and pâté-en-croûte address of Beaune, plus the Bistrot Bourguignon and Pâtisserie Berteau.
  • Faubourg Bretonnière — The mustard axis, running west out of the old Porte Bretonnière — terraced houses, the Bastion Bretonnière, and at number 31 the Edmond Fallot mill, Burgundy's last independent family moutarderie.
  • The vineyard slope — West of the ramparts the premier crus begin within 200 metres of the last street — Clos des Mouches, Les Grèves, Les Teurons climbing toward the Montagne de Beaune. No other wine capital lets you walk from a café into a grand vineyard in five minutes.
  • Out of town — The Route des Grands Crus runs south through Pommard, Volnay and Meursault and north to Savigny, Pernand-Vergelesses and Aloxe-Corton — all within 12 km. Levernois, with its Relais & Châteaux table, is five minutes east. The terroir is never centralised; you have to drive.
WHY NOW
The right moment

Late May into June the vines flower on the slope, and the cellars are quiet between the spring barrel tastings and the July festival.

HISTORY & TERROIR
Where it comes from

Beaune sits on a geological accident. About 30 million years ago the rising Alps caused the crust east of here to drop along the Bressan rift, foundering the Saône plain and tilting the Jurassic limestone of the Côte to the morning sun — a 60 km escarpment of marl and brown limestone, east-facing, perfectly drained, the whole reason wine is here at all. The Côte de Beaune, the southern half, lies on Upper Jurassic stone (Bathonian–Oxfordian, 150–165 million years) and gives the world's great dry whites (Meursault, Puligny-Montrachet, Corton-Charlemagne); the Côte de Nuits to the north stands on older Middle Jurassic rock and holds 24 of Burgundy's 33 red grand crus. Two grapes do all the work — Pinot Noir and Chardonnay — and in 1999 John Bowers and Carole Meredith at UC Davis (the same team that proved Zinfandel was Croatian two years later) showed by DNA, in Science, that Chardonnay, Aligoté, Gamay, Melon de Bourgogne, Auxerrois and a dozen more Northeastern French varieties are all crosses of Pinot Noir × Gouais Blanc: a single peasant grape, Gouais, fathered Burgundy's whole white catalogue. The earliest Burgundian Pinot Noir of which we have a record was planted by Benedictine monks at what is now Château de Pommard in 909 CE, nearly two centuries before Cîteaux. The Cistercians of Cîteaux (Abbey founded 1098, 30 km north) assembled the Clos de Vougeot from 1109–1115 onward and spent four hundred years tasting each parcel and naming it — the climats system was an invention of patient monastic palates, two centuries before the printing press. The Dukes of Burgundy made Beaune the trade capital of the Côte from the 14th century, when Burgundy stretched from Mâcon to the North Sea (Bruges, Ghent and Antwerp were Burgundian); in 1443 Nicolas Rolin, chancellor to Philip the Good, with his wife Guigone de Salins, founded the Hôtel-Dieu as a free hospital for the poor — and from the first gift of vines in 1457 onward it treated patients without charge for 528 years, until 1971, the bill always paid by the vineyard. Beaune also gave France one of the founders of the École Polytechnique: Gaspard Monge (1746–1818), the local merchant's son who invented descriptive geometry, served as Napoleon's Minister of the Marine, and helped found the school in 1794. Burgundian cooking comes straight from this geography: limestone slopes too poor for wheat, rich pasture for Charolais cattle on the plain — hence the slow braises (œufs en meurette, bœuf bourguignon, coq au vin) built on the village red that wasn't going to sell as cru. The Hospices estate today: 60 hectares, 85 % premier and grand cru, sold every third Sunday of November en primeur, by candlelight — first held in 1859, the same year Auguste-Denis Lagoute's Crème de Cassis de Dijon was given its official Dijon medal — run with Sotheby's since 2021. 166th edition: Sunday 15 November 2026.

WHAT TO EAT
At table

Three pillars. Œufs en meurette — eggs poached in a reduction of Pinot Noir, with lardons, button mushrooms and a garlic-rubbed croûton: the dish where the appellation IS the sauce, the cathedral plate of Beaune, eaten at breakfast or lunch but built on a great red. Bœuf bourguignon — its long-cooked sibling, beef shoulder or cheek browned then braised four hours in red until the sauce turns to lacquer; built, like every great Burgundian dish, on the village wine that wasn't going to sell as cru. Coq au vin — old rooster (tougher, deeper-flavoured than young hen), braised in red with lardons and small onions, the sauce sometimes finished with the bird's own blood; a peasant economy made noble by hours. On charcuterie: jambon persillé (Easter Burgundian terrine — shoulder ham simmered with tarragon and white wine, hand-torn, set in parsley jelly) — Eddy Raillard's on rue Monge is called by critic Gilles Pudlowski "a model of the genre". Cheese course: Époisses, washed-rind cow's milk from the village 86 km north-west, brined and bathed in marc de Bourgogne — said to have been Napoleon's, and historically banned from French TGV trains for its smell. Open every cellar visit with gougères — warm Gruyère choux puffs that are not a snack but a ritual, the savoury bite that resets the palate before the first wine. The escargots are Helix pomatia, scarce and seasonal; if they arrive frozen in industrial garlic butter, they aren't escargots de Bourgogne.

WHAT TO DRINK
By the glass

Two grapes and a UC Davis pedigree. Pinot Noir — the red of the entire Côte d'Or, the same vine giving a different wine every hundred metres of slope; the marker grape of the climat system. Chardonnay — the white that became the world benchmark in Meursault and Puligny-Montrachet, a few kilometres south of town; Beaune's own celebrated white is the Clos des Mouches, an entire 25.2 ha premier cru owned by Joseph Drouhin. Aligoté — the sharp third white, base of the Kir (one part crème de cassis under four to five of cold Aligoté, named for Félix Kir, Resistance priest and mayor of Dijon 1945–1968, who served it at every reception); the 1999 UC Davis DNA paper (Bowers & Meredith, Science) showed Aligoté — like Chardonnay and Gamay — is a cross of Pinot Noir × Gouais Blanc, the medieval peasant grape Philip the Bold of Burgundy banned from the Côte in 1395 to favour Pinot. Drink without driving at the Marché aux Vins (5 wines, 25 €) and at the natural-wine counter Enstase. Cellars to book: Bouchard inside the 15th-c. Château de Beaune — their flagship is the Vigne de l'Enfant Jésus, a 3.92 ha parcel inside Beaune Les Grèves once owned by the Carmelite convent of Beaune; it was named in gratitude after the 1638 birth of Louis XIV, foretold to the supposedly sterile Anne d'Autriche by the Carmelite nun Marguerite du Saint-Sacrement, and bought by Bouchard at the 1791 sale of biens nationaux, in monopoly ever since. And Patriarche, five kilometres of galleries under a former Visitandine convent — about two million bottles, the largest cellars in Burgundy. Apéritif: Kir, or Kir Royale on Crémant de Bourgogne base (méthode traditionnelle, AOC since 1975); Crème de cassis de Dijon itself was first commercialised in 1841 by Auguste-Denis Lagoute, from the Noir de Bourgogne blackcurrant. Digestif: Marc de Bourgogne (distilled from the pressed grape skins) or Fine de Bourgogne (distilled from wine lees, oak-aged 3+ years) — Marc is an AOC since 1942 (the oldest pomace-brandy AOC in France), Fine joined the club in March 2011.

A WALK
The route

Leave the Place de la Halle early and walk the ring. The Hôtel-Dieu's glazed roof catches first; cut west through the cellar streets — rue Paradis, then rue d'Enfer, Hell Street, so named because the wine vaults beneath keep it cold even in July, the venting slats breathing cool air at your ankles. Reach the ramparts at the Rempart des Dames, a double avenue of plane trees on a 1637 bastion, the prettiest stretch of a 2.5-kilometre circuit that never lost its towers. Drop down through the Faubourg Bretonnière past Edmond Fallot's mustard mill, then climb out of town: two hundred metres past the last house the vines begin, and you are in the premier crus — Clos des Mouches, then Les Grèves on the upper slope. Forty minutes, and the town reads through smell: cold cellar stone, crushed mustard seed, box hedge, and the green sap of a vineyard in flower.

WHAT TO SKIP
What to leave alone

Do not drink Burgundy by the glass on a Place Carnot café terrace and think you have met the place — the terraces pour the négoce's most anonymous cuvées at a tourist markup, and the wine that explains the slope is tasted three streets away, in a cellar, with someone who made it. Skip the "Burgundy snail" menus around the Hôtel-Dieu that serve frozen helix from elsewhere in industrial garlic butter; real escargots de Bourgogne are scarce and seasonal, and an honest bouchon will tell you so. And do not come for the day on the third Sunday of November expecting Beaune to itself: that is the Vente des Vins, the town's loudest weekend of the year — magnificent, but the opposite of quiet.

The Best

Beaune carries two Michelin stars inside the walls, and they read the town from opposite ends. Le Carmin (Place Carnot, chef Christophe Quéant) is the instinctive one — a market menu with no fixed signature, lunch from 50 €, dinner to 178 €. Clos du Cèdre (chef Jordan Billan, in the Hostellerie Cèdre) is the terroir-anchored one, starred since 2022, with a list past a thousand references. Five minutes east, in the village of Levernois, La Table de Levernois holds a third star under Philippe Augé — the grand-gesture option, Relais & Châteaux, a vegetable garden in the grounds.

Recognized — tier guide

Below the stars sit the tables the guides keep flagging. La Table du Square took a Bib Gourmand in 2024 — the best craft-for-money in town, a market menu rewritten fortnightly. Loiseau des Vignes, the Bernard Loiseau group's bistro, pours 32 Burgundies by the glass. An honest flag: Le Bénaton, on Faubourg Bretonnière, held a star from 2006 until it was withdrawn in the 2024 guide — chef Keishi Sugimura cooks to win it back, and the room is still worth the table; confirm its current standing before you book.

Stories — tables with a story (incl. Hi-Fi)

Some rooms are worth it for the building. Bouchard pours inside a fortress built for Louis XI; Marché aux Vins tastes you through the chapels of a deconsecrated Franciscan church; Garum — the table that was the one-star Jardin des Remparts until its chef gave the star back — now runs a looser room of charcuterie, oysters and natural wine at the same address by the Hôtel-Dieu. One honest note for the listening-bar traveller: Beaune has no audiophile listening bar in the jazz-kissa sense, and neither does Dijon or Lyon. The nearest are in Paris (Bambino, Listener). The Beaune equivalent ritual is a cellar at dusk and a glass that someone can talk you through — and for an electronic night, drive 45 min to La Vapeur, Dijon's SMAC, or 1h45 to Le Sucre in Lyon.

Authentic — local soul

The Burgundian soul tables are the bouchons. Ma Cuisine, down a passage, has been the reference since 1996 — à la carte only, a cellar that goes very deep. Le Bistrot Bourguignon on rue Monge does the canon straight — œufs en meurette, bœuf bourguignon — for a 33 € menu. Caves Madeleine seats you at shared tables and lets you pull wine off the shelf. Le Conty, in a 16th-century room on rue Félix Ziem, holds the same line.

Local bistro (konobe / tascas / bistrots de marché)

The bouchon is to Burgundy what the konoba is to Dalmatia and the bistrot de marché to the Jura: a short menu, a stone room, house wines by the pot, the braise that was started this morning. Every table named above is the real thing. To go further out, the village tables of the Route des Grands Crus — Pommard, Volnay, Meursault — are the natural next step, and the natural-wine counter at Enstase is where the town's own young drinkers end the night.

The Dish
Canon dishes
7 dishes
The Burgundian canon was invented to use what wouldn't sell. Œufs en meurette, bœuf bourguignon, coq au vin — the great braises of Burgundy were peasant recipes built on the everyday village red and the tougher cuts (or the old rooster, finished laying), redeemed by hours of cooking. Cru wine never has to do this. The lesson is general: every appellation's table dishes were once a way of using up the appellation's wine — and a Côte d'Or village cook will still ask which bottle you opened last night before deciding what to braise tonight.

In 1999 John Bowers and Carole Meredith published in Science the DNA proof — the same UC Davis team that traced Zinfandel back to Croatia two years later — that Chardonnay, Aligoté, Gamay, Melon de Bourgogne, Auxerrois and a dozen other Northeastern French varieties are all crosses of Pinot Noir × Gouais Blanc. Gouais was a medieval workhorse grape so disdained that Philip the Bold of Burgundy banned Gamay (one of its offspring) from the Côte in 1395 to protect Pinot — not knowing he was banning its half-cousin. The greatest white-wine catalogue in the world descends from a vine the dukes called common.
Pinot can turn white inside a single vine. In 1936 Henri Gouges of Nuits-Saint-Georges noticed a vine in his Clos des Porrets-Saint-Georges Premier Cru producing white berries after véraison — a somatic mutation on a Pinot Noir. He propagated the cuttings and after the war planted half a hectare. The mutation was registered as Pinot Gouges, and Domaine Henri Gouges still bottles a Nuits-Saint-Georges 1er Cru Les Perrières from it — Pinot's white sibling, unique to a single Burgundian family.

🗺️ The climat — Beaune's 42 premier crus TERROIR

42 premier crus, zero grand crus — a singular ratio
———

A climat is a named, mapped parcel of vines with its own history and rank — the unit the Cistercian monks invented by walking the slope for centuries. Beaune's appellation holds 42 of them at premier cru and not a single grand cru: more than three-quarters of the appellation classed at the upper tier, yet no crown. The town put its ambition into the trade. The whole system was inscribed by UNESCO in 2015. Read it at the Cité des Climats et Vins.

🟡 Edmond Fallot — La Moutarderie MUSTARD

Burgundy's last independent family mustard mill

Founded in 1840 by Léon Bouley, a miller who began producing mustard from Burgundian sénevé seeds. In 1928 it was bought by Edmond Fallot (a Doubs-born papermaker and World-War-I veteran) who renamed it and committed to preserving the granite-stone-mill technique. Today the house is run by Marc Désarménien, Edmond's grandson — the last independent family moutarderie in Burgundy, milling about 2,500 tonnes a year on granite millstones near 770 kg each. The slow stone grind keeps the paste cool and the volatile aromatics intact, a method industry abandoned. The tour shows the millstones at work; the Enjoy Fallot boutique sells the full range. The benchmark maker of AOP Époisses

In the village of Époisses, two hours north-west, the Berthaut family makes the benchmark AOP Époisses — the washed-rind, marc-bathed cow's-milk cheese at the pungent edge of the law — plus Soumaintrain and Affidélice. Too far for a casual trip, but the cheese itself reaches the Beaune Saturday market direct. Closed Sundays.

⛪ Fromage de Cîteaux — Abbaye de Cîteaux CHEESE

A washed-rind disc made by Cistercian monks

At the mother abbey of the Cistercian order — founded 1098 — the monks still make the cheese of Cîteaux: a soft, washed-rind disc from the raw milk of their own Montbéliarde herd, sold only at the abbey shop and a few fromagers. Half an hour north of Beaune, easily folded into a Côte de Nuits day. The abbey is a working monastery — silence and respect are expected.

🍯 Mulot & Petitjean PAIN D'ÉPICES

Burgundy pain d'épices — a house founded in Dijon in 1796

The Beaune boutique of the Dijon pain d'épices house Mulot & Petitjean, founded 1796 and carrying the Living Heritage Company label. Traditional spice bread, nonnettes — small glazed spice cakes filled with jam — all made by hand. The Burgundian thing to bring home, and the classic partner to foie gras or a strong cheese.

Avoid
Named traps — unambiguous
4 traps
#1
AVOID

Burgundy by the glass on a tourist terrace AVOID

Place Carnot café terraces — the négoce's most anonymous cuvée at a markup

The big café terraces pour the most generic regional Bourgogne by the glass at a tourist price, and call it the experience. The wine that explains the slope is tasted in a cellar, with someone who made or chose it — at Marché aux Vins, at Enstase, or on a négociant visit. Use the terrace for a coffee and the view, not for your impression of Burgundy.

#2
AVOID

The frozen-snail tourist menu AVOID

The 'menu escargots' clustered around the Hôtel-Dieu

Several menus around the monuments sell frozen helix from elsewhere, drowned in industrial garlic butter, as 'escargots de Bourgogne'. True local Burgundy snails are scarce and seasonal. An honest bouchon — Ma Cuisine, Le Conty — will tell you straight which it is serving. Ask the question.

#3
AVOID

Arriving for a quiet day on auction Sunday AVOID

The third Sunday of November — the Vente des Vins

If you want Beaune calm, do not come on the third Sunday of November. That is the Vente des Vins weekend — the town's loudest, with the auction in the Halles, projections on the monuments, crowds in every street. Magnificent, but the opposite of the quiet this guide is built around. Come for the auction deliberately, or avoid that weekend deliberately.

#4
AVOID

Driving the Route des Grands Crus and tasting AVOID

Cellar visits, a car, and France's drink-drive limit

It is tempting to drive Pommard–Volnay–Meursault and taste at each. France's limit is low and the gendarmerie know the season. Either walk the vineyard route, hire a driver or a cycling tour, or appoint one taster who stays dry. The wine will keep; the licence might not.
