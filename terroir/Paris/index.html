THE SOUL OF THE PLACE
What the place is, underneath

Paris began as an island. The Parisii, a Celtic tribe, held a river crossing on the Seine; Rome built its Lutetia around that oppidum and up the Left Bank. Everything else — the boulevards, the museums, the myth — grew outward from the Ile de la Cite.

Then the city was rebuilt on top of itself. Between 1853 and 1870 Baron Haussmann drove some 140 kilometres of boulevard through the medieval tangle for Napoleon III — light, air, sewers, stone. But the thing Paris actually gave the table is smaller than a boulevard: the cafe-comptoir and the bistro, the public living room where a worker and a philosopher paid the same price to stand at the same zinc.

The tension is the whole story. Paris is a museum-city — the Louvre, the Orsay, ten three-star kitchens — that stubbornly refuses to become a museum. The boulangerie still opens before dawn, the espresso is still drunk standing, and the natural-wine bar in the 11th is louder than the guidebook. The trick, as Bourdain knew, is to walk past the monument and eat where the neighbourhood eats.

The artThe Louvre spans medieval Europe, the antiquities and painting to the early nineteenth century; the Musee d'Orsay, in a converted Beaux-Arts railway station opened as a museum in 1986, holds the world's densest concentration of Impressionist and post-Impressionist work — Manet, Monet, Degas, Cezanne, Van Gogh (Musee d'Orsay). The Centre Pompidou keeps Europe's largest modern and contemporary collection but is closed for a deep renovation from 2025, due to reopen around 2030. The Musee Picasso occupies the seventeenth-century Hotel Sale in the Marais, the quarter that now doubles as the city's gallery district.

The soundParis sings in chanson — Piaf, then Brel, Brassens, Ferre and Gainsbourg, who all passed through the post-war cafes of Saint-Germain. In the late 1940s the quarter's cellar clubs, Le Tabou among them, became jazz caves where Boris Vian played and, on visits, Miles Davis sat in. Its modern export is electronic: the "French touch" of Daft Punk and, from 2003, Pedro Winter's Ed Banger label — Justice, Cassius, SebastiAn, DJ Mehdi (Rolling Stone).

The living cityThe unit of Parisian life is the terrasse: an espresso or a glass of wine, a chair turned toward the street, and the unhurried watching the nineteenth century named flanerie. The daily anchor is bread — "boulangerie" is a protected term, meaning the loaf must be kneaded and baked on the premises. Above the counter sits cafe culture proper: the literary tables of the Flore and the Deux Magots where the existentialists worked. And running under all of it, the greve — the strike, the barricade, the argument in the street — is as Parisian as the croissant.

The quintessenceThe truest ritual is the smallest: the morning walk to the boulangerie for a warm baguette de tradition, and the quignon — the heel — broken off and eaten on the way home. At the cafe, the initiated take their coffee standing at the zinc comptoir, where it costs less than at a table. And at dusk comes the apero, the drink-before-the-meal that is really an excuse to sit down and let the day end slowly.

Down a stair in the Latin Quarter, a stone-vaulted 16th-century cellar — rumoured over the centuries to have sheltered Rosicrucians and Freemasons — has hosted live jazz and dancing every night since 1946. Lionel Hampton, Count Basie and Art Blakey have played the tiny basement stage while dancers spin below; nobody stays seated for long. It is also the club whose vaults appear in the film 'La La Land'.

Created in 1910 by patissier Louis Durand, whose shop in Maisons-Laffitte, in the western suburbs of Paris, sat on the route of the Paris-Brest-Paris cycle race (first run in 1891); the race's creator, journalist Pierre Giffard, asked Durand for a commemorative cake. Paris is Lutetia, the settlement of the Parisii on the Seine that Julius Caesar recorded as an island oppidum; Rome conquered it in the first century BC and built up the Left Bank around the Montagne Sainte-Genevieve. After the empire fell, Clovis I made Paris a Merovingian capital in the sixth century, and the name of the tribe outlived the Roman name of the town (culture.gouv.fr; World History Encyclopedia).

The medieval city thickened around the island and its markets, and in 1789 the Revolution began here. The Paris we walk now, though, is largely Napoleon III's: from 1853 to 1870 Haussmann demolished the crowded medieval quarters and cut more than 140 kilometres of boulevard, laying sewers and parks — the order was to "aerer, unifier, embellir," to give the city air (Wikipedia; Britannica). The Belle Epoque, roughly 1870 to 1914, gilded the result.

Food history rode alongside. The word "bistro" is only recorded from the 1880s — the tale of Russian Cossacks shouting bystro ("quickly") in 1814 is a charming myth, too late by some seventy years to be true (Wiktionary; World Wide Words). Les Halles, "the belly of Paris," fed the city for eight centuries until the whole market was moved overnight to Rungis in March 1969. And in May 1968, students tore up the Latin Quarter's cobbles for the Night of the Barricades, touching off a general strike of some ten million workers (Britannica).

Bouillon Julien

Behind a modest Faubourg-Saint-Denis frontage waits one of the most complete Art Nouveau rooms in Paris, opened in 1906: Louis Majorelle's tin-topped mahogany bar, Armand Ségaud's strutting peacock panels, a floral mosaic floor, and — the masterpiece — Louis Trézel's four glass 'flower women' of the seasons, drawn straight from Alfons Mucha's iconography. A recent restoration by British designer John Whelan brought the whole room back to its original glow, and it is still a bouillon, meaning a three-course dinner under all this costs less than a museum ticket.

Where — 16 rue du Faubourg Saint-Denis, 10th arrondissement

The modern restaurant - individual tables, set prices, a printed menu and food on demand - was born in 1760s Paris. Folklore credits a soup-seller named Boulanger (1765) hawking restorative broths called 'restaurants,' but historian Rebecca Spang showed the tale traces to a 1782 book and found no record Boulanger ever existed, crediting instead Mathurin Roze de Chantoiseau, who opened one around 1766-67. The very word comes from those 'restaurant' bouillons said to restore one's health.

Try it — Le Procope (6th), open since 1686 - Paris's oldest cafe-restaurant, haunt of Voltaire and the Revolutionaries.

Generally credited to 1955 at Maison Dalloyau in Paris, where pastry chef Cyriaque Gavillon devised a flat, layered cake so that a single bite carried the whole flavor - thin almond joconde soaked in coffee syrup, coffee buttercream and chocolate ganache - and his wife Andree named it 'Opera' for the nearby Palais Garnier. Rival claims exist (Gaston Lenotre for 1960, and earlier the chef Louis Clichy), but Le Monde formally credited Gavillon in 1988.

Try it — Dalloyau, the origin house, at 101 rue du Faubourg Saint-Honore (8th) and its other Paris boutiques.

The romantic tale of Tatar horsemen tenderizing raw meat under their saddles is a myth traceable to the 13th-century chronicler Jean de Joinville, who never actually met a Tatar; per The Cambridge Medieval History, any meat carried under a saddle was meant to heal the horse's sores, not to be eaten. The real dish is prosaic: in early-1900s Paris it was 'steak a l'americaine,' and the 1922 edition of Escoffier's Le Guide Culinaire defines 'a la tartare' as that steak served without egg yolk but with tartare sauce - later shortened to 'steak tartare.' It was a fixture of Paris bistros by the early 20th century.

Try it — A classic Left Bank brasserie such as Brasserie Lipp (6th), or a bistro known for hand-cut tartare like Bistrot Paul Bert (11th).

This is a hinge moment: the Centre Pompidou has gone dark for a five-year rebuild, while the fringe of 2010 — third-wave coffee, the natural-wine cave, the no-white-tablecloth neo-bistro — has become the city's mainstream, with Septime sitting at No. 40 on the 2025 World's 50 Best. Paris is quietly recomposing around the bistro, again.

Galerie Vivienne

Built in 1823 to plans by architect François-Jean Delannoy and opened in 1826, this is the most graceful survivor of Paris's glass-roofed shopping passages — a neoclassical Pompeian arcade of goddesses, garlands and a daylight canopy. Underfoot runs a mosaic floor signed by Giandomenico Facchina, the master who also mosaicked the Palais Garnier. Listed as a historic monument since 1974, its 176 metres shelter an old-world bookseller and a wine merchant, and the light on a grey afternoon is the whole point.

Where — entrances at 4 rue des Petits-Champs, 6 rue Vivienne and 5 rue de la Banque, 2nd arrondissement

Opened in 1686 by the Sicilian Procopio Cutò, who fitted his rooms with crystal chandeliers, wall mirrors and marble-topped tables — the template every European café would copy — Le Procope claims to be the oldest café in Paris. La Fontaine, Voltaire, Diderot's Encyclopédistes and later Danton and Franklin argued over coffee and ice cream here. Honest footnote: the original café shut in 1872 and the space reopened as a restaurant in 1957, so it is a restored shrine to the ritual rather than an unbroken 340-year pour.

Where — 13 rue de l'Ancienne-Comédie, 6th arrondissement (also reachable through the Cour du Commerce Saint-André)

Created in Paris in 1847 at the Chiboust bakery on rue Saint-Honore - credited to the house of Chiboust and to its pastry chef Auguste Julien - and named for the street, which itself honors Saint Honore, patron saint of bakers. The early brioche-based version is said to have been rebuilt on a puff-pastry base ringed with caramel-dipped choux, and the airy creme Chiboust was born in the same shop. Some sources push the earliest version back to around 1840.

Try it — A leading Paris patisserie known for it, such as Cedric Grolet (1st/2nd) or Cyril Lignac's La Patisserie (several arrondissements).

Born in Paris in January 1891, the cognac-and-mustard lobster gratin was named for Victorien Sardou's play 'Thermidor,' which premiered at the Comedie-Francaise that month and was swiftly pulled amid uproar as too critical of the Revolution. A chef - often named as Leopold Mourier - is said to have created it to mark the opening at the restaurant Chez Marie (Maison Maire) near the theater, though some accounts credit the Cafe de Paris or place the invention later. Escoffier had codified a recipe by 1903.

Try it — A grand old seafood house like Prunier (16th/8th), or a classic Montparnasse brasserie such as La Coupole (14th).

The Parisian ham-and-butter - a split baguette, good butter, boiled ham, nothing else - took shape in 19th-century Paris as cheap fuel for the porters and workers of the Les Halles market, with the baguette becoming its standard bread in the 1920s. It remains France's benchmark sandwich: of the roughly 2.4 billion sandwiches sold nationwide each year, about half are jambon-beurre - some 1.2 billion in 2017 - though that same year, per the Gira Conseil index, 1.46 billion hamburgers were sold, overtaking it.

Try it — At its best from a top boulangerie - the annual Grand Prix de la Baguette winners, or houses like Du Pain et des Idees (10th).

The pivot point is 1992, when Yves Camdeborde left a two-star kitchen at the Hotel de Crillon to open a cramped bistro, La Regalade, near the Porte d'Orleans, serving three-star technique at a roughly 30-franc prix fixe. The critic Sebastien Demorand later fused "bistro" and "gastronomie" into bistronomie to name what followed (camdeborde.com; Wikipedia). Strip out the china, the flowers and the cheese trolley, the logic went, and you can buy the same produce and cook it as well for a fifth of the price.

At the top, Michelin still clusters thickly — for 2025 roughly ten three-star rooms, seventeen two-star and about seventy-five one-star, plus some fifty Bib Gourmand value tables (Michelin Guide). But the honest map runs the other way: the tourist-trap belt hugs the big monuments, and Parisians increasingly eat east and north, in the neo-bistros and wine caves of the 10th, 11th and 20th. This is the Bourdain principle in practice — walk away from the postcard.

Underneath sits the plumbing. Rungis, opened in 1969 to replace Les Halles, is the second-largest wholesale food market on earth after Mexico City's Central de Abasto, and it provisions the whole region before dawn (Wikipedia; france.fr). And the shift in the glass is the natural-wine wave: the caves a manger that began around 2000 with Le Verre Vole are now the default of a whole generation of Paris tables.

DISH

Steak-frites

Onglet or bavette seared hard, a raft of thin fries, often maitre d'hotel butter or a peppercorn sauce. The unofficial plate of the zinc counter — and it is judged, always, by the fries.

Origin Contested between France and Belgium; a nineteenth-century bistro dish, not haute cuisine (Britannica; Wikipedia).

DISH

Baguette de tradition francaise

By law only flour, water, salt and leaven — no freezing, no additives — which is exactly why it goes stale by nightfall. The 'tradition' is the good one; the plain 'baguette' can be industrial.

Origin Paris; the name and recipe fixed by the Decret Pain of 13 September 1993 (Legifrance).

DISH

Croissant

Lamination — butter folded into dough — is the French contribution. Order it 'au beurre'; the cheaper 'ordinaire' is made with margarine.

Origin Austrian, not French: descended from the Viennese kipferl and brought to Paris by August Zang's 1838 Viennese bakery on rue de Richelieu. The Marie-Antoinette story is unsupported (The Local; ICE; Chowhound).

DISH

Soupe a l'oignon

Onions cooked slowly to bronze, beef stock, a raft of bread and gratineed Gruyere. Peasant economy turned ceremony — long the 4 a.m. cure of market porters.

Origin French; by lore the restorative of the all-night workers of Les Halles (the market link is tradition, not documented).

DISH

Blanquette de veau

Veal, cream, a whisper of lemon, button mushrooms and pearl onions. The Sunday dish, not the restaurant dish.

Origin French home cooking; 'blanquette' from blanc — the veal is poached, never browned, so the sauce stays pale.

DISH

Confit de canard

A pre-refrigeration preservation technique now a bistro staple. Honest to say: it is a guest in Paris, native to the land of cassoulet.

Origin South-West France (Gascony), not Paris — duck legs cured and cooked slowly in their own fat.

DISH

Jambon-beurre

Baguette, good butter, jambon de Paris. France's best-selling sandwich — reportedly over three million a day — and a fair test of a boulangerie.

Origin Paris; born late nineteenth century among Les Halles workers, on the baguette from the 1920s (Wikipedia; Taste of France).

DISH

Croque-monsieur

Ham and melted cheese bound in bechamel, grilled. Crown it with a fried egg and it becomes a croque-madame.

Origin Paris; commonly attributed to a cafe on the Boulevard des Capucines around 1910, and in Proust by 1918 (Devour Tours; Grokipedia).

DISH

Escargots de Bourgogne

The garlic-parsley butter is the whole point; the snail is the delivery vehicle. A brasserie classic in the capital.

Origin Burgundy, not Paris — its terroir is east, in the vineyards.

DISH

Macaron

Two almond-meringue shells, a ganache or jam between. The Catherine de' Medici origin tale is charming and doubtful.

Origin Italian almond biscuit by descent; the double-shelled 'macaron parisien' is a twentieth-century Paris refinement credited to Laduree's Pierre Desfontaines (Wikipedia).

DISH

Le cafe (express au comptoir)

A small black coffee taken standing at the zinc, faster and cheaper than the terrasse. For decades the weak link in a great food city — until the third wave arrived.

Origin The cafe as public institution is Parisian; the espresso itself is Italian by machine.

WALK

The Two Islands & the Seine About 2–3 km, 1.5–2 hours with a stop

Start at Pont Neuf and the tree-shaded triangle of Place Dauphine on the Île de la Cité. Walk east past the Sainte-Chapelle and along the flank of Notre-Dame; for the great flying-buttress chevet, carry on to the Pont de l'Archevêché or the Left-Bank quai (the garden directly behind the cathedral, Square Jean XXIII, is fenced off for its post-fire redevelopment, due to run through 2026). Cross the pedestrian-only Pont Saint-Louis (buskers work it most afternoons) onto the Île Saint-Louis and go straight up the spine of the island, rue Saint-Louis-en-l'Île, to Berthillon at no. 29–31. Finish by dropping down to the quais (Quai d'Orléans / Quai de Bourbon) to eat your cone over the water, back toward the Right Bank.

The quietest, most complete slice of old Paris on foot — two islands, one river, no cars on the linking bridge. The eating anchor is Berthillon, the sorbet house Raymond Berthillon started in his café here in 1954; the salted-caramel and wild-strawberry are the ones to get, and the walk-up window is cheaper than sitting. The flying-buttress chevet, best seen from the Pont de l'Archevêché or across the water, is the back of Notre-Dame most visitors overlook while queuing at the front.

WALK

Le Marais — market, Pletzl & Place des Vosges About 2.5 km, 2–3 hours

Begin at the Marché des Enfants Rouges, 39 rue de Bretagne. Head down rue de Bretagne and turn onto rue Vieille-du-Temple, the axis of the Marais, then cut east into the old Jewish quarter (the Pletzl) via rue des Rosiers — L'As du Fallafel is at no. 34. Continue to rue de Sévigné or rue des Francs-Bourgeois and end under the arcades of Place des Vosges.

A collision of two food cultures in fifteen minutes' walk: Sephardic street food and Ashkenazi delis on rue des Rosiers, then the produce and hot-lunch stalls of the Marché des Enfants Rouges — Paris's oldest covered market, open since 1615, named for a nearby 16th-century orphanage whose children were dressed in charity red. Get the falafel spécial (fried aubergine, cabbage, harissa) to take away and eat it on a bench in Place des Vosges, the arcaded square built for Henri IV and inaugurated in 1612, two years after he was assassinated.

WALK

The Covered Passages About 2 km, 1.5–2 hours

Start at Galerie Vivienne, entered from 4 rue des Petits-Champs near the Palais-Royal. Walk its length, come out and thread north via rue Vivienne toward boulevard Montmartre and the Passage des Panoramas. Cross the boulevard into the twin arcades Passage Jouffroy and, at its far end, Passage Verdeau. For a food coda, walk 10 minutes south-east to rue Montorgueil and Stohrer at no. 51.

A dry-weather walk through the glass-roofed 19th-century arcades that predate the department store. Galerie Vivienne (built 1823, opened 1826) is the grand one, with a mosaic floor by Facchina; the Passage des Panoramas (1799) is the oldest and scruffiest, now wall-to-wall bistros and stamp dealers. Cap it at Stohrer, Paris's oldest pâtisserie (1730), founded by Louis XV's pastry chef Nicolas Stohrer — credited with inventing the rum baba, a tale tied to the exiled Polish king Stanisław Leszczyński.

WALK

Canal Saint-Martin About 3–4 km, roughly 2 hours

Start near Place de la République (or the covered Marché Saint-Martin at 31–33 rue du Château d'Eau, a neighbourhood market hall of the 1850s, rebuilt in 1880). Reach the canal where it surfaces around rue du Faubourg-du-Temple and walk north along the water, crossing between the two banks — Quai de Valmy (calmer) and Quai de Jemmapes (cafés) — over the green cast-iron footbridges. Pass the Hôtel du Nord facade and the shop streets rue de Marseille and rue Beaurepaire, and end at the wide Bassin de la Villette by the Rotonde.

Working-canal Paris: nine locks and swing bridges that still pivot to let boats through while everyone waits. This is picnic-and-apéro territory — the banks fill at dusk with people and bottles of natural wine from the neighborhood caves. The Hôtel du Nord is the real hotel behind Eugène Dabit's 1929 novel and Marcel Carné's 1938 film, though the movie itself was shot in a studio.

WALK

Montmartre — Abbesses to the Butte About 2.5 km but steeply uphill, 2–3 hours

Start at Abbesses métro (Guimard's original glass canopy). Walk rue des Abbesses west, turn up rue Lepic (the Café des Deux Moulins, from the film Amélie, is at no. 15) past the surviving Moulin de la Galette. Cut to rue des Saules and the Clos Montmartre vineyard, opposite the Au Lapin Agile cabaret. Climb to the Sacré-Cœur, then double back to Place du Tertre and down past La Maison Rose. End on the basilica steps for the view.

Yes, it's famous — but the food-shop streets rue des Abbesses and rue Lepic are where actual Montmartrois buy their bread and cheese, and the climb rewards you. The oddity is the Clos Montmartre, a working municipal vineyard of some 1,800 vines that yields only a few hundred litres a year, harvested each October with a neighborhood fête. Skip the Place du Tertre portrait-sellers and eat before you go up.

WALK

Latin Quarter & rue Mouffetard About 3 km, 2–3 hours

Start at Shakespeare and Company, 37 rue de la Bûcherie, facing Notre-Dame across the river. Wander the medieval lanes behind it (rue Saint-Séverin, rue Galande), then climb via the Sorbonne to the Panthéon. Drop down the far side to Place de la Contrescarpe and descend rue Mouffetard through the market to the church of Saint-Médard at the bottom.

A walk from books to cheese. The bookshop is George Whitman's Shakespeare and Company, opened in 1951 (as Le Mistral) and renamed in 1964 in homage to Sylvia Beach's original shop, which first published Joyce's Ulysses in 1922. Rue Mouffetard is one of the oldest market streets in the city — a downhill run of fromageries, stalls and hole-in-the-wall traiteurs; Androuet at no. 134 is the cheese stop. Hemingway wrote A Moveable Feast about lodging just off Contrescarpe at 74 rue du Cardinal-Lemoine. Go on a weekend morning when the market is live.

WALK

Belleville & Ménilmontant About 3 km, uphill in stretches, 2 hours

Start at Belleville métro, where four arrondissements meet. Head up rue de Belleville through the Chinatown of Vietnamese and Chinese kitchens, then detour into rue Dénoyez for the street art. Come back to boulevard de Belleville for the twice-weekly morning market, then climb through the Parc de Belleville to its belvedere. Continue into Ménilmontant (rue de Ménilmontant / rue de la Mare) and end back at the park's terrace for the skyline.

The city's most multicultural, least touristed food quarter — hand-pulled noodles and pho on rue de Belleville, North African and Turkish stalls at the boulevard market, and rue Dénoyez, the most heavily repainted alley in Paris, an open-air graffiti wall. The Parc de Belleville belvedere gives a free, wide-open panorama that rivals Sacré-Cœur without the crowds. Legend says Édith Piaf was born on the pavement at 72 rue de Belleville — a plaque there still claims it — though her birth certificate records a nearby hospital.

Paris keeps its best gift for the patient: a scatter of small museums where a serious collection comes wrapped in a garden or a courtyard you can actually sit in. You buy a ticket for the art and stay for the roses, the fig trees, the sculpture on the gravel, usually with a cafe or a pot of tea within reach and the crowds left behind at the door. These are the rooms and gardens a chain city could never fake, most of them free or nearly so, all of them unhurried.

GARDEN MUSEUM

Musee Rodin 7th arrondissement

The 18th-century Hotel Biron sits in roughly three hectares of formal grounds: some 2,000 rose bushes of about 100 varieties frame the house, with gravel walks and clipped box beyond. The Thinker broods on his plinth among the box trees, The Gates of Hell rises at the far end, and Rodin's bronze and marble stand under open sky. Indoors the collection is Rodin's own -- The Kiss, the studies, the Camille Claudel room.

This is the one everyone means when they say a museum should come with a garden. You can buy a garden-only ticket and spend a whole afternoon walking the rose alleys with The Thinker for company, the golden dome of Les Invalides over the wall.

The garden café L'Augustine, the garden cafe-restaurant set within the sculpture park not far from The Thinker (long known simply as the Cafe du Musee Rodin) -- a small interior salon that spills onto terrace tables in good weather, open Tuesday to Sunday.

Anchor Hotel Biron, built between 1727 and 1732 to designs by architect Jean Aubert (with Jacques Gabriel); Rodin took studios here from 1908, and it opened as his museum on 4 August 1919.

GARDEN MUSEUM

Musee de la Vie Romantique 9th arrondissement

A green-shuttered villa of 1830 reached down a cobbled alley, once the home of painter Ary Scheffer -- rose bushes, clematis and bellflowers around a little glass conservatory. The collection gathers George Sand keepsakes on the ground floor (furniture, jewellery, a cast of her arm) and Scheffer's Romantic canvases upstairs.

It feels like stepping out of the city into a country cottage. Scheffer held his Friday salons in these rooms, and Chopin, Delacroix and George Sand walked this same garden. The house and its tea salon reopened in February 2026 after a refurbishment.

The garden café Rose Bakery salon de the, set in the garden's glass greenhouse and on the leafy terrace, serving scones, cakes and savoury tarts; open Tuesday to Sunday through the year.

Anchor Hotel Scheffer-Renan, built 1830, the studio-home of Dutch-born Romantic painter Ary Scheffer (1795-1858); free municipal museum.

Open in Google Maps →
GARDEN MUSEUM

Musee Bourdelle 15th arrondissement

The preserved studios and apartment of sculptor Antoine Bourdelle -- a warren of ateliers opening onto green sculpture gardens where monumental bronzes, Penelope among them, stand among the trees. His great plaster halls are kept much as he left them.

Bourdelle was Rodin's pupil who became a master in his own right; the permanent collection is free, and after a two-year renovation (reopened 2023) the atelier-gardens are one of the calmest, least-visited corners in the city.

The garden café Le Rhodia, the on-site tea room and cafe-restaurant opened in the 2023 renovation, where you can linger over a gourmet menu in the shade of the master's sculptures; the cafes of Montparnasse are also a couple of minutes' walk.

Anchor The studio-home of sculptor Antoine Bourdelle (1861-1929), a former assistant of Rodin, who worked here for over four decades; free municipal museum, open Tuesday to Sunday.

Open in Google Maps →
GARDEN MUSEUM

Musee de Montmartre - Jardins Renoir 18th arrondissement

The oldest house on the hill -- a 17th-century manor -- with three terraced gardens named for Renoir, looking over the Clos Montmartre vineyard. The collection tells the story of bohemian Montmartre: Toulouse-Lautrec, the Chat Noir, and Suzanne Valadon's reconstructed studio.

Renoir rented rooms here and painted the Bal du Moulin de la Galette and The Swing (La Balancoire) in these very gardens -- you sit where the pictures were made, above the last working vines in Paris.

The garden café Cafe Renoir, tucked into the gardens overlooking the vineyard, open Wednesday to Sunday (a 6-euro garden-and-cafe ticket lets you in even without the full museum).

Anchor 12 rue Cortot, a 17th-century house; Renoir had a studio here in 1875-1876, and Valadon and Utrillo later worked in the building.

Open in Google Maps →
GARDEN MUSEUM

Musee Zadkine 6th arrondissement

A tiny house and studio hidden behind the streetfront, entered through a pocket sculpture garden shaded by birch, maple, prunus and Virginia creeper, where Zadkine's bronzes -- Orpheus, the Torso of the Destroyed City -- still stand where he placed them.

One of the smallest and most secret gardens in Paris; you can genuinely be alone with the sculptures. The permanent collection is free, open daily except Monday.

The garden café No cafe on site -- but the Jardin du Luxembourg is a minute away and the cafes of rue d'Assas and rue Vavin are around the corner.

Anchor The home-studio of Cubist sculptor Ossip Zadkine (1890-1967), who lived here from 1928 until his death; free municipal museum.

Open in Google Maps →
GARDEN MUSEUM

Musee Cernuschi 8th arrondissement

A grand 1870s mansion whose windows open straight onto Parc Monceau. Inside is one of Europe's great collections of Chinese and Japanese art -- archaic bronzes, a monumental Japanese Buddha, jades and ceramics.

Here the park is the garden: you leave the Asian bronzes and step directly into Monceau's follies, colonnade and lawns. The permanent collection is free.

The garden café No cafe inside the museum; Parc Monceau's benches and kiosks are right outside the door.

Anchor The former mansion of Italian-French banker and collector Henri Cernuschi (1821-1896); opened as a City of Paris museum in 1898.

Open in Google Maps →
GARDEN MUSEUM

Petit Palais (Musee des Beaux-Arts de la Ville de Paris) 8th arrondissement

Charles Girault's 1900 palace wraps around a semicircular arcaded courtyard garden: a gilded peristyle, palms and banana trees, three tiled ponds in blue and gold, mosaic-topped tables. The collection runs from antiquity to 1900.

One of the loveliest free things in Paris -- the permanent collection costs nothing, and the inner garden is a hidden oasis a single step off the busiest avenue in the city.

The garden café Le Jardin du Petit Palais, the cafe-restaurant set right in the arcaded courtyard garden beneath the palms (bistronomic menu by chef Amandine Chaignot), open Tuesday to Sunday; reachable even without a museum ticket.

Anchor Built by architect Charles Girault for the 1900 Exposition Universelle; a museum since 1902; free permanent collection.

Open in Google Maps →
GARDEN MUSEUM

Musee Nissim de Camondo 8th arrondissement

A mansion built in 1911-1914 in the manner of Versailles' Petit Trianon, its rear rooms and formal garden opening onto Parc Monceau. Inside, a perfectly preserved collection of 18th-century French decorative arts -- panelling, furniture, Sevres porcelain, silver.

A frozen aristocratic house with a heartbreaking story: Moise de Camondo named it for his son Nissim, an aviator killed in the First World War, and left it to the nation; the surviving family was later murdered at Auschwitz. The garden over the park is part of the hush.

The garden café No cafe in the house; Parc Monceau lies directly beyond the garden windows for a break.

Anchor Hotel Moise de Camondo, built 1911-1914 by architect Rene Sergent with a garden by landscape architect Achille Duchene; opened as a museum in 1936.

Open in Google Maps →
GARDEN MUSEUM

Grande Mosquee de Paris - tea garden 5th arrondissement

Not a museum but a Hispano-Moorish mosque of the 1920s whose cafe maure opens a tiled patio garden to anyone, no ticket needed -- fig trees, a fountain, blue-and-white zellige, sparrows in the plane trees.

Mint tea poured tall and sweet, gazelle-horn pastries, the shade of the fig trees -- one of the most transporting corners in the Latin Quarter. (A working place of worship with a public tea garden, not a museum in the strict sense.)

The garden café The Cafe Maure / salon de the itself, open daily, serving mint tea and North African pastries around the patio.

Anchor Built 1922-1926 in Hispano-Moorish style and inaugurated in 1926, raised in homage to the Muslim soldiers who died for France in the First World War.

Open in Google Maps →
GARDEN MUSEUM

Jardin des Plantes galleries (Museum national d'Histoire naturelle) 5th arrondissement

France's national botanical garden, laid out from 1635, with its long perspective of clipped planes, a rose garden and an alpine garden -- and set within it the museum's great galleries: the Grande Galerie de l'Evolution with its caravan of taxidermy animals under a glass roof, plus mineralogy. (The Palaeontology and Comparative Anatomy gallery is closed for renovation until 2027.)

The garden itself is free and open every day; the galleries are the antique heart of natural-history museum-making, and the parade of animals crossing the great hall is unforgettable for children and adults alike.

The garden café Les Belles Plantes, the garden restaurant with two large terraces (one over the wallaby enclosure, one shaded by a century-old paulownia), plus a cafe inside the Grande Galerie de l'Evolution.

Anchor The Museum national d'Histoire naturelle; botanical garden founded in 1635 as the royal medicinal garden; the Grande Galerie de l'Evolution reopened in its 19th-century building in 1994.
