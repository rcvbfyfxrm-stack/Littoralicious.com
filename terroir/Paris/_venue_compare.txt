############ v-l-arpege — L'Arpège (creme, pri 1)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: Passard put down the knife on red meat in 2001 and picked up a trowel; a quarter-century on, the cooks behind two of the city's most-loved bistros, Septime and Le Servan, came up through his kitchen.
VERD: One of the most influential kitchens in modern Paris, and you can trace the bloodline: alumni run Septime (Bertrand Grébaut trained here) and Le Servan (Tatiana Levha, ex-Arpège, ex-Astrance). In July 2025 Passard went further than anyone at his level, striking meat, fish, shellfish, dairy and eggs from the menu — the first three-star in the world to do it, with only honey from his own hives surviving. Come for the vegetables off his own gardens, not for value, and not for the egg you've read about.
SIG: The house was built on the chaud-froid d'oeuf, the "Arpège egg": a warm farm-egg yolk under cold four-spice cream sharpened with sherry vinegar and chives, a thread of maple, served in its own shell. Verify before you count on it — the July 2025 plant-based turn stripped eggs, cream and butter, so what reaches the table now is the garden: vegetables, fruit, herbs and flowers from Passard's own plots in the Sarthe and by Mont-Saint-Michel.
CAV: Not a value eat. Dinner tasting menus run roughly 420 to 540 euros (a lunch menu is nearer 175), and since July 2025 the animal cooking that made his name — the lobster, the pigeon — is gone. With meat, fish, shellfish, dairy and eggs all struck, the kitchen is now all-vegetable but for the honey from its own hives, so read it as near-vegan rather than strictly so. Portions can feel small for the money, and a table is hard-won.

############ v-plenitude-cheval-blanc-paris — Plénitude - Cheval Blanc Paris (creme, pri 2)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: Donckele already held three stars on the Riviera. He came to the Samaritaine and did it again in seven months - this time making the sauce the main course.
VERD: Donckele's second three-star kitchen, and one that lived up to its billing rather than coasting on the Cheval Blanc name: three MICHELIN stars in barely seven months - awarded at the March 2022 Cognac ceremony, straight from zero to three - and held every year since, through the 2026 guide, backed by Gault&Millau 19/20 (five toques) and No. 14 on the World's 50 Best 2025. Here the sauce, not the protein, is the dish.
SIG: The sauces themselves. Donckele works as a self-styled saucier-parfumeur, numbering his layered sauces like perfumes into what he calls his "Absolues," and the two carte-blanche menus (Fuguons ensemble in 4 acts, Symphonie in 6) are built so that langoustine, turbot or pigeon serve as condiments to their broths, vinaigrettes and jus - named plates like La Langoustine Sabayon Cardinal Thenors and Turbot Tellurienne d'Hiver, reported by Fine Dining Lovers. Pastry by Maxime Frédéric, named World's Best Pastry Chef 2025.
CAV: Not a discovery, a summit - and priced like one. The six-act Symphonie ran EUR 480 in 2025 (the four-act Fuguons ensemble EUR 445), with wine pairings up to EUR 495 on top; dinner only, Tuesday to Saturday, in a hushed 26-seat room above the Seine. The sauce-as-hero concept is cerebral and formal by design - go for the thrill of watching a master saucier work, not for comfort food or a casual night.

############ v-guy-savoy — Guy Savoy (creme, pri 3)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: The old royal mint on the Left Bank, one floor up: six salons hung with contemporary art on loan from the Pinault Collection, the Seine in the windows, and a truffle-and-artichoke soup that has been Guy Savoy's signature for decades.
VERD: Two MICHELIN stars in the 2026 Guide France (down from three since 2023), yet ranked joint World No. 1 by La Liste 2026 — a grand classical restaurant inside the Monnaie de Paris, the old royal mint, where the tasting menu (around EUR 490) still opens with the artichoke-and-black-truffle soup that has anchored his cooking for decades.
SIG: Soupe d'artichaut a la truffe noire — artichoke and black-truffle soup, served with a toasted mushroom-and-truffle brioche slicked in black-truffle butter; his longest-standing signature and the plate most often named as his defining dish.

Its "world's best" standing comes from La Liste's algorithmic aggregator — and in 2026 that top spot is shared with nine other restaurants, all scoring 99.5/100 — not a single live-judged verdict. Dinner is a special-occasion outlay (roughly EUR 490 before wine); open only Wednesday to Saturday, with Saturday dinner-only.

############ v-kei — Kei (creme, pri 4)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: A boy in Nagano, son of a kaiseki cook, watched a documentary about French cuisine on Japanese television and decided that would be his life. He moved to France in 1998, cooked for years under Gilles Goujon and then Alain Ducasse, and opened his own small room off the rue Coq-Héron in 2011. In January 2020 he became the first Japanese chef to hold three MICHELIN stars in France — a rank he still holds in 2026. The crisp-vegetable "garden" under a cloud of lemon foam took five years to perfect: nothing here is casual, and nothing is left to chance.
VERD: France's only Japanese-born three-star chef, and the room has held all three MICHELIN stars every year since 2020 — this is precision plated as spectacle, not comfort or improvisation.
SIG: Le jardin de légumes croquants — a "garden" of up to forty seasonal ingredients, crisp raw vegetables and herbs layered over Scottish smoked salmon, bound with a rocket cream and tomato vinaigrette and crowned with a cloud of lemon foam and a black-olive-and-almond crumble. It took five years to perfect and shifts with the season, so no two plates are quite alike.
CAV: This is ceremony and expense, not a spontaneous dinner. The cheapest way in is a €195 Discovery menu served only at Friday and Saturday lunch; the full tastings run €290 to €580 (the restaurant notes prices are subject to change). The room is small, and reservations — which open on the first Tuesday of each month, two months ahead — go fast. Go for the craft, not for warmth.

############ v-table-by-bruno-verjus — Table by Bruno Verjus (creme, pri 5)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: Bruno Verjus never trained in a kitchen — he was a medical student, an entrepreneur in China, a photographer, a radio food critic — and only late did he teach himself to cook and open Table by the Marche d'Aligre. From a wave-shaped counter you watch him plate the day's produce himself. No a la carte, no hiding: just the 'Couleur du jour' and whatever the tide and the fields sent. The world put him at No. 3, then No. 8 — either way, in the top ten.
VERD: The two MICHELIN stars and the No. 8-in-the-world ranking are earned, not hype: one daily seating at a wave-shaped counter, Verjus plating in front of you, ingredient-worship over technique for its own sake. Book it and you'll watch a self-taught ex-food-critic cook the day's tide and fields into a single 'Couleur du jour' tasting.
SIG: Lobster 'mi-cru mi-cuit' — half-raw, half-cooked, trap-caught off the Ile d'Yeu, the shells toasted and the flesh warmed in ghee to roughly the temperature of the mouth — the dish critics reach for first when they describe him. The meal ends, as it always does, on his chocolate-and-caper tart under a spoon of caviar.
CAV: It slipped from No. 3 (2024) to No. 8 (2025) on The World's 50 Best — still elite, but the trajectory points down, not up. There's one set seating, no a la carte, only the fixed daily tasting (reported around EUR 480, subject to change) at a counter of roughly two dozen seats, with a non-refundable deposit taken at booking — so the real obstacle is landing a reservation, and you don't get to choose your dishes.

############ v-le-cinq — Le Cinq (creme, pri 6)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: Ten years at three stars, not one demotion — while Paris's top tables keep trading places, the George V's dining room does not move.
VERD: Paris's steadiest three-star. Le Cinq has held MICHELIN's top rank for ten consecutive years under Christian Le Squer, reconfirmed in Guide France 2026 — if you want grand haute cuisine that never wobbles, this is the safest three-star bet in the city.
SIG: Bar de ligne, caviar et lait ribot de mon enfance — line-caught sea bass with caviar and the fermented Breton milk of his childhood, the dish Le Squer devised at Pavillon Ledoyen and carried to Le Cinq, and the plate the kitchen is judged by (Gault&Millau, 19/20 — five toques).
CAV: Gilded, formal and punishingly expensive — a room built for occasion, not intimacy. The shadow of Jay Rayner's savage 2017 Observer takedown still trails the name, though the kitchen has held three stars without interruption since.

############ v-datil — Datil (rising, pri 7)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: A sabre champion who quit the piste for the pass. Before Manon Fleury cooked for anyone, she fenced for France — and something of that discipline survives at Datil, where the meal begins not with butter and luxury but with a bouillon of potato peelings, and ends with a tea steeped from plum stones. Nothing is thrown away, no one shouts across the line, and the animal on the plate has been reduced to a whisper. It is the table that made "sustainable" stop sounding like an apology.
VERD: One of Paris's clearest proofs that a near-meatless tasting menu can carry a MICHELIN star — and, since 2026, a Green Star — without a single luxury crutch: no caviar, no wagyu, no truffle-for-show, animal protein used only as a condiment.
SIG: The zero-waste tasting menu: it opens with a bouillon drawn from vegetable peelings (potato peel, at last report) and closes with a tisane brewed from plum stones — a nod to the restaurant's name. Plants lead; meat plays a background extra.
CAV: Tasting menu only, and not cheap — the restaurant's own site now lists EUR 95 for a five-course lunch and EUR 160 for a seven-course dinner (wine pairings EUR 65 / EUR 85); older Le Fooding listings still show EUR 65 / EUR 120, so prices have climbed — check when booking. A small room that books out fast, and if you want meat at the centre of the plate, this is the wrong table.

############ v-septime — Septime (rising, pri 8)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: In 2011 a graphic-designer-turned-cook who had trained under Alain Passard at Arpege opened a plain oak-and-steel room on rue de Charonne with his high-school friend Theophile Pourriat. They meant to build a good bistro; instead they wrote the template half of Paris has been copying ever since.
VERD: One MICHELIN star (held into the 2026 Guide) and No. 40 on The World's 50 Best 2025, yet lunch is five courses for 85 euros and there is no menu to choose from: you eat the carte blanche the kitchen sends, rewritten by the week's harvest. The neo-bistro that set the template for a generation of Paris kitchens, and still one of the hardest of them to book.
SIG: No fixed signature dish, by design: the carte blanche tasting menu is the whole offer, five steps at lunch, seven at dinner, driven by a near-obsessive micro-seasonality. Recent plates cited by The World's 50 Best: cepes and girolles in peppercorn sauce, and young leeks in a mussel-and-pimenton butter under wafer-thin lardo.
CAV: Getting in is the trial, not the meal. Booking is online only, opens daily at 10am for three weeks out, and goes fast; closed Saturday and Sunday. There is no a la carte, so anyone who wants to order for themselves, or avoids seafood, should look elsewhere.

############ v-le-rigmarole — Le Rigmarole (rising, pri 9)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: They met over the pass at Guy Savoy, an American-Taiwanese pastry commis and a Franco-American cook, cooked their way through three-star kitchens in Paris and New York, and then opened a walnut counter of their own — where his Japanese charcoal and her Italian pasta finally eat off the same menu.
VERD: One MICHELIN star, held since 2020 and still standing in the 2026 guide, for a walnut counter where Robert Compagnon grills yakitori over Japanese binchotan while the kitchen rolls its pasta by hand. It is tasting-menu-driven, books up, and is not cheap, so treat it as a plan-ahead splurge rather than a walk-in. Hours and format have shifted over time (mid-week tastings, more casual weekend and lunch service), so check what is on before you go.

############ v-le-servan — Le Servan (cult, pri 12)

WHY: A 1936 café off rue Chapon — copper bar, gilded ceiling, worn vintage tiles — where Sarah Michielsen runs the room and Bastien Fidelin runs a thousand-bottle cellar from the wine shop across the street, and the plate is written to argue with the wine rather than the other way round.

############ v-chez-l-ami-jean — Chez L'Ami Jean (authentique, pri 14)

############ v-le-baratin — Le Baratin (authentique, pri 15)
HOOK: (none)
WHY: Up a quiet Belleville side street, an Argentine cook named Raquel Carena has worked the same handful of covers since 1987 — no printed menu, just a chalkboard of brains, pig's ears and braised things that other Paris chefs, Pierre Hermé among them, come to eat on their own time.
VERD: Belleville's benchmark natural-wine bistrot: a daily blackboard of offal-forward, deeply personal cooking that Paris food writers credit Raquel Carena with pioneering — bistronomie before it had a name — paired to one of the city's deepest natural-wine cellars (20-plus by the glass, independent-focused). ############ v-allard — Allard (authentique, pri 18)

############ v-bouillon-chartier — Bouillon Chartier (story, pri 20)

############ v-le-grand-vefour — Le Grand Véfour (story, pri 22)
