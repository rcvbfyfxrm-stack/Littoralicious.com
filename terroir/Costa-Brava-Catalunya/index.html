Costa Brava

Catalunya · Spain
“Where the Pyrenees drown in the sea”
Terroir Briefing · 04 June 2026
Districts· Why Now· History· Eat· Drink· Tables· Walk· Avoid

The Costa Brava is the precise point where the Pyrenees drown in the Mediterranean. Cap de Creus, the easternmost tip of the Iberian Peninsula, is cut from Precambrian schist 580 to 540 million years old — the oldest rock in Catalonia — folded by the Variscan orogeny into the contorted shear zones that made it a textbook for structural geologists and a stage-set for Dalí, who summered just over the ridge at Cadaqués and bought his fisherman's hut at Portlligat in 1930. Behind this hard, wind-scoured coast lies the opposite landscape: the fertile alluvial plain of the Empordà, drained by the Muga, Fluvià and Ter, where rice has grown since the fifteenth century. The name was coined by the poet Ferran Agulló on 12 September 1908 — *costa brava*, the wild coast. Three truths to lock in first: eat the *gamba de Palamós* where it is landed, watch it sold at the descending auction on the Palamós quay before it ever reaches a plate; spoon a *garoina* raw from the half-shell in the depth of winter, when its roe is fullest; and drink a glass of DO Empordà — Garnatxa and Carinyena off low staked bush vines — under the tramuntana that keeps those vines dry and free of mildew.

Empúries was founded around 575 BCE under the Greek name Emporion — literally "market" — by Greeks from Phocaea; it is the only site in Spain with a Greek and a Roman city side by side, and the doorway through which vine cultivation is traditionally said to have entered the Iberian Peninsula.

THE NEIGHBORHOODS
Where it lives

The Costa Brava reads as a string of towns standing in for neighborhoods: the inland gastronomic capital (Girona), the whitewashed fishing villages of Cap de Creus, the twin coves of Palafrugell, the working port of Palamós, the salt town of L'Escala, and the volcanic hinterland behind.

  • Girona — The inland capital and gastronomic pole: a medieval Old Town (Barri Vell) above the ochre Cases de l'Onyar restored in 1983–84, one of Europe's best-preserved Jewish quarters (El Call), and the orbit of the Roca brothers — El Celler de Can Roca (three stars since 2009), Restaurant Massana, the Mercat del Lleó, and Jordi Roca's Rocambolesc gelateria.
  • Cadaqués & Portlligat — A whitewashed fishing village at the tip of Cap de Creus, a cobbled maze that fixed itself in Dalí's imagination; Portlligat next door holds the house-museum, the fisherman's hut he bought in 1930 and worked in for fifty years. Compartir Cadaqués, from the elBulli trio, is the town's best table.
  • Calella de Palafrugell & Llafranc — Twin coves linked by the cliff-hugging camí de ronda, an arcaded fishermen's quarter (Les Voltes at Port Bo), the spiritual home of the garoinada. Casamar sits on the Llafranc headland, its door opening straight onto the path's steps.
  • Palamós — A working fishing port and home of the cult red prawn; the late-afternoon auction is the region's commercial heart. La Gamba on Plaça de Sant Pere, the Espai del Peix at the llotja, the Museu de la Pesca on the quay.
  • L'Escala & Empúries — The salt-and-anchovy town beside the only site in Iberia with both a Greek and a Roman city. The ruins of Empúries, the working salting houses, the Anchovy and Salt Museum, and Anxoves Solés on the Plaça de la Sardana.
  • Roses — The bay town carrying the elBulli legacy: the Cala Montjoi site reopened as the elBulli1846 museum on 15 June 2023, and Rafa's — a five-table counter beloved by Ferran Adrià — serves grilled and raw seafood with nothing else.
  • Pals & Peratallada — Two of the best-preserved medieval stone villages in Spain, ringed by the paddies of arròs de Pals (cultivated since 1452). Can Bonay sits under the arcades of the Plaça de les Voltes in Peratallada — pedra tallada, "carved stone."
  • Out of town — Olot, La Garrotxa & Besalú — The volcanic hinterland: Olot, capital of a natural park with forty-odd volcanic cones, and Besalú, a near-perfect medieval town with a fortified twelfth-century Pont Vell and a Jewish mikveh discovered in 1964 — the first found in Spain.

HISTORY & TERROIR
Where it comes from

It begins at Empúries — Emporion, Greek for "trading place, market" — founded around 575 BCE by Phocaean Greeks from Massalia, the largest Greek colony on the Iberian Peninsula and the gateway through which vine cultivation is said to have entered Iberia. In 218 BCE Rome landed here to counter Hannibal, and the conquest of Hispania began from this beach; it is the only site in Spain with a Greek and a Roman city side by side. The Franks took the region around 785; a separate County of Empúries appeared around 813, split from Roussillon in 989 under Hugh I, and moved its capital to Castelló d'Empúries in the eleventh century — whose church of Santa Maria is so vast that locals call it the "Cathedral of l'Empordà." Through the Middle Ages the Benedictine monastery of Sant Pere de Rodes (recorded from 878) kept the vineyards alive; the county passed into the orbit of the Crown of Aragon by 1325. The modern layer is Salvador Dalí — born in Figueres on 11 May 1904, the Portlligat hut bought in 1930 — and the smugglers who ran tobacco, penicillin and lingerie into the hidden coves of the *camí de ronda* between 1940 and 1958, when autarky Spain was cut off from the world.

WHAT TO EAT
At table

Start with the *gamba de Palamós*, the deep-water red prawn simply seared with salt, the head sucked for its coral — sweeter than any other Mediterranean prawn because of the cold canyon water it is trawled from. In winter, *garoines* (sea urchins, *Paracentrotus lividus*) spooned raw from the half-shell onto toasted bread, all iodine. The salt-cured *anxoves de l'Escala*, hand-filleted and laid in olive oil, eaten on bread with tomato. *Arròs de Pals* in the local seafood *arrossos*. *Suquet de peix*, the fishermen's stew of monkfish, *escórpora* and potato bound by a mortar *picada* of garlic, nuts, fried bread and saffron. And *recuit de drap*, the fresh Empordà curd drained in its cloth over seventeen to twenty-four hours, eaten with honey — closer to dessert than cheese.

WHAT TO DRINK
By the glass

DO Empordà is a wine made by the tramuntana. The wind dries the canopy and suppresses mildew, so the three Garnatxa — *blanca*, *gris* and *negra*, known here as *Lledoner* — and Carinyena (called *Samsó* locally) grow on low staked bush vines, many of them fifty to a hundred and twenty years old, low-yield and concentrated. Look for *Lledoner Roig*, the "grey Grenache" of which roughly sixty of Catalonia's sixty-one hectares sit in the Empordà, and for *Garnatxa de l'Empordà*, the sweet, sun-dried, oxidative fortified style. Drink Espelt (organic, two hundred hectares at Vilajuïga), the biodynamic Mas Estela at Selva de Mar, the Cadaqués wines of Martín Faixó grown at three hundred metres inside the Cap de Creus park, or the sulphite-free natural wines of La Vinyeta at Mollet de Peralada — and a chilled local white or cava alongside the raw urchin.

A WALK
The route

Start at first light in Calella de Palafrugell, beneath the whitewashed Les Voltes arches at Port Bo, where flat-bottomed boats are still drawn up on the sand and the air carries brine, wet pine and tar. Pick up the *camí de ronda* (GR®92) at the north edge of the cove — the old patrol path the Guardia Civil walked against smugglers — and follow the cliff-cut trail to Llafranc: about 5.5 km, 164 m of climb, an hour and a half to two hours. Pine runs to the waterline; you dip through El Canadell, then Cala d'en Cosme and Port Pelegrí, across one rougher half-hour of cut steps and short rock tunnels hugging the sea. Gulls, the slap of swell in the rock clefts, cicadas warming. The path climbs to the headland and the door of Casamar — which opens directly onto the camí de ronda steps — before dropping into Llafranc's quieter bay; push on up to the Sant Sebastià lighthouse for the panorama back over both coves.

The Dish
Canon dishes
7 dishes
A single garoina carries up to 25% of its body weight in roe at the February–March peak — roughly five times the off-season amount, which is exactly why the garoinada is a winter festival, not a summer one.
The gamba de Palamós is sold backwards — a descending, Dutch-style auction where the buyer stops the falling price, Monday to Friday from four o'clock on the Palamós quay.
#1
DISH

Gamba de Palamós

The deep-water red prawn, sweeter than any other in the Mediterranean.

Aristeus antennatus, trawled at 400 m and deeper off the canyon between Cap de Begur and the Ridaura, graded into three sizes and sold at the afternoon Dutch auction. Seared briefly with salt; the head, full of coral, is the prized part, sucked or grilled. Protected by the "Gamba de Palamós" quality mark. Eat it at La Gamba in Palamós or at Rafa's in Roses.

#2
DISH

Garoines (sea urchins)

Paracentrotus lividus, spooned raw from the half-shell in deep winter.

Hand-gathered by divers and at their peak from January to March, when the roe fills to a quarter of the animal's body weight. Eaten raw, half-opened, all iodine, with toasted bread and sometimes lemon, or worked into scrambled eggs and rice. The garoinada campaign across Palafrugell's three coves runs 16 Jan–29 Mar 2026.

#3
DISH

Anxoves de l'Escala

Salt-cured anchovies, a craft the town has practised since the 1700s.

Engraulis encrasicolus layered head-to-tail in barrels with coarse salt, sealed under a salt cap, pressed and cured six to eighteen months, then hand-cleaned, deboned and laid one by one into tins of olive oil — historically the work of the anxoveres, passed mother to daughter. Buy them at Anxoves Solés.

#4
DISH

Suquet de peix

The fishermen's stew, born on the boats from unsold fish.

Monkfish, escórpora (scorpionfish) and hake with potato, allioli and a mortar picada — garlic, parsley, nuts, fried bread, paprika, often a saffron-tomato sofregit. Its coastal cousin, suquet d'anxova, is strongest at Tossa de Mar and L'Escala. Definitive at Cal Tet.

#5
DISH

Arròs de Pals

Rice grown on the Baix Ter plain since 1452.

Bahia and Bomba traditionally, with newer Carnaroli and Nembo, grown around Pals inside the Montgrí–Medes–Baix Ter park. The long, mild Empordà autumn gives grains that hold their shape when cooked; they carry the local seafood arrossos. On the menus at Els Tinars and the coastal marisqueries.

#6
DISH

Recuit de drap

Fresh Empordà curd drained in its cloth, eaten with honey.

Goat and/or sheep curd named for the drap (cloth) it drains and wraps in, its creaminess coming from a very long curdling of seventeen to twenty-four hours. Eaten with honey or sugar as dessert. Named producers include Mas Marcè/"Nuri" at Ullastret, Casa Martell in the Baix Empordà, and Làctics Pauet at Pau.

#7
DISH

Botifarra

Catalan fresh pork sausage, from blanca to the sweet dolça.

Botifarra blanca and negra (raw or cooked), the cooked botifarra de perol of the Girona–Maresme, and the festive botifarra dolça — a dessert sausage sweetened with sugar and lemon, an Empordà curiosity. Served amb seques, with white beans. Find the embotits at the Mercat del Lleó.

Drink
Wines, spirits, coffee
7 entries
Of 61 hectares of Lledoner Roig in all Catalonia, about 60 are in the Empordà — the "grey Grenache" is almost entirely a Costa Brava affair.
The low staked vine is an answer to the wind — the tramuntana, a katabatic north wind, spills down from the Pyrenees at up to 200 km/h and dries the canopy, naturally suppressing mildew and powdery mould.
#1
DRINK

Garnatxa (Lledoner) — the three colours

White, grey and black Grenache, the spine of DO Empordà.

Garnatxa, known locally as Lledoner, in its blanca, gris and negra forms, grown on low staked bush vines the tramuntana keeps dry and free of mildew. Old vines (vinyes velles) of fifty to a hundred and twenty years give low yields and concentration. Espelt at Vilajuïga and Vinyes dels Aspres at Cantallops are reference growers.

#2
DRINK

Carinyena (Samsó)

Carignan, reintroduced from France by the Roig family in the 1890s.

Called Samsó locally, Carinyena is the other native red, often co-planted with Garnatxa in old field blends. The Roig family brought grafts back from France in the 1890s after phylloxera. It gives structure, acid and dark fruit to the Empordà reds.

#3
DRINK

Garnatxa de l'Empordà (sweet)

The signature sweet wine — sun-dried, oxidative, fortified Grenache.

A vi dolç natural made by sun-drying and oxidising Garnatxa, the lineage running from the Greeks at Empúries through the monks of Sant Pere de Rodes to today. Distinct from the dry reds, it is the dessert wine of the coast — try Mas Estela's Moscatell Estela and Estela Dolç Solera alongside it.

#4
DRINK

Lledoner Roig (grey Grenache)

A Costa Brava rarity — almost all of Catalonia's plantings are here.

Of roughly sixty-one hectares of Lledoner Roig in all Catalonia, about sixty sit in the Empordà. Pink-grey-skinned, it gives textured, faintly oxidative whites with weight and salinity — one of the most distinctive things the DO grows.

#5
DRINK

Mas Estela — biodynamic

Sixteen hectares at Selva de Mar, biodynamic since 1999.

Organic from 1989 and biodynamic since 1999, hand-harvested estate fruit farmed to lunar and planetary cycles with no synthetic inputs — Grenache (noir and gris), Muscat, Syrah and Samsó. The cult names are Vinya Selva de Mar (red and white) and Quindals. The Cap de Creus terroir in a glass.

#6
DRINK

Martín Faixó — Cadaqués

A family winery at 300 m on the Cadaqués pass, inside the Cap de Creus park.

About eleven hectares at three hundred metres on the Cadaqués pass, "surrounded by the sea" inside the natural park — Picapoll, Muscat and Grenache under the Cadaqués brand. The dramatic coastal-mountain setting, schist underfoot and tramuntana overhead, is the signature.

#7
DRINK

La Vinyeta — natural

A young mixed farm at Mollet de Peralada reviving the old masia model.

A small, sustainability-minded estate combining vines, olives and cattle, making natural wines without added sulphites alongside its own olive oil and honey — the new-generation Empordà spirit. Drink it by the glass at La Tabarra in Girona.

16 Jan – 29 Mar 2026, Palafrugell + Calella, Llafranc, Tamariu

The sea-urchin campaign running since 1992 across Palafrugell and its three coves, a dozen-plus restaurants serving garoina menus at the winter peak. Tasting menus around €65. Verify the exact 2026 dates with each restaurant.

#2
EVENT

Calçotada season

November–April, peak January–March, across Catalonia

Charred calçots (long spring onions) peeled by hand and dipped in romesco, eaten outdoors with red wine and botifarra — the great Catalan winter feast, peaking in the first months of the year.

#3
EVENT

Cantada d'Havaneres, Calella de Palafrugell

First Saturday of July, Port Bo beach

The famous habanera singalong on Calella's cove — Cuban-rooted sailors' songs over flamed cremat rum, drawing thousands to the beach in early July. Verify the year's date.

#4
EVENT

Festival Castell de Peralada

July–August, Peralada castle gardens

Opera, recitals and ballet in the open-air gardens and church of the Perelada castle estate through high summer — the region's flagship classical festival.

#5
EVENT

Cap Roig Festival

July–August, Cap Roig botanical gardens

International pop, jazz and classical concerts in the cliff-top botanical gardens between Calella de Palafrugell and Palamós, with the sea below the stage.

#6
EVENT

Fira d'Indians, Begur

September, Begur old town

Begur's colonial-revival fair celebrating its nineteenth-century Indiano mansions, built by returnees from Cuba — Caribbean music, rum and costume through the hilltop streets. Verify the year's dates.

#1
WALK

Camí de ronda: Calella → Llafranc

Dawn at Port Bo · 5.5 km · ~1.5–2 hrs · 164 m climb

The signature coastal walk: from the Les Voltes arches at Calella, the cliff-cut camí de ronda through pine, El Canadell, rock tunnels and the door of Casamar to Llafranc's bay and the Sant Sebastià lighthouse.

#2
WALK

Cadaqués lanes at dawn

First light · the cobbled maze · ~45 min

The whitewashed labyrinth of Cadaqués before the day-trippers — church bell, fishing boats in Portlligat bay, the alternative dawn walk to the Palafrugell coves.

#3
WALK

Cap de Creus to the lighthouse

Paratge de Tudela · sculpted schist · 1–2 hrs

From the Palau de Tudela across the surreal wind- and sea-carved rock formations of the Paratge de Tudela to the lighthouse at the easternmost point of Iberia — the Dalí landscape in three dimensions.

#4
WALK

Empúries shore path

Sant Martí d'Empúries → L'Escala · seafront · ~30 min

Along the ancient harbour from the medieval hamlet of Sant Martí d'Empúries past the Greek and Roman ruins to L'Escala, the Mediterranean on one side and two thousand five hundred years of trade on the other.

#5
WALK

Pals to the rice paddies

Gothic hilltop core · the Baix Ter plain · flat

From the stone lanes of medieval Pals out across the rice fields of the Baix Ter — the paddies worked since 1452, flat and green under the Montgrí massif.

Markets

Where the local week is bought. Note when an in-market table is worth a stop in its own right; otherwise the producer comes home with you.

#1
MARKET

Mercat del Lleó, Girona

Mon–Sat morning, before 1:30pm

Girona's historic 1944 covered market, around sixty stalls where the city's chefs shop. Seasonal bolets (wild mushrooms), local embotits, Empordà cheeses, and fish landed at Roses and Palamós. Arrive before it closes at half past one; closed Sunday.

#2
MARKET

Llotja de Palamós (fish auction)

Mon–Fri afternoons, ~4–8pm

The working fish auction on the Moll Pesquer, where the gamba de Palamós is graded and sold at a descending Dutch auction. Watch it through the window from the Espai del Peix, which also runs guided visits and show-cooking on the quay.

Producers

Cellars, affineurs, salt-harvesters, distillers — most need a phone call and a half-day. The ones below are worth the drive.

#1
PRODUCER

Espelt Viticultors

Vilajuïga (Alt Empordà) — visits by appointment

The largest of the named DO Empordà growers, around two hundred hectares, organic for over twenty years and focused on native varieties — now led by Anna Espelt, with a modern winery beside the family farmhouse and a second estate, Mas Marès, above Cala Montjoi.

AddressMas Espelt, Vilajuïga
#2
PRODUCER

Celler Mas Estela

Selva de Mar (Cap de Creus) — biodynamic since 1999

Sixteen hectares of estate fruit farmed biodynamically since 1999 to lunar and planetary cycles — Grenache, Muscat, Syrah and Samsó, hand-harvested. The cult names are Vinya Selva de Mar and Quindals, plus the sweet Moscatell Estela.

AddressSelva de Mar
#3
PRODUCER

Celler Martín Faixó

Cadaqués — 300 m inside the Cap de Creus park

A family winery of about eleven hectares on the Cadaqués pass at three hundred metres, surrounded by the sea inside the natural park — Picapoll, Muscat and Grenache under the Cadaqués brand.

AddressCadaqués
#4
PRODUCER

Anxoves Solés

L'Escala — factory-museum, five generations since 1888

The heritage anchovy house of L'Escala, founded in 1888 by Francisco Solés Oliveras and run by five generations, salt-curing anchovies the traditional way and running a factory-museum in town.

AddressL'Escala
#5
PRODUCER

Empordàlia (Oli de Pau)

Pau — the DOP olive-oil and wine cooperative

Formed in 1995 from the old Pau, Roses and Vilajuïga oil cooperatives, Empordàlia presses DOP Oli de l'Empordà from the native Argudell olive under the Oli de Pau and ADN Argudell brands — clean, fruity, balanced bitter-pungent oil — alongside its wines.

AddressPau (Alt Empordà)

PRODUCER

Anxoves Solés shop, L'Escala

The definitive Empordà larder souvenir, on the Plaça de la Sardana.

Salt-cured anchovy fillets in olive oil, whole salted anchovies and seitons (boquerones), bought at source from a five-generation house. The other historic producer, Anxoves de l'Escala, is nearby.

Jordi Roca's gelateria — the Roca universe you can walk into.

The panet (gelato in brioche), the soft-serve with toppings from the Willy-Wonka cart, and seasonal Roca flavours on the Carrer de Santa Clara — the most accessible taste of the three-star brothers, no booking required.

Avoid
Named traps — unambiguous
4 traps
#1
AVOID

Lloret de Mar beachfront strip

Lloret de Mar — the front-line promenade — package-resort food and €28 photo-paella.

Souvenir shops, generic clubs and tourist-menú paella joints. Step back into the old town or out to Fenals and Cala Boadella; better still, eat at the working ports of Palamós, L'Escala and Roses instead.

#2
AVOID

Front-line "tourist menú" paella

Any resort promenade — the €25 paella-with-sangría photo board.

Reheated, saffron-coloured rice aimed at footfall, anywhere a board shows a photo of the dish. Real arròs is one street back, or at the rice and seafood houses inland.

#3
AVOID

High-August front-line dining

The busy coves in peak August — crowds, queues, marked-up menus.

This coast keeps its best season in winter and shoulder months. In high August the coves are full and prices climb; come for the garoinada, the spring, or the autumn instead.

#4
AVOID

Unverified phone bookings

Phone numbers in this guide are intentionally blank.

No phone numbers are listed because none were independently verified. Book through each venue's website or Google Maps listing, and confirm the 2026 garoinada and festival dates directly.
