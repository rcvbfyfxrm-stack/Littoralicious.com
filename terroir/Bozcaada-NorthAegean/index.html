Bozcaada is a wind-raked wine island in the mouth of the Dardanelles — between a third and a half of it under vine, worked without irrigation on a heritage three thousand years old. In May 2026 National Geographic named it the only Turkish entry on its list of the best places in the world for food right now, praising the millennia-old winemaking and an Aegean table drawn straight from the strait. The plate carries both halves of the island’s record, Greek and Turkish — and the wine is its own pre-phylloxera grapes, not the international clones that replaced them everywhere else.

THE SOUL OF THE PLACE
What the place is, underneath

Bozcaada is a forty-square-kilometre island of wind and vine in the mouth of the Dardanelles, where the meltem blows so steadily that vegetation grows low and hard against it. The constant breeze is the whole argument: it dries the morning off the grapes before disease can take hold, so the vines are trained gobelet-style, knuckled close to the dusty ground, unsprayed. Between a third and a half of the island lies under vine — you are never quite reading a landscape here so much as reading a single three-thousand-year-old crop, raked by salt air and ringed by a sea the colour of slate before rain.

The quintessenceYou are standing on a pre-phylloxera wine island. The root louse that levelled almost every vineyard on Earth in the nineteenth century did not reach Bozcaada until the 1940s, and the vines here are still the island’s own grapes, not the famous international ones: Çavuş, the delicate table grape; Vasilaki, the high-acid white grown nowhere else; the dark, tannic Karalahna with the lighter Kuntra for red. This is not the polished Tuscan-clone Aegean of the brochures. It is the older, stranger one those clones replaced.

HISTORY & TERROIR
Where it comes from

In antiquity the island was Tenedos — named in Virgil’s Aeneid as the bay where the Achaean fleet hid to sell Troy the wooden horse; its coins were stamped with grapes and a double-headed axe.

After Byzantine, Genoese and Venetian hands, the island and its castle were taken and rebuilt in 1455 by Sultan Mehmet II. Under the 1923 Treaty of Lausanne, Tenedos and Imbros were the only Aegean islands left to Turkey and explicitly exempted from the Greek–Turkish population exchange. Yet the Greek Orthodox community that had made the island’s wine for centuries fell from roughly 5,420 in 1912 to about 22 by 2000 — and the first Turkish-Muslim winery, Çamlıbağ, was founded in 1925 in a former Greek shop. The plate you eat today carries both halves of that record.

WHY NOW
The right moment

Come in the first week of September for the bağ bozumu, the grape-harvest festival the municipality has run since the mid-1990s, when tractors and donkeys carry visitors out to a different vineyard each day to pick and crush — the one moment the Çavuş grape, too delicate to travel, is actually in your hand. Failing that, come late May–June: warm, uncrowded, the cellar doors open. Avoid August, when the beds fill and Ayazma beach crowds. And know this is not a year-round island — almost three-quarters of the registered population leaves in winter, and the kitchens go dark with them.

WHAT TO EAT
The canon

Bozcaada eats off two heritages at once — the grilled squid and raw fish of a fishing island, and the fried dumplings, dried-vegetable dolma and syrup-soaked Rum sweets of the Greek community that shaped it.

The island treatment of squid is grilled, not battered — laid over coals the way they cook it at Asmalı Meyhane (founded 1950) under the vine canopy that names the meyhane, where raw fish with torn basil is the quieter signature. The dumpling to know is ada mantısı, fried before it is sauced, hand-pleated at the counter of Dört Hanımeli. From the Wednesday producers’ market in the castle square comes the island’s calling-card preserve, domates reçeli — tomato jam against a wedge of Çanakkale goat cheese, the salt cutting the sugar. And the desserts date the table: mafiş, the bow-shaped Rum tatlısı fried light and soaked in syrup, older than the Republic-era wineries down the road.

WHAT TO DRINK
The island’s own grapes

Wine is the island’s whole argument, made from four indigenous grapes — Çavuş and Vasilaki for white, Karalahna and Kuntra for red — that grow essentially nowhere else.

Corvus put Bozcaada back on the world wine map in 2002; its Çavuş white argues a snacking grape can carry a serious dry wine, and Jancis Robinson scored its Corpus red the highest Turkish mark in her tasting. Talay, family-run since 1948, and Çamlıbağ, the island’s first Muslim winery from 1925, work the native vines in a plainer, working-cellar key; Amadeus, run by the island-born Austrian Oliver Gareis, pours its Grillo white himself at the cellar door. The fixed partner to the meze, though, is rakı — not an island product, but the anise spirit poured under the vine while the raw fish and squid come out in waves. The wine is the souvenir; the rakı is the meal.

WHERE THE MONEY SITS
What funds the table

Wine is the engine, but tourism is the till — and the gap between them is the whole story of the island’s plate.

Almost half the island is planted to vine, worked without irrigation, near-organic, on a three-thousand-year heritage — but commercial wine fights a labour shortage, high alcohol tax and distant drinking cities, so many islanders make wine in small batches for themselves rather than for sale. The real cash is no longer in the bottle; it is in the day-tripper — over 1.5 million visitors a year, summer occupancy near ninety per cent. A chef’s money is well spent at source: at the cellar door, on GI-registered Çavuş table grapes, on the island’s young-fig and almond produce. It is wasted on the harbour-front group-tour tables that exist for volume, not for the kitchen. The seam of value is the home-batch wine and the GI grape — the things too small or too taxed to scale, which is exactly why the tour buses never touch them.

BEFORE YOU GO
Getting there, and the one honest warning

There is no airport. The short ferry runs from Geyikli (Yükyeri İskelesi) to Bozcaada in about thirty minutes, hourly through the day with a late 23:00 sailing in season; from Çanakkale it is roughly 60 km by road, turning off before Ezine for Geyikli. A summer-only seabus runs from Çanakkale city but takes no vehicles.

Come for the wine and the grilled squid, in season — not in winter, when the island empties and the kitchens go dark.
