Where the Chefs Eat

Cadaqués, Cap de Creus and the Empordà coast — the elBulli and Roca heartland. Not the pilgrimage tables, but the everyday ones the people who cook here actually sit at.

This coast holds two of the most famous kitchens on earth — El Celler de Can Roca in Girona and Disfrutar in Barcelona, both former World's 50 Best number ones. You go to those to be astonished. You go here to eat the way the chefs who work them eat on a day off: fresh fish, a hot plancha, and sourcing so good it makes technique beside the point.

How this list is built (and how to build it for any port)
  1. Weight a named chef's personal pick, an Opinionated About Dining placing, or a serious critic over any crowd-review score.
  2. Trust "where I eat on my day off" interviews, local food press and Bib Gourmand; distrust the Ramblas-facing tourist board.
  3. Prefer the room that lives or dies on its sourcing over the one that lives on its view.
  4. Name the source for every pick, and re-check it each season — the best small places close, move, or open only when the catch is good.

The Tables

Compartir Cadaqués mid–upper

Opened in 2012 by Oriol Castro, Eduard Xatruch and Mateu Casañas — three head chefs of elBulli — as the casual, shared-plates counterpart to their Barcelona fine-diner, Disfrutar. Modern cooking laid lightly over the local catch; the clearest place to taste where this coast's ideas went after elBulli closed.

Source: compartircadaques.com; El Español (2021).

Rafa's Roses mid

A spartan storefront with a handful of tables that Ferran Adrià himself is repeatedly named as eating at — the chef's-chef seafood shack. It opens only when the fish is good enough; the kitchen barely cooks, letting Alt Empordà prawns, razor clams and sea urchin arrive at the right temperature and no more. Listed on OAD's Casual Europe ranking.

Source: Jancis Robinson; En Primeur Club / OAD.

Casa Anita Cadaqués mid

Grilling fish over the same plancha since the 1960s, at shared tables under old photographs — a Dalí haunt in his day. No printed menu: you eat what Juanito has, usually sole, turbot or John Dory, with sardines and prawns straight off the grill. The template for everything the coast means by "authentic."

Source: Time Out Girona; cadaques.co.uk.

Restaurant La Gamba Palamós splurge

By the Palamós port since 1967, run by the Quadrat family, this is the benchmark for the region's signature product — the gamba de Palamós, the deep-water red prawn, served barely touched and its portions individually numbered. A destination for the prawn alone; worth the price once, to know what the fuss is.

Source: Red Costa Brava; lagambapalamos.com.

The pilgrimage tables, for contrast: El Celler de Can Roca (Girona, three Michelin stars, twice World's 50 Best No. 1) and Disfrutar (Barcelona, the Compartir chefs' fine-diner, World's 50 Best No. 1 in 2024). Book those months out; the list above you can mostly walk into.
