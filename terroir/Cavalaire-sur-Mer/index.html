The name

Caccabaria — the antique port-name that scholars say probably lies behind Cavalaire. The descent is given in the conditional (the modern name serait derived from Caccabaria), so read it as a strong probability, not a proven line.

From Greek kakkabe (Kakkabaria), partridge — the reading the standard toponymic reference (encyclopedie.arbre-celtique.com) endorses: paired with Herakleia, for Herakles/Hercules, the name reads roughly as 'Heraclea of the partridge,' the game-birds of the Maures scrub rather than the shape of the sea.

From the same word in its other sense — Greek kakkabos / Latin caccabus, a three-legged cooking-pot or cauldron — a near-homonym of the partridge word, which is why the two compete. It flatters the round basin of the bay held between Cap Cavalaire and Cap Lardier and is the version travellers like to repeat, but scholarly sources treat the pot as a folk reading and lean to 'partridge.'

First attested Heraclia Caccabaria (Greek Herakleia Kakkabaria) — the earliest recorded form, a coastal staging-port listed in the Antonine Itinerary (Itinerarium Antonini, at 505,4 and 505,6), c. 3rd century CE, placed beyond Forum Iulii (Frejus) and the Sinus Sambracitanus (Golfe de Saint-Tropez) and before Alconis (bay of Bormes); sources disagree on whether the port was originally Greek/Massaliote or, as some say, Phoenician.

A cauldron of a bay, not a stretch of coast. Cavalaire sits at the bottom of its own basin on the southern shoulder of the Saint-Tropez peninsula, in the Var, roughly midway between Le Lavandou and Saint-Tropez. The water is held between two capes, Cap Cavalaire to the west and Cap Lardier to the east, while behind the town the Massif des Maures climbs past 500 metres (528m at the coastal crest) and the Pradels ridge blunts the Mistral, so the bay stays warm and calm. It faces the sea and turns its back on the mountain; a folk reading of its name even likes to call it a cooking-pot for that shape, though the linguists trace the Greek to a partridge. Be honest about the flip side: no Byblos, no legend of Bardot, that theatre plays out twenty minutes away over the peninsula. Cavalaire is the un-chic, family-and-caravan sibling, and lives frankly off its sand.

What made it was how late it arrived. For nearly nine centuries this was a shore people fled: Saracens, then Barbary corsairs off the sea kept the coast unsafe, so the population held to fortified high ground while only a fishing anchorage clung on. The modern town is astonishingly young. The metre-gauge coastal railway, the Train des Pignes or Macaron that ran the Maures shore from 1889 to 1948, had already been bringing bathers for a generation when Cavalaire finally split from neighbouring Gassin to become its own commune in 1929. War interrupted the idyll in August 1944; then tourism finished the job the railway began, and Cavalaire was classified a beach resort in 1956. The fishing village became, fully and frankly, a summer machine of about 8,100 year-round residents that multiplies many times over from June to September.

The real theatre is the working morning. There was never a jet-set golden age to inherit, so the defining scene is small and daily. The fishing boats come back into the port late each morning and sell the day's catch straight off the quay, roughly between ten and noon: sea bream, the odd loup, spider crab, and the small rockfish that become soup. On Wednesday mornings, year-round, the Provencal market fills Place Jean Moulin with goat cheese, olives, tapenade and split tomatoes, the Var in one square. And where the last beach bar ends, the wild protected coast of Cap Lardier and the Gigaro coves begins: borrowed beauty and real, the antidote the beach clubs cannot sell.

The bay feeds the table, the hills fill the jar. The catch becomes soupe de poissons de roche with rouille; the pale Cotes de Provence rose goes copper on the port at dusk; and up in the Maures, Collobrieres' chestnuts turn to creme de marrons and marrons glaces. Come in the shoulders, late May to June or September into early October, when the sea is warm, the light is long and the town is still a town. July and August are the honest trade-off: gorgeous lifeguarded sand, but the population swells and the single coast road clogs at beach o'clock. Anchor a visit on the Wednesday market (Place Jean Moulin, roughly 8:30 to 13:00) and the fish quay from around ten; the one Michelin table, La Palmeraie at Chateau de Valmer, sits above Gigaro beach in neighbouring La Croix-Valmer and opens only for the summer season, so book well ahead.

Getting there. There is essentially one way in and out. The best way: Leave the car and take the boat. Les Bateaux Verts run a summer navette straight across the gulf from Cavalaire's Quai Marc Pajot to Saint-Tropez, skipping the peninsula road entirely (the Cavalaire line is seasonal; confirm 2026 dates and times on bateauxverts.com).

Cavalaire started life as Heraclea Caccabaria, a Greek (Massaliote) then Roman port logged as a coastal way-station between Fréjus and Giens on the Antonine Itinerary, Rome's road-and-sea directory.

For roughly 139 years Cavalaire was a wine-rich hamlet of hilltop Gassin; it won 'administrative section' status in 1920 and full commune status on 5 August 1929 - freed a full five years before neighbouring La Croix-Valmer separated in 1934.

THE SOUL OF THE PLACE
What the place is, underneath

Cavalaire-sur-Mer sits at the bottom of its own bay on the southern shoulder of the Saint-Tropez peninsula, in the Var, halfway between Le Lavandou and Saint-Tropez. The water is held in the crook of two capes — Cap Cavalaire to the west, Cap Lardier to the east — and behind the town the first wooded ridges of the Massif des Maures climb to over 500 metres, the Pradels crest breaking the Mistral so the bay stays warm and calm. It is a place that faces the sea and turns its back on the mountain: a long arc of fine sand, a marina of eleven-hundred-odd masts, and a grid of low apartment blocks and campsites that empties in winter and quadruples in July.

It carries a serious history lightly. On 15-16 August 1944 this bay was Alpha sector of Operation Dragoon, the "other" Allied landing in the south of France; American divisions and, the following evening, General de Lattre's Army B came ashore across these beaches. A Landing Monument on the seafront marks it, and every August the town parades vintage military vehicles — but by ten in the morning the same sand is umbrellas and pédalos again.

Be honest about what Cavalaire is not. It has none of Saint-Tropez's yacht-and-paparazzi glamour, no Byblos, no legend of Bardot — that theatre plays out twenty minutes north, over the capes. Cavalaire is the un-chic, affordable, family-and-caravan sibling: a resort town of roughly eight thousand year-round residents that swells enormously in summer, with the honesty of a place that lives frankly off its beaches. Its beauty is borrowed and real all at once — the wild, protected coast of Cap Lardier and the Gigaro coves begins where the last beach bar ends.

The quintessenceThe truest Cavalaire ritual is small and daily: the fishing boats coming back into Port Héraclea around ten in the morning and selling the night's catch straight off the quay, rock fish and the odd loup still smelling of the sea. On Wednesday mornings, year-round, the Provençal market fills Place Jean Moulin from about 8:30 to 1 — goat cheese, olives, tapenade, sun-split tomatoes, oilcloth and espadrilles — the Var in one square. And then the evening version of the same town: a cold, pale Côtes de Provence rosé on the port as the light goes copper over the Maures and the returning windsurfers become silhouettes.

Cavalaire's story starts as a name on a Roman sailing chart. The Greeks established trading posts along this coast — Olbia and Heraclea Caccabaria — around the 4th century BCE, and under Rome the site became Caccabaria, a maritime staging-post listed in the Antonine Itinerary. At Pardigon, on the eastern edge of the bay, archaeologists have found Gallo-Roman villa ruins where, tradition holds, land was parcelled out to deserving legionaries; the coastal estates here made and shipped wine. Roman order held until roughly 400 CE, and then the coast became one of the most dangerous in the Mediterranean.

What followed was almost a thousand years of leaving. Visigoths at the close of the 5th century, Ostrogoths in the 6th and 7th, and repeatedly from the 8th century the Saracens, then corsairs and Turks from the sea, made the shore uninhabitable; the population retreated to fortified high ground for the better part of nine hundred years, and only a "Cavalaira" market stop clung on as a place ships put in. Medieval Cavalaire belonged first to the monks of the collegiate church of Pignans and, from the 15th century, to the powerful House of Castellane. For centuries the bay meant fishing above all — the wider Provençal coast worked the madrague, the ancient labyrinth of fixed tuna nets that peaked from the 17th century and faded through the 19th, with France's last madrague set off Marseille in 1914.

The modern town is astonishingly young. Cavalaire was only detached from neighbouring Gassin and made its own commune in 1929; the little Saint-Raphaël-Toulon railway (the "train des Pignes") had reached the shore and the first villas and bathers were arriving. War interrupted the idyll — the August 1944 landings — and then tourism finished the transformation the trains had begun: Cavalaire was classified a beach resort in 1956, and by the 21st century the fishing village had become, frankly and fully, a summer machine, its population of about 8,100 (2023) multiplying many times over between June and September.

Cavalaire runs on two engines, and both point at the summer. The first is the beach economy in the narrow sense: the long sand, the water-sports base, the beach restaurants that mark up a plate of grilled fish and a carafe of rosé to seafront prices for eight weeks a year. The second is the marina — Port Héraclea, run by a public company (SPL Port Héraclea), with roughly 1,100 berths and a 3-star "Station Nautique" label since 2008 — which sells moorings, chandlery, diving and day-charter to the same seasonal tide. The town has chased official tourism status hard (classified beach resort 1956, "Station de Tourisme" in 2013, "Famille Plus" for families) because the label is the product.

A Cavalaire gem · Market
Wednesday market on Place Jean Moulin

Once a week the town's square fills with around a hundred stalls and the whole Provençal register: tapenade and anchoïade, olives, honey and olive oils, cheeses and charcuterie, mounds of seasonal fruit — and the rosé of the Gulf of Saint-Tropez, of course. It is the unhurried, morning-only kind of market where you assemble a beach lunch by tasting your way down the aisle. Go early; by one o'clock it folds away.

Where — Place Jean Moulin, Cavalaire; every Wednesday morning, roughly 8:30–13:00, year-round

The deeper economics are written in the housing. Property portals (SeLoger, Century 21) report that only around 30 percent of Cavalaire's dwellings are primary residences against roughly two-thirds second homes, with house prices reported near €5,550 per square metre — a town largely owned by people who sleep in it a few weeks a year. That is the fault line a visitor can taste: the beach-club terrace versus where a local actually eats. The value is at the Wednesday market on Place Jean Moulin, at the fishermen's direct sale on the quay around ten each morning, and in the bottle rather than the setting — a €10-15 pale Côtes de Provence rosé from the Var vineyards that surround the Maures, or a structured, Mourvèdre-based Bandol (AOC since 1941) from just along the coast, costs a fraction of what the same glass fetches with a sea view.

Rosé de Provence (Côtes de Provence & Bandol)

A Cavalaire gem · Ritual
The Saint-Pierre fishermen's fête and the burning boat

At the end of June the fishing community keeps the feast of their patron, Saint-Pierre: a procession through the streets, then a second procession out to sea from the Esplanade Sainte-Estelle, gathering fishermen, the old wooden pointus, divers and the SNSM lifeboats. A great sardinade is grilled on the beach at dusk, there is dancing, and the night ends — the oldest, strangest, most beautiful part — with an old boat set alight on the sand and left to burn against the dark water, a gesture that once meant solidarity with the poorest fisherman.

Where — Cavalaire beach and port; late June (the Saint-Jean/Saint-Pierre weekend), run by the town's traditions association — exact dates vary yearly

The true drink of this coast and the honest local product. Côtes de Provence is the region's giant AOC, and rosé is roughly 80 percent of what Provence makes — the pale, near-salmon style from short skin contact and cold fermentation. Just west, tiny Bandol (about 1,600 ha) makes a darker, structured, age-worthy rosé and red built on Mourvèdre. Drink it cold, young, and cheaper than the sea view suggests.

Origin Var / Provence; Côtes de Provence and Bandol (Bandol AOC created 1941), vines here since Roman times

DISH

Bouillabaisse

The famous one, and worth being honest about: it is a Marseille dish, not a Cavalaire native, descended from the Phocaean fishermen's cauldron ('kakavia'). Several bony Mediterranean fish added one at a time to a saffron-and-fennel broth, the soup served first with rouille, the fish on a platter after. A real one is expensive and slow; a cheap 'bouillabaisse' on a tourist terrace is usually just fish soup — the 1980 Marseille Charter exists precisely because of that drift.

Origin Marseille (Greek Phocaean roots, ~600 BCE); Charter de la Bouillabaisse, 1980

DISH

Aïoli (le grand aïoli)

Less a sauce than a Friday institution: a stiff emulsion of garlic pounded with olive oil, served as the centrepiece of a spread of poached cod, boiled potatoes, carrots, green beans, snails and egg. Ancient Mediterranean in root, mentioned in medieval Provençal texts. Order it on a day you have nowhere to be.

Origin Provence / wider Mediterranean; garlic-and-oil emulsion of great antiquity

DISH

Tapenade

The apéritif of the coast: black (or green) olives, capers, anchovy, garlic and olive oil pounded to a dark paste, spread on bread with that first glass of rosé. The name comes from Provençal 'tapena', caper. Its invention is traditionally credited to a Marseille chef around 1880 — plausible, and worth hedging.

Origin Marseille, credited to around 1880 (attribution traditional, not certain)

DISH

Panisses

Chickpea-flour cooked to a paste, set, sliced and fried into golden batons — crisp outside, soft within, eaten with salt like chips. A working-class street food that came to Marseille's Estaque district with Ligurian and Piedmontese immigrants in the 19th century. Cousin of Nice's socca, but cut thick and fried.

Origin Marseille (Estaque), 19th century; Ligurian/Piedmontese roots

DISH

Daube provençale

The winter dish of the Var interior: beef cut in big chunks, marinated overnight in Provençal red wine, then simmered for hours with pork belly, carrots, tomato, black olives, orange peel and a bouquet of thyme and bay. Born as a preservation and thrift dish, now the region's Sunday and celebration stew — the leftovers traditionally sauce a plate of ravioli.

Origin Provence; a medieval/early-modern braise, elevated over time

DISH

Pieds et paquets

For the curious: lamb tripe rolled into little parcels around pork belly, garlic and parsley (the 'paquets'), stewed slowly with lamb trotters (the 'pieds') in white wine and tomato. A 19th-century Marseille workers' dish, still an emblem of Provençal winter tables — offal-forward and not for everyone, which is exactly why it's genuine.

Origin Marseille, 19th century (Sisteron also claims a version)

DISH

Tarte tropézienne

The one truly local sweet, from over the capes: a sugar-crusted brioche split and filled with a blend of pastry cream and buttercream. Created in Saint-Tropez in 1955 by Polish-born baker Alexandre Micka; Brigitte Bardot, filming 'And God Created Woman', is said to have suggested the name, which Micka trademarked in 1973. The closest thing to a hometown dessert on this gulf.

Origin Saint-Tropez, 1955, Alexandre Micka; named c.1956, trademarked 1973

A Cavalaire gem · Cove
The douaniers' coves below Cap Lardier

The sentier des douaniers slips out of the confidential eastern corner of Gigaro beach and threads the wildest stretch of this coast toward Cap Lardier, past a string of coves most people never learn the names of: the tiny, intimate crique de la Douane just after the start, the sandier Jovat with the ruins of a fishing cabin, and Brouis — the most feral of them, where you are more or less guaranteed to be alone. Because it all sits inside the Port-Cros national park's protected land (acquired by the Conservatoire in 1978) and a Natura 2000 zone, it stays startlingly calm even at the height of August.

Where — Sentier du littoral from Gigaro (La Croix-Valmer) toward Cap Lardier; roughly 4.4 km one way, marked in yellow, sometimes steep — sturdy shoes

It smells of saffron and rockfish before it ever smells of luxury.

Start with the catch. The everyday dish here is not bouillabaisse (a Marseille invention, and a costly one) but its thrifty ancestor, soupe de poissons de roche: rock fish too small to sell, simmered with tomato, fennel, olive oil and saffron, then sieved to a rust-coloured broth and served with croutons, grated cheese and rouille, the garlic-and-saffron mayonnaise you float on the bread. The fish behind it is landed a few metres away: Cavalaire keeps a small working fleet, its fishermen governed by a centuries-old prud'homie, and the boats sell straight off the quay each morning, rascasse, girelle and chapon among them, the port acting as its own fishmonger.

Then the ritual dish, le grand aioli, less a sauce than a Friday institution: a stiff emulsion of garlic pounded with olive oil, set as the centrepiece of poached salt cod, boiled potatoes, carrots, green beans, snails and egg. Order it on a day you have nowhere to be. On the Nouveau Port, Le Comptoir des Saveurs plates a proper aioli complet around 29.50 euros, the value counterweight to the beach-club terraces; and tapenade, black olives, capers and anchovy pounded dark, is the standing partner to that first cold glass.

The third pillar comes down from the mountain. The Massif des Maures carries a chestnut forest of some 2,000 hectares, with Collobrieres as its capital, and here the prized nut is the marron, a selected chestnut that fills its burr as a single, rounder, unpartitioned kernel. It becomes creme de marrons and marrons glaces in the village confiseries, sweetens a winter daube, and gets its own three-Sunday Fete de la Chataigne every October (the 11th, 18th and 25th in 2026). Sea below, chestnut above: the two halves of this terroir on one short drive.

This is rose country in the literal sense. Cotes de Provence, delimited as a VDQS in 1951 and raised to full AOC by decree on 24 October 1977, is the region's giant appellation, and rose is roughly nine parts in ten of what it makes: the near-salmon style built on short skin contact and cool fermentation, from Grenache, Cinsault, Syrah and Mourvedre, with Tibouren the local heritage grape. Drink it young and cold. Just west along the coast, tiny Bandol (AOC since 1941) makes the serious counterpoint, a darker, structured, age-worthy rose and red built on sun-loving Mourvedre.

A Cavalaire gem · Vineyard
Cru Classé rosé, drunk facing the Îles d'Or

La Croix-Valmer grows one of Provence's rare classified growths: Domaine de la Croix, founded in 1882 and ranked Cru Classé in the 1955 classification, its vines running down schistous hillsides toward the sea at the exceptional Bastide Blanche site by the water. Buy a bottle of the pale 'Irrésistible' rosé at the cellar, then carry it down to Gigaro beach for an evening apéro with the Îles d'Or on the horizon — Port-Cros floating out to sea. The wine was made within sight of where you drink it: a classified growth, poured on the shore it grows above.

Where — Cellar at 816 Boulevard de Tabarin, La Croix-Valmer (Mon–Sat 10:00–13:00 & 15:00–19:30; guided cellar visits by appointment, from March); apéro on Gigaro beach

The vineyards run almost to the water here, and the classification proves it: the 1955 Cotes de Provence roll of Crus Classes, twenty-three estates in all, includes Domaine de la Croix at La Croix-Valmer, founded in 1882, its schist hillsides tipping toward the sea at the Bastide Blanche site. Buy the pale bottle at the cellar, carry it down to Gigaro beach, and drink a classified growth in sight of the vines that made it, with Port-Cros floating on the horizon. story

Before the umbrellas went up, the war came ashore in this exact bay.

On 15 August 1944 Cavalaire's bay was Alpha, the westernmost of the three assault sectors of Operation Dragoon, the other and half-forgotten Allied landing in the south of France. At H-Hour, 0800, the 7th Regimental Combat Team of Major General John 'Iron Mike' O'Daniel's 3rd Infantry Division stormed Alpha Red in the bay, preceded by Duplex-Drive amphibious Sherman tanks of the 756th Tank Battalion swimming ashore ahead of the infantry. By midday the division had pushed its beachhead line inland.

The reality on the sand was less cinematic than the plan. German resistance on Alpha and Delta was light, the coastal Osttruppen surrendered quickly, and the real danger was mines rather than machine guns. The next evening, around seven on 16 August, General de Lattre's French Army B began coming ashore here at Cavalaire on its way to take Toulon and Marseille, both liberated inside a fortnight. Dragoon was overwhelmingly successful and, unlike Normandy some ten weeks earlier, is barely remembered.

Here is the honest turn: this is not a solemn coast. By mid-morning the same sand is pedalos and parasols again, and each August the landing is marked with commemorations and vintage military vehicles before the beach reopens for trade. The one thing to know on the ground is that the assault shore of 15 August is largely the Plage du Debarquement, just east in neighbouring La Croix-Valmer, an uninterrupted continuation of Cavalaire's Grande Plage marked by a memorial and a large car park, while Cavalaire's own seafront carries the granite Monument du Debarquement de Provence at the harbour entrance, on the spot where de Lattre's Army B came ashore the next day.

Curiosities
The odd, the true, the only-here — facts to drop at dinner
16 notes
  • NAMEThe town's name descends from Heraclea Caccabaria, the ancient Greek (Massaliote) trading post that became the site's Roman harbour.
  • HILLTOPLong before Rome, a Ligurian oppidum crowned Montjean, the 460-metre ridge still looming over the bay, built from around the 6th century BC.
  • BURIEDAt Pardigon, on the bay's edge, lie the remains of what is described as one of the largest maritime Roman villas known in the Mediterranean - today buried out of sight.
  • MADRAGUEThe wider Golfe de Saint-Tropez once ran madragues, fixed-net tuna traps of a roughly two-thousand-year lineage set from spring for the bluefin run; old fishing leases place four of them around the gulf - Cap de la Moutte, Pampelonne, Les Canebiers and La Nartelle.
  • POINTUSThe pointu - the double-ended Provençal fishing boat whose lines trace back to antiquity - still works this coast; its Marseille cousin, the barquette marseillaise, is inscribed on France's national inventory of intangible cultural heritage.
  • CATCHThe sheltered bay is still fished on a small scale for Mediterranean staples like rouget (red mullet) and langouste (spiny lobster).
  • WILD COASTJust east, the Cap Lardier-Taillat-Camarat headlands have been shielded by the Conservatoire du littoral since 1987 - among the last un-built coastline between Cavalaire and Saint-Tropez, today managed by the Port-Cros National Park.
  • WWIIEvery 15 August the town marks the Provence landing with a parade of period military vehicles, costumed troops and historical reenactments.
  • WINEGassin released Cavalaire in 1929 and La Croix-Valmer in 1934 - both prized less for their beaches than for the wine revenue they had long poured into the hilltop commune's coffers.
  • SAINT-TROPEZCavalaire never grew past a modest anchorage; the gulf's great harbour became Saint-Tropez, across the peninsula, rebuilt and repopulated by a Genoese-led colony from 1470.
  • RAILThe town once sat on the coastal railway between Hyères and Saint-Raphaël, which reached Cavalaire in 1890; the line is long gone, but the old station survives as an exhibition hall.
  • DIVINGMore than a dozen wrecks rest off the bay, from the mine-sunk cargo Togo to the submarine Rubis scuttled nearby in 1958, ranking Cavalaire among the coast's great wreck-diving grounds.
  • CHAPELThe seafarers' chapel of Notre-Dame de l'Annonciade dates to 1639, was shut during the Revolution in 1793, and its ruins were finally cleared during the WWII occupation.
  • TWIN TOWNIts unlikely twin town sits across the Atlantic: New Port Richey, Florida.
  • Alpha Beach, 1944In Operation Dragoon the bay carried the code-name Alpha, the westernmost landing sector; the US 3rd Infantry Division came ashore on 15 August 1944 and General de Lattre's French Army B followed on the 16th - a seafront monument marks it, and the town parades every 15 August.

Avoid the traps
What to skip, and what to do instead
4 traps
Using Cavalaire as a car-base to "do" Saint-Tropez in August. The famous village is only 17 km away on the map, but in peak season you will spend the holiday idling on the peninsula's beach roads rather than in it.
Instead Take the Bateaux Verts navette from Quai Marc Pajot across the water, or move at dawn and dusk. Keep the car for the quiet western Corniche des Maures instead (Rayol-Canadel, Le Lavandou), where the road actually flows.
Chasing the Pampelonne club-beach fantasy from here. Those celebrity sand-lots sit on the far side of the peninsula, an hour of traffic and a three-figure sunbed away.
Instead Cavalaire has its own 2 km-plus sand bay, and the wild coves of Cap Lardier and Gigaro are a short hop east: protected, undeveloped, free. That is the local's beach day, not the paid mattress.
Arriving in deep winter expecting the summer resort, then reading the shuttered seafront as "nothing here." Come January half the terraces are closed and the port empties.
Instead Come off-season for the real terroir: the Wednesday Provencal market on Place Jean Moulin (year-round, ~8:30-13:00), the daily fisherman's criee at the port from around 10:00, the Domaine du Rayol garden open all year, and the mimosa blazing gold along the corniche in February.
Ordering "fresh fish" on a seafront terrace without asking where it came from. Plenty of resort tables plate frozen catch at high-season prices, a short walk from real day-boats.
Instead Buy at the port criee in the morning, where a handful of Cavalaire fishermen sell the actual day's catch when the weather let them out: daurade, loup, rascasse for soupe. Then ask which kitchen cooks its own.
Cavalaire rewards subtraction: leave the car, skip the club-beach, buy off the boat, and the coast the crowds drove past is suddenly yours.

What to drink — the pours
What to actually order — the regional glass, and the growers to look for
7 pours
POUR

Côtes de Provence Rosé — the pale, bone-dry archetype

The colour is a decision, not a dilution: these rosés are direct-pressed or briefly macerated, so the pink stays onion-skin pale while the wine drinks bone-dry and saline — the vines sit close enough to the sea to taste it. It is not the sweet blush the tint suggests. One thing to carry: buy the youngest vintage on the shelf and drink it cold this summer, not next.

Look for Domaine de la Croix (La Croix-Valmer); Domaine de la Bastide Blanche (its organic seaside estate, La Croix-Valmer)

POUR

Cru Classé Rosé — Provence's own 1955 ranking

Long before today's appellation rules, a 1955 ministerial decree classified a handful of Provence estates; of the original list, eighteen still carry the words 'Cru Classé' on the label, and Domaine de la Croix — founded 1882, left derelict, then bought and wholly replanted after 2001 — is one of them. On the schist and clay above the bay it makes a rosé with more grip and density than the crowd. The pedigree is real, but it was awarded on seniority, not a tasting — a fact worth knowing before you pay for it.

Look for Domaine de la Croix (La Croix-Valmer)

POUR

Seaside sand-terroir Rosé with Tibouren

Tibouren is the peninsula's old grape — savoury, faintly orange-tinged, scented with garrigue rather than fruit-candy, and long tied to this stretch of the Saint-Tropez coast. On the sandy, wind-scoured strips between Cap Lardier and Cap Taillat, where Bastide Blanche's vines run nearly to the cliff, it gives a rosé that smells of hot pine and salt. Both houses are certified organic; taste this against a supermarket Provence and you learn what 'saline' actually means. (Bastide Blanche and La Croix are one estate under two labels — don't chase them as rivals.)

Look for Domaine de la Bastide Blanche (Domaine de la Croix's organic seaside estate, vines between Cap Taillat and Cap Lardier); Domaine La Tourraque (Ramatuelle, in the Trois Caps site, organic)

POUR

Côtes de Provence Blanc — Rolle by the water

Rolle is simply the local name for Vermentino, and by this coast it turns out a white of citrus pith and sea-spray minerality — the natural partner to the fishermen's morning catch on the quay. It is far rarer than the rosé and rarely leaves the region; if you see it, that is the bottle to carry back to the boat.

Look for Domaine de la Croix (La Croix-Valmer); Domaine La Tourraque (Ramatuelle)

POUR

Côtes de Provence Rouge — Mourvèdre for the cool evening

Provence is not only pink. Mourvèdre and Syrah on the warmer soils give a dark, herb-and-leather red built for a mistral-cool night off the water. Serve it lightly chilled — around 15°C, not room temperature — which is the single correction most people need to make with southern reds.

Look for Domaine de la Croix (Cru Classé, La Croix-Valmer); Domaine La Tourraque (Ramatuelle)

POUR

Pastis — the shore aperitif ritual

The ritual is fixed: a measure of pastis, then roughly five of cold water poured over ice until the clear spirit clouds — the 'louche' — drunk before lunch in the shade. Henri Bardouin blends more than 65 plants and spices where the industrial anise leans on a handful. Honest caveat: it is distilled inland at Forcalquier, in the Haute-Provence back-country, not on the coast — the seaside owns the ritual, the hills own the bottle.

Look for Henri Bardouin (Distilleries et Domaines de Provence, Forcalquier) — the artisan pour, versus Ricard / 51 from Marseille

POUR

Bière du Golfe — the local craft beer

The gulf's first microbrewery, with a certified-organic range brewed in the Var — a genuinely local craft pour among a sea of imported lager. Note it is made in Sainte-Maxime, across the Gulf of Saint-Tropez, not in Cavalaire itself; ask for it by name at a caviste rather than expecting it on every terrace tap.

Look for Bière du Golfe / Brasserie Artisanale du Golfe (Sainte-Maxime)

Monument du Débarquement, Plage de Cavalaire (Alpha Beach)

An open-air landing memorial on the seafront promenade, marking where the Allied invasion of southern France came ashore. Free, always open; walk it, don't queue for it.

Le Sentier du Littoral — Les Trois Caps (Gigaro → Cap Lardier → Cap Taillat)

The signature coastal walk of this coast: from Gigaro beach the yellow-marked sentier hugs the sea to Cap Lardier, drops along the Briande bay to the sand isthmus of Cap Taillat, and loops back inland through cork oak and maquis. The full Gigaro loop is roughly 14 km / about 4 hours; you can also just walk out-and-back to a beach.

This is the last long undeveloped stretch of coast between Cavalaire and Saint-Tropez, and the reason is public money, not luck. Cap Lardier was classified under the 1930 landscape law (a site classé) back in 1966, then bought parcel by parcel by the Conservatoire du littoral from 1978 onward — it holds the longest protected coastal fringe of the three caps, some 6 km. It is managed with the Port-Cros National Park and the commune and has been a Natura 2000 zone since 2006. The lesson the walk teaches: villas stop exactly where the Conservatoire's boundary begins.

CALA

Cap Taillat

A former island tethered to the mainland by a thread-thin sand tombolo, with a turquoise swim beach on each side — the wild Baie de Briande to the west, and the l'Escalet-facing beach on the Ramatuelle side to the east. About 142 hectares of protected headland, no road, only the coast path.

The isthmus is a living thing: east and west winds and the currents constantly redistribute its sand, and winter storms chew at it, so it is fenced with ganivelles to hold the dune together. The Conservatoire du littoral bought Cap Taillat in 1987 to head off a Club Med development, and it lies in the Port-Cros National Park adhesion zone. On 24 July 2017 a fire ran from Gigaro to l'Escalet, burning more than 500 hectares and reducing Cap Taillat to ash; the trail reopened only eight months later, after some 2,000 charred trees were felled and the path was rerouted to stop walkers trampling the recovering ground. The maquis greened back fast — but the umbrella pines will need decades to return.

SECRET GEM

Domaine du Rayol — Le Jardin des Méditerranées

A ~20-hectare coastal estate whose 7 hectares of gardens, designed by landscape gardener Gilles Clément, recreate the landscapes of the world's Mediterranean-climate regions and their dry-climate kin — Chile, California, South Africa, south-west Australia, the Canaries and more — tumbling down the maquis slope to the sea, past a 1910 pergola and the Hôtel de la Mer.

The story is the terroir. In 1908 Alfred Courmes, a Bormes man who made his fortune in the colonies, bought about 40 hectares of wild maquis and had architect Guillaume Tronchet build the villas, the ancient-style pergola (1910) and the Hôtel de la Mer (1912–19); aviation magnate Henry Potez took it over in 1940. When it was about to be carved up for a 284-flat seaside development at the turn of the 1980s, residents formed 'Les Amis du Rayol-Canadel' (1980) and fought — and the Conservatoire du littoral bought the 20-hectare estate in 1989. Clément's radical idea was to stop fighting the drought and instead garden with plants from every Mediterranean climate on Earth. In summer the estate also runs the sentier marin, a guided mask-and-fins swim in the Baie du Figuier billed as the garden's marine landscape.

Marina Viva Plage

A private-beach restaurant and cocktail bar on the sand, with signature drinks timed to sunset — and one of the addresses that throws a party on the two nights the town genuinely does.

It earns its place for a specific, honest reason: on the two dates Cavalaire truly comes alive after dark — the Fête de la Musique on 21 June and Bastille Day on 14 July — the action is on the sand at the beach clubs, and Marina Viva is one that hosts it, staying open into the evening for the fireworks over the bay. That is the lesson about this town's nightlife in a sentence: it lives on the beach and on the calendar, not in a fixed club. Its bar otherwise winds down around 19:00, so any other night it is a sunset cocktail with your feet near the water — modest, seasonal, and none the worse for it.

Signal Allée des Anthemis; private-beach restaurant + cocktail bar (creations by Thomas), season opens 24 Apr 2026; hosts a Fête de la Musique tapas-and-rosé evening (21 Jun) and a 14-Juillet fireworks evening per the venue site (marinavivaplage.fr)
