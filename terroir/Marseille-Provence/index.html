Marseille

Provence · France
“The oldest city in France, the noisiest, the most itself — a Phocaean Greek colony that never became entirely French, where the bouillabaisse is a charter document and the port smells of fish in the best possible way.”
Terroir Briefing · July 2026
The name From Massalia, the Phocaean Greek name given when the colony was founded around 600 BC — the Phocaeans came from Phokaia (modern Foça, Turkey) and named it for the sea-meadow, from the Greek masa or masal. Rome called it Massilia. The Occitan and Provençal forms converged on Marcelha before the French settled on Marseille in the sixteenth century; locally it remains la cité phocéenne.
History·Eat·Tables·Calanques·Walks·Culture·Quartiers·Gastronomy·Bars·Sources

Marseille is the oldest city in France and the most unbothered about it — founded around 600 BC by Phocaean Greeks who sailed from what is now Turkey, traded with the Celts inland and planted olive trees and vines that are still there; the Romans called it Massilia, punished it for backing Pompey against Caesar by stripping its fleet and library, and left it to its own devices for four centuries. That independence of temperament persisted. Marseille became French by treaty in 1481, a long century after Paris absorbed the rest of Provence, and it still behaves as though the merger is provisional.

The city's character was made by the port, by successive waves of arrivals who came through it and many of whom stayed: Italians in the 19th century, Armenians fleeing the genocide, Algerians and West Africans after independence, a layering of languages and foods that produced the Noailles market, the street-level commerce of Le Panier, and a civic food culture that is simultaneously proud and unsentimental. The bouillabaisse is not a romantic dish: it was the fishermen's way of using the fish the markets wouldn't buy, the ugly, spiny, bony rockfish of the Provençal shelf. The Abbaye Saint-Victor and Notre-Dame-de-la-Garde watch the harbour from opposite hills, but the harbour itself is functional — the fishing boats still come in before dawn and the catch still goes on the pavement before seven.

What changed since 2013 — the year the MuCEM opened as part of the European Capital of Culture programme — is that the city's gastronomic seriousness became undeniable. Alexandre Mazzia earned Marseille's first three-star restaurant in January 2021; Gérald Passédat has held three stars at Le Petit Nice since 2013. In April 2025 Coline Faulquier opened Auffo at the Vallon des Auffes and earned a star in her first season. A city that once exported its cooking talent to Paris is now making them come south.

Come in April–June or September–October: the Calanques are accessible without permits, the sea is swimmable, the markets have spring produce, and the tourist pressure on the Vieux-Port drops to a level the restaurants can handle. In July and August, access to the Calanques requires a reservation system managed by the Parc National — book weeks ahead or plan to miss them.

The bouillabaisse is a legal document. In 1980, the restaurant owners of Marseille drafted and signed the Charte de la Bouillabaisse — a binding agreement among participating restaurants specifying that the dish must contain at least five species of fish, that rascasse (scorpionfish) is non-negotiable, that the broth must be saffron-coloured and poured at table, and that rouille and croûtons are served on the side. Any restaurant claiming to serve bouillabaisse without meeting these criteria is technically in breach of the charter, which is displayed framed on the wall of every signatory. The charter also specifies that the dish costs what it costs, and that is not a negotiating position.

The soul of the city
The city that didn't become a museum

Every coastal city in the Mediterranean has a version of the same problem: the beautiful part gets preserved and the rest becomes infrastructure for tourism. Marseille avoided this more successfully than anywhere in the north-west Mediterranean basin — not because the city is ugly, but because it refused to be merely picturesque. The Vieux-Port is a working port of call, not a set. The boats that come in before dawn on the north quai are the boats that supply the Vieux-Port fish market, and the fish market supplies the restaurants, and the restaurants supply a cooking culture that is now, quietly, among the best-performing in France.

What Marseille has in excess is what most French cities have carefully managed away: friction. The city is loud, the traffic is aggressive, the streets in Noailles sell everything at full volume, the basilica on the hill is painted in competing Gothic-Romanesque colours that should not work and do. The food culture reflects this: the bouillabaisse is served with an instruction manual, the navettes have been unchanged since 1781, the pizza at Chez Etienne is made the same way it was in the 1940s and there is no printed menu. None of this is nostalgia — it is the operating logic of a city that has always been too busy doing things to curate its own story.

The sea is the constant. The Corniche Kennedy runs for four kilometres along the coast and is genuinely used — by runners, by families with children in the water, by old men with pétanque sets — in a way that the equivalent promenade in Nice is not. The Calanques begin seven kilometres from the city centre and constitute one of the most extraordinary coastlines in Europe: white limestone cliffs dropping vertically into water that reads turquoise in photographs and is actually that colour. You take a bus or hike in. There is no resort infrastructure at any of them. This is not an accident.

History
2,600 years compressed
Brief

Around 600 BC, Phocaean Greeks sailing from the eastern Aegean founded a colony at a natural harbour at the mouth of the Lacydon river — a site used for millennia before them — and named it Massalia. They traded Greek wine and olive oil for Gaulish tin and amber, planted the first olive trees and vines in what would become France, and built city walls that sections of the later city ran along. The colony grew to roughly 50,000 people and founded its own daughter colonies along the coast.

Massalia backed Pompey against Julius Caesar and paid for it in 49 BC: Caesar stripped the city of its fleet, its treasury and its library. Under the Empire it remained prosperous but no longer independent; the Romans built the structures the city now excavates under its pavements. By the medieval period Marseille was a staging post for the Crusades — the port from which five crusading expeditions sailed between 1096 and 1270 — and was acquired by France from the House of Anjou in 1481.

The 19th century transformed it. The opening of the Suez Canal in 1869 made Marseille France's gateway to its colonial empire; the city doubled in population between 1800 and 1850, and doubled again by 1900. The new arrivals were Italians (who brought pizza to Le Panier), Armenians after 1915, then Algerians and West Africans throughout the mid-20th century. Each community deposited a layer of food culture that survived.

In 2013 Marseille was the European Capital of Culture. The MuCEM opened on the J4 jetty, cantilevered over the sea in lace-patterned concrete. It changed how the city thought about itself without changing what the city actually is.

Eat
What the city cooks and why
Overview

The city's cooking divides cleanly: there is the bouillabaisse, which is not casual; there is the everyday fish, which is supremely casual; there is the pizza of Le Panier, which is neither fish nor French; and there is the new gastronomy of the Vieux-Port and the Corniche, which is doing something that hasn't been done here before.

Bouillabaisse — the charter dish — is the fishermen's use of unsaleable rockfish: rascasse (scorpionfish), vive (weever), galinette (red gurnard), grondin (grey gurnard), saint-pierre (John Dory), congre (conger eel). The broth is saffron and tomato and fennel, poured first; the fish arrive in pieces on a platter, the rouille — a saffron-garlic mayonnaise, properly made with a mortar — spread on stale bread and dragged through the bowl. It takes a minimum of two people and costs EUR 70–90 per head at a charter restaurant; attempts to reduce the price produce something else. The 1980 Charte de la Bouillabaisse lists signatory restaurants — ask to see the charter before ordering.

Panisse — not to be confused with the Nice socca — is a thick chickpea flour cake, fried in cylinders or slices, eaten as a snack or side; the street version is found around the Vieux-Port and Noailles markets. Navettes are the official pastry of the city: orange-blossom-water biscuits, boat-shaped, dry and twice-baked, made since 1781 at the Four des Navettes and blessed annually by the Bishop. Pastis is the official drink: any bar, any time of day; the ratio is one part pastis to five parts cold water, poured over ice, and the resulting cloud is the colour of the sea in winter.

Why now
What makes 2026 the moment
2026

Auffo opened in April 2025 at the address where the legendary L'Épuisette had served fine seafood until its coastal-occupation licence expired on 31 December 2024. Coline Faulquier's restaurant earned a Michelin star in its first full season — the fastest star in recent Marseille history — and the terrace above the Vallon des Auffes is the most desired seat in the city right now.

AM par Alexandre Mazzia has now held three Michelin stars for five years, long enough for the booking infrastructure to mature and for the restaurant to settle into its identity: a 24-seat room built around a continuum of smoke, garden and sea. The reservations are still difficult; the cooking has only deepened.

Calanque access is tightening permanently. The Parc National introduced a mandatory reservation system for the most popular inlets in 2022; the system now covers Sugiton and En-Vau through the high season, with more calanques likely to follow. The window to walk into a calanque without planning is closing. Come in the shoulder season — April to June, September to October — while free access still exists.

Calanques
The five main inlets of the National Park
5 calanques

The Parc National des Calanques extends 20 km east of Marseille into the limestone massif. Sugiton and En-Vau require advance slot booking via calanques-parcnational.fr in peak season (late June–early September). Access roads are closed to cars in summer; come by bus, boat or on foot.

  • Calanque de Sormiou

    The most accessible from the city — bus 23 from Rond-Point du Prado to the terminus, then a 45-minute walk down to a creek with two restaurants open in season and the best direct swimming from a beach. The water is clear enough to see fish over the white pebbles. In summer the road is closed to private cars after 09:00; the restaurants at the bottom (Le Château and Le Nautic) serve grilled fish and cold rosé without ceremony.

  • Calanque de Morgiou

    A longer approach than Sormiou, accessible by car in off-season (the road closes at a different time) or on foot from the Luminy campus. The creek ends in a small port used by local fishermen and a handful of pleasure boats; the restaurant at the water's edge (La Nautic de Morgiou) serves the day's catch at outdoor tables. Cosquer Cave — the Palaeolithic cave decorated 27,000 years ago, now accessible only to divers through a flooded entrance — opens into this massif.

  • Calanque de Sugiton

    A 40-minute walk from the Luminy university campus, no road access, access slot required in peak season (book via calanques-parcnational.fr). The narrowest of the main creeks — white cliff walls rising close on both sides, the water deep-green where the walls block the sky and turquoise in the open — and consequently the most photographed. Not suitable for anyone unsteady on their feet on rocky descents.

  • Calanque d'En-Vau

    The most dramatic in profile — vertical white limestone needles on both sides, the narrowest throat, the deepest water, access by boat or a long hike from the Cassis side (1.5 hours each way); access slot required in peak season. The boat approach from the Vieux-Port or from Cassis (closer) is the practical option for most visitors. Arrive by 08:00 on foot to avoid the permit window. There is no infrastructure of any kind at the bottom.

  • Calanque de Port-Miou

    The first calanque east of Cassis and the easiest: accessible by car, no permit needed, a 15-minute walk from the Cassis car park. It functions as a yacht anchorage — boats moored end-to-end on both sides of the long inlet — rather than a swimming destination, though swimming here is excellent. The contrast between the white limestone and the rigging of a hundred boats is implausible and real. Best approach from Cassis, not Marseille.

Walks
On foot in and around the city
5 routes
  • Corniche Kennedy: Fort Saint-Nicolas to Malmousque (4 km one way)

    The sea-level promenade south from the Vieux-Port entrance along the rocky coast — past the Vallon des Auffes at the 1 km mark, past the natural pools of Malmousque (where Marseillais have swum since the Belle Époque), ending at the Plage du Prophète and the Parc Borély beyond. The Corniche is in daily use, not a tourist route; the road traffic beside it is constant but the sea is always present and the walking is flat. Do it at dawn or dusk.

  • Le Panier: Accoules steeple to the Vieille Charité (30 min)

    Start at the Montée des Accoules (the stepped lane climbing from the Vieux-Port) and work uphill through the oldest inhabited quarter of the city — streets that follow the Roman grid, walls covered in murals by the collective Ateliers du Midi, the sudden square of the Vieille Charité at the top. The Vieille Charité is a 17th-century almshouse built to Pierre Puget's design around a baroque oval chapel; its two museums (Egyptian antiquities and Mediterranean archaeology) are worth the entry.

  • Calanque de Sormiou: bus 23 terminus descent (45 min each way)

    Take bus 23 from Rond-Point du Prado metro to its terminus (Calanque de Sormiou), then walk down the sealed road (closed to cars) to the water. The descent through garrigue — rosemary, cistus, pine — with the sea appearing over the cliff edge at the 20-minute mark is the best introduction to the Calanques without a boat or a long approach. The restaurants at the bottom are open in season; bring water for the return climb in summer.

  • Notre-Dame-de-la-Garde to Vieux-Port on foot (45 min descent)

    Take bus 60 or the tourist petit train to the basilica at the summit (154 m) and walk down through the Endoume and Roucas-Blanc neighbourhoods to the Vieux-Port. The city opens below you in full from the basilica terrace — the islands, the Calanques massif to the south, the harbour, the hinterland to the north. The downhill walk passes through residential streets that the tourist circuit misses entirely, with local bakeries and corner grocers.

  • L'Estaque: the port and Cézanne's views (1.5 hr loop)

    The working-class neighbourhood on the northern coast of Marseille, reachable by train (15 minutes from Saint-Charles) or boat, where Cézanne painted his proto-Cubist series of the 1870s and where Braque followed in 1908 — the views he chose are identifiable. The port itself is industrial but the fishing quai is still active and the restaurant on the harbour (Chez Magali, long-established) serves the catch of the day at plastic tables. L'Estaque is the version of Marseille before the renovation. Walk the cliff path above the village for the panorama Cézanne worked from.

Culture
Five places beyond the food

MuCEM — Musée des Civilisations de l'Europe et de la Méditerranée
The museum the city built to explain itself to itself

Rudy Ricciotti's J4 building opened in 2013 as the centrepiece of the European Capital of Culture programme: a cube of lace-patterned concrete cantilevered over the sea at the entrance to the Vieux-Port, connected to Fort Saint-Jean by a bridge 25 metres above the harbour. The permanent collection covers the archaeology and anthropology of the Mediterranean basin; the temporary exhibitions have been consistently ambitious. The roof terrace café has the best panoramic view in the city — the Vieux-Port in one direction, the Calanques massif in the other.

Vieille Charité
Pierre Puget's baroque almshouse, now two archaeological museums

Built between 1671 and 1749 to Pierre Puget's design as a refuge for beggars — the city had a problem with street poverty and this was a serious architectural attempt at a solution; the oval baroque chapel at the centre is Puget's masterpiece, and the three tiers of arcaded galleries around it form a space of improbable grandeur for what was officially a poorhouse. Now houses the Egyptian antiquities collection and the museum of Mediterranean archaeology. In Le Panier at the top of the Montée des Accoules.

HoursTue–Sun 10:00–18:00 (summer) / 10:00–17:00 (winter)

Notre-Dame-de-la-Garde — La Bonne Mère
The basilica on the hill that watches the city and the sea

The highest point in Marseille (154 m), occupied since the 13th century by successive chapels and rebuilt in Romano-Byzantine style between 1853 and 1864 by the architect Henri-Jacques Espérandieu. The gilded Virgin on the bell tower is 11 metres tall; the ex-votos inside — painted ship models, photographs, drawings offered by sailors and fishermen who survived — cover every wall and are the most authentic folk art in the city. The panoramic view from the terrace covers the Calanques, the islands, the port and the urban sprawl; on a clear day you can see the Sainte-Baume ridge. Bus 60 from the Vieux-Port or walk up through Roucas-Blanc.

Abbaye Saint-Victor
The oldest Christian site in Marseille, built above a pagan quarry

Founded in the 5th century above the grave of the martyr Victor by John Cassian — the same monk who brought Eastern desert monasticism to western Europe and wrote its first rulebooks; the current fortress-church is 13th century, but the catacombs below are 4th century and contain some of the oldest Christian sarcophagi in France, alongside earlier pagan quarry cuts in the rock. The annual Chandeleur ceremony (2 February), at which the Bishop blesses the navettes from the Four des Navettes opposite, has been performed here for centuries. Three minutes on foot from the south side of the Vieux-Port.

Cité Radieuse — Unité d'Habitation
Le Corbusier's vertical city, still lived in

Built between 1947 and 1952 by Le Corbusier at 280 boulevard Michelet in the 8th arrondissement: 337 apartments across 18 floors, a hotel on the 3rd floor, a school on the roof, a gymnasium and internal shopping street on floors 7 and 8 — a complete vertical community. Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2016 (as part of Le Corbusier's 17 architectural works). The restaurant Le Ventre de l'Architecte occupies the gymnasium; the roof terrace is publicly accessible and the view from it — the city, the sea, the Sainte-Baume — is extraordinary. Take the metro to Rond-Point du Prado or bus 21.

Quartiers
The neighbourhoods that matter for food and daily life
5 areas

Le Panier is the oldest continuously inhabited quarter of the city — the Roman grid is still legible in the street plan, the Montée des Accoules climbs the hill as it did in the 1st century, and the neighbourhood was the first landing point of every wave of immigration. Italian, then Armenian, then North African: the demographic history is in the walls. The pizza at Chez Etienne on rue de Lorette arrived with the Italian wave. The quarter was largely demolished by the Nazis in 1943 (they wanted to clear a partisan stronghold) and rebuilt without the original density; the murals visible on every second wall were painted by successive resident artists as a response to that emptiness. It is now partly gentrified and entirely charming and the Vieille Charité at its summit is worth the climb on its own.

Vieux-Port is the functional centre: the overnight fishing boats tie up on the north quai (Quai du Port) and the fish market runs from about 06:30 to 09:00 on the Quai des Belges at the harbour's head. By 10:00 the tourists arrive and the quais become a promenade. The fort entrance on the south side (Fort Saint-Nicolas, built by Louis XIV to control the city as much as to defend it) frames the harbour mouth. Bar de la Marine on the Rive Neuve is where Pagnol set his trilogy and where the pastis-and-pétanque culture still holds.

Noailles is the city's densest commercial district and the most North-African-inflected: the streets radiating from the covered Capucins market are the best place in Marseille to buy spices (cumin, harissa, dried mint, preserved lemons), halal meat, fresh North African produce, and the cheapest fruit in the city. The Marché des Capucins on rue du Marché des Capucins sets up before dawn and trades until mid-afternoon; the surrounding streets extend the market informally. One arrondissement from the Vieux-Port, ten minutes on foot.

Cours Julien is the city's creative neighbourhood — record shops, second-hand book dealers, independent galleries, bars with live music and the best concentration of café terrace tables outside the Vieux-Port. It was the city's central market from the 17th century until the Capucins replaced it; the large, almost empty square is now surrounded by the city's most active street-art concentration. The surrounding streets (rue des Trois Mages, rue Crudère) have small restaurants that open and close on three-year cycles; whatever is currently there will change.

Vallon des Auffes is the most visually arresting part of Marseille: a miniature inlet cut into the limestone at the northern end of the Corniche Kennedy, a stone's throw from the coast road above it, where fishing boats still tie up on a quai that has not changed since the 1900s and where the Corniche bridge passes overhead. Auffo has replaced the legendary L'Épuisette on the water's edge. Walk down via the steps from the Corniche; the cafe and restaurant at the inlet serve a captive audience of people who came for the view and stayed for the oursins in winter.

Gastronomy
The bouillabaisse protocol, navettes, pastis
Deep-dive

The charter dish

How to order bouillabaisse correctly

The 1980 Charte de la Bouillabaisse de Marseille specifies the following as non-negotiable: at least five species of fish from the local shelf (rascasse / scorpionfish is mandatory; weever, gurnard, John Dory and conger customary); a saffron-tomato-fennel broth prepared at a boil and poured first at the table in a separate vessel; fish brought separately on a platter and served whole or in pieces; rouille (saffron-garlic mayonnaise) on croûtons rubbed with garlic on the side. Signatory restaurants are listed on the charter displayed in the dining room — ask to see it. The dish requires a minimum of two people and costs EUR 70–90 per head at a charter restaurant. There is no acceptable budget version. Miramar co-drafted the charter; Le Petit Nice serves a deconstructed modern version as a pure essence broth.

The institutional biscuit

Navettes at the Four des Navettes since 1781

The navette is a boat-shaped biscuit flavoured with orange blossom water, dry and twice-baked, made at the Four des Navettes on rue Sainte since 1781 — the oldest recipe in continuous production in the city. On Chandeleur (2 February) the Bishop of Marseille blesses the first batch at the adjacent Abbaye Saint-Victor, a ceremony reported annually in La Provence and unbroken for over two centuries. The biscuits keep for two to three weeks and are sold in boxes. They are not expensive and are not meant to be: the navette is the city's everyday institutional product, as unchanged as the bouillabaisse charter and as stubbornly itself.

The social lubricant

Pastis: ratios, brands and when

Pastis is anise-and-liquorice spirit, served at a ratio of one part pastis to five parts cold water poured over ice — the mixture clouds (the louche) as the anethole precipitates. The three major brands are Ricard (Paul Ricard invented the modern pastis formula in 1932 in Marseille; the company is still headquartered here), Pastis 51 (named for the year the French government re-legalised anise spirits after Vichy-era prohibition), and Pernod (which acquired Ricard in 1975, forming the Pernod Ricard group). The local debate over which is correct is mild and permanent; in any bar you order a pastis and receive whichever house brand is on the back shelf. At Bar de la Marine on the Rive Neuve you can also, technically, order lunch.

Bars
Where to drink in Marseille
Practical

Drinking in Marseille divides into three modes: the pastis bar (any bar, all day, the Bar de la Marine being the most storied), the wine bar of the Cours Julien area (independent, natural-wine focused, open from the early evening), and the harbour-front terrace (the Rive Neuve south quai has the best views at sunset).

The Cours Julien neighbourhood has the best concentration of serious wine bars — the area around rue des Trois Mages and rue Crudère changes its lineup frequently, but in recent seasons has included natural-wine bars and cocktail rooms that function as de facto neighbourhood restaurants after 21:00. The best rule is to arrive around 18:00 and see what is posted on the boards outside.

The Vieux-Port's south quai (Rive Neuve) fills with aperitivo-hour drinkers from about 18:30 in the summer months; the north quai (Quai du Port) is more local, less tourist, and marginally cheaper. The Bar de la Marine at 15 quai de Rive Neuve opens in the morning and stays open past midnight; it is the default answer to the question of where to have a pastis on the harbour.
