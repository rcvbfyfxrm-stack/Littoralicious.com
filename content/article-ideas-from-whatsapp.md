### Tier 1 — strongest patterns, write from these

**1. The sourdough commons** — `littoral-heritage` + Blueprint companion
- Pattern: the most recurring thread across two groups — 18+ starter asks Feb–Jun (Fort Lauderdale ×5, Genoa ×3, La Ciotat ×2, St Maarten, Nassau, Antibes, Nice, Viareggio, English Harbour, Aspen). A free starter exchange runs beneath the luxury industry, with its own folk technology: dehydrate-and-vacuum for flights, stiff dough in a ziplock for 36-hour trips, bottled spring water because water-maker output kills cultures, 50/50 rye feeds.
- Evidence: a crossing crew killed a chef's named starter ("Jemima") and binned the frozen backup (May); one veteran travels with a 42-year-old starter — "I can forget my make-up tools but not my starter" (Apr); an 80-year-old Tromsø starter handed to a stranger on a dock (Jun).
- Angle: mutual aid hiding inside a competitive industry; the one ingredient aboard that money can't buy. Carries a starter transport/revival Blueprint.

**2. Provisioning intel that isn't on Google** — `port-call` / `shore-larder`
- Pattern: the groups' real currency is hyper-local sourcing knowledge: in Great Exuma the fish contact is the hairdresser opposite the Shell station, her freezer stocked daily by local fishermen (grouper, wahoo, lobster, mahi); Grenada's rhythm — "the island receives provisioning Mondays and Wednesdays so Tuesdays and Thursdays are best"; a St Maarten butcher who flies fish in Thursdays; contact cards land within minutes of any ask.
- Angle: the agent's list vs the chef's list — how provisioning knowledge actually moves. Series potential ("The Hairdresser's Freezer"); feeds every future Port Call.

**3. The dock-to-dock favor economy** — `signal-fire` essay
- Pattern: the group as physical infrastructure: emergency eggs found within the hour via a neighbouring boat at a gated Bahamas marina; Metro wholesale cards borrowed in Viareggio, San Remo, Imperia, Antibes (~5 asks); packages held in Palma for boats not yet arrived; Prague powder, pectin and piping bags gifted; informal reference checks on boats and crew.
- Angle: competitors built a cooperative under a cut-throat industry. A dozen true micro-anecdotes carry the piece.

**4. What yacht chefs actually earn, 2026** — `trade-winds`
- Pattern: pay is THE recurring argument in all three groups, with numbers nobody publishes. Ad ladder: €2,500–3,500 cook/stew → €5–6k thick middle (30–40 m sole chef) → $8–10k (50 m+ charter); day rates $200–400. The arguments: "your wages have not moved since 2017, when any other position on board has gone up 50% or more" vs the tips math ($9k ÷ 30 days ÷ 14 h = $21.40/h vs "80k tax free in tips a year"); "10k a month — same pay rate for the past 25 years" (Apr); the freelancer who held her rate until the boats that passed called back; the theory that "Below Deck is as damaging to the yacht industry as Food Network was to the restaurant industry."
- Angle: the definitive salary map from ~200 ads plus the war around it. Supersedes the old "Salary Benchmarks 2026" entry. Fold in rotation (10:10 and 2:2 now reaching chef ads; "5:1 isn't rotation").

**5. Feeding the feeders: crew food on €20 a head** — `trade-winds` + crew-feeding core
- Pattern: rare hard numbers — polls and per-boat answers converge on €15–35/head/day (outliers $12 tanker, $45) while "owners and their guests have no budget." A dedicated crew-chef sub-market frames crew nutrition as morale infrastructure — balancing "healthy eaters and the classic 'fried beige' lovers"; one ad sells crew meals as the chef's test kitchen.
- Angle: cooking well at €20 while the other end of the boat eats unpriced; the Friday-cook-for-the-weekend convention; derive-crew-from-guest doctrine.

**6. The Ozempic charter** — `the-evidence` / `the-method`
- Pattern: first field guidance for GLP-1 guests (May): small portions are the key ("they can't eat much"), avoid fatty/rich/spicy/high-GI or "they can react very badly," meds stored in the chef's fridge, desserts often skipped entirely.
- Angle: appetite suppression meets charter excess — timely, practical menu-planning payoff, and a food-culture question bigger than yachting.

**7. The 90-day clock: EES vs the Med season** — `trade-winds`
- Pattern: the Schengen biometric entry-exit rollout (April circular) collided with crew reality: non-EU crew can't be stamped out onto a vessel unless it provably departs within 10 days; "a friend lost 4 crew members today, given 24 hours to be on a plane home" (Apr 30); enforcement uneven — "Italy is the hardest… Palma are just carrying on as before"; keep boarding passes, demand physical stamps. Side thread: one forwarded memo caused industry-wide panic until the veteran corrective — ignore forwarded WhatsApp law, "your captain will tell you."
- Angle: a regulation reshaping who cooks in the Med this season, plus the misinformation cycle inside crew WhatsApp culture.

**8. Hired by bunk** — `trade-winds` / `signal-fire`
- Pattern: dozens of ads specify sex — "male only / female only due to cabin arrangements," some formally citing the Equality Act 2010 occupational-requirement clause; on small crews the two-berth cabin chart, not talent, is the first hiring filter. Adjacent fine print: "no visible tattoos," "no drama queens," "ideally on the younger side."
- Angle: pure structural analysis, zero gossip — how boat architecture decides careers; the last open gender clause in hiring.

**9. "We do not pay to cook": anatomy of a chef-targeted scam** — `trade-winds` service piece
- Pattern: the group dismantled a €1,500 pay-to-be-seen "villa event" in 48 hours — "It's illegal to charge crew members to get a job. - Angle: predation in a desperation-priced labour market, written as a red-flag checklist with receipts.

**10. The preference sheet lied** — `the-method` essay / Tight Ship

**11. Cook the port** — `shore-larder` / house thesis

## WHAT THE DATA REVEALS ABOUT LITTORALICIOUS'S AUDIENCE

### What yacht chefs actually need (ranked by message volume):
1. **Provisioning contacts** — port-by-port, with phone numbers, with honest reviews
2. **Equipment recommendations** — from peers, not marketing
3. **Career/financial guidance** — salary benchmarks, tax strategy, contract negotiation
4. **Technique sharing** — practical, tested, specific to galley constraints
5. **Community** — people who understand the life

### What yacht chefs don't need (absent from conversations):
- Food photography styling tips
- Molecular gastronomy trends
- Celebrity chef profiles (unless directly relevant, like Noma)
- Wine pairing theory (barely discussed)
- Plated dessert aesthetics

### The gap Littoralicious can fill:
The WhatsApp groups are the primary information source for working yacht chefs. But the information is ephemeral — buried in thousands of messages, unsearchable, lost when you join a group late. Littoralicious can be the **permanent, searchable, verified version** of what these groups discuss daily. Not replacing the community — indexing it.
