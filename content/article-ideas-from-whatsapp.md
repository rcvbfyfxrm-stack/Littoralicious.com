# Article Ideas — Extracted from Yacht Chef WhatsApp Groups

**Source:** "Chefs Helping Chefs" (~1,025 members, 7,400 messages) and "Yacht Chef Group" (~300+ members, 16,914 messages). Period: July 2025 – January 2026.

**Method:** Full conversation analysis across both groups, categorised by engagement intensity (message volume, reply depth, emotional charge). Each article idea maps to a Littoralicious content category and includes the real-world data points from the conversations that justify it.

### 1. The Provisioning Bible: Mediterranean

**Category:** Port Call (operations-10)
**Why it matters:** Provisioning questions account for approximately 40–50% of all messages across both groups. Chefs constantly ask "who do you use in [port]?" and the answers are scattered across thousands of messages. No single consolidated resource exists.

**Data from chats:**
- Simon Jones / The Meat & Fish Society (Mallorca/Ibiza) — mentioned 4+ times, universally praised
- Kostas / Food Galore (Greece, ships from Athens) — recommended for all Greek islands
- Riccardo Fish and Cheese (Porto Cervo) — "the cheese van guy"
- Carina / Frutas Ramon / Top Tin (Santa Catalina Market, Palma) — expanded to carry international goods
- Io Sono La Chianina (Naples/Capri) — "insane t-bones"
- Bread flour (12%+ protein) in Palma: Caputo flour 13% protein recommended
- Montenegro: "all pretty terrible" — Dubrovnik recommended instead; Montefish for fish; Voli Horeca for Asian goods
- Gibraltar: Morrison's supermarket manager hostile to yacht provisioning
- Metro card issues: Italian Metro card does NOT work in France (tied to Italian tax code)
- French Metro card requires: boat registration, crew list, captain's passport, 3 cards per boat
- Gourmet Deliveries (16 votes in supplier poll), We Supply Yachts (10 votes), Ocean Pantry (6 votes)
- Suppliers warned against: BFG Provisions (owed money), Yachting Spirit (20% markup on total bill)

**Article structure:** Port-by-port guide covering Palma, Barcelona, Sardinia (Porto Cervo, Olbia, Cagliari), Naples/Capri, Genoa/La Spezia/Viareggio, La Ciotat, Antibes/Cannes/Nice, Monaco, St Tropez, Athens, Mykonos, Corfu, Malta, Montenegro, Istanbul. Real contacts with phone numbers (with permission). Metro card logistics per country. Supplier red flags.

### 5. Atlantic Crossing: The Yacht Chef's Meal Planning Guide

**Category:** Tight Ship (operations-09)
**Why it matters:** Crossing preparation generates long, detailed advice threads. Experienced chefs share hard-won knowledge. New chefs are genuinely anxious.

**Data from chats:**
- James Lawrence: "Stock your drystores. Canned stuff — duck confit etc. Rice, pasta, tinned toms, tinned veggie. Pack the freezer with protein. Buy loads of longer lasting veggies: potatoes, carrots, root veggies"
- Kate Purdy: "UHT milk is your friend and I would get some full fat powdered milk... If you're able to buy unrefrigerated vegetables and eggs, they will last longer... Don't skimp on snacks and hot drink materials — they will be very important for crew on night watches"
- Jason: "Prep as much as possible and freeze for bad weather situations... lasagnas, meat loaves, curries, sauces, braised meats, savoury pies"
- Emily Cabanas: "Start pre making and freezing quick pop into oven crew meals, get soups, purees... People get seasick and sometimes a chef can get seasick... Prep, prep, prep. Even prep and freeze cookies and cakes because morale can get low mid-Atlantic"
- Creative storage: using spare guest cabins as cold rooms with A/C turned down
- Owner joining the passage: "I'll just eat crew food" — "that lasted two days"

**Article structure:** Timeline (4 weeks out, 2 weeks out, 1 week out, departure day). Dry store checklist. Freezer prep list. Rough weather meals. Night watch provisions. Morale management. Realistic capacity per fridge/freezer volume.

**Article structure:** Salary benchmarks by boat size, type (private/charter), and position. Rotation standards. Tip distribution norms. Tax strategy (UK seafarer exemption, Italian residency, US obligations). Red flags in contracts. Negotiation tactics.

### 7. Chef Shoes: The Definitive Test

**Category:** Tight Ship (operations-09)
**Why it matters:** Discussed in both groups, repeatedly, with extreme passion. This is a topic every yacht chef has an opinion on and most have gotten wrong at least once.

**Data from chats:**
- Birkenstocks: divided — loved by many Brits, "worst chef shoes on the market" by others, "not recommended by podiatrists"
- On Cloud waterproof: liked but "slippery AF"
- Puma Safety: Tobias swears by them — "no foot problems anymore, 18hr+ shifts"
- Skechers slip-ons: Jan Richards switched from 12 years of Birkenstocks, huge improvement; "go in the washing machine too"
- Hoka Bondi SR: non-slip but lean outward over time
- Hoka (general): "Best shoes I've had for being on my feet 12 to 14 hours a day"
- Barefoot shoes (Feelgrounds): one lone advocate
- Sticos: "very stylish"
- Nike running shoes: Ross — "engineered for time on feet"
- Dansko clogs: "great but bulky to travel with as a freelancer"
- Kybun (Swiss): "really fly bit expensive but top"
- Universal advice: get custom orthotics from an orthopedic specialist

**Article structure:** Side-by-side comparison. Durability at sea. Non-slip testing (galley conditions, wet decks). Support for 14+ hour shifts. Travel-friendliness for rotation. Podiatrist input. Price per month of use.

### 8. B1/B2 Visa: What Changed in 2025 and What It Means for 2026

**Category:** Trade Winds (intelligence-08)
**Why it matters:** Generated the most fearful, anxious messages in both groups. ICE enforcement in Florida is real and chefs are being caught.

**Data from chats:**
- New rule effective November 1, 2025: B1/B2 must be issued in country of nationality, not wherever you happen to be
- Emily Cabanas' detailed testimony: ICE boarding vessels, comparing arrival dates to bank account activity to contract dates, checking social media, making crew open phones and bank accounts. "4 crew members earlier this year in WPB" were caught
- M/Y Rosa incident — video shared in group
- "If you're a yachtie and you get banned from the states, that means any boat you join that heads to Caribbean won't hire you if they go to USVI and US territories"
- B1 allows work on yachts with full-time contract for non-US company only; must leave immediately when contract ends
- American crew: some insurance companies limit Americans; repatriation costs cited as reason
- Dual passport holders advised to use non-US passport

**Article structure:** What the law actually says (cited). What changed in 2025. What ICE is actually doing in Florida ports. How to protect yourself. What to do if approached. Immigration lawyer contacts. Flag state implications.

## WHAT THE DATA REVEALS ABOUT LITTORALICIOUS'S AUDIENCE

### What yacht chefs actually need (ranked by message volume):
1. **Provisioning contacts** — port-by-port, with phone numbers, with honest reviews
2. **Equipment recommendations** — from peers, not marketing
3. **Career/financial guidance** — salary benchmarks, tax strategy, contract negotiation
4. **Technique sharing** — practical, tested, specific to galley constraints
5. **Community** — people who understand the life

### What yacht chefs don't need (absent from conversations):
- Food photography styling tips
- Molecular gastronomy trends
- Celebrity chef profiles (unless directly relevant, like Noma)
- Wine pairing theory (barely discussed)
- Plated dessert aesthetics

### The gap Littoralicious can fill:
The WhatsApp groups are the primary information source for working yacht chefs. But the information is ephemeral — buried in thousands of messages, unsearchable, lost when you join a group late. Littoralicious can be the **permanent, searchable, verified version** of what these groups discuss daily. Not replacing the community — indexing it.

*Generated from analysis of ~24,000 WhatsApp messages across 2 yacht chef groups, February 2026.*
