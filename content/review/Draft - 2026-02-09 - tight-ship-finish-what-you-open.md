## Finish What You Open
*Category: Waste Reduction / Storage / Provisioning*

**The Pain:**
You open the fridge and find half an onion in cling film turning translucent, a third of a passata bottle growing a skin, two tablespoons of coconut milk in a ramekin covered in foil, and a wedge of lemon drying out next to yesterday's half-avocado now the color of a bruise. You wrapped them all with good intentions. Now you're throwing them all away — plus the plastic you used to save them.

**The Protocol:**
1. **Open it, finish it.** If a recipe calls for half a tin of passata, use the full tin — thicken a sauce, build tomorrow's staff meal base, reduce it for pizza sauce. Plan the second use *before* you open the first.
2. **Write tonight's crew meal around today's offcuts.** Half an onion, a pepper end, two carrots past their prime — that's a soup, a stew, a fried rice, a stock. The crew meal is your waste-elimination system, not an afterthought.
3. **Menu forward, not ingredient backward.** When planning the day, scan what's already open in the fridge *first*. Build lunch or crew dinner from there. Provision and prep around what needs finishing, not what's easiest to start fresh.
4. **Kill the cling film reflex.** Before wrapping anything: will I actually use this within 4 hours? If not, use it now or convert it into something stable (stock, purée, dressing, marinade). A half-onion wrapped in plastic is not storage — it's procrastination.
5. **On passage: cook in complete units.** Crossing days multiply the problem. Every half-ingredient becomes inventory to track, rotate, and eventually throw overboard. Provision in exact quantities. Cook in whole tins, whole onions, whole packets. A 14-day Atlantic crossing with half-portions accumulating in the fridge is how you lose control of your galley.

**Why It Works:**
The instinct to save half an ingredient feels efficient. It isn't. It creates cognitive load — you have to remember what's in there, check if it's still good, find a use before it turns. Every wrapped fragment is a micro-decision deferred. Multiply that across a week, and you're spending 15-20 minutes daily just managing the graveyard shelf. The "finish what you open" rule eliminates the decision entirely. There's nothing to check, nothing to remember, nothing to throw away. The fridge stays clean because nothing in it is waiting to die.

**The Proof:**

| Metric | Before (saving halves) | After (finish what you open) |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Fridge audit time | 15-20 min/day | 3-5 min/day |
| Food waste (weekly) | 2-3 kg of wrapped fragments | Under 500g (peels, trims only) |
| Cling film / plastic use | 1 roll per 10-day charter | 1 roll per 3-4 weeks |
| "Mystery containers" in fridge | 5-8 at any time | 0-1 |
| Crew meal planning time | 20 min (starting from scratch) | 5 min (built from what's open) |

**Where Most Go Wrong:**
You tell yourself "I'll use that tomorrow." You won't. Tomorrow has its own menu, its own prep list, its own ingredients. That half-tin of coconut milk will sit there for three days until you smell it and bin it. The problem isn't laziness — it's optimism. You're betting your future self will remember and prioritize a ramekin of leftover ingredient over a clean fresh start. Future you has other plans. Use it now.

**The Catch:**
This requires menu flexibility. If the preference sheet locks every meal to a fixed recipe, you lose the ability to absorb offcuts into crew meals or tomorrow's lunch. Works best when crew meals and at least one guest meal per day are chef's choice. On rigid charter menus, adapt by provisioning in exact quantities from the start — which takes more planning upfront but eliminates the problem entirely.

Also: some ingredients genuinely store well halved — hard cheeses, cured meats, dense root vegetables. The protocol targets perishables and opened containers, not everything. Use judgment. But when in doubt: finish it.
