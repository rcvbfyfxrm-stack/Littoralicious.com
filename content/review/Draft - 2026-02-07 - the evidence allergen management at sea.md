# Allergen Management at Sea: When Nobody Else Cares

**The Study:**
This is not a single-study briefing. This is an evidence-based operational protocol built on two foundations: the legal framework governing allergen management in food service (Natasha's Law, EU FIC Regulation 1169/2011, US FALCPA, and flag state food safety requirements) and documented incidents from working yacht chefs that demonstrate how allergen failures actually happen at sea — not in theory, but in galleys where the chef is the only person who takes the risk seriously.

**Key Finding:**
Allergen incidents on yachts are caused not by the chef's ignorance but by other crew members' indifference. The chef knows the risk. The stew, the first mate, the captain — frequently, they do not. And on a vessel at sea, there is no ambulance. There is no hospital. There is a satellite phone, a med kit, and whatever the chef had the foresight to prevent.

---

## The Incidents That Prove the Point

### Incident One: The Peanut Bowl

A stewardess placed loose peanuts in the crew mess. An anaphylactic crew member was aboard. The vessel was preparing for a long ocean passage — weeks at sea with no realistic access to emergency medical facilities.

When the chef raised the concern, the stewardess and her boyfriend (the first mate) dismissed it: "The watchkeeper isn't a baby and he can decide what he eats."

This is the response that nearly killed someone. The logic — that an adult can simply avoid eating peanuts placed in an open communal space — ignores the fundamental reality of anaphylaxis. Airborne peanut protein can trigger reactions in highly sensitised individuals. Contact transfer from surfaces, utensils, and hands creates exposure pathways that have nothing to do with the victim's dietary choices. An open bowl of peanuts in a crew mess is not a snack option. It is an uncontrolled allergen release.

The chef put the concern in writing to the captain. All loose peanuts were removed. The written record was the decisive action — it created liability documentation that the captain could not ignore.

### Incident Two: The Hidden Nuts

A chief stewardess hid nuts in a dish, leading to a child's anaphylactic shock. A child. On a yacht. At sea.

The details matter less than the pattern: a non-chef crew member made a food decision that nearly killed a guest because they did not understand — or did not care about — the clinical reality of allergen exposure.

### The Pattern

Both incidents share the same structure:

1. The chef identified the allergen risk
2. A non-chef crew member introduced or concealed the allergen
3. When confronted, the non-chef crew member minimised the risk
4. The chef had to escalate formally to get the risk addressed
5. In both cases, the outcome could have been fatal

## The Legal Framework

### Natasha's Law (UK, October 2021)

Named after Natasha Ednan-Laperouse, who died at age 15 after eating a Pret a Manger baguette containing sesame that was not labelled. Natasha's Law requires all food businesses in the UK to provide full ingredient and allergen labelling on all pre-packed for direct sale (PPDS) food.

**Applicability at sea:** Natasha's Law applies directly to vessels registered under the UK flag (Red Ensign Group). For other flag states, the principle — that the food provider bears legal responsibility for allergen disclosure — is embedded in the ISM Code's requirement for safe working practices and in most flag state food safety regulations. Regardless of technical jurisdiction, the standard of care is clear: if you serve food, you are responsible for knowing and communicating what is in it.

### EU Food Information for Consumers (FIC) Regulation 1169/2011

Requires declaration of 14 specified allergens in all food served to consumers, including in catering. Applies to EU-flagged vessels and any vessel operating in EU waters serving food commercially (charter yachts).

## The Liability Chain on a Yacht

When an allergen incident occurs at sea, the liability chain is:

1. **The chef** — as the food provider, bears primary responsibility for allergen identification, communication, and prevention
2. **The captain** — as the vessel's master, bears overall responsibility for safety on board, including food safety
3. **The management company** — responsible for the Safety Management System (SMS) under the ISM Code, which must include food safety protocols
4. **The owner** — ultimately liable as the employer and vessel operator

If a guest or crew member suffers anaphylaxis because allergen information was not communicated, documented, or acted upon, every level of this chain is exposed. If the chef raised the concern in writing and was overruled by the captain, the liability shifts upward. If the chef did not document the risk, the liability remains with the chef.

**Documentation is not bureaucracy. It is legal protection.**

## The Allergen Documentation Protocol

### Before Every Charter / New Guest

1. **Obtain the preference sheet.** Read every line. Highlight all allergen declarations. If the preference sheet says "no allergies" but lists "doesn't eat shellfish," treat shellfish as an allergen until confirmed otherwise — some guests do not understand that an allergy and a preference are different clinical categories
2. **Confirm allergens directly.** If possible, speak to the guest or their PA directly. Ask: "Is this a preference or a medical allergy? If allergic, what is the severity — mild intolerance or anaphylactic risk?" Document the answer in writing
3. **Brief the entire crew.** Every stewardess, every deckhand, everyone who might offer a snack, set a table, or stock a bar. The allergen list is not the chef's secret — it is the vessel's safety protocol
4. **Label all stored food.** Pre-prepared items in the fridge and freezer must be labelled with contents and allergen flags. Masking tape and a Sharpie. It takes 10 seconds per container. It prevents cross-contamination by crew members who access the fridge

### During Service

5. **Maintain physical separation.** In a small galley, cross-contamination is the primary risk vector. Dedicated cutting boards, dedicated utensils, and separate prep zones for allergen-free dishes. Colour-coded boards are effective — red for allergen-free prep
6. **No substitutions without re-checking.** If you change an ingredient mid-service — because you ran out, because the fish was off, because the guest changed their mind — re-check the allergen matrix before the plate leaves the galley
7. **Communicate verbally with service staff.** When a dish leaves the galley for an allergic guest, tell the stewardess: "This is the allergen-free plate for [guest name]. Confirm placement." Verbal confirmation creates accountability

### After Service

8. **File the menu with allergen annotations.** Every menu served should have a corresponding allergen record — which dishes contained which allergens, which dishes were modified for specific guests. Store digitally and in hard copy
9. **Debrief on near-misses.** If a stewardess nearly served the wrong plate, if a crew member introduced an allergen into the crew mess, if a provisioning order included an unexpected ingredient — document it. Near-misses are warnings. Treat them as such

## When Other Crew Dismiss the Risk

This is the hardest part. The chef's authority over food safety is not universally respected on yachts. Stewardesses stock snack bars. Deckhands prepare BBQ setups. Captains make provisioning decisions. Any of these actions can introduce undocumented allergens.

**What works:**

1. **Put it in writing.** In the first incident, the decisive action was writing to the captain. A written record of an allergen risk creates a document that the captain, management company, and insurers will all see. Verbal warnings are deniable. Emails are not.
2. **Cite the law.** "Natasha's Law" and "anaphylaxis" are words that change the tone of a conversation. Use them. The legal framework exists because people died.
3. **Escalate to the DPA.** The Designated Person Ashore exists to handle safety concerns that the captain does not address. If the captain dismisses an allergen risk, the DPA is your next call.
4. **Frame it as the captain's liability.** The captain bears legal responsibility for safety on board. An allergen incident on the captain's watch is the captain's problem — professionally, legally, and personally. Make this clear. Respectfully, factually, in writing.

## The Numbers

| Statistic | Data | Source |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Anaphylaxis fatality rate (untreated) | 0.5–1% of anaphylactic episodes | Sampson et al., *JACI*, 2006 |
| Time to cardiac arrest (severe anaphylaxis) | 5–30 minutes | Pumphrey, *Clinical and Experimental Allergy*, 2000 |
| EpiPen effectiveness window | Must be administered within minutes of symptom onset | Simons et al., *JACI*, 2011 |
| Distance to hospital from mid-ocean | 0 hospitals. 0 ambulances. Satellite phone only | Reality |

**What This Means in the Galley:**
Every allergen protocol exists because the alternative is a crew member or guest in anaphylactic shock, 400 nautical miles from the nearest hospital, with an EpiPen and a satellite phone as the only medical resources. The chef is the last line of defence. Not the first — the last. Because if the chef does not catch it, nobody else will.

**What This Does Not Mean:**
That the chef bears sole moral responsibility for allergen safety. The systemic failures documented here — stewardesses introducing undocumented allergens, officers dismissing medical risks, management companies without allergen protocols in their SMS — are institutional failures. The chef's responsibility is to document, communicate, and escalate. The institution's responsibility is to listen.

**Limitations:**
- Anaphylaxis statistics are drawn from land-based medical studies. Maritime-specific data on allergen incidents is extremely limited due to underreporting
- Flag state enforcement of food allergen regulations on yachts is inconsistent and often absent
- The documented incidents in this article are self-reported by chefs in WhatsApp groups and have not been independently verified through legal or medical records

## The Minimum Kit

Before next charter, have these in place:

1. **Allergen card** — one page, laminated, posted in the galley. Guest name, allergen, severity, date confirmed, confirmed by whom. Update every charter.
2. **EpiPen location** — known to all crew, not just the chef. Check expiry monthly.
3. **Written allergen brief** — emailed to all crew at the start of every charter. Not verbal. Written. Deniable warnings are not warnings.
4. **Colour-coded cutting board** — one board, one colour, dedicated to allergen-free prep. Never used for anything else.
5. **Menu allergen log** — every menu served, annotated with which dishes contain which of the 14 allergens. Stored digitally and in hard copy for 12 months minimum.

**Your Move:**
Create the allergen card this week. It takes 10 minutes. Laminate it. Post it. Brief the crew. Put your concerns in writing — every time, without exception. The chef who documents is the chef who is protected. The chef who assumes everyone knows is the chef who gets blamed.
