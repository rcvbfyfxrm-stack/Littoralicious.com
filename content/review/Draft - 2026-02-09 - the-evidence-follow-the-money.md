## Industry-Funded Nutrition Research Produces Favorable Results Up to 8 Times More Often Than Independent Studies

**The Study:**
In 2016, Cristin Kearns, a dentist-turned-researcher at UCSF, published what she found in the archives of a deceased Illinois sugar industry executive: internal documents proving that in 1967, the Sugar Research Foundation (now the Sugar Association) paid three Harvard scientists — including the head of the nutrition department — the equivalent of $50,000 in today's currency to publish a review in the *New England Journal of Medicine*. The review's conclusion: dietary fat, not sugar, was the primary driver of coronary heart disease. The funding was never disclosed. The lead Harvard researcher, D. Mark Hegsted, went on to draft the U.S. government's first dietary guidelines. For fifty years, the world ate low-fat, high-sugar products based on research bought and paid for by the sugar industry.

This is not conspiracy theory. It was published in *JAMA Internal Medicine* (2016), one of the most rigorous peer-reviewed journals in medicine. The documents are public.

**The Pattern:**
Kearns' finding was dramatic. But the Cochrane Collaboration — the gold standard for systematic evidence review — had already quantified the broader problem. Lundh et al. (2017) analyzed the relationship between industry sponsorship and research outcomes across all medical fields: industry-sponsored studies were significantly more likely to report favorable efficacy results and favorable conclusions than non-industry studies. In nutrition specifically, Lesser et al. (2007) in *PLOS Medicine* found that industry-funded studies were **4 to 8 times** more likely to produce conclusions favorable to the sponsor than independently funded research.

Not sometimes. Not occasionally. Four to eight times.

**The Modern Version:**

The laboratory has moved. The mechanism hasn't.

In the 1960s, the sugar industry bought journal papers. Today, the supplement industry buys podcasts. The structure is identical: a trusted voice delivers a narrative that sounds like independence — *they don't want you to know this, the system is broken, here's the real science* — and between the revelations, reads an advertisement for a $79/month electrolyte powder, a greens supplement with 47 ingredients and zero clinical trials at that specific formulation, a "science-backed" sucrose drink that is, chemically, sugar water with salt.

The podcaster is not lying. The podcaster believes. That's what makes it work. The most effective advertising doesn't feel like advertising — it feels like a friend sharing what helped them. The FTC requires disclosure. The disclosure happens. Nobody listens to it. The narrative has already done its work.

This is not cynicism. This is peer-reviewed, documented, reproducible reality. When money enters the room, conclusions tilt toward whoever is paying. Not always. Not every time. But often enough, and systematically enough, that the pattern is no longer debatable.

**What This Means in the Galley:**

Every nutritional claim that reaches you — from a journal, a podcast, a supplier, a guest who "read somewhere that..." — deserves one question before any other: **Who paid for this?**

Not "is this true?" That comes second. First: who funded the research? Who sponsors the show? Who profits if you change your behavior? The answer doesn't automatically invalidate the claim. But it tells you where to apply pressure, where to demand a second source, where to hold the claim against the light and check for fingerprints.

The sugar industry didn't fund bad science. They funded *incomplete* science — studies designed to ask the right question the wrong way, to spotlight one variable while shadowing another. The result looked like knowledge. It was, technically, peer-reviewed. It shaped what the world ate for half a century.

A chef who reads a study funded by the olive oil board saying olive oil is the healthiest fat should not throw the study away. But they should find the study that wasn't funded by the olive oil board and see if it says the same thing. If it does — good. If it doesn't — now you know something.

**What This Does Not Mean:**

Not all industry-funded research is wrong. Some of the best food science comes from companies that make food. The problem isn't funding — it's undisclosed funding, and the systematic tilt that comes with it. Independent research can also be flawed, biased, or poorly designed. The point is not to distrust everything. The point is to check.

And not all free content is trustworthy. Free content funded by advertising has the same structural problem as industry-funded research — the money comes from somewhere, and that somewhere has interests.

**Limitations:**
- The Kearns study examined historical documents; the specific researchers named are deceased and could not respond
- Lesser et al. meta-analysis covers nutrition broadly — not all subcategories show equal bias
- Podcast/influencer research is nascent; most evidence is observational or case-study
- The supplement industry includes legitimate products with genuine evidence alongside unregulated claims — blanket skepticism is as lazy as blanket trust
