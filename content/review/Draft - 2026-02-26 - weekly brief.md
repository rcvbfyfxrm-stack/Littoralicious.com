# Weekly Brief — Week of February 26, 2026

## THE THREAD

Spring anchovies are weeks away. The best Mediterranean *Engraulis encrasicolus* will be caught between late March and July, when fat composition peaks and the flesh is ideal for salt-curing. But the colatura — the amber fermentation liquid drawn from barrels of salt-packed anchovies — that's peaking right now, because the barrels packed two and three years ago have had the longest time to develop. This connects to a zero-heat pasta technique that is the canonical dish of the Amalfi Coast, the proteolysis science that explains how endogenous enzymes turn muscle protein into 7,300 mg/L of free glutamic acid, and a town called Cetara where this process has continued unbroken since Roman fish-processing facilities gave the place its name.

One mechanism ties the thread: proteolysis. The fish's own enzymes — trypsin, chymotrypsin, cathepsin L — dismantle its proteins into free amino acids over months and years in a barrel. The result is the most concentrated natural umami source in the Mediterranean pantry. Understanding this process is the difference between using colatura as a condiment and understanding it as a 2,000-year-old biotechnology.

## SHORE LARDER — Salt-Cured Anchovies

*Engraulis encrasicolus · Mediterranean/Eastern Atlantic · Catching peak: March–July · Colatura peak: 24–48 months aged*

**The species.** European anchovy, 12–18 cm, family Engraulidae. Approximately 30% of total Mediterranean fishery production — annual landings averaging nearly 400,000 tonnes. The fish that built an industry.

**The catch window matters.** For Colatura di Alici di Cetara DOP, the production specification mandates anchovies harvested between March 25 (Feast of the Annunciation) and July 22 (Feast of Mary Magdalene), from waters within 12 nautical miles off the coast of Salerno province. Spring-caught fish have superior fat composition. The Ortiz family in Cantabria buys exclusively from the spring season for the same reason.

| Compound | Per 100 g Raw | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Protein | 17–26 g | Varies by season; spring fish optimal |
| EPA + DHA (omega-3) | 1.3–2.1 g combined | DHA averaging 9.4% of total lipid, EPA 7.9% |
| Free glutamic acid (colatura) | 5,600–7,300 mg/L | Increases substantially between 24 and 48 months of barrel aging |
| Histamine (properly produced) | 44 mg/kg at 1 month, declining | EU limit: 400 mg/kg. Extended aging *decreases* histamine in properly handled product |

**DOP status.** Colatura di Alici di Cetara received Denominazione di Origine Protetta in October 2020 — the first Italian DOP product in the fishery sector. Specifications require: anchovies from Salerno coastal waters, traditional *terzigni* (chestnut wood barrels), minimum 12 months aging, bottled in transparent glass (50–1,000 mL).

**Sourcing:** When transiting the Amalfi Coast, buy direct from Cetara — Delfino Battista and Nettuno are the principal artisanal producers. European yacht provisioners (Gourmet Deliveries in Cannes/Monaco/Antibes, Just Provisions across the Med, Riviera Gourmet on the Italian coast) carry DOP-certified bottles. For salt-packed whole anchovies: Ortiz (Cantabria, Spain) and Recca (Sicily) are benchmark brands available at most provisioning stops.

## THE METHOD — Spaghetti con Colatura di Alici di Cetara

**The Move:** Build a raw sauce in a bowl. Never heat the colatura. The pasta's residual heat does all the work — warming the sauce to ~55°C, enough to release volatile aromatics developed over years of barrel aging, not enough to destroy them.

This is the canonical dish of Cetara, traditionally served on Christmas Eve (*La Vigilia*). It is also the single best demonstration of what colatura can do.

### Ingredients (serves 4)

- 350 g spaghetti (high-quality durum wheat)
- 80 ml extra virgin olive oil
- 40–60 ml colatura di alici di Cetara DOP
- 2 cloves garlic, minced paper-thin
- 1 small bunch flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- Peperoncino (dried red chilli), to taste
- **No additional salt**

### Method

1. Bring a large pot of water to boil. **Do not salt the water.** The colatura provides all seasoning.
2. While water heats, prepare the condiment in a large bowl — not on heat. Combine olive oil, colatura, garlic, parsley, and crumbled chilli. Stir to emulsify. This stays raw.
3. Cook spaghetti to al dente.
4. Reserve 1–2 ladles of pasta cooking water. Drain.
5. Immediately toss hot pasta into the bowl with the raw condiment. Residual heat warms the sauce without cooking it. Add a splash of pasta water for silkiness.
6. Toss vigorously. Plate immediately. Garnish with additional parsley. Some versions add toasted breadcrumbs (*pangrattato*) for texture.

| Factor | What Happens | Adjust By |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Colatura heated directly | Volatile aromatics destroyed, flavour flattens | Always combine in a cold bowl; pasta heat only |
| Water salted | Dish becomes inedibly salty | Zero salt in boiling water — colatura is the salt |
| Cheap colatura | Thin, one-dimensional, possibly high histamine | DOP-certified, minimum 24-month aged |

**You know it worked when:** Every strand is coated in a thin, glossy, amber film. The aroma hits before the fork reaches the mouth. The garlic whispers. The chilli lingers. The colatura does everything.

## THE EVIDENCE — Proteolysis and Amino Acid Evolution in Colatura

Ferrara, L. et al. (2023). "Evolution of Free Amino Acids, Histamine and Volatile Compounds in the Italian Anchovies (*Engraulis encrasicolus* L.) Sauce at Different Ripening Times." *Foods*, 12(1): 126. DOI: 10.3390/foods12010126

A study tracking the chemical evolution of colatura di alici at 12, 24, and 48 months of barrel aging. The key finding: everything that matters — flavour, safety, complexity — improves with time.

| Metric | 12 Months | 24 Months | 48 Months |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Total free amino acids | Baseline | Increased | Significantly increased |
| Glutamic acid (umami) | Dominant | Dominant | Dominant, concentration rising |
| Histamine | Present | Declining | Further declined |
| Volatile complexity | Developing | Complex | Most complex |

**The enzymes doing the work:** Trypsin and chymotrypsin (from visceral tissues, ~25.6–26.1 kDa, optimal at pH 8.0 / 45°C) drive initial protein breakdown. Cathepsin L (from muscle tissue, ~25.8 kDa, optimal at pH 6.0 / 50°C) becomes dominant by month 9. All three remain remarkably active at the high salt concentrations (250–300 g/kg NaCl) and acidic pH (5.2–5.8) of the brine environment.

**What this means in the galley:** Longer-aged colatura is categorically superior — more umami, more complexity, less histamine risk. If you're choosing between a 12-month and a 36-month bottle, pay the premium. The chemistry justifies it. For guests with histamine sensitivity, extended-age DOP colatura is actually the safer product.

**What this does NOT mean:** That all fish sauces are equivalent. Colatura's amino acid profile (high glutamate, aspartate, glycine, alanine) mirrors archaeological garum residues from Pompeii, but its volatile superstructure is distinct from Vietnamese nuoc mam and Thai nam pla. Same umami backbone, different aromatic architecture.

**Supporting reference:** Li, H., Zhu, F., and Li, G. (2025). "Advancing safer and healthier fermented fish products through innovative processing strategies." *Food Innovation and Advances*, 4(3): 293–303. DOI: 10.48130/fia-0025-0029

## LITTORAL HERITAGE — Colatura di Alici and the Town That Never Stopped

*Cetara, Amalfi Coast, Campania*

The town name itself is the evidence. Roman fish-processing facilities were called *cetariae* — the word that became Cetara. When Vesuvius buried Pompeii in 79 AD, it preserved the Garum Shop (*Officina del Garum*) mid-production: six sealed *dolia* containing anchovies and small fish being fermented whole, operated by Aulus Umbricius Scaurus, whose branded containers have been found in over 50 locations across Pompeii and Boscoreale. The finest garum sold for 1,000 sesterces per 12 pints — roughly a legionary's monthly wages.

After Rome fell, garum disappeared across most of Europe. Except here. Cistercian monks at the monastery of San Pietro a Tuczolo near Cetara continued salting anchovies in wooden barrels through the medieval period. The tradition holds that they discovered colatura by accident — noticing a flavourful amber liquid collecting at the bottom of curing barrels, seeping through the wood. They recognised it as the successor to the garum described in classical texts.

In 900 AD, Saracen settlers further established Cetara's identity as a fishing centre. For centuries afterward, colatura remained exclusively local — made by virtually every family for domestic use, never traded beyond the coast.

| Era | What Happened | What Survived |
|-----|---------------|---------------|
| 1st century AD | Roman *cetariae* produce garum from anchovies | The facility name becomes the town name |
| 79 AD | Vesuvius preserves the Garum Shop at Pompeii | Archaeological proof of anchovy fermentation |
| 5th–9th century | Monks continue barrel-aging anchovies | Technique bridges Roman collapse |
| 900 AD | Saracen settlement at Cetara | Fishing community identity solidifies |
| 2003 | Slow Food Presidium designation | Artisanal heritage formally recognised |
| October 2020 | DOP registration | First Italian fishery-sector DOP |

**The chemical proof of continuity:** Analysis of amino acid profiles from archaeological garum residues at Pompeii shows a pattern dominated by glutamate, glycine, and alanine — the same amino acid profile found in modern colatura di alici. This is not cultural mythology. It is molecular evidence of an unbroken 2,000-year technique.

**On board:** Transit the Amalfi Coast. Anchor off Cetara — it's between Vietri sul Mare and Erchie, small harbour, working fishing boats. Walk to one of the producers. Buy the colatura from the people who make it in the same town where the Romans named the process. Make the spaghetti that night. Tell the guests this liquid is older than Christianity and younger than the barrel it came from.

**Pairing:** Fiano di Avellino DOCG — Pietracupa or Ciro Picariello. Mineral, structured, Campanian granite.
