# Yacht Chef Content Creation: Camera, Lights, CapCut

*Category: Equipment / Workflow*

**The Pain:**
You cook 14 hours a day and have nothing to show for it. No portfolio. No footage. No proof that the 400-cover charter week happened. When it comes time to find the next boat, your CV is words on a page while the chef who shoots content has a reel that gets them hired in 48 hours.

**The Protocol:**

1. **Shoot on your phone, stabilise with a DJI gimbal.** The DJI OM series (currently OM 7) fits in a drawer, pairs in seconds, and produces smooth tracking shots of plating and galley work. A dedicated camera is unnecessary unless you're building a YouTube channel. Your phone sensor is good enough for Instagram, TikTok, and portfolio reels.

2. **Light with a Nanlite Halo 18 ring light for static shots.** Galley lighting is almost always overhead fluorescent — harsh, green-tinted, and unflattering to food. The Halo 18 runs at 26 W, adjusts from 3200K to 5600K, and folds flat for storage. Mount it above your plating station during final presentation. For quick handheld shots, a foldable LED panel (any brand, minimum 95 CRI) clips to a shelf and takes 10 seconds to position.

3. **Edit in CapCut.** It is free, runs on mobile, and handles cuts, speed ramps, text overlays, and colour grading without a learning curve. Export at 1080p for Instagram Reels and TikTok. For longer-form YouTube content, DaVinci Resolve (free desktop version) gives you professional-grade colour correction and audio tools.

4. **Batch your shooting into 5-minute windows.** Do not try to film while cooking for service. Shoot during plating — the last 90 seconds of a dish — and during prep when your hands are clean and the light is set. Two clips per service, 15-30 seconds each, is enough raw material for one finished reel per week.

5. **Build a portfolio reel, not a content calendar.** The goal is a 60-90 second highlight reel you can send to agents and captains. Update it monthly. Ten strong clips across different cuisines and presentations is worth more than daily posts that no one outside the algorithm sees. Chef Danny Davies / Behind the Line and the Superyacht Galley Designs YouTube channel are reference points for what yacht-specific content looks like done well.

**Why It Works:**
Content creation fails for yacht chefs because they treat it as an additional job rather than a documentation habit. The protocol above adds less than 10 minutes per day. The DJI gimbal eliminates shaky footage without a tripod. The ring light solves the single biggest quality killer (bad galley lighting). CapCut removes the editing barrier. The constraint — only shoot during plating and prep — prevents content from interfering with service.

**The Proof:**

| Metric | Without Protocol | With Protocol |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Time spent on content per day | 0 min (nothing captured) | 8-10 min |
| Usable portfolio clips per month | 0 | 12-16 |
| Equipment stored | None | Gimbal (drawer), ring light (shelf), LED panel (clip) |
| Editing time per reel | N/A | 15-20 min in CapCut |

**The Catch:**
Some boats have strict no-camera policies, especially on private vessels with high-profile owners. Confirm with your captain before shooting anything. Charter vessels are generally more permissive, but never film guests, guest areas with personal items visible, or anything that could identify the vessel without explicit written permission. Your content rights may also be addressed in your contract — read it.
