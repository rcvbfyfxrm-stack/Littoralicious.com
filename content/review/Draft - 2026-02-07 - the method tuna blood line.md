# The Tuna Blood Line: Four Uses for the Most Discarded Cut at Sea

**The Move:** Stop throwing away the blood line. The dark, iron-rich strip running the length of every tuna loin is one of the highest natural sources of glutamic acid in any fish. Smoke it, dry it, powder it, or simmer it — it becomes a umami concentrate that earns its place in dashi, pasta doughs, seasoning blends, and broths.

## 1. Smoked Blood Line

**Do This:**
1. Remove the blood line cleanly from the loin. Trim any silver skin. You want a solid strip, roughly 2–3 cm wide by the length of the loin. Rinse briefly in cold water to remove surface blood.
2. Dry brine: coat with 3% salt by weight plus 1% sugar. Refrigerate uncovered on a rack for 12 hours. The surface should feel tacky — this pellicle is essential for smoke adhesion.
3. Cold smoke at 20–25°C for 4–6 hours using fruitwood or oak. If you lack a cold smoker, a stovetop smoking setup with a hotel pan, wire rack, and hardwood chips at the lowest gas setting works — monitor with a probe to keep ambient below 30°C.
4. The smoked blood line is ready to eat as-is (shaved thin over rice, salads, or carpaccio), or proceed to drying.

**Why It Works:**
Tuna blood line tissue is dense with myoglobin and haemoglobin — the same iron-containing proteins that make it dark red. These proteins are rich in free amino acids, particularly glutamic and aspartic acid. Cold smoking introduces phenolic compounds that inhibit lipid oxidation (the main cause of the metallic "off" flavour people associate with blood line), while simultaneously adding volatile aromatics. The result shifts the flavour profile from ferrous and sharp to deep, savoury, and complex — closer to katsuobushi than to raw tuna.

**You Know It Worked When:**
The surface is uniformly mahogany, dry to the touch, and the aroma is clean smoke with no metallic sharpness. Internally, the flesh should be firm but not brittle, and taste intensely savoury without any ammonia notes.

## 2. Blood Line Dashi

**Do This:**
1. Take 200 g of raw or smoked blood line. Cut into 3–4 cm chunks.
2. Combine with 1 litre of cold water and 10 g of kombu in a pot. Bring to 60°C over 30 minutes. Hold at 60°C for 20 minutes. Do not boil — temperatures above 80°C will extract bitter compounds from both the kombu and the blood proteins.
3. Remove the kombu at 60°C. Raise the temperature to 75°C and hold for 10 minutes more. The blood line will release a deep, amber liquor.
4. Strain through muslin. Season with a few drops of usukuchi soy if needed, but taste first — the glutamate concentration is often sufficient on its own.

**Why It Works:**
The slow extraction at sub-boiling temperatures dissolves free glutamic acid and inosinic acid (IMP) from the muscle tissue without denaturing the proteins into cloudiness or bitterness. This is the same synergistic umami mechanism that makes traditional ichiban dashi work: glutamate from the kombu and inosinate from the fish amplify each other by a factor of up to 8x, according to Yamaguchi's 1967 research on umami synergy. The blood line's unusually high concentration of both compounds makes it a more potent base material, gram for gram, than standard bonito.

**You Know It Worked When:**
The broth is clear amber, not cloudy. Taste it plain — it should coat the tongue with a persistent savoury depth that lingers for 10+ seconds. If it tastes metallic or thin, the extraction temperature was too high or too low, respectively.

**At Sea:**
This dashi keeps refrigerated for 5 days and freezes well in 250 ml portions. Build it from trim during tuna breakdown and you have a base stock that costs nothing and outperforms most commercial dashi concentrates.

## 3. Blood Line Powder

**Do This:**
1. Start with smoked blood line (from method 1 above) or raw blood line that has been dry-brined at 3% salt for 24 hours.
2. Slice into strips no thicker than 5 mm. Dehydrate at 65°C for 8–12 hours until completely brittle — it should snap, not bend.
3. Grind in a spice grinder to a fine powder. Sieve through a fine-mesh strainer to remove any fibrous pieces.
4. Store in an airtight container with a silica packet. Keeps for 3 months at room temperature, 6 months refrigerated.

**Why It Works:**
Dehydration concentrates the free amino acids by removing water while preserving the glutamic acid intact — the same principle behind katsuobushi, bottarga, and dried shrimp. The resulting powder contains approximately 1,200–1,500 mg of glutamic acid per 100 g (comparable to aged Parmesan), making it a potent finishing seasoning. Mixed into pasta dough at 2–3% of flour weight, it adds colour, umami, and structural protein without altering the dough's hydration.

**You Know It Worked When:**
The powder is uniformly fine, rust-coloured, and smells deeply of smoked fish and sea salt — not metallic, not rancid. A pinch on the tongue should produce immediate, intense salinity and savouriness.

## 4. Noodle Dough with Blood Line

**Do This:**
1. For 500 g of 00 flour, add 15 g of blood line powder (3% of flour weight) to the dry flour. Mix thoroughly before adding liquid.
2. Hydrate with 4 whole eggs and 2 yolks, plus 5 ml of squid ink if you want the colour to shift from rust to deep ocean black. Mix and knead to a smooth dough. Rest for 30 minutes minimum, wrapped.
3. Roll and cut as ramen, udon, or fettuccine. The dough handles identically to standard egg pasta but with slightly more resistance during rolling — the added protein tightens the gluten network.
4. Cook in well-salted boiling water. Fresh noodles take 90–120 seconds. Serve in the blood line dashi (method 2) with shaved smoked blood line (method 1) as garnish.

**Why It Works:**
Blood line powder adds both flavour and function. The additional protein (primarily myoglobin and haemoglobin) integrates into the gluten matrix, increasing the dough's tensile strength. This produces a noodle with more bite — al dente texture that holds in hot broth longer than standard egg pasta. The Maillard reaction during any subsequent high-heat cooking (pan-frying, for instance) is accelerated by the amino acid concentration, producing deeper browning.

**You Know It Worked When:**
The raw dough is deep rust or ochre. The cooked noodle is firm, bouncy, and carries a distinct savoury undertone even before adding broth or sauce.

**At Sea:**
The powder is the gateway to all four methods — it stores indefinitely, weighs nothing, and turns a piece of waste into a building block. Make it in bulk whenever you break down tuna. The cost is zero. The yield, across dashi, seasoning, and pasta, is weeks of use from a single fish.

**The Variables:**

| Factor | What Happens | Adjust By |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| Blood line freshness | Older than 24 hours unfrozen develops metallic, ammonia-like off flavours | Process same day as tuna breakdown. If freezing first, vacuum-seal immediately after trimming |
| Smoking temperature above 30°C | Fat renders, texture becomes mushy, shelf life drops | Monitor ambient with a probe. Cold smoke only |
| Dashi extraction above 80°C | Cloudiness, bitterness, loss of clean umami | Hold at 60–75°C. Use a thermometer, not intuition |
| Dehydration below 60°C | Too slow — bacterial risk in the 25–55°C range on high-protein tissue | Maintain 65°C minimum. If your dehydrator can't hold that, use an oven on its lowest setting with the door ajar |
| Powder storage | Moisture absorption leads to rancidity within weeks | Airtight container, silica packet, refrigerate if ambient humidity exceeds 60% |
