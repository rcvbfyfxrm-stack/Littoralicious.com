# Atlantic Crossing: The Yacht Chef's Meal Planning Guide

*From provisioning to landfall. Everything you need, in the order you need it.*

Every November, the WhatsApp groups light up with the same anxiety. A chef — often a first-timer — posts: "About to do my first crossing. Any advice on provisioning and meal planning?" What follows is a thread of detailed, hard-won knowledge from chefs who have done it before, some of them many times.

This guide consolidates that knowledge into a single planning document, structured as a timeline. The crossing referenced is the standard westbound Atlantic passage: departure from the Canary Islands (or Cape Verde), arrival in the Caribbean, approximately 2,500-3,000 nautical miles over 14-21 days depending on vessel speed and weather.

The principles apply to any extended offshore passage. The details are specific to feeding a yacht crew and (sometimes) guests across two to three weeks of open ocean with zero provisioning opportunities.

---

## The Fundamentals

### Storage Reality

Before you plan a single meal, audit your storage. The limiting factor on a crossing is not your menu creativity — it is your cubic metres of refrigeration and freezer space.

A rough calculation for a 14-day crossing feeding 12 crew:

| Storage Type | Minimum Required | What It Holds |
|-------------|-----------------|--------------|
| Freezer | 400-500 litres | 14 days of protein for 12 people, plus pre-made meals |
| Refrigeration | 300-400 litres | 5-7 days of perishables (first week), dairy, condiments |
| Dry storage | 200-300 litres | Pasta, rice, canned goods, flour, UHT milk, snacks |

If your numbers do not add up — if the boat has 200 litres of freezer and you are feeding 14 people for 18 days — you have a conversation with the captain now, not at sea.

### Creative Storage

One technique shared by multiple chefs: **use spare guest cabins as supplementary cold rooms.** Turn the A/C down to its lowest setting, store root vegetables, hard cheeses, cured meats, and other semi-perishables in the cabin. It is not refrigeration, but at 16-18°C it extends shelf life significantly beyond a warm galley or storage locker.

### The Owner Variable

If the owner is joining the passage: plan for guest-level meals for the full duration. The frequently shared anecdote from the groups: the owner says "I'll just eat crew food." That lasted two days. Plan as if the owner will want a proper dinner every night, because they will.

## Four Weeks Out: Strategy and Sourcing

This is planning, not cooking. Your goal is to have a complete provisioning list and confirm supplier availability before the pressure starts.

### Menu Architecture

Build your crossing menu around three tiers:

**Tier 1: Freezer meals (days 1-21).** Pre-made, vacuum-sealed, heat-and-serve. These are your insurance policy for rough weather days when you cannot stand at a stove.

**Tier 2: Fresh cooking with stored ingredients (days 1-10).** Fresh produce, refrigerated proteins, dairy. This is normal galley cooking using perishables in order of shelf life.

**Tier 3: Dry store and canned cooking (days 10-21).** Pasta, rice, lentils, canned proteins, preserved vegetables. This is where your culinary skill matters most — making day 18 taste as considered as day 2.

### The Master List

Start your provisioning list now. Divide it by storage type:

**Freezer (pre-made meals to prep in weeks 2-1):**
- Lasagnas (portioned for crew numbers)
- Meat loaves
- Curries (coconut-based freeze well; dairy-based do not)
- Braised meats (short ribs, osso buco, lamb shoulder — all improve with freezing and reheating)
- Savoury pies (fully assembled, raw pastry, freeze unbaked)
- Bolognese and ragu sauces (in 2-litre portions)
- Soups and purees (butternut, leek and potato, tomato — all freeze clean)
- Cookie dough (portioned, freeze on trays)
- Cake batters or finished cakes (banana bread, carrot cake, brownies)

**Freezer (proteins for fresh cooking):**
- Chicken (whole and portioned)
- Beef (steaks, mince, stewing cuts)
- Pork (chops, shoulder, belly)
- Fish (individually vacuum-sealed fillets)
- Prawns, squid, mussels (frozen seafood is better than no seafood on day 15)

**Refrigeration (consume in order of perishability):**
- Salad leaves and herbs (days 1-3)
- Berries and soft fruit (days 1-4)
- Fresh milk and cream (days 1-5)
- Citrus, apples (days 1-10)
- Hard cheeses, cured meats (days 1-14+)
- Eggs — see note below
- Butter (salted lasts longer; stock heavily)

**Dry store:**
- Pasta (multiple shapes — variety matters on day 16)
- Rice (basmati, arborio, sushi rice)
- Canned tomatoes (minimum 20 x 400g tins for 12 crew over 14 days)
- Canned beans and lentils
- Canned tuna, sardines, anchovies
- Canned duck confit (a luxury that stores indefinitely and transforms a meal)
- UHT milk (full fat — "UHT milk is your friend")
- Full-fat powdered milk (backup for UHT, also useful for baking)
- Flour (bread flour and plain — enough for daily bread if you bake)
- Sugar, salt, oils, vinegars
- Soy sauce, fish sauce, coconut milk, curry pastes
- Dried spices (full audit — you cannot buy cumin mid-Atlantic)
- Coffee and tea (critical — see night watch section)
- Snacks: biscuits, crackers, nuts, dried fruit, chocolate, crisps
- Porridge oats
- Honey, peanut butter, Nutella (crew morale in a jar)

### The Egg Strategy

Unrefrigerated eggs last significantly longer than refrigerated ones. If you can source unwashed, unrefrigerated eggs before departure, they will hold 3-4 weeks at ambient temperature. Refrigerated eggs, once chilled, must stay chilled and last 2-3 weeks. Buy both: refrigerated eggs for the first week, unrefrigerated for weeks 2-3.

## Two Weeks Out: Prep Begins

### Freezer Meal Production

This is the intensive period. You are running a small food production operation alongside normal galley duties. Block 3-4 hours per day for crossing prep on top of regular service.

**The batch list (adjust quantities for your crew size):**

| Meal | Portions | Freezer Format | Defrost Method |
|------|----------|---------------|----------------|
| Lasagna | 4 x 6-portion trays | Foil trays, sealed | Fridge 24h, oven 180°C 40 min |
| Beef curry | 3 x 6-portion batches | Vacuum bags, flat | Fridge 12h, simmer 20 min |
| Braised short ribs | 2 x 6-portion batches | Vacuum bags with sauce | Fridge 24h, oven 160°C 30 min |
| Bolognese | 4 x 2L batches | Vacuum bags, flat | Fridge 12h, simmer 15 min |
| Chicken pie filling | 2 x 6-portion batches | Vacuum bags | Fridge 12h, add pastry, bake |
| Butternut soup | 3 x 3L batches | Vacuum bags, flat | Fridge 12h or direct from frozen, simmer |
| Meat loaf | 3 x loaves | Foil-wrapped | Fridge 24h, oven 170°C 35 min |
| Cookie dough | 100+ portions | Sheet trays, then bags | Bake from frozen, add 2 min |
| Banana bread | 4 x loaves | Wrapped in film, then foil | Ambient 4h or fridge overnight |
| Brownies | 2 x trays | Portioned, wrapped | Ambient 2h |

**Label everything.** Date, contents, reheating instructions, portion count. At 03:00 in a rolling sea, you will not remember which bag is curry and which is bolognese.

### Vacuum Sealing Protocol

Flat-pack everything possible. Flat bags stack efficiently in a freezer and defrost faster than blocks. Freeze bags flat on sheet trays, then stack vertically once solid. This alone can increase your effective freezer capacity by 20-30%.

## One Week Out: Final Provisioning

### Fresh Produce Strategy

Buy in order of shelf life, working backwards from departure:

**7 days before departure:** Root vegetables (potatoes, carrots, onions, sweet potatoes, beetroot, turnips, celeriac). These are your weeks 2-3 vegetables. Store cool and dry.

**5 days before:** Brassicas (cabbage, cauliflower, broccoli), hard squash, peppers, avocados (buy green). Citrus and apples.

**3 days before:** Tomatoes, cucumbers, courgettes, aubergine. Soft herbs (store wrapped in damp paper, refrigerated).

**1 day before:** Salad leaves, fresh herbs, berries, bread. Anything that will not last more than 3-4 days.

### The Last Fresh Meal

Plan your departure day dinner as the best meal of the crossing. You have maximum fresh ingredients and (probably) calm seas. Use it. Seared fish with salad, a proper cheese course, fresh fruit dessert. From day 3 onward, the menu tilts increasingly toward preserved, frozen, and dry store ingredients.

## Departure Day: Systems Check

### Fridge Organisation

Organise your refrigerator for chronological consumption:

- **Front/top:** Items to use in days 1-3 (salad, soft fruit, fresh fish)
- **Middle:** Items to use in days 4-7 (harder produce, dairy, packaged proteins)
- **Back/bottom:** Items to use in days 7-14 (hard cheese, cured meats, eggs, butter, condiments)

### Freezer Organisation

Similar principle, but inverted — items needed soonest should be most accessible:

- **Top/accessible:** Meals for days 3-7 (move to fridge to defrost 24h before needed)
- **Middle:** Meals for days 7-14
- **Bottom:** Proteins and meals for days 14-21, bread loaves, emergency reserves

### The Defrost Calendar

Pin a defrost schedule to the galley wall:

| Day | Move to Fridge |
|-----|---------------|
| Day 1 | Short ribs (for day 3 dinner) |
| Day 2 | Curry (for day 4 lunch), cookie dough (for day 3 snack) |
| Day 3 | Lasagna (for day 5 dinner), soup (for day 5 lunch) |
| Day 4 | Bolognese (for day 6 dinner) |

Continue for the full crossing. Build this calendar before departure. Adjust daily based on weather, consumption rate, and crew preferences.

## Underway: Day-by-Day Principles

### Days 1-3: Fresh Phase

Cook normally. Use the most perishable ingredients first. Salads, fresh fish, grilled vegetables, soft fruit. This is when crew morale is high and seasickness has not yet set in. Make the most of it.

### Days 4-7: Transition Phase

Fresh produce is diminishing. Lean on root vegetables, brassicas, eggs, and the first round of freezer meals. This is where your pre-made soups and curries start rotating in.

Daily bread baking, if you do it, becomes an anchor for crew routine. The smell of fresh bread in a galley at sea is not trivial — it is morale infrastructure.

### Days 7-14: Dry Store Phase

Fresh produce is limited to root vegetables, cabbage, citrus, and apples. Freezer meals are in heavy rotation. Pasta, rice, and lentils form the base of most meals. This is where creativity matters: a well-spiced lentil dal with fresh-baked naan, a proper risotto with frozen porcini, pasta with canned San Marzano and good olive oil.

Eggs should still be available (unrefrigerated eggs holding well). A proper omelette or shakshuka with canned tomatoes, onion, and spice is a high-morale meal that requires almost nothing fresh.

### Days 14-21: Endgame

If the crossing runs long, you are in deep dry store territory. Canned proteins, dried pasta, rice, lentils. This is not failure — this is planning. The meals that work in this phase: risotto, pasta e fagioli, bean stews, tuna pasta, fried rice with frozen vegetables and soy sauce, pancakes and porridge for breakfast.

The freezer should still have emergency meals and desserts. Do not use them all by day 10. Ration the good stuff for when morale is lowest — typically days 14-17, when the novelty has worn off and landfall is still days away.

## Rough Weather Meals

Rough weather changes everything. A chef who is bracing against the galley counter cannot produce plated service. Plan for it.

### The Rough Weather Protocol

1. **Pre-heat oven to 160°C.** Oven-based meals are safer than stovetop in heavy weather. The food is contained. Nothing can slosh or spill.
2. **Pull a freezer meal.** This is exactly what you prepped them for. Lasagna, pie, braised meat — anything that goes from container to oven to plate.
3. **Soups in thermoses.** Pre-make soup, ladle into insulated thermoses, distribute to crew. No bowls, no spoons, no surfaces. Drinkable meals.
4. **Bread and cheese.** No cooking required. Pre-slice bread, set out hard cheese, cured meats, butter. A self-service station that works in any sea state.
5. **Accept reduced standards.** Nobody expects five courses in 3-metre swells. Feed people. Keep them warm. Move on.

### Seasickness Contingency

"People get seasick and sometimes a chef can get seasick." This is real. Prep is your insurance. If you are incapacitated for 12-24 hours, the freezer meals and bread you prepped allow someone else — a stew, an officer, anyone who can operate an oven — to feed the crew without your involvement.

Ginger tea, plain crackers, and dry toast should be accessible without entering the galley. Stage these in the crew mess before weather deteriorates.

## Night Watch Provisions

Night watches are 4-6 hours in the dark, often cold, often alone. The crew on watch need food and hot drinks available without waking the chef.

### The Night Watch Station

Set up before you finish for the evening:

- **Thermos of soup** (hearty — not consomme, not broth, something substantial)
- **Thermos of hot chocolate or coffee** (pre-made, strong)
- **Sandwich platter** (wrapped, labelled, refrigerated if ambient temperature is high)
- **Snack box:** nuts, dried fruit, biscuits, chocolate bars, protein bars
- **Fruit:** apples, bananas, oranges (whatever is still fresh)
- **Tea and coffee supplies** with hot water available (confirm the crew mess kettle works before departure)

"Don't skimp on snacks and hot drink materials — they will be very important for crew on night watches." This is direct advice from experienced crossing chefs. The crew on the 02:00-06:00 watch, cold and tired, will remember whether the chef left them something decent. It matters for onboard relationships far beyond the crossing.

## Morale Management

"Prep, prep, prep. Even prep and freeze cookies and cakes because morale can get low mid-Atlantic."

This is not soft advice. This is operational intelligence. On a 14-21 day passage, the psychological arc is predictable: excitement at departure, settling in during the first week, restlessness and fatigue during the second week, anticipation (and impatience) as landfall approaches.

Food is the primary morale lever the chef controls. Specific tactics:

- **Fresh bread daily.** The effort is real. The impact is disproportionate.
- **Baked goods at unexpected moments.** Fresh cookies at 16:00 on day 12 shift the mood of the entire crew.
- **A "special" dinner mid-crossing.** Pull out the duck confit, make a proper dessert, open a decent bottle. Mark the halfway point.
- **Variety.** Even within the constraints of dry store cooking, changing the cuisine — Italian one night, Thai the next, Mexican, Indian — prevents menu fatigue.
- **Comfort food.** Late in the crossing, when everyone is tired of being at sea, serve the most comforting thing you can produce. Shepherd's pie. Mac and cheese. Chicken soup. Nobody wants innovation on day 17. They want warmth.

## The Checklist

Print this. Pin it to the galley wall.

### 4 Weeks Out
- [ ] Audit freezer, fridge, and dry storage capacity (litres)
- [ ] Confirm crew count and any dietary requirements
- [ ] Confirm whether owner/guests are joining the passage
- [ ] Plan 3-tier menu (freezer meals, fresh cooking, dry store cooking)
- [ ] Build master provisioning list by storage type
- [ ] Contact suppliers, confirm availability and delivery dates

### 2 Weeks Out
- [ ] Begin freezer meal production (target: 15-20 pre-made meals)
- [ ] Vacuum seal and flat-pack all freezer meals
- [ ] Label everything: contents, date, portions, reheating instructions
- [ ] Bake and freeze cookies, cakes, brownies
- [ ] Confirm egg sourcing (unrefrigerated if possible)

### 1 Week Out
- [ ] Purchase root vegetables and long-life produce
- [ ] Stock dry stores completely
- [ ] Confirm UHT milk, powdered milk, coffee, tea quantities
- [ ] Build defrost calendar for full crossing duration

### 3 Days Out
- [ ] Purchase medium-life produce (brassicas, peppers, citrus)
- [ ] Final freezer organisation (chronological access)
- [ ] Prep night watch station supplies

### 1 Day Out
- [ ] Purchase salad, herbs, berries, fresh bread
- [ ] Organise fridge chronologically
- [ ] Set up night watch station
- [ ] Brief crew on self-service food locations

### Departure Day
- [ ] Cook the best meal of the crossing
- [ ] Pin defrost calendar to galley wall
- [ ] Confirm all freezer meals accessible in correct order
- [ ] Check crew mess kettle, thermoses, snack station

## The Bottom Line

An Atlantic crossing is not improvised. It is the most planned cooking you will do all year. The chef who spends 30 hours prepping in the two weeks before departure will have a manageable, even enjoyable crossing. The chef who wings it will be exhausted, under-provisioned, and cooking under conditions that make everything harder.

Prep, prep, prep. Then freeze it. Then label it. Then plan when to defrost it.

The sea does not care about your menu plans. But if you have built enough redundancy — enough frozen meals, enough dry store depth, enough flexibility — the sea does not need to care. You are ready for it.
