# Luke Summersgill Smith: Cook What Makes Them Happy

*Yacht Chef · Mediterranean / Caribbean circuit · 8+ years at sea*

**The Idea:**
Kill your ego before the job does it for you. The yacht chef who cooks what the guest wants -- not what the chef wants to cook -- survives longer, earns more, and paradoxically becomes better at the craft.

**How They Got There:**
Eight years ago, Luke arrived on his first yacht with a culinary school portfolio and the conviction that every plate needed his signature. Foams. Sous vide. Techniques borrowed from stages at restaurants he couldn't afford to eat at. The guests didn't care. They wanted a good steak. They wanted pasta that tasted like the trattoria they'd eaten at the night before in Portofino -- except on their own boat, at midnight, without a reservation. The breakthrough wasn't gradual. It was a specific charter where a principal pushed back an entire tasting menu and asked for a burger. Luke cooked the burger. It was the best burger he'd ever made. The guests tipped well. He stopped fighting.

"8 years ago I thought everyone needed my ego-enhanced cooking," Luke wrote in the Yacht Chef group. "More often than not people just want good wholesome food. I have zero ego for food anymore and have succumbed to the fact I'm a culinary prostitute and I'll cook whatever makes you happy."

The phrase stuck. It became the most quoted line in a group of 300+ professional yacht chefs.

**The Idea in Practice:**

| Application | What They Do | Why It Works |
|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| Menu planning | Build menus around guest preferences, not seasonal ambition. Ask the stew what they actually ate last charter, not what the preference sheet says. | Preference sheets lie. Observation data doesn't. A guest who writes "healthy eating" but orders truffle pasta three nights running is telling you what they want with their fork. |
| Technique allocation | Spend 80% of technique on execution (temperature, timing, seasoning) and 20% on presentation. Reverse the culinary school ratio. | Damian Harvey's principle: "More Mallman, less Blumenthal." Fire, salt, fat, heat. The techniques that make food taste correct are invisible. The ones that make food look impressive are often irrelevant to the guest. |
| Social media vs. reality | Stop cooking for Instagram. The viral dips, the Bijin-Hime strawberries, the TikTok trends -- execute them when requested, but don't build a repertoire around content that exists for a camera. | A charter guest who saw something on TikTok wants it once. They want food that tastes right every night. Chasing trends burns prep time on one-offs instead of building systems for consistent delivery. |

**What You Can Steal:**
The next charter, ask yourself before every menu: "Is this for the guest or for my ego?" If the answer is ego, simplify. Put the technique into the execution -- a perfectly rested, correctly tempered côte de boeuf with a flawless béarnaise -- instead of the concept. Build your reputation on the guest who says "that was exactly what I wanted" rather than the one who photographs a plate they didn't finish.

**At Sea:**
This idea doesn't just adapt to yacht conditions -- it was born from them. The constraints of a single-chef galley, the isolation from a brigade, the direct and immediate feedback loop from guest to plate with no front-of-house buffer: these conditions strip away every pretension. Experienced yacht chefs arrive at Luke's conclusion not because they lack ambition but because the sea is the most honest editor in the business. Frozen meatballs with grape jelly and Heinz chilli sauce got 26 votes of disbelief in the group chat -- but if the owner's kids want it, you make it. That's the job.
