# Weekly Brief — Week of March 5, 2026

## THE THREAD

Last summer, 62% of the Mediterranean surface experienced a marine heatwave classified as "strong or higher" — the worst ever recorded in the basin. A buoy near Mallorca hit 31°C. Mean sea surface temperature in July reached 26.68°C, roughly 3°C above the historic average. The consequences are arriving now: mussel production collapsed in Italy's Adriatic lagoons (75% losses in Scardovari, 100% in Canarin after the 2023 heatwave — 2025 was worse), Vibrio bacteria populations exploded in warmer coastal waters, and provisioning reliable Mediterranean shellfish this spring means understanding which waters are safe and which are stressed.

This connects to the science of bivalve purging — a technique that becomes critical when pathogen loads rise with water temperature — a Neapolitan mussel tradition born from exactly this kind of coastal resourcefulness, and a Bangkok restaurant where German twins just earned three Michelin stars by making the unfamiliar feel inevitable.

One temperature ties the thread: 15°C. Above it, Vibrio replication accelerates. Below it, bivalves are safer, slower, and sweeter. The Atlantic is colder. Source accordingly.

## SHORE LARDER — Mussels

*Mytilus galloprovincialis (Mediterranean) · Mytilus edulis (Atlantic) · Peak: October–March*

**75%.** That's how much Italian mussel production dropped in the Scardovari lagoon after the 2023 Mediterranean heatwave. The 2025 heatwave was worse — mean SST anomalies of 5–6°C above average off French and Spanish coasts. If you're sourcing Mediterranean mussels this spring, know what you're buying.

The issue isn't the mussel itself — it's the water it grew in. Filter-feeding bivalves accumulate whatever's in the water column: nutrients, flavour compounds, microplastics, and pathogens. When water temperatures exceed 15°C, Vibrio parahaemolyticus and Vibrio vulnificus populations spike. Mediterranean coastal waters stayed above 15°C through most of the 2025 winter in the eastern basin.

| Compound | Per 100 g Raw | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Protein | 11.9 g | Complete amino acid profile; high glycine (sweet note) |
| Omega-3 (EPA+DHA) | 0.5–0.8 g | Varies by season; winter mussels have higher fat |
| Iron | 3.95 mg | ~22% DV; one of the best shellfish sources |
| Selenium | 44.8 µg | ~81% DV |
| Zinc | 1.6 mg | Lower than oysters but significant |

**Sourcing this spring:** Galicia (Rías Baixas — *mejillones de batea*, rope-grown on floating platforms), Brittany (Baie du Mont-Saint-Michel AOP), Ireland (Bantry Bay, Cromane), and Scotland (Shetland, Loch Fyne) are your reliable Atlantic sources. Colder waters, lower pathogen risk, meatier product. Mediterranean sourcing — Taranto, Chioggia, Ebro Delta — requires verification of recent water temperature data and depuration certificates. Ask your supplier. If they can't tell you the water temperature at harvest, find one who can.

## THE METHOD — Bivalve Purging and Depuration

**The Move:** Purge mussels and clams in cold, salted water before service — not to clean the outside, but to flush sand, grit, and residual gut contents from the digestive tract. In a season of elevated water temperatures and pathogen loads, this step is non-negotiable.

### Standard Galley Purge

1. Discard any mussels that are open and don't close when tapped. Discard any with cracked shells.
2. Place live mussels in a large non-reactive container. Cover with cold water (8–12°C, not tap temperature). Add 35 g sea salt per litre — approximate seawater salinity.
3. Optional: add 1 tablespoon cornflour per 2 litres. The mussels ingest it and expel grit as they filter.
4. Refrigerate at 4–5°C for 1–2 hours. No longer — extended immersion in still water depletes oxygen and kills the mussels.
5. Lift mussels out (don't pour — the grit stays at the bottom). Debeard by pulling the byssus threads firmly toward the hinge. Rinse under cold running water.

### Why Cold Matters

Vibrio parahaemolyticus doubles every 9 minutes at 37°C and every 20–30 minutes at 20°C. At 4°C, replication is effectively halted. Every degree you keep the product below 15°C during handling reduces pathogen load exponentially. This is why the first thing you do when mussels arrive on board is get them into the coldest part of the cold room — not the countertop.

| Factor | What Happens | Adjust By |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Water too warm (>15°C) | Vibrio replication accelerates; mussels stressed | Use refrigerated water or add ice to bring to 8–12°C |
| Purge too long (>4 hours) | Oxygen depletion kills mussels; dead mussels open | Maximum 2 hours; check for closures |
| No salt in purge water | Mussels close up; no filtering, no purging | 35 g/L sea salt matches seawater |

**You know it worked when:** The bowl has visible sand and grit at the bottom. Mussels are tightly closed or close when tapped. No mud smell — only clean brine.

## THE EVIDENCE — Mediterranean Marine Heatwave 2025

Copernicus Marine Service / Mercator Ocean International, July 2025. Supported by: Nature Scientific Reports, 2026 (DOI: 10.1038/s41598-026-35813-x)

The 2025 Mediterranean marine heatwave broke every record in the observational dataset.

| Metric | Data | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| June 2025 mean SST | 23.86°C | Warmest June ever recorded in the Mediterranean |
| June 29 peak | 26.01°C (mean) | ~3°C above historic average for that date |
| July peak | 26.68°C (mean) | Western Med peaked at 30°C off Spain |
| Mallorca buoy | 31°C | Highest single-point reading |
| Coverage | 62% of surface | Strong-or-higher category marine heatwave — worst ever |
| Previous impact (2023) | 75% mussel loss in Scardovari | 100% loss in Canarin lagoon |

**What this means in the galley:** Mediterranean shellfish — mussels, clams, oysters — are under thermal stress. Warmer water promotes Vibrio bacteria, alters growth rates, and affects flavour (stressed bivalves taste muddy, metallic, or thin). Farmed seabass and seabream from Greek and Turkish operations may show slower growth and smaller grading. Sea urchin roe from warmer waters may be less full and less flavourful.

**What this does NOT mean:** That Mediterranean seafood is unsafe. Depuration systems, cold chain management, and EU shellfish hygiene regulations (Regulation 853/2004) exist precisely for this. It means your margin for error in handling is smaller, and your sourcing decisions matter more.

**Your move:** For premium shellfish service this spring, source Atlantic. For Mediterranean finfish (seabass, seabream, red mullet), verify origin waters and cold chain. Microplastics are a compounding factor — the Mediterranean holds 1% of global ocean water but 7% of global microplastics. Red mullet (*Mullus surmuletus*) consistently shows the highest microplastic contamination in studies.

## LITTORAL HERITAGE — Impepata di Cozze

*Naples, Campania*

"Impepata di cozze" — peppered mussels. The dish exists because mussels grew wild on every piling, rock, and sea wall in the Bay of Naples, and pepper was the one spice that never left the Neapolitan pantry after the spice trade routes established it as a commodity. This is not a recipe that was invented. It accumulated — the simplest possible answer to the question of what to do with free protein and one spice.

The preparation: mussels in a wide pan, lid on, high heat. No water, no wine, no oil. The mussels open in their own liquor — seawater trapped in the shells provides all the liquid. Three minutes, lid off. Generous black pepper — coarse, cracked, not ground. A squeeze of lemon. Bread on the side. Nothing else.

The liquor is the point. Neapolitan grandmothers call it *l'acqua delle cozze* — mussel water — and it's served in the bowl with the mussels, drunk directly or soaked into bread. It is, chemically, a natural broth: glycine and glutamic acid from the mussel proteins, sodium chloride from the seawater, and piperine from the pepper stimulating saliva production and amplifying perceived flavour.

| Element | Traditional | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| No added liquid | Mussels cook in trapped seawater | Concentrated flavour; adding wine dilutes |
| No oil | Served in their own broth | Fat would coat the tongue and mute the brine |
| Coarse black pepper | Cracked, not ground | Piperine release is slower; heat builds gradually |
| Bread | Dense, unsalted Neapolitan | Absorbs the liquor without competing |

**On board:** Transit the Amalfi Coast or Bay of Naples. Source mussels from Taranto or — better this year — from Galicia or Brittany. The dish takes 5 minutes. Serve communal, directly from the pan, bread torn by hand. Tell the guests this is how mussels have been eaten in Naples for centuries: no chef, no recipe, just heat and pepper and the sea.

**Pairing:** Falanghina dei Campi Flegrei DOC — volcanic, mineral, saline. Same bay.

## THE HORIZON — Sühring Earns Three Michelin Stars in Bangkok

*November 27, 2025 · Bangkok, Thailand*

Twin brothers Thomas and Mathias Sühring earned three Michelin stars in the Michelin Guide Thailand 2026 — making Sühring Thailand's second three-star restaurant and the first three-star German restaurant in Asia. The twins trained under Sven Elverfeld at Aqua (Wolfsburg), Jonnie Boer (Netherlands), and Heinz Beck (Rome) before opening their restaurant in a restored midcentury villa in Bangkok's Yen Akat neighbourhood.

The menu is modern German — fermenting, pickling, curing — built from seasonal ingredients and family recipes, served in a space that feels more like someone's home than a fine dining room. First starred in the inaugural 2018 Thailand guide, promoted to two stars in 2019, held that distinction for seven consecutive years.

What matters: this is German cooking earning the highest honour in Southeast Asia. The technical repertoire — fermentation, curing, preservation — is the same skill set yacht chefs deploy daily. The Sühring brothers prove that mastering a classical tradition and transplanting it honestly to a foreign context works. No fusion. No compromise. Just technique applied with precision in a place nobody expected it.

**Source:** Michelin Guide Thailand 2026, November 27, 2025.

## TRADE WINDS — EU Agrees 2026 Fishing Quotas: Atlantic Cuts Dominate

*December 13, 2025 · Brussels*

EU Fisheries Ministers agreed on 2026 fishing quotas after intense negotiations. The headline: significant cuts across key Atlantic species that yacht chefs provision regularly.

| Species | Region | 2026 Change | Impact |
|---------|--------|-------------|--------|
| Common sole | Kattegat | **-45%** | Severe — Dover sole prices will rise |
| Common sole | Skagerrak-Kattegat | **-44%** | Same fishery, same pressure |
| Common sole | Bay of Biscay | **-1%** | Stable |
| North Sea cod | North Sea | Sharp cuts (exact % varies by zone) | Cod supply tightens further |
| Norway lobster | South Bay of Biscay | **+54%** | Bright spot — langoustine supply improves |
| Norway lobster | Portuguese waters | **-23%** | Iberian langoustine more expensive |
| Monkfish | Portuguese/Azores | **-1%** | Marginal |

Separately, ICCAT set the Eastern Atlantic and Mediterranean bluefin tuna TAC at **48,403 tonnes** for 2026–2028 — a **19.3% increase** over the previous period. The EU secured 25,164 tonnes per year. Modestly improved supply for fresh bluefin in Mediterranean markets (peak catch: May–July).

**Your move:** Dover sole (common sole) prices will increase substantially in 2026 — the 45% Kattegat cut is the story. If sole is a charter menu staple, build in the price increase now or propose alternatives (turbot, John Dory, halibut). Langoustine from the southern Bay of Biscay is the one bright spot — the 54% quota increase means better availability and potentially stable prices. Bluefin: source ICCAT-compliant, MSC-certified where available.

**Source:** EU Fisheries Council, December 13, 2025; ICCAT Annual Meeting, Seville, November 24, 2025.
