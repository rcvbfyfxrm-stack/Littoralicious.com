# Sourdough at Sea: Your Starter Is Not Precious

**The Move:** Stop babying your starter. A healthy culture tolerates neglect, temperature swings, and 48-hour fridge holds. The yacht chef who treats sourdough as a system -- not a pet -- bakes consistent bread across hemispheres, climates, and provisioning gaps.

**Do This:**

**Maintaining the Starter**

1. Feed once daily at a 1:5:5 ratio (starter:flour:water) by weight when baking regularly. Use 10 g mature starter, 50 g flour (a blend of 40 g bread flour at 12%+ protein and 10 g whole wheat or rye), 50 g water at 30-32C. Mix until no dry flour remains. Cover loosely. At 24-26C ambient, peak activity occurs in 8-12 hours.
2. When not baking, feed the same ratio and refrigerate at 3-5C immediately after mixing. The starter holds for 5-7 days without feeding. Pull it out 12 hours before you need it, feed once at room temperature, and it is ready.
3. In hot climates (galley ambient above 30C, common in the Caribbean and Gulf), reduce the starter portion to 1:10:10 or use cold water at 18-20C. High temperatures accelerate fermentation and produce excess acetic acid -- the bread turns sour, the crumb tightens, and the dough over-proofs before you finish service. Slowing the culture down is the single most important adjustment for tropical baking.
4. In cold climates or air-conditioned galleys below 20C, increase the starter portion to 1:3:3 or find a warm spot. The top of a running dishwasher, near the combi oven exhaust, or inside a switched-off oven with only the light on (typically 28-32C) all work. Wrap the container in a towel to buffer temperature swings.

**Dehydrating for Travel and Rotation**

5. Spread 50 g of active, freshly fed starter in a thin layer on a silicone mat or parchment. Leave uncovered in a dry environment (air-conditioned cabin works) for 24-48 hours until completely brittle. Break into flakes. Store in an airtight container or vacuum-sealed bag. These flakes remain viable for 6+ months at room temperature.
6. To reactivate: dissolve 10 g dried flakes in 30 g warm water (30C). Wait 15 minutes. Add 30 g flour. Feed at 1:5:5 every 12 hours for 2-3 days. By day 3, the culture should double in 6-8 hours. If it does not double by day 4, the flakes were too old or too wet when dried. Start again with flakes from another boat -- sourdough starters are shared between yachts in every major port. Monaco, Fort Lauderdale, and Palma marinas all have active starter-sharing networks.

**The Yacht Sourdough Formula**

This produces a 1.2 kg loaf with an open crumb and moderate tang. Tested across 3 seasons in Mediterranean and Caribbean conditions.

| Component | Weight | Baker's % |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Bread flour (12-13% protein; Caputo Tipo 1 or equivalent) | 450 g | 90% |
| Whole wheat flour | 50 g | 10% |
| Water (30C in tropics, 35C in cold galleys) | 375 g | 75% |
| Mature starter (at peak, doming, passing float test) | 100 g | 20% |
| Fine sea salt | 10 g | 2% |

**Method:**
1. Autolyse: mix flours and water. Rest 30-60 minutes. This hydrates the gluten network before salt and acid from the starter begin tightening it.
2. Add starter and salt. Mix by hand using a pinching and folding motion until fully incorporated, approximately 3-4 minutes.
3. Bulk fermentation at 24-26C for 4-5 hours. Perform 3 sets of stretch-and-folds at 30-minute intervals during the first 90 minutes (6 folds per set, pulling from the bottom of the container up and over). After the folds, leave untouched. The dough is ready when it has increased 50-75% in volume, feels aerated, and jiggles when the container is shaken.
4. Pre-shape into a round on an unfloured surface. Rest 20 minutes. Final shape into a batard or boule, building surface tension with a bench scraper. Place seam-side up in a floured banneton or a towel-lined bowl dusted with rice flour (rice flour does not absorb moisture and prevents sticking better than wheat flour).
5. Cold retard: cover and refrigerate at 3-5C for 12-18 hours. This is where yacht baking becomes practical -- shape after dinner service, bake before breakfast.
6. Bake directly from the fridge. Score with a lame or razor blade (one decisive slash at 45 degrees, 1 cm deep). Bake in a preheated Dutch oven or combi oven with steam: 250C (482F) with steam for 20 minutes, then remove lid or vent steam and reduce to 220C (428F) for 20-25 minutes. Internal temperature at the centre should reach 96-98C. If using a Rational, programme Stage 1 at 250C/100% humidity/20 min, Stage 2 at 220C/0% humidity/22 min.

**Why It Works:**
Sourdough fermentation is driven by a symbiotic culture of wild yeast (*Saccharomyces* species, primarily *S. cerevisiae* and *S. exiguus*) and lactic acid bacteria (mainly *Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis*). The LAB produce lactic and acetic acid, which lower the dough pH to 3.5-4.0, inhibiting spoilage organisms and extending shelf life -- critical on a vessel where bread is baked every 2-3 days, not daily. The cold retard shifts the bacterial balance toward lactic acid production (milder, more complex flavour) and away from acetic acid (sharp, vinegary). The autolyse allows glutenin and gliadin to hydrate fully before the acid environment begins denaturing gluten proteins, producing a more extensible dough with better oven spring.

**The Variables:**

| Factor | What Happens | Adjust By |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| Galley temperature above 30C | Bulk fermentation completes in 3 hours instead of 5. Over-proofed dough collapses, producing a dense, gummy crumb with large irregular holes at the top. | Reduce starter to 15% (75 g). Use cold water. Shorten bulk to 3-3.5 hours. Watch the dough, not the clock. |
| Low-protein flour (below 11%) | Weak gluten network. Dough spreads flat, poor oven spring. | Add 1-2% vital wheat gluten (5-10 g per 500 g flour). Source: health food stores in most ports carry it. |
| Rough seas during bulk fermentation | Constant motion acts as continuous folding -- over-development risk if the dough is also being manually folded. | Skip the manual stretch-and-folds. The vessel's motion does the work. Reduce bulk time by 30-60 minutes. |
| Altitude (high-altitude ports, e.g., inland provisioning stops) | Lower atmospheric pressure accelerates gas expansion. Dough over-proofs faster. | Reduce starter to 15%. Shorten bulk. Increase oven temperature by 10C and reduce bake time by 3-5 minutes. |

**You Know It Worked When:**
The crust is deep mahogany (not pale gold -- pale means under-baked or insufficient steam in stage 1). The internal temperature reads 96-98C. When you tap the bottom, it sounds hollow. The crumb, when sliced after cooling for at least 2 hours (do not cut hot bread -- steam is still setting the crumb structure), shows an even distribution of holes ranging from 3-8 mm with no dense band at the bottom. The bread stays fresh in a paper bag or bread box for 3 days at room temperature. It toasts well on day 4.

**At Sea:**
The cold retard is the yacht baker's best tool. Shape the dough at 22:00 after evening service. Refrigerate overnight. Score and bake at 06:00 -- fresh bread for breakfast with zero morning prep beyond loading the oven. The starter lives in the fridge between bakes. Feed it the night before bake day. This system survives 5:2 rotation because the incoming chef can maintain the same schedule, and a dehydrated backup in the dry store means a dead starter is a 3-day inconvenience, not a disaster.
