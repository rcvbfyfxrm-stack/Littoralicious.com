# Sole Chef on a 50-Metre: The Workload Nobody Talks About

*The data on why chefs leave. The systems used by those who stay.*

Across two major yacht chef WhatsApp groups — combined 1,300+ members, approximately 24,000 messages analysed from July 2025 to January 2026 — the single most emotionally charged topic is not provisioning, not equipment, not salary. It is the unsustainable workload imposed on sole chefs aboard yachts in the 40-50 metre range.

Captains complain they cannot retain chefs. Chefs explain exactly why. A poll asked members whether they would join a boat with a known problematic galley and demanding sole chef role. The result: **50 voted "No I wouldn't join that boat." 0 voted "Yes."**

Fifty to zero.

This article has two parts. The first documents the reality — data, numbers, working conditions — because you cannot fix what you do not name. The second provides the operational systems used by sole chefs who have survived and even thrived in the role. Not because the conditions are acceptable, but because if you are in the middle of a charter season, you need tools that work today.

---

## PART ONE: THE REALITY

### What the Numbers Look Like

A sole chef on a 50m yacht during charter typically feeds 12 guests and 10-14 crew. Three meals per day for guests (breakfast, lunch, dinner), with canapes, afternoon tea, and late-night snacks often added. Three meals per day for crew. On a single charter day, a sole chef produces 50-70 individual plates across 6-8 service windows, plus prep for the following day.

That is a small restaurant's output. With no sous chef, no commis, no kitchen porter.

### What 18 Hours Looks Like

16-18 hour days are consistently documented. Here is the typical charter day:

| Time | Activity | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| 05:00 | Wake, crew breakfast prep | — |
| 06:30 | Crew breakfast service | — |
| 07:00 | Guest breakfast mise en place | Varies by guest wake times |
| 08:00-10:00 | Guest breakfast service | Cooked to order, staggered |
| 10:00-11:30 | Clean down, lunch prep | — |
| 11:30 | Crew lunch service | — |
| 12:00-14:00 | Guest lunch service | — |
| 14:00-15:00 | Clean down, afternoon tea prep | — |
| 15:00-16:00 | Afternoon service, begin dinner prep | — |
| 16:00-17:30 | Dinner mise en place | — |
| 17:30 | Crew dinner service | — |
| 18:30 | Canape service | — |
| 19:30-22:00 | Guest dinner service (3-5 courses) | — |
| 22:00-23:00 | Clean down, next-day prep, late-night snacks | — |

That is 18 hours. Every day of a charter.

### The Falsification Problem

The Maritime Labour Convention (MLC) requires a minimum of 10 hours of rest in any 24-hour period, with at least 6 consecutive hours. The schedule above provides 5-6 hours.

"The whole faking hours of work and rest needs to stop." — quoted directly from the groups.

Compliance is achieved on paper through falsified rest logs. This is widespread, openly discussed, and universally understood by captains, management companies, and crew. It is not an allegation. It is a structural reality described by the people living it.

### The Capacity Data

Working chefs have shared their operational limits with unusual candour:

| Chef | Scope | Key Insight |
|------|-------|------------|
| **Chef A** | 12 guests + 4-5 crew | Note the crew number: 4-5, not 10-14. Guest count is manageable. Crew numbers are the compounding factor. |
| **Chef B** | 51 charters as sole freelance, 10 crew + 12 guests | 51 charters is a significant dataset. Freelance — can walk away after each charter. Permanent sole chefs cannot. |
| **Chef C** | 12 guests + 18 crew, sole | "Depends on appliances and fridge/freezer space." The galley determines what is possible more than stamina. |
| **Chef D** | (from group) | "How am I meant to produce Michelin grade dishes while also cooking breakfast lunch and dinner for 12 crew?" |

The answer is: you are not. But the expectation persists.

### The Galley Multiplier

The workload conversation cannot be separated from the galley. On yachts under 50m, the galley is frequently an afterthought — whatever space remains after guest cabins, crew quarters, engine room, and entertainment areas.

One chef described it bluntly: "One domestic sized oven, fridge and freezer — all half the size of ones in my home. Lack of storage and prep area can be an absolute joke."

Another noted: "Galley size and quality is not discussed enough."

From the groups, recurring: "The beating heart of a boat is the galley — why are they so shit on below 50m?"

A sole chef working 18-hour days in an under-equipped galley is not just fatigued — they are physically constrained. A single domestic oven cannot produce a 5-course dinner for 12 while simultaneously preparing crew meals. The maths does not work, regardless of the chef's talent.

### The Rotation Double Standard

Captains and officers work rotational schedules — 6:6, 8:6, sometimes 10:10. The same boats frequently employ a sole chef with no rotation. The chef is on board for the season, or on a 10-month contract with 2 months leave.

One widely-shared post — "5:1 is NOT rotation" — received universal agreement. Being on board for 5 months with 1 month off is not a rotational schedule. It is a permanent position with leave.

Captains against rotation for chefs but having it for themselves. The structural unfairness is not lost on the community.

## PART TWO: THE SURVIVAL SYSTEMS

You cannot fix the structural problem from inside the galley during charter. What you can do is build systems that convert a chaotic 18-hour day into a structured one — reducing cognitive load, eliminating redundant work, and creating pockets of rest that would not exist otherwise.

These are the protocols used by sole chefs who have sustained the role across multiple seasons. They are not aspirational. They are operational.

### System 1: The Three-Block Day

Stop thinking in 8 service windows. Think in three blocks. Each block has a defined output, a defined cleanup, and a defined transition.

| Block | Time | Output | Ends With |
|-------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Morning** | 05:00-11:00 | Crew breakfast, guest breakfast, lunch prep | Galley clean, lunch mise complete |
| **Midday** | 11:00-17:00 | Crew lunch, guest lunch, afternoon service, dinner prep | Galley clean, dinner mise complete |
| **Evening** | 17:00-23:00 | Crew dinner, canapes, guest dinner, next-day prep | Galley clean, next morning's dry mise staged |

Each block is 6 hours. Each block ends with a clean galley and the next block's mise in place. The discipline is in the transitions: when the morning block ends, the galley is reset. You do not carry mess from one block into the next. This seems like extra work. It is not. The 3-minute wipe-and-reset between blocks eliminates the 15-minute panic when you cannot find a clean surface at 17:30.

> **Why this works:** Cognitive load research (Sweller, 1988) shows that working memory degrades when tasks are not segmented. An 18-hour unstructured day creates decision fatigue by hour 10. Three defined blocks with clear start-stop boundaries reduce the cognitive demand of each segment. You are not working 18 hours. You are working three 6-hour shifts.

### System 2: The Monday Batch

Every Monday — or the first day of each charter — invest 3 hours in batch production that eliminates daily scramble for the rest of the week.

| Component | Quantity | Time | Covers |
|-----------|----------|------|--------|
| **Stock** (chicken or vegetable) | 10L | 30 min active (simmer unattended) | Sauces, risottos, soups all week |
| **Grain/starch** (rice pilaf, roasted potatoes, couscous) | 5 kg | 20 min | Crew lunches and dinners, 5 days |
| **Sauce base** (bolognese, curry, tomato sugo) | 3L | 30 min | Crew meals, staff meals, pasta service |
| **Vegetable prep** (roasted roots, blanched greens, pickled onions) | 3 kg | 25 min | Crew and guest garnish, salads |
| **Protein prep** (marinated chicken thighs, portioned fish, braised crew protein) | 5 kg | 30 min | Crew protein + guest mise backups |
| **Dressings and vinaigrettes** | 1.5L total | 15 min | Crew salads, guest salads, all week |

**Total active time: ~2.5 hours.** Much of it runs concurrently (stock simmers while you prep veg).

**What this buys you:** Crew lunch goes from 30-45 minutes of reactive cooking to 10-15 minutes of assembly. Crew dinner: same. That is 40-60 minutes saved per day, every day, for the rest of the charter. Over a 7-day charter, the Monday batch saves 4-7 hours of cumulative time.

Those saved hours are not theoretical. They are the difference between starting dinner prep at 16:00 with your mise done and starting at 16:00 from scratch.

### System 3: The Crew Meal Machine

Crew meals are the workload multiplier. Not because they are difficult — because they are unstructured. Every meal becomes a negotiation.

**The protocol (detailed in the separate Crew Meal Diplomacy article):**

1. **Set dietary terms at embarkation.** Allergies: accommodated, always. Medical conditions: accommodated. TikTok diets adopted last Tuesday: not accommodated. State this clearly, once, at the start.

2. **Cook one meal with built-in flexibility.** Base protein + starch + vegetable. Salad bar or grain bowl station crew can customise. The vegan gets the vegetables, the rice, and a batch-prepped plant protein you made on Monday. The ketchup crew member gets ketchup. Ego is the enemy here.

3. **Use the Monday batch.** Monday's 2.5 hours of prep converts every crew meal from a 30-minute cook to a 10-minute assembly. Reheat the grain. Portion the protein. Dress the salad. Done.

4. **Escalate, do not absorb.** When a crew member requests bespoke daily cooking (keto bread, full carnivore, raw food), respond to the captain: "This requires X additional hours per week. I can accommodate it if Y is adjusted." Let management decide. That is their job.

| Metric | Without system | With system |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Daily crew meal prep time | 30-45 min per meal | 10-15 min per meal |
| Dietary complaints per week | 3-5 | 0-1 |
| Chef decision fatigue from crew requests | High (every meal is a negotiation) | Low (system handles 90% of variation) |

### System 4: The Prep Cascade

Prep for tomorrow during today's downtime, not at the end of the day when you are destroyed.

| Today's Window | Tomorrow's Prep |
|----------------|-----------------|
| **After guest breakfast** (10:00-10:30) | Portion tomorrow's fish. Marinate tomorrow's crew protein. |
| **After guest lunch** (14:00-14:30) | Prep tomorrow's canape bases. Roll tomorrow's pasta if applicable. |
| **After afternoon service** (15:30-16:00) | Prep tomorrow's dessert components (set panna cotta, churn ice cream, bake tart shells). |
| **During dinner service waits** (between courses) | Prep next-day breakfast mise (portion smoked salmon, prep hollandaise base, cut fruit). |

The principle: never prep tomorrow's food after 21:00. By the time dinner service ends, tomorrow's mise should be 70-80% complete. The 22:00-23:00 slot becomes cleanup and staging only — not a second full prep session on top of a 17-hour day.

> **Why this works:** The prep cascade exploits the natural dead time between services. These 15-30 minute windows exist in every charter day but are typically wasted on phone-checking, chatting, or standing still because the next task is not defined. Assigning each window a specific next-day prep task converts waste time into production time without adding a single minute to the working day.

### System 5: The Freezer Is Your Sous Chef

If you have no sous chef, your freezer is the closest substitute. Use it as a labour bank — deposit time on slow days, withdraw it on busy days.

**What to freeze in advance (before charter, or on travel/setup days):**

| Item | Freeze method | Shelf life | Deploys as |
|------|--------------|------------|------------|
| Soups (3-4 varieties) | Portion into 500ml containers | 3 months | Crew lunch. Heat and serve. 5 minutes. |
| Sauces (bolognese, curry, ragu) | Flat-freeze in bags, 500ml portions | 3 months | Crew dinner base. Defrost and reheat. |
| Braised meats (short rib, lamb shank, oxtail) | Vacuum-sealed in cooking liquid | 3 months | Guest dinner main. Reheat from frozen in 45 min. |
| Bread dough (portioned) | Wrap in cling film, bag | 1 month | Proof from frozen (2h room temp). Fresh bread daily without daily mixing. |
| Cookie and cake batters | Scooped or portioned | 2 months | Bake from frozen. Fresh dessert, zero day-of prep. |
| Pie and tart shells (blind-baked) | Stack with parchment between | 2 months | Fill and finish. 15 min to a plated dessert. |
| Canape bases (blinis, crackers, pastry cups) | Flat-freeze on trays, then bag | 1 month | Defrost 30 min before service. Top and go. |

**Before every charter:** Spend one full day building a freezer arsenal. 8 hours of pre-charter batch cooking replaces 2-3 hours of daily scramble across a 7-day charter. That is 14-21 hours recovered, redistributed from charter days (when you are drowning) to a prep day (when you are not).

### System 6: The Steward Handoff

You cannot do everything alone. You should not. The stew team has hands, and some of what currently lands on your plate belongs on theirs.

**What a trained stew can take off your hands:**

| Task | Time saved per day | Training required |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Breakfast mise assembly (fruit, yogurt, granola, OJ, coffee setup) | 20-30 min | 15 min one-time walkthrough |
| Plating crew meals from your assembled components | 10-15 min | Show once, they repeat |
| Afternoon tea assembly (from your pre-made components) | 15-20 min | Photograph the setup, they replicate |
| Late-night snack station setup | 10-15 min | Pre-prep the station, they deploy |
| Running dishwasher between services | 10-15 min per cycle | Zero training needed |

**Total potential daily saving: 65-95 minutes.**

> **The catch:** This requires captain and chief stew buy-in. Some stew teams are willing. Some are not. Some chief stews will fight it. Do not make it a battle — present it to the captain as a workload redistribution request: "If the stew team handles breakfast assembly and dishwasher runs, I gain 45 minutes of prep time that directly improves guest dinner quality." Frame it as a quality argument, not a fairness argument. Captains respond to quality.

### System 7: The Menu Architecture

Do not design menus in isolation. Design them as a system where every guest dish generates crew meal components.

| Guest dinner (Day 2) | Crew lunch (Day 3) |
|----------------------|-------------------|
| Seared duck breast, cherry reduction, celeriac puree | Pulled duck leg (from carcass), cherry vinaigrette salad, celeriac gratin (from puree trim) |
| Pan-roasted sea bass, beurre blanc, asparagus | Fish cakes (from bass trim and offcuts), asparagus and grain salad |
| Chocolate fondant, salted caramel, coffee crumb | Chocolate brownie (from excess fondant batter), caramel popcorn (crew snack from excess caramel) |

**The principle:** Nothing goes in the bin that can go on a crew plate tomorrow. Plan this at the menu design stage, not after service when you are staring at trim.

Over a 7-day charter, this cross-utilisation reduces crew-specific prep by approximately 30% and food waste by 15-20%. That is time and money — both of which you need.

## PART THREE: WHAT TO NEGOTIATE

If you are considering a sole chef position, or renegotiating an existing one, these are the non-negotiables that experienced sole chefs — the ones who survived — say matter most.

### Before You Accept

| Question | Why It Matters | Minimum Acceptable |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| What oven? | A domestic oven cannot produce charter-level output. | Commercial combi (Rational iCombi Pro or equivalent) |
| Fridge/freezer capacity? | Ask in litres, not "how many fridges." | 800L+ fridge, 500L+ freezer for 12+12 |
| Crew count? | Crew meals are the multiplier. | Know the exact number. Factor it into your decision. |
| Stew support in galley? | Breakfast assembly, dishwasher, afternoon tea. | Written agreement on specific tasks. |
| Rotation? | If the captain rotates, you rotate. | 2:2 or 3:3. "5:1" is not rotation. |
| Day rate for extra charters? | Overtime must be compensated. | Agreed per-charter supplement in contract. |

### The Walk-Away Test

If a boat offers:
- Domestic appliances, single fridge, no freezer capacity
- 12+ crew with no stew galley support
- No rotation for the chef while officers rotate
- "We need Michelin quality" with a Mercadona-grade galley

Walk. The retention crisis is your leverage. Good yacht chefs are in demand. Positions that destroy their people do not deserve to be filled.

## The Structural Problem

This article provides survival systems. It does not pretend the conditions are acceptable.

The sole chef role on sub-50m yachts has crossed from demanding into unsustainable for many positions. The retention crisis is not a recruitment problem, not a skills gap, not a generational attitude issue. It is the predictable consequence of asking one person to do a job that requires two, in a space designed for zero, for 18 hours a day, with falsified rest logs and no rotation.

504 meals per week. One chef. Do the division.

The industry will not fix this from the top. It will fix it when enough qualified chefs refuse to accept the conditions that make the role untenable. Until then, use the systems. Protect your time. Negotiate from strength. And know that the 50-to-zero poll result means you are not alone in recognising what this job actually costs.

## Your Move

If you are a sole chef mid-charter: implement the three-block day and the Monday batch this week. They cost nothing and return hours.

If you are between positions: negotiate galley specifications, crew count, stew support, and rotation before you sign. The walk-away test is not arrogance. It is self-preservation.

If you are a captain reading this: the galley is not a lesser department. The work is not less demanding. The chef who feeds your guests and your crew — to the standard you demand — deserves the same structural support you give your bridge team. Rotation, proper equipment, and honest rest logs are not chef luxuries. They are the minimum conditions for sustainable performance.

The chef at 11pm, 17 hours into a charter day, cleaning down a galley alone — that chef is the reason your guests come back. Act accordingly.
