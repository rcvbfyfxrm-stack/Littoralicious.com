# Provisioning Agents Compared: Mediterranean vs Caribbean

*When to use one, when to do it yourself, and the maths that should drive the decision.*

You have two choices every time you provision: do it yourself, or pay someone to do it for you. The answer is not always the same. It depends on the port, the timeline, and whether the markup buys you time or just costs you money.

This is the honest comparison.

---

## The Economics

A provisioning agent charges a markup. That markup pays for sourcing, logistics, quality control, delivery, and the relationships the agent has built with suppliers over years. Whether that markup is worth paying depends on exactly one calculation:

**What is your time worth vs what does the agent cost?**

| Scenario | Provision Value | Agent Markup | Agent Cost | Self-Source Time | Effective Hourly Rate |
|----------|----------------|-------------|------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Small top-up | EUR 2,000 | 20% | EUR 400 | 3h | EUR 133/h |
| Mid charter | EUR 8,000 | 18% | EUR 1,440 | 6-8h | EUR 180-240/h |
| Full load | EUR 15,000 | 15-20% | EUR 2,250-3,000 | 8-12h | EUR 190-375/h |

If you have 8 hours free and the agent markup is EUR 2,500, self-sourcing pays you EUR 312/h of your time. If you dock at 16:00 and charter starts at 10:00 tomorrow with a full guest brief to plan, the agent earns every cent.

**The real question is not "can I save money?" — it is "what else could I do with that time?"**

---

## Mediterranean Agents

The Med has a mature provisioning infrastructure. Competition between agents keeps markups lower and quality higher. Most agents operate across multiple ports in a corridor (Balearics, Cote d'Azur, Italian Riviera) rather than single-port.

### The Top Tier

**Gourmet Deliveries**
- **Ports:** Palma, Antibes, Cannes, Nice, and across the Cote d'Azur
- **What they do:** Full provision sourcing and delivery. Broad catalogue. They know yacht timelines and accommodate last-minute changes.
- **Reputation:** Most-voted supplier in a recent yacht chef community poll (16 votes for Palma alone). Consistently recommended across both major WhatsApp groups.
- **Markup:** In line with market (estimated 15-20%). Competitive on staples.
- **Best for:** Full charter provisions where you want one point of contact handling everything. Strong when your itinerary spans Balearics to French Riviera — same supplier, same account, no re-establishing relationships.
- **Contact method:** Direct order. Delivery to STP and Club de Mar (Palma), marina delivery on Cote d'Azur.

**We Supply Yachts**
- **Ports:** Palma (primary), expanding
- **What they do:** Full provision with good range. Competitive pricing on standard items.
- **Reputation:** Second most-voted in the same poll (10 votes). Reliable.
- **Markup:** Competitive. Worth getting quotes from both Gourmet Deliveries and We Supply for comparison.
- **Best for:** Palma-based provisions where price matters. Good backup if Gourmet is stretched during peak season (July-August).

**Ocean Pantry**
- **Ports:** Palma
- **What they do:** Smaller, more boutique operation. Specialty items.
- **Reputation:** 6 votes in the poll. Niche but valued.
- **Best for:** Specialty and hard-to-find items. Not your bulk provision agent — use alongside a primary supplier for the specific things they source better.

### Specialist Suppliers (Not Agents, But Act Like One for Their Category)

**Simon Jones / The Meat & Fish Society**
- **Ports:** Mallorca, Ibiza, ships to Sardinia
- **What they do:** Guest-level proteins. Wagyu, dry-aged, premium seafood.
- **Why listed here:** Multiple chefs use Simon as a single-contact solution for all proteins, the way you would use an agent. He sources, he delivers, he knows yacht timelines.
- **Best for:** Anyone provisioning proteins in the Balearics. Contact first, then fill gaps elsewhere.

**Kostas / Food Galore**
- **Ports:** Athens, ships to all Greek islands
- **What they do:** Full provision sourcing with island delivery. Place your order before departure, product waiting at your next port.
- **Why listed here:** In Greece, this single relationship solves most provisioning challenges. Functions as an agent without the typical agent markup structure.
- **Best for:** Any Greek island itinerary. The logistics of shipping provisions to Mykonos, Santorini, or smaller islands are complex — Kostas handles it.

### Med Agents to Avoid

**BFG Provisions** — Multiple reports across both WhatsApp groups of outstanding debts to yacht chefs and boats. Proceed with extreme caution. Verify with your network before placing any order.

**Yachting Spirit** — Reported 20% markup applied to the total bill (not per-item — on the entire invoice total). This is on top of already-marked-up per-item pricing. The effective markup can exceed 35-40%. Verify invoices line by line. If you use them, negotiate the billing structure in writing before the first order.

**Delicioso (Palma)** — Marketed for crew meal delivery. Multiple reports of Mercadona-grade products sold at premium prices. The markup does not buy quality — it buys packaging.

## Caribbean Agents

The Caribbean operates differently. Fewer agents, less competition, more logistical complexity. Import taxes (up to 40% in the Bahamas) add a layer of cost that does not exist in the Med. The agent's value here is often not just sourcing — it is navigating customs, managing inter-island freight, and knowing which suppliers actually have stock this week.

### The Key Players

**Epicerie De Marie / Stephan (St Maarten)**
- **Port:** SXM (primary hub)
- **What they do:** Specialty items, European imports, quality control to Med standards. Delivers to marinas.
- **Reputation:** "This guy is the best" — consensus across both chef groups. The most frequently recommended supplier in the Caribbean.
- **Best for:** Guest-level specialty provisioning in SXM. Functions as both a supplier and a curator — he knows what is available on the island and what needs to be sourced. Build this relationship on arrival in the Caribbean. It will serve you for the entire season.
- **The move:** Contact Stephan before your first SXM port call. Send your provision list in advance. He pre-sources what is available and flags what is not — so you can adjust before arrival rather than scrambling at the dock.

**LuxC'mar Yacht Services (St Maarten)**
- **Port:** SXM
- **What they do:** Full-service yacht provisioning. Logistics-heavy orders, multi-vendor coordination.
- **Best for:** When you need one company to handle the entire SXM provision — sourcing across multiple vendors, consolidating, delivering. More structured than Epicerie De Marie, less personal.

**Faye Gastronomie Caraibes / Marcel (St Lucia)**
- **Port:** St Lucia
- **What they do:** Operates a refrigerated truck. Can fly items in from other islands or Miami.
- **Reputation:** Described as a "game changer" for St Lucia by multiple chefs.
- **Best for:** Making St Lucia workable as a provisioning port. Without Marcel, St Lucia is limited to local produce and whatever the supermarkets carry. With Marcel, you can cover most charter requirements.
- **The move:** Establish contact before arrival. Lead time matters — inter-island freight is not next-day.

**Horizons Suppliers (Antigua)**
- **Port:** Antigua
- **What they do:** Yacht provisioning coordination across multiple vendors.
- **Best for:** Coordinating an Antigua provision where options are limited and scattered. They know which vendors have what, which saves you a day of driving around.

**Dion Provisioning (Bahamas — Exumas)**
- **Port:** Exumas
- **What they do:** The only reliable provisioning option in the Exumas, where grocery infrastructure is near-zero.
- **Best for:** Any boat heading to the Exumas. Contact well in advance — this is not a next-day operation.

**Megayacht Provisions (Panama)**
- **Port:** Panama City
- **What they do:** Full-service for yachts transiting the Canal or cruising the Pacific side.
- **Markup:** Expensive.
- **Best for:** When time is the constraint and you need one supplier for everything. Panama City has excellent self-sourcing options (RIBA Smith, Merca Panama), so use the agent only when your schedule demands it.

**Sea Masters (Costa Rica)**
- **Port:** Pacific coast marinas
- **What they do:** Yacht provisioning logistics for the Pacific side of Central America.
- **Best for:** Coordinating provisions in a region where infrastructure is improving but still patchy.

## The Comparison: Med vs Caribbean

| Factor | Mediterranean | Caribbean |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| **Agent availability** | Multiple agents per major port. Competition keeps quality up, prices honest. | One or two agents per island. Limited competition. |
| **Typical markup** | 15-20% on provision value | 18-25% on provision value (before import tax) |
| **What the markup buys** | Convenience, time savings, relationship access | Convenience + logistics expertise + customs navigation + inter-island freight |
| **Self-source viability** | Excellent in most ports. Metro, markets, wholesale all accessible. | Excellent in hub ports (SXM, Panama). Difficult to impossible on smaller islands. |
| **When the agent is worth it** | Peak season time crunch. Last-minute charter brief changes. Multi-port itinerary with tight turnarounds. | Almost always on small islands. Hub ports: only when time-pressed. |
| **When to self-source** | Any port where you have 6+ hours and know the suppliers | Hub ports (SXM, Panama, Nassau) when you have time. Always for local produce and markets. |
| **Import tax factor** | Minimal (EU single market for most ports) | Significant. Bahamas: ~40%. Most islands: 15-30%. Duty-free exceptions: SXM (Dutch side), some free zones. |
| **Red flag density** | Some bad actors (see above), but market corrects quickly | Fewer agents means less accountability. Verify harder. |

## The Smart Hybrid: How to Minimise Cost Without Losing Your Mind

The most efficient approach is neither full-agent nor full-self-source. It is a split.

### The 70/30 Rule

**Self-source 70%:** Dry store, general grocery, crew provisions, produce, bread, wine, ethnic/specialty. These are the categories where self-sourcing is straightforward, the savings are largest (bulk items have the highest absolute markup), and quality control requires your own eyes anyway.

**Agent the 30%:** Guest-level proteins, specialty items, anything requiring inter-island freight or customs handling, and the ultra-perishables where timing is critical and you cannot risk a supplier no-show.

### In Practice

| What You Do | What the Agent Does |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Online order to general supermarket (D-4) | Source guest proteins from specialist suppliers (D-2 delivery) |
| Wholesale run for dry store and frozen (D-3) | Handle specialty items you cannot find locally |
| Market run for produce, fish, bread (D-1) | Coordinate inter-island freight if needed |
| Ethnic shop run if needed (D-3 or D-2) | Deliver consolidated order to marina |

### The Maths on the 70/30 Split

On a EUR 12,000 provision:
- **Full agent:** EUR 12,000 + 18% = EUR 14,160. Cost: EUR 2,160. Time saved: 8-12h.
- **Full self-source:** EUR 12,000. Cost: EUR 0 markup. Time spent: 8-12h.
- **70/30 split:** EUR 8,400 self-sourced + EUR 3,600 via agent at 18% = EUR 8,400 + EUR 4,248 = EUR 12,648. Cost: EUR 648 markup. Time spent: 5-6h self-sourcing. Time saved: 3-4h on the complex stuff.

The 70/30 split costs EUR 648 instead of EUR 2,160, and frees 3-4 hours you would have spent chasing specialty items across town.

## The Bottom Line

Agents are a tool, not a luxury and not a shortcut. The good ones earn their markup by handling the logistics you should not waste your expertise on. The bad ones charge you 20% for a phone call and a Mercadona run.

Know your port. Know your timeline. Know which categories benefit from an agent's relationships and which benefit from your own eyes and judgement. Split the provision accordingly.

And keep a list of what you paid, from whom, and what the quality was like. That list, after two seasons, is worth more than any guide — including this one.
