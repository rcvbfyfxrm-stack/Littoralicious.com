## Oignon
*Allium cepa · Central Asia · Year-round*

**Sulfuric acid.** That's what an onion produces in your eyes. When the knife breaks cell walls, the enzyme alliinase converts amino acid sulfoxides into syn-propanethial-S-oxide — a volatile gas that drifts upward, dissolves in the moisture on your cornea, and forms sulfuric acid. The tears are real. The pain is real. The chemistry is warfare: a defense system evolved over millions of years to punish anything that damages the tissue. You damage it anyway, because nothing else does what an onion does.

**What It Actually Is:**
Not a root. The layers you peel are modified leaf bases — concentric sheaths of food-storage tissue wrapped around a compressed stem at the base. A biennial plant banking energy for a second-year flower stalk it will never produce in your kitchen. Each cell is packed with sugars (8-13% by weight in a sweet variety, roughly the same as a Coca-Cola) and sulfur compounds that serve double duty: pest defense when raw, flavor architecture when cooked. The onion is a chemical arsenal disguised as a pantry staple. It has been cultivated for at least 5,000 years. No cuisine on earth omits it.

**The Story That Sticks:**
In 1155 BCE, the body of Ramesses IV was prepared for eternity. The embalmers placed onions in his eye sockets. Concentric rings — no beginning, no end — symbolized eternal life in Egyptian cosmology. Small onions were tucked along the body and bound into the linen wrapping. This was not a funerary afterthought. Workers building the pyramids were paid partly in onions — alongside bread and beer, the three staples of Egyptian labor. The Ebers Papyrus (1550 BCE) prescribes onion for at least 21 ailments. The onion was not humble. It was sacred geometry you could eat.

**The Tradition:**
French onion soup. The origin: Les Halles, Paris, 18th century. Market porters arriving before dawn, bone-cold, needing fuel. Cheap onions slow-cooked in beef fat until collapsed and mahogany. Layered into bowls with stale bread, blanketed in Gruyère, run under heat until bubbling. Working-class sustenance that became a national icon served at 3 AM to everyone from dock workers to opera-goers stumbling out of the Palais Garnier.

The 45-minute caramelization is non-negotiable. What happens in that time: heat converts sulfur compounds into over 50 new flavor molecules through two distinct chemical processes happening in sequence and in parallel. There is no shortcut. The recipes that promise caramelized onions in 10 minutes are producing something else entirely — browned, perhaps. Scorched, likely. Caramelized, no.

**The Compound That Matters:**

| Compound | When Present | What It Does |
|----------|-------------|--------------|
| Syn-propanethial-S-oxide | RAW — volatile, dissipates with heat | The lachrymator. Makes you cry. Forms instantly when cells rupture. Enzymatic warfare. Gone within minutes of cooking. |
| Thiopropanal S-oxide | RAW — breaks down above 60°C | Raw onion pungency, the sharp bite. The reason a raw onion sandwich makes you wince. Heat dismantles it. |
| Furanones + pyrazines | COOKED — created by Maillard and caramelization at 130-165°C | Sweet, savory, complex. These do not exist in the raw onion. They are born from heat, sugar, and time. The entire personality shift of the cooked onion lives here. |

**Three Pairings You Haven't Tried:**
*Based on shared volatile compounds and flavor bridging:*

1. **Slow-cooked onion + aged Gruyère + thyme** — The furanones in caramelized onion share a chemical family with the nutty lactones in aged Gruyère. Thyme's thymol provides an aromatic bridge. This is why French onion soup works at a molecular level — not tradition, not comfort, chemistry.
2. **Raw red onion + blood orange + black olive** — Sicilian logic. The onion's pungent sulfur compounds are tamed by the orange's citric acid, while shared aldehyde aromatics link citrus and allium. A salad that needs nothing else.
3. **Shallot + brown butter + capers** — Shallot's subtle sulfur harmonizes with the pyrazines in browned milk solids. Capers add salt and mustard-oil pungency that pulls the combination into focus. The classic sauce for skate, and the best reason to keep shallots on board.

**The Science That Surprises:**
"Caramelize onions: 10 minutes." This instruction appears in cookbooks, food magazines, and recipe websites with astonishing frequency. Tom Scocca investigated the claim and found it is, at best, aspirational fiction. Real caramelization requires two distinct chemical processes: Maillard reactions (amino acids reacting with sugars above 130°C) producing savory depth and brown color, and true caramelization (sucrose decomposition above 160°C) producing sweetness and further browning. Both need sustained low heat, periodic stirring, and patience. Two large onions, properly caramelized: 45-60 minutes minimum.

The shortcut everyone reaches for — a pinch of baking soda to raise pH and accelerate Maillard browning — works in small quantities. A generous pinch. Beyond that, you get mushy texture and soapy off-flavors from alkaline degradation of the sugars. A pinch helps. A teaspoon ruins.

**Where Most Go Wrong:**

1. **High heat.** Charring is not caramelizing. High heat creates acrylamide and bitter Maillard products — the acrid, harsh compounds that form when sugars burn rather than transform. Low heat, patience, occasional stirring. If the onions are blackening at the edges after 10 minutes, your flame is too high. Turn it down. Walk away. Come back.
2. **Cutting without thinking.** Cut with the grain — root to stem — for slower cooking and structural integrity: French onion soup, tarts, garnishes where you want the onion to hold its shape. Cut against the grain — along the equator — for faster breakdown and softer melt: sauces, soffritto, curries. The same onion, cut two ways, behaves like two different ingredients. The knife decides the outcome before the pan does.
3. **Holding cut onion.** A sliced onion develops bitter, harsh compounds within 30 minutes through enzymatic oxidation. The raw onion you cut for a salad garnish two hours ago now tastes sulfurous and acrid, nothing like what it tasted when the knife first went through. Cut fresh. Use immediately. This is the "finish what you open" principle applied to the most basic ingredient in the kitchen.

**Sourcing:**
- **Yellow** — the workhorse. Balanced sugar and sulfur. Best for cooking, caramelizing, building sauce bases. If a recipe says "onion" without qualifying, it means yellow.
- **Red** — higher quercetin (an antioxidant flavonoid), more visual impact, better raw or quick-pickled. Acid stabilizes the anthocyanin pigments and keeps the color vivid.
- **Sweet** (Vidalia, Walla Walla, Tropea) — higher sugar (12-13%), lower pyruvate, less pungent. But they caramelize less deeply because they have less sulfur for the Maillard reactions. Better fresh, grilled, or where sweetness leads.
- **Shallots** — more complex sugar profile, subtler sulfur. The French kitchen's quiet weapon. Worth the price in any sauce, vinaigrette, or mignonette.
- Avoid: soft spots, sprouting, wet neck (bacterial rot advancing inward from the stem end).

**On Board:**
- **Storage:** Cool, dry, well-ventilated, and never next to the potatoes. Potatoes release ethylene gas, which accelerates sprouting in onions. Separate bins. Whole onions hold 2-3 months in proper ventilated dry store.
- **Shelf life:** 2-3 months whole. Once cut, use within 24 hours or discard. The oxidation is real and it is not subtle.
- **The move:** Quick-pickled red onion. Thinly slice 1 red onion, submerge in 100ml warm rice vinegar, 1 teaspoon sugar, 1 teaspoon salt. Stir. Let sit 20 minutes. The acid denatures the lachrymator compounds, the sugar balances, the vinegar preserves. Use on tacos, grilled fish, salads, sandwiches — anything needing brightness and crunch. Lasts 2 weeks refrigerated. A universal condiment. Three minutes of work.
