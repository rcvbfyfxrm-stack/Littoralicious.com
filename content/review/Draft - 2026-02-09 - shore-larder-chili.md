## Piment
*Capsicum spp. · Mesoamerica · Year-round (fresh & dried)*

**It's not a flavor.** When you bite into a habanero and your mouth catches fire, no taste is occurring. Capsaicin — the molecule responsible — binds to TRPV1 receptors, the same ion channels that detect scalding heat above 43°C. Your brain receives a pain signal, not a taste signal. The burning is real. The temperature is not. A fruit tricked your nervous system into believing it's on fire, and you came back for more. No other food in history has turned a mammalian defense mechanism into a global craving.

**What It Actually Is:**
A berry. Botanically, every chili pepper is a berry of the genus *Capsicum*, family Solanaceae — nightshades, alongside tomatoes, potatoes, and eggplant. Not a pepper. Not remotely related to black pepper (*Piper nigrum*). Columbus, arriving in the Caribbean in 1492 and finding the Taino people using a pungent spice, called them *pimientos* because he was looking for the pepper trade and saw what he wanted to see. The name stuck. Five domesticated species account for nearly all cultivated varieties: *C. annuum* (bell peppers, jalapenos, serranos), *C. chinense* (habaneros, Scotch bonnets), *C. frutescens* (tabasco, piri piri), *C. baccatum* (aji amarillo), and *C. pubescens* (rocoto). The heat lives in the placenta — the white membrane holding the seeds — not in the seeds themselves. If you've been discarding seeds to reduce heat, you've been scraping the wrong tissue.

**The Story That Sticks:**
In 1912, Wilbur Scoville, a pharmacist at Parke-Davis in Detroit, devised a test. He dissolved chili extract in sugar water and had a panel of tasters drink progressively diluted solutions until the heat disappeared. The number of dilutions became the Scoville Heat Unit. A bell pepper: 0 SHU. A jalapeno: 2,500-8,000. A habanero: 100,000-350,000. Pure capsaicin: 16,000,000. The test was subjective — human tongues, human fatigue, human variation — but it did something no instrument had done: it gave heat a number. Today, HPLC (high-performance liquid chromatography) measures capsaicinoids directly, but we still convert to Scoville units because the scale Scoville built in a Detroit lab with sugar water and volunteers became the language the world uses to talk about heat. The man gave fire a ruler.

**The Tradition:**
In Oaxaca, smoke drifts from underground pits where jalapenos dry slowly into chipotles — a preservation method that predates European contact by centuries. In Gochang county, South Korea, red peppers are split, seeded, and sun-dried on rooftops before being ground into *gochugaru* — the coarse crimson flakes without which kimchi, tteokbokki, and jjigae do not exist. Korean cuisine before the 17th century had no chili at all; Portuguese traders brought *Capsicum* to Asia, and within a century it became inseparable from Korean identity. In Hungary, paprika — the dried and ground flesh of specific *C. annuum* cultivars — is the national spice. In Sichuan, the *erjingtiao* chili is prized not for searing heat but for its fragrant, fruity warmth, used alongside the numbing *huajiao* peppercorn to create *mala* — the numb-hot sensation that defines the cuisine. In southern India, Guntur district produces nearly half of India's chili crop. Each tradition selected and bred different *Capsicum* varieties for different purposes — heat, color, smoke, sweetness — over centuries. The chili didn't arrive with a single identity. It arrived with a toolkit.

**The Compound That Matters:**

| Compound | Also Found In | What It Does |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| Capsaicin | Unique to *Capsicum* | 70% of perceived heat. Binds TRPV1 pain receptors. Fat-soluble — water won't help, dairy will (casein strips capsaicin from receptors). Triggers endorphin and dopamine release — the neurochemical basis of chili addiction. |
| Dihydrocapsaicin | Unique to *Capsicum* | 20% of perceived heat. Slower onset, longer burn, more diffuse than capsaicin. The "creeping" heat that builds after you swallow. |
| Capsanthin | Red peppers, paprika | Carotenoid pigment responsible for red color. No heat. Powerful antioxidant — paprika's deep crimson is capsanthin, not capsaicin. The reason Hungarian paprika dyes everything it touches. |

**Three Pairings You Haven't Tried:**
*Based on shared volatile compounds:*

1. **Habanero + mango** — Shared fruity esters (ethyl hexanoate, ethyl butanoate). A habanero is one of the most aromatic chilies on earth — beneath the heat lies a tropical fruit profile that mango mirrors and extends. Dice both raw into a ceviche. The molecular overlap makes the combination coherent, not contrived.
2. **Guajillo chili + dark chocolate** — Roasted pyrazines bridge dried chili's smoky depth to chocolate's bitter backbone. This is not a modern invention — the Aztec *xocolatl* included ground chili before sugar existed in the recipe. Toast dried guajillo, grind, fold into ganache at 2g per 100g chocolate. The heat whispers. The smoke lingers.
3. **Gochugaru + honey + aged cheese** — Capsaicin's fat solubility means it dissolves into the lipid matrix of cheese, distributing heat evenly instead of assaulting the palate. A 24-month comte, a drizzle of raw honey, a pinch of gochugaru flakes. Three ingredients, no cooking, and a flavor conversation that doesn't end.

**The Science That Surprises:**
Why do cuisines in the hottest climates use the most chili? The antimicrobial hypothesis, tested by Sherman and Billing (1999, *The Quarterly Review of Biology*), analyzed 4,578 recipes from 36 countries and found a significant correlation between mean annual temperature and the number of spices per recipe — with chili the single most effective antimicrobial spice tested. Capsaicin inhibits or kills up to 75% of food-borne bacteria. In the centuries before refrigeration, populations in tropical climates that flavored food with capsaicin suffered fewer foodborne illnesses and produced more surviving offspring. The preference was selected for. Chili didn't become popular in hot countries because people there "like spicy food." It became popular because it kept them alive. The taste followed the survival advantage — not the other way around.

**Where Most Go Wrong:**

1. **Chasing Scoville numbers.** The Carolina Reaper at 2.2 million SHU is a novelty, not an ingredient. Past a certain threshold, capsaicin obliterates every other compound — the fruity esters, the smoky pyrazines, the earthy terpenes that make each variety distinct. Heat without flavor is just pain. An aji amarillo at 30,000 SHU will teach you more about chili than a Reaper ever will.
2. **Cooking dried chilies without toasting.** You rehydrated that ancho in hot water and blended it straight into sauce. You missed the step that matters. Toasting dried chilies in a dry pan for 30-60 seconds — until fragrant, not smoking — triggers Maillard reactions on the skin and volatilizes aromatic compounds. The difference between a flat sauce and one with depth is 45 seconds of dry heat you skipped.
3. **Reaching for water.** Capsaicin is nonpolar and hydrophobic — water spreads it across more receptor surface area, amplifying the burn. Full-fat dairy works because casein, a lipophilic protein, surrounds capsaicin molecules and pulls them off TRPV1 receptors. Alcohol dissolves capsaicin but in practice doesn't help much — you'd need concentration levels beyond what's in wine or beer. Fat or casein. That's the exit.

**Sourcing:**
- Look for: Dried chilies that are pliable, not brittle — flexibility indicates retained oils and moisture. Strong aroma when you hold one to your nose. Uniform color without white patches (mold) or excessive dust.
- Avoid: Pre-ground chili powder with unlisted additives (many contain salt, garlic powder, cumin, and anticaking agents — you're buying a spice blend, not chili). Dried chilies that shatter like glass — they've been over-dried or sitting too long.
- Good vs. great: **Ancho** (dried poblano) — sweet, raisin-like, mild heat, the backbone of mole and adobo sauces. **Guajillo** — tangy, bright, medium heat, clean flavor for salsas. **Chipotle** (smoked jalapeno) — smoke, depth, moderate heat. **Gochugaru** (Korean) — fruity, slightly sweet, the essential flake for Korean cookery. **Piment d'Espelette** (Basque, AOP-protected) — gentle warmth, fruity, the finishing chili for European palates. Fresh: serranos for reliable clean heat, habaneros for tropical aromatics.

**On Board:**
- **Storage:** Dried whole chilies in airtight bags or jars, away from light and moisture — they'll hold 12-18 months. Gochugaru and ground chili in sealed containers in the fridge or freezer; the high surface area accelerates oxidation of capsanthin and aromatic compounds. Fresh chilies in a paper bag in the vegetable drawer — 1-2 weeks maximum.
- **Shelf life:** A dried guajillo stored properly outlasts most provisions on board. Ground chili powder fades within 3-4 months. If it doesn't smell like anything when you open the jar, it's pigment without purpose.
- **The move:** Split 4-5 dried guajillos, remove seeds, toast 45 seconds in a dry pan. Steep in 200ml warm stock for 20 minutes. Blend smooth with a clove of raw garlic, a tablespoon of honey, and a pinch of smoked salt. Strain. You now have a chili glaze that goes on grilled fish, roasted squash, or brushed over pork ribs in the last 5 minutes of cooking. Keeps 10 days refrigerated. One jar covers a charter week.
