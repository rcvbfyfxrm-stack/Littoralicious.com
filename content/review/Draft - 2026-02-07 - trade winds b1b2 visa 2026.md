# B1/B2 Visa: What Changed in 2025 and What It Means for You

*February 2026 · United States — Florida, USVI, US Territories*

**The Development:**
Effective November 1, 2025, B1/B2 visa applicants must now apply at a US Embassy or Consulate in their country of nationality — not wherever they happen to be. This single procedural change has thrown the traditional yachting visa pathway into chaos. Simultaneously, ICE enforcement in South Florida ports has escalated from sporadic to systematic. Crew are being boarded, phones checked, bank accounts opened, and social media cross-referenced against contract dates and arrival records. This is not rumour. This is documented, first-hand testimony from working crew.

**Who's Affected:**
Every non-US crew member working on yachts that operate in US waters, transit US territories, or visit the USVI. If your vessel's itinerary touches the United States at any point, this is your problem.

---

## What the Law Actually Says

A B1 visa permits a foreign crew member to work aboard a yacht in US waters under the following conditions — all of which must be true simultaneously:

1. **You hold a full-time employment contract** with a non-US company (the yacht's management company or owning entity)
2. **Your employer is registered outside the United States**
3. **You are paid by that non-US entity** — not by a US-based company, individual, or trust
4. **You must depart the US when your contract ends** — you cannot remain in the US after termination, even to look for another position
5. **The work you perform must be on the vessel itself** — you cannot perform shore-based work under a B1

A B2 visa is a tourist visa. It does not authorise any form of employment. Working on a yacht — even informally, even unpaid — on a B2 is a violation.

---

## What Changed on November 1, 2025

**Before:** A crew member could apply for or renew a B1/B2 at any US Embassy or Consulate worldwide. A South African chef working in the Mediterranean could renew in Paris, London, or Rome — wherever was logistically convenient between rotations.

**After:** You must apply at a US Embassy or Consulate in your country of nationality. That South African chef now needs to fly to Pretoria or Johannesburg. An Australian chef must apply in Canberra or a consular office in Australia. A British chef must apply in London or Belfast.

The practical impact is severe. Renewal timelines have stretched from weeks to months. Chefs on rotation now need to coordinate visa applications with leave schedules. For crew from countries with limited US diplomatic presence, the logistics become a genuine career barrier.

---

## What ICE Is Actually Doing in Florida

A crew member provided detailed, first-hand testimony in both WhatsApp groups. This is not secondhand. This is not speculation.

**The process as described:**

1. ICE agents board vessels in West Palm Beach and Fort Lauderdale
2. They compare crew arrival dates against B1 visa entry stamps
3. They request access to bank accounts — checking for US-source income or payments that contradict the "employed by non-US entity" requirement
4. They check social media accounts for posts that indicate US-based work or extended stays inconsistent with the visa category
5. They ask crew to open their phones and review messages
6. They cross-reference contract dates against entry/exit records

**Confirmed outcome:** Four crew members in West Palm Beach were caught in a single enforcement action earlier in 2025. The M/Y Rosa incident — video of which was shared in the group — further documented the reality of active enforcement.

This is not new law. This is existing law being enforced with new intensity and new investigative methods.

---

## The Consequences of Getting Caught

If ICE determines you are working in violation of your visa status:

- **Deportation.** You will be removed from the United States.
- **Entry ban.** A violation can result in a 3-year, 10-year, or permanent bar from re-entry, depending on the circumstances and duration of the violation.
- **Career damage.** If you are banned from the US, you cannot work on any yacht that visits US waters, the USVI, or US territories. As one chef put it: "If you're a yachtie and you get banned from the states, that means any boat you join that heads to Caribbean won't hire you if they go to USVI and US territories." Your employability narrows dramatically.
- **Vessel consequences.** The yacht itself may face scrutiny, fines, and enhanced inspections going forward. Captains and management companies are increasingly aware that one crew member's violation creates liability for the entire operation.

## How to Protect Yourself

### Documentation

1. **Carry a physical copy of your employment contract** with the non-US management company clearly identified as employer
2. **Ensure your payslips show payment from a non-US entity** — if you are paid through a US intermediary for any reason, get this corrected immediately
3. **Keep your crew list current** and ensure it matches your visa category and contract dates
4. **Maintain a clean social media presence.** ICE agents are checking. Posts about "living in Florida" or working on US-based projects while on a B1 are evidence

### Behaviour

5. **Do not perform shore-based work under a B1.** Provisioning runs and boat-related errands are grey areas. Running a catering side business is not.
6. **Do not overstay.** When your contract ends, leave. The grace period is not a holiday extension.
7. **Do not volunteer information beyond what is asked.** Be polite, be truthful, but do not elaborate. If ICE boards your vessel, answer questions directly and factually. Do not offer context, stories, or explanations for things you were not asked about.

### For Dual Passport Holders

If you hold both a US passport and another nationality, some immigration lawyers advise using the non-US passport for yacht crew documentation to simplify the employment structure. Consult a maritime immigration attorney for your specific situation — this is not universal advice.

### For American Crew

American crew face a separate complication: some yacht insurance companies limit or exclude American crew members due to repatriation cost liabilities. If you are American and applying for yacht positions, ask directly about the vessel's insurance policy regarding US nationals before accepting a contract.

## What to Do If ICE Boards Your Vessel

1. **Remain calm.** This is a law enforcement encounter. Cooperate.
2. **Present your passport, B1 visa, and employment contract.** Have these accessible, not buried in your cabin.
3. **Answer questions truthfully and concisely.** Do not lie. Do not volunteer.
4. **Do not consent to a phone search unless compelled.** You may be asked, not ordered. Know the difference. In practice, refusal may escalate the encounter, so weigh the consequences. Consult legal counsel before this situation arises.
5. **Document the encounter afterward.** Write down what was asked, what was said, and who was present. Report to your captain, DPA, and management company immediately.
6. **Contact an immigration attorney.** If you are detained, ask for legal representation. You have the right to an attorney, though one will not be provided for free in immigration proceedings.

**Your Move:**
Three actions, in this order:

1. **Check your expiry.** If your B1/B2 expires within 12 months, start renewal now — in your country of nationality. Build the flight into your rotation schedule.
2. **Audit your documentation.** Employment contract naming a non-US entity, payslips showing non-US payment, crew list matching visa dates. If anything is ambiguous, fix it before the vessel enters US waters.
3. **Brief the crew.** If your vessel's winter itinerary includes Florida, every crew member's paperwork must be current and compliant before arrival. One person's violation creates liability for the entire boat.

The cost of proactive compliance is a flight home. The cost of enforcement is your career.
