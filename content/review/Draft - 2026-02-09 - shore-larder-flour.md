## Farine
*Triticum aestivum · Fertile Crescent, ~10,000 BCE · Year-round*

**70%.** Seventy percent of the world's cultivated land grows cereal grains, and wheat leads them all. It feeds more people than any other crop on earth — roughly 2.5 billion daily. Not because it tastes remarkable, or because it's the most nutritious. Because of a single property no other grain possesses: when you add water and apply force, two otherwise inert proteins — glutenin and gliadin — bond into a viscoelastic network that traps gas, stretches under heat, and sets into structure. That network is gluten. Gluten is why bread rises, pasta holds its shape, and croissants exist. Without it, wheat is just another starch. With it, wheat built civilization.

**What It Actually Is:**
The endosperm of the wheat kernel — the starchy interior that feeds the embryo — ground to powder. That endosperm is roughly 70-75% starch, 10-14% protein, and 14% moisture. The starch provides bulk and tenderness. The protein provides structure. The ratio between them — the protein percentage printed on the bag — determines everything. A bread flour at 12-14% protein builds the strong gluten network a sourdough needs. A cake flour at 7-9% produces the tender crumb that falls apart on the tongue. Same plant. Different milling. Different worlds. The bran (outer husk) and germ (embryo) are removed in white flour — and with them go most of the B vitamins, iron, fiber, and flavor. Whole wheat keeps them. The trade-off: the bran's sharp edges physically shear gluten strands during kneading, which is why 100% whole wheat bread is denser and heavier. Not worse — different. The physics demand it.

**The Story That Sticks:**
In the ruins of Shubayqa, a Natufian site in northeast Jordan, archaeologists from the University of Copenhagen found charred flatbread crumbs in a stone hearth — dated to 14,400 years ago (Arranz-Otaegui et al., 2018, *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*). Four thousand years before agriculture. Before anyone planted a seed on purpose, someone was already grinding wild cereal grains, mixing them with water, and baking them on hot stones.

Bread did not follow farming. Bread preceded it. The desire to make this food — portable, storable, transformative — may have been the reason humans settled down and started cultivating grain in the first place. Not the other way around. Flour didn't emerge from civilization. Civilization may have emerged from flour.

**The Tradition:**
In Naples, the pizzaiolo uses Tipo 00 — flour milled to a fineness that feels like talc between your fingers, protein around 12.5%, with gluten that stretches rather than snaps. The dough ferments 24-72 hours at cool temperatures, building flavor through slow bacterial activity while the gluten network relaxes into extensibility. In a 485°C (905°F) wood oven, 60-90 seconds. The result requires this flour and no other.

In rural France, the boulanger tracks flour by the *type* number — T55 for baguettes, T80 for pain de campagne, T150 for whole wheat — where the number indicates ash content after incineration, a proxy for how much bran remains. In Japan, tempura batter demands low-protein flour mixed with ice water and barely stirred — because every stroke of the chopstick develops gluten, and gluten is the enemy of the shatter. The same molecule that makes bread possible makes tempura impossible. Context is everything.

**The Compound That Matters:**

| Compound | Also Found In | What It Does |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| Glutenin | Wheat, spelt, kamut, some barley | The elastic protein — it snaps back. Long, coiled chains that form disulfide bonds when hydrated and kneaded. Provides strength and chew. Bread flour is high in glutenin. Without it, dough tears instead of stretching. |
| Gliadin | Wheat, rye, barley | The extensible protein — it flows. Compact, globular molecules that allow the dough to stretch without breaking. Pasta flour is high in gliadin. Also the primary trigger in celiac disease — the immune system mistakes it for a pathogen. |
| Amylose | Rice, potatoes, corn, all starch-containing plants | The linear starch molecule that retrogrades (recrystallizes) as bread cools — the reason bread goes stale. Staling is not drying out. It's amylose molecules re-ordering into crystalline structures, pushing water aside. Reheating reverses it temporarily. That's why day-old bread improves in the oven. |

**Three Pairings You Haven't Tried:**
*Based on shared volatile compounds and complementary chemistry:*

1. **Toasted flour + brown butter** — Dry-toast flour in a pan until it smells like roasted hazelnuts. That aroma is 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline — the same compound responsible for basmati rice's fragrance and popcorn's pull. Combined with brown butter's pyrazines and furanones, you get a roux that tastes deeper than the sum of its two ingredients. Use it as the base for a bechamel. The sauce will taste like it took hours. It took four minutes.
2. **Buckwheat flour + honey** — Not wheat, not a grain — buckwheat is a fruit seed related to rhubarb. Its dominant volatile, salicylaldehyde, overlaps with the phenylacetaldehyde in dark honeys like chestnut or buckwheat honey. Buckwheat galettes with warm honey and creme fraiche. Brittany in a single bite.
3. **Rye flour + caraway + chocolate** — Rye's earthy furanones and caraway's carvone share aromatic space with dark chocolate's pyrazines. A rye shortbread with caraway and dark chocolate chips. Sounds Nordic, tastes universal. The kind of petit four that makes someone ask what it is and not believe the answer.

**The Science That Surprises:**
Bread staling has nothing to do with moisture loss. A sealed loaf goes stale at the same rate as an open one. Baik and Chinachoti (2000, *Cereal Chemistry*) demonstrated that staling is driven by amylose retrogradation — the linear starch molecules reorganizing from their disordered, gelatinized state back into tight crystalline structures. This process expels water from the starch network, making the crumb feel dry and rigid even though total moisture content barely changes. The rate is fastest at refrigerator temperature — 4°C accelerates retrogradation compared to room temperature. Your fridge is the worst place to store bread. Room temperature for a day or two, then the freezer. Freezing halts retrogradation entirely. Reheating at 60°C (140°F) or above temporarily melts the crystals back to their gelatinized state — which is why toasting stale bread works. You are not drying it further. You are undoing the physics of staling.

**Where Most Go Wrong:**

1. **Overworking the dough — or underworking it.** Gluten development is not binary. A pizza dough needs 10-15 minutes of kneading or 24 hours of cold fermentation to build the extensible network that stretches thin without tearing. A scone dough needs 30 seconds of folding — any more and you've built the gluten structure of a bread roll, not a tender crumb. The mistake isn't kneading. It's not knowing which outcome you're after. Ask yourself before you touch the flour: do I want structure, or tenderness? Then handle accordingly.
2. **Ignoring protein content.** All-purpose flour is a compromise — 10-12% protein — adequate for most things, ideal for almost nothing. Using it for a Neapolitan pizza dough that needs 12.5% gives you a crust that won't stretch. Using it for a cake that needs 8% gives you a crumb that chews. Read the bag. The protein percentage tells you what the flour wants to become. Ignore it and you fight the physics for every result.
3. **Storing flour like it doesn't matter.** Flour is alive with enzymes, residual moisture, and — in whole wheat — oils from the germ that oxidize and go rancid within weeks. White flour lasts 6-8 months in a cool, dry, sealed container. Whole wheat lasts 1-3 months at room temperature before the oils turn. On a yacht crossing the Atlantic with a dry store at 28°C and ambient humidity of 70%, that timeline halves. Smell your flour before you use it. Rancid flour smells like old paint. You know the smell. You've just been ignoring it.

**Sourcing:**
- **Look for:** Protein percentage stated on the bag. Milling date — not just "best by." Stone-ground for whole wheat (preserves germ oils better than roller-milled, because lower heat during milling slows oxidation). Origin country if possible.
- **Avoid:** Bleached flour — chlorine gas or benzoyl peroxide used to whiten and "mature" flour artificially. Bromated flour — potassium bromate strengthens gluten but is a probable carcinogen, banned in the EU, Canada, and Brazil, still legal in the US. If the ingredients list anything beyond flour and possibly malted barley flour, ask why.
- **Good vs. great:** King Arthur and Caputo are the professional standards — consistent protein, reliable behavior, available globally through yacht provisioning. For bread: strong bread flour, 12-14% protein. For pastry: a soft flour, 8-9%. For pasta: semolina (durum wheat), hard, golden, high in gliadin. The move from good to great is specificity — stop using one flour for everything and start matching the flour to the job.

**On Board:**
- **Storage:** Airtight containers, sealed against humidity and pests. Weevils are not a matter of if but when in warm climates — bay leaves in the container are a traditional deterrent, and they work. Whole wheat and rye flour in the freezer if you have the space. White flour in a cool, dry locker, off the hull where condensation forms.
- **Shelf life:** White flour, 6-8 months sealed. Whole wheat, 1-3 months (freezer extends to 6). Semolina, 6 months. Nut flours, 3 months maximum at room temperature — their high fat content turns rancid fast.
- **The move:** The fastest bread on a yacht — no-knead. Mix 400g strong flour, 8g salt, 3g instant yeast, 350ml cool water. Stir until shaggy. Cover. Leave 12-18 hours at room temperature. The long fermentation develops both gluten and flavor without kneading — time does the work your hands would. Fold it once, shape it, drop it into a screaming-hot Dutch oven at 230°C (450°F), lid on for 30 minutes, lid off for 15. The crust shatters. The crumb is open and tangy. The galley smells like a bakery. Guests will not believe it came from a boat.
