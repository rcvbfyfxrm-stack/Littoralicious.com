# Provisioning San Juan: The One-Page Playbook

*US wholesale pricing, no import duty, and the last full-infrastructure port before the eastern Caribbean goes thin.*

San Juan is the provisioning port that most Caribbean-bound chefs overlook. They fly into Miami, load the boat in Fort Lauderdale, and head south. But if your route touches Puerto Rico — and many transatlantic arrivals, USVI-bound charters, and eastern Caribbean itineraries do — you are standing in a US territory with Costco, Sam's Club, Restaurant Depot, Amazon Prime delivery, and zero import duty. Everything your boat needs, at mainland prices, without crossing to Florida.

The chandler markup here is largely irrelevant because there are few dedicated yacht provisioning agents in San Juan. That is not a weakness — it is the point. The island is small enough, the wholesale infrastructure dense enough, and the roads short enough that self-sourcing a full provision takes one focused day. Two days if you want to be thorough. No agent required.

The single most important thing to know: Puerto Rico is the last place heading east where you have full US retail infrastructure with no import tax. Once you leave for the USVI, BVI, or eastern Caribbean, prices on imported goods rise 30-60% and selection drops off a cliff. Whatever your storage can hold, fill it here.

## Category Detail

### Fish & Seafood

**Go in person:**
- **La Placita de Santurce** — The market district in San Juan's nightlife neighbourhood. By day, fish vendors sell local catch from the surrounding waters: red snapper (chillo), mahi-mahi (dorado), Caribbean spiny lobster, yellowtail. Quality is good when the product is fresh — and here, fresh means it was swimming yesterday. Cold chain discipline varies by vendor, so inspect. Touch, smell, check the eyes. You know the drill.
- **Pescadería del Atlántico** — More consistent than the market vendors. Carries both local and imported seafood. Better for specific orders or when you need a guaranteed quantity rather than whatever came in.

> **Smart tip:** Puerto Rican waters produce excellent local fish that is genuinely local — not frozen-and-thawed imports relabelled as "fresh." Ask for "chillo entero" (whole red snapper). Whole fish is cheaper per kg than fillets, and you control the portioning and butchery. A 2kg whole chillo at La Placita costs roughly half per kg what you would pay for pre-cut fillets at the supermarket — and the quality is incomparable.

**Buy here:** Whole local snapper, mahi-mahi, Caribbean lobster when in season
**Skip:** Imported salmon and tuna at the market — buy those at Costco where the cold chain is guaranteed

### Meat & Protein

**Go in person:**
- **Restaurant Depot** (Carolina, 20 minutes from Old San Juan) — Professional cuts, bulk pricing, USDA Prime and Choice. Same free membership as every Restaurant Depot in the US — bring boat registration and a business card on your first visit. The meat section here is properly stocked: whole tenderloins, bone-in ribeyes, rack of lamb, pork belly, duck breast. Case lots of chicken thighs and ground beef for crew.
- **Costco** (Carolina or Caguas) — USDA Choice and Prime at Costco pricing. Vacuum-packed, consistent quality. The default for loading the freezer in bulk.

> **Smart tip:** Buy 2-3 weeks of frozen proteins here. Vacuum-seal everything, blast-freeze if your boat has the capacity, or pack tight in the freezer with cardboard dividers between categories. Once you leave Puerto Rico heading east, meat quality drops, availability becomes unreliable, and prices rise 30-50%. The proteins you load here are the proteins you will serve in Antigua, St Barths, and the BVI — where the same cuts either do not exist or cost double.

**Buy here:** Full freezer load — tenderloins, ribeyes, lamb racks, duck, chicken in bulk, crew proteins in case lots
**Skip:** Pre-marinated or pre-seasoned proteins. You are a chef. Buy clean protein and do the work yourself.

### General / One-Stop

**Order online:**
- **Amazon Prime** — Delivers to Puerto Rico at standard US rates with no import duty. This is not obvious to everyone, and it changes the maths entirely. Order specialty dry goods, pantry items, equipment, vacuum bags, cling film — anything non-perishable that you would normally pick up at a specialty shop. Place orders D-5 to D-4. Standard Prime shipping (2-3 days to PR addresses) means your boxes are waiting when you start the in-person runs.

**Go in person:**
- **Costco** (Carolina or Caguas) — Same Costco card you use on the mainland. Same product. Same prices. The Carolina location is closer to the marinas; Caguas is larger. Either works. This is your bulk general grocery run: dairy, eggs, beverages, snacks, cleaning supplies, paper goods, canned goods, condiments, and the overflow from every other category.

> **Smart tip:** Do not underestimate how much you can accomplish in a single Costco run here. Dairy, bulk proteins, dry goods, produce basics, beverages, cleaning, crew snacks — one trolley, one trip, 60% of your general provision sorted. Then you fill the gaps with specialist stops.

**Buy here:** Everything you normally buy at a bulk warehouse
**Skip:** Guest-level specialty items. Costco is crew-and-pantry. La Placita and Río Piedras are where the guest-level sourcing happens.

### Bread & Bakery

**Order online (phone):**
- Local panaderías deliver — call 24h ahead for volume. Ask your marina or a local crew member for the nearest bakery that will do a morning delivery run.

**Go in person:**
- **Panadería España** (Hato Rey) — Puerto Rican pan sobao (soft, slightly sweet enriched bread) and pan de agua (crusty water bread, the local baguette equivalent). Both are excellent crew bread. For guest service, the pan de agua makes a credible toast or crostini base.

> **Smart tip:** Puerto Rican bakery bread is fresh, cheap, and genuinely good — but it does not keep. Buy D-1 or D-0. For longer storage, buy from Costco (packaged artisan loaves) and freeze, or stock flour and bake on board. If you need high-protein bread flour (12%+), buy it at Costco or Sam's Club in bulk before you leave PR. Once you reach the eastern Caribbean, bread flour is scarce and overpriced.

**Buy here:** Pan sobao, pan de agua for crew. Bread flour in bulk for baking on board.
**Skip:** Expecting Parisian-level artisan sourdough. This is Caribbean bread culture — different tradition, different strengths.

### Produce & Fruit

**Go in person:**
- **Plaza del Mercado de Río Piedras** — The working market of San Juan. Not a tourist market — a neighbourhood market where locals buy their produce daily. Plantains (green for tostones, ripe for maduros), avocados, recao (culantro — visually similar to cilantro but a different plant with a deeper, more complex flavour), ají dulce peppers (sweet Caribbean pepper, zero heat, extraordinary aromatic profile), breadfruit, yams, calabaza, tropical fruit in season (guava, passion fruit, papaya, soursop, starfruit).

> **Smart tip:** Lean into what Puerto Rico grows. The tropical produce at Río Piedras is local, fresh, and cheap. Imported European produce — asparagus, Belgian endive, French beans — costs double what it does in Miami for the same quality. Adapt your guest menu to the market: a breadfruit velouté, a mahi ceviche with ají dulce and local avocado, a tropical fruit dessert with guava and passionfruit. The ingredient quality supports it and the guest gets a story with their plate.

**Buy here:** Recao (in bulk — freezes well), ají dulce, plantains, local avocados, tropical fruit, calabaza
**Skip:** Imported European vegetables. Buy those at Costco if you must, or wait until your next hub port.

### Dry Store & Pantry

**Order online:**
- **Amazon Prime** — Standard US delivery to PR addresses. No import duty. Order non-perishable specialty items D-5: specific oils, vinegars, specialty flours, spice blends, vacuum bags, parchment paper, anything that weighs a lot and is annoying to carry. It arrives at a PR address in 2-3 days with Prime.

**Go in person:**
- **Costco / Sam's Club** — Bulk dry goods at mainland wholesale prices. Olive oil (5L tins), flour (bulk bags), sugar, rice, pasta, canned tomatoes, coconut milk, cleaning supplies, cling film, aluminium foil, bin bags.

> **Smart tip:** Load canned goods, oils, flour, sugar, rice, and pasta here. These items cost 40-80% more once you reach islands with import duty. On a 40-footer heading to the eastern Caribbean for 3 weeks, you can easily save USD 500-800 on dry stores alone by loading in San Juan vs buying the same items in Antigua or St Lucia. Your storage capacity is the only limit. Fill every locker.

**Buy here:** Everything non-perishable in bulk. This is the best-priced loading opportunity before the eastern Caribbean.
**Skip:** Nothing. If it is dry, canned, or shelf-stable and you need it, buy it here.

---

### Frozen

**Go in person:**
- **Costco / Sam's Club** — Frozen proteins, frozen vegetables, frozen seafood, ice cream, frozen prepared items for crew convenience. Standard US frozen range at US prices.

> **Smart tip:** Frozen goods are the single biggest price differential between Puerto Rico and the eastern Caribbean. A case of frozen shrimp that costs USD 28 at Sam's Club in Carolina costs USD 45-55 in Antigua. Frozen vegetables follow the same pattern. Load the freezer to capacity. Organise by category (proteins, veg, seafood, crew), label everything, and draw a freezer map so you are not digging through chaos at sea.

**Buy here:** Maximum freezer load. Every category.
**Skip:** Cheap frozen prepared meals for crew when you can buy crew-grade fresh proteins at the same price and cook properly.

### Ethnic & Specialty

**Go in person:**
- **Pitusa** (multiple locations across San Juan metro area) — Dominican and Caribbean grocery chain. Carries Caribbean staples that Costco does not: specific rice brands, dried beans (habichuelas), sazón, achiote, sofrito, mojo, Caribbean hot sauces, green banana, yautía, malanga. Multiple locations — find the nearest to your marina route.
- **Asian markets in Santurce** — Small shops with basic Asian staples: soy sauce, rice vinegar, sesame oil, rice noodles, coconut milk, curry paste. The selection is limited compared to Miami's 168 Market. If your menu relies heavily on Asian ingredients, stock those in Miami or order via Amazon Prime before arrival.

> **Smart tip:** Puerto Rico's ethnic grocery strength is Caribbean and Latin American — not Asian. If you need lemongrass, galangal, kaffir lime, miso paste, or specialty Japanese/Thai ingredients, either order them via Amazon Prime D-5 or plan to source them in Miami. For Caribbean cooking, Pitusa and Río Piedras market have everything you need at the right price.

**Buy here:** Caribbean staples — sofrito, sazón, achiote, dried beans, Caribbean hot sauces, mojo
**Skip:** Expecting a full Asian pantry. Source that in Miami or online.

---

### Wine & Spirits

**Go in person:**
- **Costco** (spirits section) — Standard Costco spirits pricing. Puerto Rico has lower spirits excise tax than the mainland US, so rum in particular is well-priced. Vodka, gin, whisky, tequila — all at bulk pricing.
- **Bodegas Compostela** — Wine specialist. Better selection and curation than Costco for guest-level wines. Staff know what they carry. Worth the stop if the wine list matters for the charter.

> **Smart tip:** Puerto Rican rum at source is a provisioning advantage. Ron del Barrilito (three-star) is a serious aged sipping rum that costs USD 40-50 on-island and is difficult to find off-island. Don Q Gran Añejo and Reserva are solid mid-range options. Stock the boat's rum selection here — your Caribbean guests will expect good rum, and sourcing it at origin is both cheaper and a better story than buying Bacardi at a duty-free shop.

**Buy here:** Rum — full range from mixing to sipping. Spirits in bulk at Costco. Wine for the charter at Bodegas Compostela.
**Skip:** Premium Champagne and Bordeaux — better selection in SXM (duty-free, European import) or Miami.

## Provision Schedule

Work backwards from charter day. One major run per day, nothing rushed.

| When | What | Method | Where |
|------|------|--------|-------|
| **D-5** | Amazon Prime orders (specialty dry goods, vacuum bags, equipment) | Online | Deliver to marina or PR address |
| **D-3** | Bulk dry store, frozen, cleaning, bulk beverages | In-person wholesale run | Costco + Sam's Club (same trip, they are 10 min apart in Carolina) |
| **D-2** | Meat, dairy, cheese, wine, spirits | In-person | Restaurant Depot (AM) + Costco overflow + Bodegas Compostela |
| **D-1** | Fish, produce, bread, ethnic staples | In-person morning run | La Placita (07:00) + Río Piedras market + panadería + Pitusa |
| **D-0** | Ultra-perishables, herbs, last-minute requests | As needed | Pueblo or Econo (nearest supermarket chain) |

> **Smart tip:** The D-3 Costco + Sam's Club run is the big one — allow 3-4 hours including loading. Restaurant Depot on D-2 is faster (1-2 hours) because you know exactly what you need by then. The D-1 market run is the early morning — La Placita by 07:00, Río Piedras by 09:00, bread by 10:00, done by lunch. This schedule means you provision an entire boat in three working days with one early start.

The numbers that matter. San Juan vs what you would pay at the next Caribbean port.

| Item | In Eastern Caribbean (Antigua) | In San Juan | Source | Saving |
|------|-------------------------------|-------------|--------|--------|
| Butter /kg | USD 18 | USD 9 | Costco | ~50% |
| USDA Choice striploin /kg | USD 55 | USD 32 | Costco | ~42% |
| Bread flour 5kg | USD 14 | USD 6 | Sam's Club | ~57% |
| Olive oil 5L | USD 40 | USD 22 | Costco | ~45% |
| Frozen shrimp /kg | USD 32 | USD 18 | Sam's Club | ~44% |
| Canned tomatoes /case (12) | USD 30 | USD 14 | Costco | ~53% |

**Bottom line:** On a USD 8,000 full provision, self-sourcing in San Juan instead of the eastern Caribbean saves approximately USD 3,000-4,000. That is 6-8 hours of your time across three days at an effective rate of USD 375-660/h. There is no version of this maths where provisioning at the next port makes sense — unless you enjoy paying import duty for the experience.

## If You Have No Time — The Agent

There is no dedicated yacht provisioning agent in San Juan comparable to Epicerie De Marie in SXM or Gourmet Deliveries in Palma. This is not a problem. It is a feature.

San Juan is compact. The marina-to-Costco drive is 20 minutes. Restaurant Depot is on the way. La Placita is in town. The entire island is 165km long. You do not need an agent — you need a rental car and one focused day.

If you genuinely have zero time (you dock at noon, charter starts at 18:00 tomorrow, you have not provisioned), your options are:

1. **Instacart** — Schedule Costco and Walmart deliveries to the marina or a nearby address. Same-day delivery available. Covers general grocery, bulk, and frozen. Not guest-level, but it gets the boat fed.
2. **Call the marina** — Marina staff in San Juan know local runners and fixers. Ask for a recommendation. Expect to pay a premium for the urgency, but someone will make the runs for you.
3. **Split the work** — Send a crew member to Costco with the bulk list. You go to La Placita and Río Piedras for the guest-level product. Panadería and Pitusa on the way back. Two people, 4 hours, done.

> **Smart tip:** Option 3 is the real answer. It is not an agent — it is delegation. One person handles Costco (the easy list), you handle the markets (the judgement calls). This is faster than any agent and costs nothing beyond the rental car.

## Local Gems

What San Juan does better or cheaper than anywhere else on your Caribbean route. Stock up — you will not find this quality or price at the next stop.

| Item | Where | Why Here |
|------|-------|----------|
| Recao (culantro) | Río Piedras market | Caribbean herb that adds depth and complexity to everything — pennies per bunch. Freezes well. Buy 20 bunches and vacuum-seal. |
| Ají dulce peppers | Río Piedras market | Sweet Caribbean pepper, zero heat, extraordinary perfume. Unavailable off-island. Use in ceviches, sauces, garnishes. Freeze what you do not use immediately. |
| Puerto Rican coffee (Yaucono, Alto Grande, Café Lareno) | Any supermarket | Excellent quality, USD 5-8 per 400g bag. Guests love the provenance story. Buy 5-10 bags. |
| Ron del Barrilito (three-star) | Specialty liquor shops or El Barrachina (Old San Juan) | Aged sipping rum. Impresses guests who think they know Caribbean rum. USD 40-50 at source. |
| Local honey | Río Piedras market | Puerto Rican wildflower honey. Small-batch, unpasteurised. Guest breakfast staple. |
| Sofrito (fresh, homemade) | Pitusa or Río Piedras market vendors | The base of Puerto Rican cooking. Buy fresh, freeze in ice cube trays. Transforms rice, beans, braises, sauces. |
| Pique (local hot sauce) | Río Piedras market / small producers | Vinegar-based Caribbean hot sauce with ají caballero. Each producer's is different. Buy 3-4 bottles from different vendors. |

## Marinas & Logistics

**Where yachts dock:**
- **Marina Puerto del Rey** (Fajardo, east coast) — The largest marina in the Caribbean. 1,000+ slips. Full services. 45 minutes from San Juan metro area. If you are berthed here, Costco Caguas is closer than Carolina.
- **Club Nautico de San Juan** — In the heart of San Juan. Convenient for market runs but limited megayacht facilities.
- **San Juan Bay Marina** — Near the cruise port area. Functional, central.

**Rental car:** Essential. Unlike Miami where you might use delivery services, San Juan provisioning requires driving between stops. Rent on arrival, return after the D-1 market run. 3-4 days. Budget USD 120-200 total.

**Delivery to marina:** Instacart delivers to addresses near marinas but not to dock side. Have packages and online orders sent to a marina-adjacent address or the marina office if they accept deliveries (confirm in advance).

> **Smart tip:** If you are berthed at Puerto del Rey in Fajardo, adjust the schedule. Costco Caguas and Restaurant Depot are 30-40 minutes west. La Placita and Río Piedras are 45 minutes. Plan for longer drives but the same sequence. Alternatively, there are small fish markets and produce vendors in the Fajardo area — ask the marina staff.

## Provision Forward: What Disappears After PR

This is the critical intelligence. Once you leave San Juan heading east, the following become scarce, expensive, or both:

| Item | Available in PR | Available in USVI/BVI | Available in Eastern Caribbean |
|------|----------------|----------------------|-------------------------------|
| USDA-grade beef | Costco, Restaurant Depot | Limited, expensive | Rare, very expensive |
| Bread flour 12%+ | Costco, Sam's Club | Scarce | Very scarce |
| Frozen shrimp (quality) | Costco, Sam's Club | Available, marked up | Available, heavily marked up |
| Asian ingredients | Santurce shops, Amazon | Very limited | Near-zero |
| Bulk dry goods (wholesale) | Costco, Sam's Club | Some (Cost U Less in USVI) | Limited, import-taxed |
| Caribbean produce | Río Piedras market | Some local | Excellent local on some islands |
| Fresh herbs | Río Piedras, supermarkets | Limited | Very limited (BVI growers are the exception) |

**Load everything in the first five rows here. The last two rows improve as you go south — Caribbean produce and herbs get better on the islands that grow them.**

## The Bottom Line

San Juan is the most underused provisioning port in the Caribbean. It has US wholesale infrastructure, zero import duty, Amazon Prime delivery, and enough local food culture to make the provisioning interesting rather than just transactional. The Río Piedras market is worth visiting even if your freezer is full.

The single best move: treat San Juan as a full loading port. Costco + Restaurant Depot + La Placita + Río Piedras + one Amazon order. Three days. Full boat. USD 3,000-4,000 less than provisioning the same items in the eastern Caribbean.

Then head east with a full freezer, full lockers, and the knowledge that you are carrying recao, ají dulce, and Ron del Barrilito — three things that will make your time in the Caribbean taste like the Caribbean.

Prices and availability shift season to season. Verify before you commit a full provision to any single source. But the structural advantage — US pricing, no duty, full infrastructure — does not change. San Juan is a loading port. Use it as one.
