# Salary, Tips, and Getting Screwed: A Financial Guide for Yacht Chefs

*February 2026 · Global — All Vessel Types*

**The Development:**
Salary data in yachting is deliberately opaque. Management companies benefit from chefs not knowing their market rate. Captains benefit from tip structures that nobody questions. The result: chefs are routinely underpaid by 15–30% because they accepted the first number offered without benchmarks. This guide consolidates real salary data from working yacht chefs across both major WhatsApp groups (1,300+ members combined), cross-referenced against agency postings and contract reviews from the past 12 months.

**Who's Affected:**
Every yacht chef — from crew chefs on 30m boats to head chefs running brigades on 100m+ vessels. Also relevant for chefs transitioning between yacht, private, and chalet work.

## Tip Distribution: Where the Money Disappears

A poll across both WhatsApp groups (55 respondents) produced a clear result: **54 voted that tips should be distributed per charter, 1 voted per season.** The industry standard is per-charter distribution. If your captain holds tips until end of season, that is not standard practice — it is a red flag.

### Common Tip Problems

**Delayed cash tips.** Charter guests sometimes tip in cash. That cash should reach crew within days, not weeks. Reports of 4+ week delays are common and unacceptable. Cash sitting in the captain's safe for a month is cash you cannot verify.

**Captain's share.** The captain traditionally takes a larger percentage. On some vessels this is contractually defined and transparent. On others, the captain simply takes more and nobody sees the breakdown. Ask for the tip split formula in writing before you sign.

**Tips withheld on departure.** If you leave a vessel — for any reason — tips earned during your tenure belong to you. Multiple chefs have reported tips being withheld as informal "punishment" for leaving mid-season. This is wage theft. Document every charter you worked and the guest count.

## Rotation: What the Numbers Mean

One widely-shared post — "5:1 is NOT rotation" — received universal agreement across both groups.

| Rotation | Reality |
|----------|---------|
| 2:2 | Industry gold standard. Two months on, two months off. Sustainable long-term |
| 3:3 | Acceptable compromise. Common on larger vessels with established programmes |
| 10:10 | Ten weeks on, ten weeks off. Considered ideal by chefs who've had it |
| 5:1 | Five months on, one month off. This is not rotation. This is exploitation with a holiday |
| "60 days leave" | Often marketed as rotation but you're told when to take it. If you don't control the dates, it's not leave — it's permission |

One chef in the group reported successfully negotiating rotation only after handing in his notice. The captain suddenly found budget for a relief chef. The leverage was real the entire time — the chef just hadn't used it.

## Tax Strategy

### UK Seafarer's Earnings Deduction

If you are UK tax-resident and spend 182.5 days or more outside the UK per tax year, you qualify for the Seafarer's Earnings Deduction (SED). This can reduce your UK tax liability to zero on qualifying earnings. The qualifying period must be at least 365 consecutive days, and no single visit to the UK during that period can exceed 183 consecutive days.

**Recommended accountant:** Marine Accounts — GBP 200–250 for annual filing. They specialise in seafarer tax. Worth every penny compared to a high-street accountant who has never heard of SED.

### US Tax Obligations

American yacht chefs face the most punishing tax situation. The US taxes worldwide income regardless of where you live or work. The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) for 2025 is approximately USD 126,500 — income above that threshold is taxed at full US rates. California residents face additional state tax that can approach 50 cents on the dollar at high income levels.

### Italian Residency

Some chefs maintain Italian fiscal residency for tax purposes. An Italian Metro card is tied to your Italian tax code (codice fiscale). Be aware: an Italian Metro card does NOT work in France. The systems are nationally siloed.

### Mortgage Access

Yacht chefs with irregular income patterns struggle with traditional mortgage applications. Two brokers recommended repeatedly in the groups: **Cherry Finance** and **John Charcoal** — both experienced with seafarer income structures.

## Red Flags in Contracts

Watch for these. Each one was reported by a working chef in the past 12 months.

1. **No written tip split formula.** If the captain won't put it on paper, assume the worst.
2. **Tips held until end of season.** Per-charter distribution is the standard. Deviation requires justification.
3. **"Rotation to be discussed."** If it is not in the contract, it does not exist.
4. **Insurance starting after 3 months.** Medical coverage should begin on day one. A 3-month delay is universally condemned and is a genuine safety risk.
5. **No overtime structure.** If your contract says "hours as required" with no compensatory mechanism, you are agreeing to unlimited free labour.
6. **Verbal promises about galley upgrades.** If the galley is broken now, it will be broken in six months. Get equipment commitments in writing with deadlines.

## Negotiation: What Actually Works

**Know your number before the call.** Use the benchmarks above. If the offer is below the range for your vessel size and position, name the range and cite market data. "The current rate for a sole chef on a 50m is USD 8,000–10,000. I'm looking for USD 9,500 based on my charter experience."

**Negotiate the package, not just the salary.** Rotation schedule, tip structure, flight allowance, food budget per head, and galley equipment commitments are all negotiable. A chef who accepts USD 8,000 with 2:2 rotation and a functioning galley is better positioned than a chef at USD 10,000 with 5:1 and a broken Miele oven.

**Get it in writing.** Every verbal promise should appear in the contract or a signed addendum. If the captain says "we'll sort that out later," the answer is no.
