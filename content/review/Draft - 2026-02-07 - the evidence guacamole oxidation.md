# Red Onion Slows Guacamole Oxidation Better Than Citrus or Oil

**The Study:**
A yacht chef ran a controlled side-by-side experiment: batches of guacamole prepared identically, with the only variable being the oxidation-prevention method. The test compared red onion mixed throughout, lime juice, olive oil surface barrier, plastic wrap pressed to the surface, and a control with no treatment. The batches were held at 4°C and visually assessed at 2, 4, 8, and 24 hours. The red onion batch stayed noticeably greener at every interval. The finding aligns with published food science on allium-derived sulfur compounds and their interaction with polyphenol oxidase (PPO), the enzyme responsible for avocado browning.

**Key Finding:**
Red onion mixed into guacamole inhibits enzymatic browning more effectively than citrus acid, oil barriers, or surface wrapping alone.

**The Numbers:**

| Method | Visible Browning at 4 Hours | Visible Browning at 24 Hours | Mechanism |
|--------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|-----------|
| Red onion (mixed throughout) | Minimal — slight dulling only | Surface browning confined to top 2-3 mm | Sulfur compounds (thiosulfinates) inhibit PPO activity directly |
| Lime juice | Moderate browning at edges | Significant browning, acid effect diminished | Ascorbic acid reduces quinones temporarily; depleted over time |
| Olive oil surface layer | None where oil contact maintained | Moderate — oil migrates, exposes surface | Physical oxygen barrier only; no enzymatic inhibition |
| Plastic wrap (contact) | None where contact maintained | Moderate at any air gaps | Physical oxygen barrier only |
| Control (nothing) | Heavy browning | Fully oxidised surface | Unrestricted PPO + oxygen reaction |

**What This Means in the Galley:**
If you're prepping guacamole for a charter lunch buffet at 0700 and serving at 1300, mix the red onion in during prep — do not hold it as a garnish to add later. The sulfur compounds need to be distributed throughout the avocado matrix to inhibit PPO at every surface. This also means your mise en place changes: dice the red onion first, mix it in immediately after mashing, then add your acid and seasoning. For maximum hold time, combine methods — red onion mixed in, lime juice for flavour and secondary antioxidant effect, then press plastic wrap directly to the surface to limit oxygen exposure. That triple approach should hold presentable colour for 6-8 hours at 4°C.

**What This Does Not Mean:**
That onion replaces acid in guacamole. Lime juice contributes flavour and a secondary antioxidant pathway (ascorbic acid reducing quinones). The red onion is not a flavour substitute — it is an enzymatic inhibitor. You need both. This also does not mean white onion or shallot will perform identically; red onion has higher concentrations of quercetin and specific thiosulfinates that contribute to the effect.

**Limitations:**
- The galley experiment was observational and small-scale — visual assessment, not spectrophotometric measurement of browning
- Avocado variety, ripeness, and growing region affect PPO concentration and therefore oxidation rate — results may vary
- The sulfur compound mechanism is supported by peer-reviewed literature (see below), but the specific guacamole application has not been studied under controlled laboratory conditions

**Your Move:**
Next time you prep guacamole for buffet service, dice your red onion first and fold it in immediately. Test it yourself — make two batches, one with onion mixed in, one with onion on top as garnish. Check at 4 hours. The difference is visible.

**Source:** PPO inhibition by allium-derived sulfur compounds: Lee, C.Y., "Enzymatic Browning and Its Prevention," in *Enzymatic Browning and Its Prevention*, ACS Symposium Series, 1995. Thiosulfinate chemistry in alliums: Block, E., *Garlic and Other Alliums: The Lore and the Science*, RSC Publishing, 2010. Galley experiment data from yacht chef community testing, January 2026.
