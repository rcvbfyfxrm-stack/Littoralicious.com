# Breaking Into Yachting: The Real Pathway for Professional Chefs

*February 2026 · Global — Entry-Level Recruitment*

**The Development:**
Every month, chefs with solid shore-side experience post the same question in yacht chef groups: "I have 5 years in restaurants — how do I get on a boat?" The answers are consistent across hundreds of these threads, and they contradict most of what crew agencies publish on their websites. Word of mouth remains the primary hiring channel. Formal applications through online portals account for a minority of placements. The path in is narrower, more specific, and more achievable than the marketing suggests — if you know the sequence.

**Who's Affected:**
Professional chefs with commercial kitchen experience considering a transition to superyacht work. This applies regardless of nationality, though visa and certification requirements vary by flag state and operating region.

**What It Means:**
The barrier to entry is not talent — it is paperwork and positioning. A chef with a strong CV, the correct certifications, and a physical presence in the right port during hiring season will find work. A chef with a Michelin background who applies exclusively online from landlocked Europe will not. The industry hires on trust, and trust requires proximity.

**Key Details:**

| Detail | Specifics |
|--------|-----------|
| Minimum certifications | STCW Basic Safety Training, ENG1 medical certificate, Food Safety Level 2 (Level 3 preferred) |
| STCW cost | GBP 800-1,200 depending on provider and country |
| ENG1 cost | GBP 100 (UK), comparable in other flag states |
| Primary hiring ports | Fort Lauderdale (Oct-Mar), Antibes (Mar-Sep), Palma (Apr-Sep), St Maarten (Nov-Feb) |
| Key job platforms | Dockwalk, Crewdar app, Kraftyachtcrew.com, Coastleap |
| Yacht-specific training | Galley Gang (Cornwall, UK) — the only recognised yacht chef course; covers galley operations, provisioning, and service standards |
| CV resource | Hugo Ortega's CV cheatsheet via Superyacht Sunday School — widely shared in groups as the format hiring captains expect |

**The Pathway, Sequenced:**

1. **Get certified.** STCW, ENG1, and Food Safety Level 2 minimum. Do not fly to a port without these. You will not be considered.
2. **Build a yacht-specific CV.** One page. Professional photo. List certifications first. Highlight adaptability, dietary range, and crew size capacity — not your Michelin pedigree. Use Hugo Ortega's template as a baseline.
3. **Go to Fort Lauderdale or Antibes.** Physically. During hiring season. Walk the docks. Register with crew agencies in person. Eat at the crew bars. The first job is found face-to-face.
4. **Take the first gig offered.** It will be a day rate, a delivery, or a fill-in. Your first yacht job is a credential, not a career. Take it, perform, collect a reference.
5. **Consider Galley Gang.** The Cornwall-based course bridges the gap between shore-side kitchen skills and yacht galley realities. Multiple working yacht chefs cite it as the single most useful pre-entry investment.

**The Catch:**
Online applications alone do not work for first-timers. Agencies prioritise candidates with existing yacht references. The catch-22 is real: you need experience to get hired, but you need to get hired to gain experience. The workaround is physical presence in a hiring port, willingness to accept short-term or low-paying initial gigs, and relentless networking. Chef T from Cape Town — 22 years old, applying remotely — received no responses. The chefs who break in are the ones who show up.

**Your Move:**
If you are considering the transition: complete STCW and ENG1 first, book a one-way ticket to Fort Lauderdale or Antibes during peak season, and budget for 2-4 weeks of dock-walking before your first placement. Register on Crewdar and Dockwalk before you arrive.

**Source:** Aggregated advice from Chefs Helping Chefs WhatsApp group (~1,025 members) and Yacht Chef Group (~300+ members), July 2025–January 2026. Galley Gang Cornwall. Superyacht Sunday School (Hugo Ortega).
