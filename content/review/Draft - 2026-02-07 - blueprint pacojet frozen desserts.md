# Pacojet Frozen Yogurt and Ninja Creami Hacks

**The Pacojet produces frozen desserts with a particle size under 2 microns. The Ninja Creami produces respectable results for one-twentieth the price. Both have a place in a yacht galley — if you understand what each machine actually does.**

The Pacojet is not a blender that happens to be cold. It is a micro-shaving device: a blade spinning at 2,000 RPM shaves frozen product from the top down, one micron-thin layer at a time, incorporating minimal air and producing a density and smoothness that no churn can replicate. The Ninja Creami is a domestic machine that uses a different mechanism — a drill-style blade that bores into frozen product from the top, breaking it into particles and folding in air. The results are coarser, lighter, and less stable, but for crew desserts, quick sorbets, and frozen yogurts where Pacojet precision is not required, it fills the role at EUR 200 instead of EUR 5,000.

## INGREDIENTS

### THE PACOJET FROZEN YOGURT FORMULA

*This is the base formula shared across yacht chef groups. It works because the ratio of sugar to water controls the freezing point, and the yogurt's protein and fat content provide body without stabilisers.*

#### GROUP A: Base

| Ingredient | Weight | Volume | Notes |
|------------|--------|--------|-------|
| Greek yogurt (10% fat) | 1,000 g | ~950 ml | Full fat, strained. Not regular yogurt — the protein content matters |
| Caster sugar | 350 g | 1.75 cups | 26% of total weight. This is not optional sweetness — it is the freezing point depressant |
| Lemon juice, fresh | 60 ml | ~2 lemons | Adds acidity for flavour balance and inhibits ice crystal growth |
| Lemon zest | 6 g | ~2 lemons | Microplaned. Adds volatile oils that survive freezing |

*Total base weight: ~1,416 g. Fills approximately 1.5 Pacojet beakers.*

#### EQUIPMENT NEEDED

- Pacojet with beakers (2 for this yield)
- Digital scale
- Microplane
- **Helpful:** Pacojet beaker lids (product degrades in an open freezer)

### THE NINJA CREAMI ADAPTATION

*The same base requires modification for the Ninja Creami. The machine incorporates more air and generates more heat during processing, so the base needs more body and a slightly lower water content to compensate.*

|------------|--------|--------|-------|

- Ninja Creami (any model)

## METHOD

### PACOJET PROTOCOL

#### PHASE 1: Base Preparation
*10 minutes active*

**1.** Combine 1,000 g Greek yogurt and 350 g sugar in a bowl. Whisk until the sugar is fully dissolved — 2 minutes of continuous whisking. Undissolved sugar creates gritty pockets in the final product because the Pacojet's micro-shaving does not re-dissolve granules.

**2.** Add 60 ml lemon juice and 6 g zest. Fold through with a spatula. Do not whisk aggressively — you want minimal air in the base. The Pacojet does not benefit from pre-aeration; it controls density by design.

> **Checkpoint:** Taste the base. It should taste too sweet and too tart at room temperature. Freezing suppresses both sweetness and acidity perception by 30-40%. If it tastes balanced now, it will taste flat frozen.

#### PHASE 2: Freeze
*Minimum 24 hours*

**3.** Pour the base into Pacojet beakers, filling to 1 cm below the rim. Seal with lids. Place upright in a freezer set to -22°C (-8°F). The base must freeze completely solid — a minimum of 24 hours. The Pacojet cannot pacotise a partially frozen base; the blade will skip and produce uneven texture.

#### PHASE 3: Pacotise
*2 minutes*

**4.** Remove the beaker from the freezer. Lock into the Pacojet. Run one cycle. The blade descends through the frozen base, shaving micro-layers and compressing them into a dense, smooth frozen yogurt. One cycle is sufficient for yogurt bases. Two cycles over-aerates and produces a mousse-like texture that melts too fast on the plate.

> **Done when:** The surface is smooth and matte, not icy or crystalline. A spoon drawn across the surface leaves a clean line with no granular resistance. Internal temperature immediately after pacotising: approximately -12°C to -8°C (10-18°F).

---

### NINJA CREAMI PROTOCOL

#### PHASE 1: Base Preparation
*10 minutes active*

**5.** Combine 450 g Greek yogurt, 120 g sugar, and 30 g cream cheese in a bowl. Whisk until completely smooth — the cream cheese must be fully incorporated with no visible lumps. The cream cheese serves two purposes: it adds fat (which slows melting) and protein (which improves the Creami's ability to create a smooth texture from its coarser processing).

**6.** Add 25 ml lemon juice and 3 g zest. Fold through.

> **Checkpoint:** Same principle — taste too sweet and too tart at room temperature.

#### PHASE 2: Freeze
*Minimum 24 hours*

**7.** Pour into a Ninja Creami pint container. Fill to the max line — underfilling causes the blade to spin in air and produces inconsistent results. Seal. Freeze at -22°C for a minimum of 24 hours. The base must be frozen completely solid.

#### PHASE 3: Process
*3-5 minutes*

**8.** Remove the pint from the freezer. Let it sit at room temperature for 5 minutes — unlike the Pacojet, the Creami needs the outer edge slightly tempered to prevent the blade from stalling on rock-hard frozen product.

**9.** Lock the pint into the machine. Select the "Ice Cream" programme (not "Sorbet" — the yogurt's fat content requires the denser programme). Run one full cycle. Inspect the result. The Creami almost always requires a re-spin: use a spoon to push any unfrozen chunks from the edges toward the centre, then run a second "Re-spin" cycle.

> **Done when:** The texture is smooth and scoopable, with no visible ice chunks. It will be noticeably lighter and airier than the Pacojet version — this is inherent to the machine, not a defect. Internal temperature after processing: approximately -8°C to -5°C (18-23°F).

## THE SCIENCE

### Sugar as Freezing Point Depressant

**What's happening:** Sugar (sucrose) in solution lowers the freezing point of water through colligative properties — the dissolved sugar molecules interfere with ice crystal formation. At 26% sugar by weight (the Pacojet formula), the base's freezing point drops from 0°C to approximately -3°C. This means that at serving temperature (-10°C to -8°C), a portion of the water remains unfrozen as a concentrated syrup between the ice crystals. This unfrozen syrup is what gives frozen yogurt its scoopable, creamy texture rather than the rock-hard block you get with insufficient sugar.

**Why it matters:** Reduce the sugar and the product freezes harder, scoops poorly, and tastes icy. Increase the sugar and the product won't freeze firm enough, weeps liquid, and tastes cloying. 350 g per 1,000 g yogurt is the tested ratio. Do not adjust it for "less sweet" preferences — use a more tart yogurt instead.

*Source: Goff, H.D. and Hartel, R.W., Ice Cream, 7th edition, Springer, 2013.*

### Pacojet vs. Churn: Particle Size and Density

**What's happening:** A traditional churn dasher scrapes frozen product from the walls of a spinning canister, incorporating 30-50% air (overrun) and producing ice crystals in the 30-50 micron range. The Pacojet's descending blade shaves frozen product into particles under 2 microns — 15 to 25 times smaller — with minimal air incorporation (under 10% overrun). The result is a denser, smoother product with a perceived richness that has nothing to do with added fat and everything to do with particle physics.

**Why it matters:** The Pacojet's density means the frozen yogurt melts slower on the plate, holds its shape during service, and delivers flavour more intensely because the smaller particles coat more surface area on the tongue. The Ninja Creami sits between a churn and a Pacojet — its particle size is larger (estimated 10-20 microns) and its overrun is higher (~20-30%), producing a lighter, more ice-cream-like texture. Neither is wrong. They are different products.

*Source: Pacojet AG, technical specifications. Goff and Hartel, 2013.*

## ELEVATION

### TIER 1: No Extra Time

| Modification | What It Does | How Much |
|--------------|--------------|----------|
| Replace lemon with lime | Brighter, more tropical acidity | Same weight — juice of 3 limes + zest |
| Fold in passion fruit pulp after pacotising | Tart, aromatic contrast with visible seeds | 50 g per beaker, folded by hand |
| Drizzle of extra virgin olive oil at service | Fat contrast, peppery finish | 5 ml per portion, high-quality oil only |

### TIER 2: Worth the Extra Effort

| Modification | What It Does | Extra Time | How To |
|--------------|--------------|------------|--------|
| Honey-roasted fig swirl | Sweet, jammy pockets with caramelised depth | +20 min | Halve 200 g figs, roast with 30 g honey at 180°C for 15 min. Cool. Chop roughly. Fold into pacotised yogurt |
| Cardamom sugar | Warm, floral sweetness that transforms the base | +5 min | Toast 8 g green cardamom pods, grind, sift into sugar before mixing with yogurt |

### TIER 3: Restaurant-Level

| Modification | What It Does | Extra Time | Notes |
|--------------|--------------|------------|-------|
| Frozen yogurt quenelle with aged balsamic | Acid-acid-sweet interplay, visual contrast | +2 min | Quenelle between two spoons warmed in hot water. 3 drops of 25-year balsamic around the plate |
| Dehydrated yogurt shards | Textural contrast — crunchy, tangy | +8 hours | Spread 200 g yogurt thin on a silpat. Dehydrate at 55°C for 8 hours. Snap into shards. Store airtight |

## TROUBLESHOOTING

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Icy, granular texture (Pacojet) | Base not frozen solid, or sugar undissolved | Freeze minimum 24 hours at -22°C. Whisk sugar until fully dissolved |
| Icy, granular texture (Creami) | Not enough fat in base, or skipped re-spin | Add 30 g cream cheese. Always re-spin |
| Melts too fast on plate | Over-pacotised (2+ cycles) or served too warm | One cycle only. Serve immediately. Chill plates to 4°C |
| Too dense, cannot scoop | Sugar too low | Weigh sugar precisely — 350 g per 1,000 g yogurt is non-negotiable |
| Blade stalls (Creami) | Base frozen too hard with no tempering | Let pint sit at room temperature for 5 minutes before processing |
| Tastes bland when frozen | Base was not tart or sweet enough before freezing | Taste the base at room temperature — it should taste 30-40% more intense than your target |

## PACOJET vs. NINJA CREAMI: THE HONEST COMPARISON

| | Pacojet | Ninja Creami |
|--|---------|--------------|
| Price | EUR 4,500-5,500 | EUR 180-230 |
| Texture quality | Exceptional — sub-2 micron | Good — 10-20 micron estimated |
| Overrun (air) | Under 10% | 20-30% |
| Density | High — slow melt, intense flavour | Medium — faster melt, lighter body |
| Noise | Significant — 2 min of high-pitched grinding | Moderate — 60-90 seconds per cycle |
| Durability | Built for commercial daily use. Will last decades | Domestic build. Expect 2-3 years of galley use before motor degradation |
| Beaker/pint cost | EUR 30-50 per beaker | EUR 5-10 per pint |
| Versatility | Frozen desserts, sauces, mousses, soups, savoury applications | Frozen desserts only |
| Galley footprint | 20 x 30 cm, heavy | 18 x 18 cm, light |
| The verdict | The professional choice. Buy once. | "A Temu Paco" — but a useful one for crew desserts and backup |

The Ninja Creami does not replace the Pacojet. It supplements it. Keep the Pacojet for guest service where texture and density matter. Use the Creami for crew frozen yogurts, sorbets, and the late-night ice cream run that nobody wants to waste a Pacojet beaker on.
