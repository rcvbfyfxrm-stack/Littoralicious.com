# Weekly Brief — Week of March 19, 2026

## THE THREAD

Paul Marcon won the Bocuse d'Or in Lyon on January 27, 2025 — thirty years after his father Régis took the same gold. Denmark second, Sweden third. In the parallel World Pastry Cup, Japan took gold. Meanwhile, Norway slashed its king crab quota 44%, Japan's Hiroshima oyster farms reported 80–90% mortality, and the Superyacht Chef Competition in Monaco announced its 7th edition for April 2.

Competition season — for chefs and for the ocean. The Marcon dynasty story is about transmission: technique passed through lineage, refined but not reinvented. The king crab and oyster crises are about scarcity: premium ingredients becoming scarcer as oceans warm and quotas tighten. The thread connecting them is this — the gap between what a chef can do and what a chef can source is widening. Excellence in technique matters more when the ingredients are harder to get.

## SHORE LARDER — King Crab

*Paralithodes camtschaticus (Red King Crab) · Norway/Alaska · Season: October–January (Alaska), year-round with quotas (Norway)*

**900 tonnes.** That's Norway's 2026 male king crab quota — a 44% cut from the previous year's scientific advice, set after the Institute of Marine Research recommended not exceeding 850 tonnes. The government added 50 tonnes above scientific advice. The closed season now extends through May to protect moulting stock.

Norwegian king crab is the premium live product on Nordic-itinerary yachts. Tromsø and Kirkenes are the provisioning ports. At an export benchmark of approximately NOK 739/kg (roughly EUR 63/kg), this was already the most expensive crustacean in the European market. With a 44% supply reduction, expect prices to rise further.

| Source | 2026 Quota/Supply | Price Range | Notes |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| Norway (red king crab) | 900 tonnes (-44%) | ~EUR 63/kg | Closed season extended through May |
| Norway (snow crab) | 12,336 tonnes (stable) | Lower | Good value alternative |
| Alaska (Bristol Bay red) | 2.68 million lbs (+17%) | USD 80–125/lb retail | Recovering but ultra-premium |
| Alaska (snow crab) | Doubled from 2024/25 | Significantly lower | Second season back from closure |
| Alaska (Tanner crab) | 11.3 million lbs | Moderate | Largest quota since 2015 |

**Sourcing:** For Norwegian itineraries, secure supplier relationships in Tromsø and Kirkenes early — supply will be tight. Snow crab (stable quota, 12,336 tonnes) is the value alternative with excellent flavour for bisques, salads, and crab cakes. For US-based charters, Alaska Tanner crab (11.3 million lbs — largest since 2015) and snow crab (quota doubled) offer the best price-to-quality ratio. Bristol Bay red king crab is recovering but remains at ultra-premium pricing.

## THE METHOD — Live Shellfish Handling: From Port to Plate

**The Move:** Keep live crustaceans at 2–4°C in damp conditions with no standing water. The goal: slow metabolism without killing. A live king crab held properly survives 48–72 hours out of water. Held improperly, it dies in hours and the meat turns to mush.

1. **On arrival:** Inspect. Legs should move when touched. Eyes should respond. Discard any that don't react — dead crustaceans deteriorate within 30 minutes of death as autolytic enzymes (cathepsins, calpains) begin breaking down muscle tissue.
2. **Storage:** Place in the coldest section of the cold room (2–4°C). Cover with damp newspaper or seaweed — never submerge in freshwater (osmotic shock kills them) or standing seawater (oxygen depletes). No sealed containers — they need air circulation.
3. **Timing:** Cook within 24 hours of purchase for optimal quality. Maximum hold: 48 hours for king crab, 72 hours for lobster, 24 hours for Dungeness.
4. **Killing:** The RSPCA and EFSA recommend stunning before cooking. Two methods: (a) Place in freezer at -20°C for 20 minutes to induce torpor, then knife through the nerve ganglia; (b) Electrical stunning device if available. Direct plunge into boiling water without stunning is increasingly questioned on welfare grounds and is banned in Switzerland.
5. **Cooking:** Boil in heavily salted water (60 g/L — approximate ocean salinity) at a rolling boil. King crab legs: 8–10 minutes from frozen, 5–6 minutes from thawed. Internal temp target: 63°C.

| Factor | What Happens | Adjust By |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Storage in freshwater | Osmotic shock → death in minutes | Damp newspaper or seaweed only |
| Temperature above 8°C | Accelerated metabolism → stress → death | Coldest section of cold room, 2–4°C |
| Dead before cooking | Autolytic enzymes destroy texture | Inspect on arrival; cook immediately if dying |

**You know it worked when:** The shell is vivid red-orange after cooking. The meat pulls cleanly from the shell in intact pieces. The flavour is sweet, briny, with no ammonia.

## THE EVIDENCE — Hiroshima Oyster Die-Off: 80–90% Mortality

SeafoodSource, November 20, 2025; Japan Times, December 12, 2025; Undercurrent News, December 8, 2025.

Hiroshima Prefecture — which produces approximately 60% of Japan's farmed Pacific oysters — reported 80–90% mortality in its 2025 crop. Die-offs also hit Okayama and Hyogo Prefectures across the Seto Inland Sea. Estimated losses: JPY 6 billion (approximately USD 40 million). Both the current crop and younger seed oysters for the next season were devastated.

| Metric | Data | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Hiroshima mortality | 80–90% | Produces ~60% of Japan's oysters |
| Financial loss | JPY 6 billion (~USD 40M) | Government issued emergency aid |
| Seed stock | Also destroyed | Next season's production compromised |
| Suspected cause | Higher water temps + elevated salinity from low rainfall | Under investigation |
| French parallel | Arcachon Bay: 80–90% losses before harvest | Summer mortality syndrome (Vibrio + warming) |

Separately, Canadian researchers identified a previously unknown virus — a distant relative of SARS-CoV-2 — "strongly associated" with mass Pacific oyster die-offs in British Columbia. The convergence of thermal stress, bacterial pathogens, and now viral threats makes Pacific oyster (*Crassostrea gigas*) the most vulnerable commercial shellfish species globally.

**What this means in the galley:** Japanese Hiroshima kakis — prized for creamy sweetness on Pacific-itinerary yachts — will be in severely short supply through 2026. The seed stock destruction means recovery takes at minimum 18–24 months. For reliable oyster sourcing right now: Irish (Kelly, Flaggy Shore), Scottish (Loch Ryan, Cumbrae), and New Zealand (Bluff, Te Matuku) operations are stable. French Fines de Claire and Marennes-Oléron are available but subject to seasonal mortality. Tasmania is a stable Pacific alternative.

**What this does NOT mean:** That all oysters are unsafe. The Hiroshima event is a farming/environmental crisis, not a food safety emergency in markets. Proper cold chain management (below 5°C from harvest to shuck) and verified depuration remain the safeguards.

## LITTORAL HERITAGE — Plateau de Fruits de Mer

*Cancale, Brittany / Normandy Coast, France*

The *plateau de fruits de mer* is not a recipe. It is a philosophy of restraint made physical — raw shellfish arranged on crushed ice, presented as found, uncooked, with only the sea as seasoning. It exists because the coastline provided, and the best thing to do with what it provided was nothing.

The tradition is concentrated along the Brittany and Normandy coasts — Cancale (the oyster capital), Saint-Malo, Étretat, Honfleur — where cold Atlantic waters produce bivalves of extraordinary quality: *huîtres creuses* (Pacific oysters), *huîtres plates* (native flat oysters, Ostrea edulis), *palourdes* (clams), *bigorneaux* (winkles), *bulots* (whelks), *langoustines*, *crevettes grises*, *tourteau* (brown crab), *oursins* (sea urchins).

The arrangement matters. Two tiers: the upper tier for larger items (crab, lobster split lengthwise, langoustines), the lower for raw bivalves (oysters on the half shell, clams, whelks). Crushed ice packed throughout. Garnish: lemon halves, shallot mignonette (red wine vinegar, finely diced shallots, cracked black pepper), rye bread, salted butter.

| Element | Traditional | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Crushed ice, not cubed | Stable platform, even cold distribution | Cubed ice shifts; shells slide; presentation collapses |
| Two tiers | Visual architecture; separates cooked from raw | Prevents cooked-shellfish liquid from dripping onto raw |
| Mignonette, not cocktail sauce | Acid cuts brine; shallot bridges | Cocktail sauce obliterates shellfish flavour |
| Rye bread + butter | Dense, sour, absorbs liquor | White bread disintegrates; rye holds |

**On board:** This is the ultimate anchor-day service for Brittany, Normandy, or Atlantic Spain itineraries. Source that morning from the nearest market or directly from oystermen. The technique is pure cold-chain management and presentation — no cooking required. A well-built plateau for twelve guests, set on the aft deck at sunset, is the single most dramatic first impression a yacht chef can deliver. Build it at the table if space allows. The guests should see the ice, hear the shells, smell the brine.

**Pairing:** Muscadet Sèvre et Maine sur lie — Domaine de la Pépière or Luneau-Papin. Saline, taut, Atlantic.

## THE HORIZON — Bocuse d'Or 2025: The Marcon Dynasty

*January 27, 2025 · Lyon, France*

Paul Marcon won the Bocuse d'Or — thirty years after his father Régis Marcon took gold at the same competition. Commis Camille Pigot won Best Commis. Denmark (Sebastian Holberg) took silver, Sweden (Gustav Leonhardt) bronze. Norway won Best Plate. Hungary won Best Platter.

The dynasty detail is the story: Régis Marcon, three Michelin stars at Le Clos des Cimes in Saint-Bonnet-le-Froid (Auvergne), won the Bocuse d'Or in 1995. His son Paul trained under him, absorbed the same rigour, and faced the same pressure — blindfolded, effectively, because the competition's mystery-basket format rewards preparation depth, not recipe memory.

In the parallel World Pastry Cup, Japan took gold (Masanori Hata, Yuji Matoba, Ryu Miyazaki), France silver, Malaysia bronze. Belgium won the Eco-Responsible Prize. Japan's dominance in pastry competition — precision, obsessive repetition, technical range — continues to define the discipline's global standard.

The Nordic podium sweep in the Bocuse d'Or (Denmark second, Sweden third, Norway best plate) reinforces what the Michelin stars already prove: Scandinavia's technical depth in savoury cooking is now structural, not aspirational.

**Source:** Bocuse d'Or Official, January 27, 2025; Euronews; Falstaff.
