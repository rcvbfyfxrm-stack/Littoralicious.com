# Power Dynamics on Board: How Captain-Stew Relationships Affect the Galley

*February 2026 · Global*

**The Development:**
"The ol' Captain + Chief Stew combo = red flag." This observation, made in a yacht chef WhatsApp group of 300+ members, generated universal recognition -- not a single dissenting reply. It names a structural problem in the superyacht industry: when the captain and chief stewardess are in a romantic relationship, the power balance on board tilts. The galley is where the consequences land.

This is not gossip. It is a pattern with documented effects on tip distribution, leave allocation, working conditions, and crew mental health. In an industry where a captain holds near-absolute authority over hiring, firing, and disciplinary action -- and where the chief stew controls the service interface between galley and guest -- a personal relationship between these two positions creates a power consolidation that the Maritime Labour Convention's complaint mechanisms were not designed to address.

**Who's Affected:**
Every chef on a vessel where the captain and chief stew are in a relationship. The effects are most acute for sole chefs who have no departmental ally and whose tip share, leave schedule, and performance evaluations are determined by the same two people.

**What It Means:**
The documented effects, drawn from hundreds of messages across two groups, are specific and recurring:

**Tip disputes.** Tips withheld or reduced when a chef gives notice. Captains keeping a larger share. Cash tips delayed 4+ weeks. The chef who is least politically connected to the captain-stew axis has the least leverage to challenge distribution.

**Leave and scheduling.** Preferential time off for the chief stew. Rotation schedules that accommodate the relationship rather than operational need. One chef noted the captain's partner "will get time off and a raise" while the rest of the crew absorbs the workload.

**Operational friction.** Galley support eroded: stews not assisting with juice service, not restocking galley towels, not managing the fruit bowl -- tasks that fall to the chef when the stew department answers to a chief stew whose position is protected. Debate about whether stews should assist with plate service reflects a breakdown in role boundaries that correlates with the power dynamic.

**Safety.** The most severe documented cases go beyond working conditions. A stewardess was murdered by an engineer in the Bahamas. A widely-shared post about male accountability on board generated a significant thread. A detailed account emerged of a crew agency placement involving a fake yacht listing, hidden cameras in crew housing, and characteristics of a potential trafficking situation.

**Key Details:**

| Detail | Specifics |
|--------|-----------|
| Structural cause | Captain holds hiring/firing/disciplinary power; chief stew controls service interface and guest feedback |
| Tip impact | Cash tips delayed 4+ weeks; larger captain share; withholding upon chef departure |
| Leave impact | Preferential scheduling for romantically connected crew |
| Safeguards (existing) | MLC complaint procedures, DPA escalation, management company oversight |
| Safeguards (missing) | No flag state regulation addresses romantic relationships between command and department heads |

**Your Move:**
Before accepting a position, ask the crew agent directly: are the captain and chief stew in a relationship? This is not an inappropriate question -- it is due diligence about your working conditions. If you are already on a vessel where this dynamic is affecting your tip share, leave, or workload, document everything in writing. Send concerns to the DPA. The DPA exists outside the vessel's chain of command. If the management company does not have a DPA or will not provide their contact details, that is your signal to start looking.

**Source:** Yacht chef professional communities, documented accounts July 2025 – January 2026. Maritime Labour Convention, 2006, Regulation 5.1.5 (On-board complaint procedures).
