# The Great Burger Grind

**The blend is 50% short rib, 25% chuck, 25% sirloin. The fat ratio is 70:30 lean to fat. And the grind order is backwards from what you were taught.**

Most burger grinds fail not because of the meat selection but because of the grind sequence. The standard advice — coarse grind first, fine grind second — produces a uniform, dense puck. Reversing the order, grinding through the small plate (6 mm) first, then the large plate (8-10 mm), creates an irregular particle structure that holds juice, binds loosely, and cooks with a texture that is unmistakably hand-ground. This is the method.

### GROUP A: The Meat

*All beef must be well-chilled — ideally partially frozen at -2°C to 0°C (28-32°F) for 30 minutes before grinding*

| Ingredient | Weight | Volume | Notes |
|------------|--------|--------|-------|
| Bone-in short rib, deboned | 540 g | — | 50% of total. The flavour anchor. Fat content ~30-35% |
| Chuck roll | 270 g | — | 25% of total. The binder. Fat content ~20% |
| Sirloin | 270 g | — | 25% of total. The clean beef note. Fat content ~10-12% |

*Total: 1,080 g raw. Target finished weight after trimming and fat rendering: ~1,080 g (minimal trim loss with proper cuts)*

**Target fat ratio: 70:30 lean to fat.** This is not a lean burger. The short rib carries the fat load. If your butcher has already trimmed the short rib lean, add 50-80 g of beef suet to compensate.

### GROUP B: Seasoning

*Applied after grinding, never before*

| Ingredient | Weight | Volume | Notes |
|------------|--------|--------|-------|
| Fine sea salt | 10 g | 2 tsp | 1% of total meat weight |
| Freshly ground black pepper | 2 g | 1 tsp | Coarse grind |

Nothing else. No egg. No breadcrumb. No Worcestershire. The meat is the recipe.

## METHOD

### PHASE 1: Preparation
*20 minutes active + 30 minutes chilling*

**1.** Cut all beef into strips approximately 3 cm wide and 8 cm long — sized to feed through your grinder throat without forcing. Remove any sinew, silverskin, or glands. Leave all fat intact. This is where the 70:30 comes from.

**2.** Spread the cut strips on a parchment-lined sheet tray in a single layer. Place in the freezer for 30 minutes. The meat should be firm and stiff but not frozen solid — a probe should read -2°C to 0°C (28-32°F) at the centre. Warm meat smears through the grinder instead of cutting cleanly. Smeared fat coats the lean protein and prevents the loose bind that makes a great burger.

**3.** Chill your grinder parts — plates, blade, worm, and housing — in the freezer alongside the meat. Every surface that touches the beef must be cold.

> **Checkpoint:** The meat is firm, the grinder is cold, and the strips are cut to uniform size. Do not skip the chilling. This is the single most important variable.

### PHASE 2: The Reverse Grind
*10 minutes*

**4.** Assemble the grinder with the **small plate (6 mm) first.** Mix the short rib, chuck, and sirloin strips together and feed them through in a steady, unpressured stream. Let the worm do the work — forcing meat through the grinder generates friction heat, which smears the fat. Grind into a chilled bowl.

**5.** Without pausing, switch to the **large plate (8-10 mm).** Feed the already-ground meat through a second time. This is the counterintuitive step. The first grind through the small plate breaks the muscle fibres and distributes the fat evenly at a fine level. The second grind through the large plate then re-structures those fine particles into larger, irregular clusters that hold together loosely — like a handful of gravel rather than a ball of clay.

> **Checkpoint:** The ground meat should look shaggy and irregular, with visible fat distributed throughout. If it looks like a smooth paste, the meat was too warm. Start again with properly chilled beef.

### PHASE 3: Forming and Seasoning
*5 minutes*

**6.** Sprinkle 10 g salt and 2 g pepper over the ground meat. Using a fork — not your hands — gently fold the seasoning through the meat with 4-5 strokes. Do not knead. Do not compress. Overworking activates myosin, which cross-links the proteins and produces the dense, springy texture of a sausage. You are making a burger, not a meatball.

**7.** Divide into 180 g portions using a scale. Form each patty by gently pressing the meat together — not compressing it into shape. Final thickness: 2.5 cm. Press a shallow dimple into the centre of each patty with your thumb. This counteracts the protein contraction during cooking that causes burgers to dome.

> **Done when:** Each patty holds its shape when lifted but feels fragile — like it might fall apart if you squeezed it. That fragility is correct. It means the bind is loose.

### PHASE 4: Cook
*4-6 minutes*

**8.** Heat a cast iron pan or plancha to 220°C (428°F) — measured with an infrared thermometer, not guessed. The surface must be ripping hot. Add a thin film of neutral oil with a smoke point above 230°C (rapeseed or refined sunflower).

**9.** Place patties in the pan. Do not move them for 2.5 minutes. Flip once. Cook a further 2-2.5 minutes for medium-rare (internal temperature 52-54°C / 126-129°F) or 3 minutes for medium (57-60°C / 135-140°F). Use a probe thermometer. Do not press the patty with a spatula — that forces out the rendered fat you spent 2 hours protecting.

**10.** Rest for 2 minutes on a wire rack. The carryover will add 2-3°C.

## THE SCIENCE

### Why Small Plate First

**What's happening:** A standard coarse-then-fine grind produces progressively smaller, more uniform particles. The result is a dense, homogeneous mass with high myosin extraction — essentially pre-emulsified meat. Reversing the sequence creates a different particle geometry. The fine first pass distributes fat evenly throughout the lean at a micro level. The coarse second pass then re-aggregates those fine particles into irregular, loosely bound clusters with fat pockets trapped between them. When heated, those pockets render and baste the meat internally rather than pooling in the pan.

**Why it matters:** The texture difference is immediately obvious. A standard-grind burger is bouncy and uniform. A reverse-grind burger is craggy, tender, and juicy — with irregular edges that crisp unevenly on the plancha, creating a surface that is part Maillard crust, part rendered fat, part barely-held-together meat.

*Source: Lopez-Alt, J.K., The Food Lab, 2015. Butchery technique: professional practice.*

### The 70:30 Fat Ratio

**What's happening:** At 70:30, the fat content is high enough to keep the patty self-basting through the cook but not so high that it collapses on the grill. Below 80:20, most burgers begin to dry out at medium doneness. At 70:30, there is enough intramuscular and intermuscular fat to sustain rendering through the full cook without the patty losing structural integrity.

**Why it matters:** Short rib carries 30-35% fat naturally, which is why it anchors the blend. Chuck adds connective tissue that melts into gelatin during cooking, contributing body and mouth-coating richness. Sirloin is the lean counterbalance — clean, mineral beef flavour without excess fat. The three together produce a burger that tastes like beef, not like grease.

*Source: McGee, H., On Food and Cooking, 2004. Fat content data: USDA FoodData Central.*

## ELEVATION

### TIER 1: No Extra Time

| Modification | What It Does | How Much |
|--------------|--------------|----------|
| Smoked salt instead of fine sea salt | Adds smoke depth without a grill | Same 10 g ratio |
| Aged beef trimmings blended in | Adds funky, concentrated beef flavour | Replace 50 g of the chuck with dry-aged trim |

### TIER 2: Worth the Extra Effort

| Modification | What It Does | Extra Time | How To |
|--------------|--------------|------------|--------|
| Bone marrow butter | Adds richness that carries across the whole bite | +10 min | Roast split marrow bones at 200°C for 15 min. Scoop marrow, fold into softened butter 1:1. Chill. Slice a coin and place on patty during rest |
| Dry-aged blend | Concentrated beef flavour, deeper Maillard | +0 min (sourcing) | Replace 25% chuck with dry-aged chuck trim (28-day minimum). Grind the same way |

### TIER 3: Restaurant-Level

| Modification | What It Does | Extra Time | Notes |
|--------------|--------------|------------|-------|
| Double griddled smash-thin patties | Maximum crust-to-meat ratio | +3 min | Divide into 90 g balls. Smash to 5 mm on a 230°C plancha. 90 seconds per side. Stack two patties per burger |
| Rendered beef fat as cooking medium | Beef flavour amplified in the crust | +5 min | Render suet trimmings low and slow. Use the tallow in the pan instead of neutral oil |

## TROUBLESHOOTING

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Burger is dense and springy | Meat too warm during grind, or overworked during forming | Chill meat to -2°C. Form gently with minimal handling |
| Patty falls apart on the grill | Fat ratio too high, or patty not chilled enough before cooking | Verify fat ratio. Chill formed patties for 15 min before cooking |
| Grey, steamed exterior | Pan not hot enough, or too many patties crowding the surface | 220°C minimum. Cook maximum 2 patties per pan load |
| Fat pooling in pan, burger dry | Fat smeared during grinding (warm meat + warm grinder) | Every surface must be cold. Grind in a cold room if possible |
| Dome shape after cooking | No dimple pressed in centre | Press thumb dimple 5 mm deep before cooking |
