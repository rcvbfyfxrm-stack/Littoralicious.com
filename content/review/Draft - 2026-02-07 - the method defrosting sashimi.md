# Defrosting Sashimi-Grade Fish: The 3% Salt Water Method

**The Move:** Defrost sashimi-grade tuna and other frozen fish in a 3% salt brine at 40°C for exactly 1 minute, then finish overnight in the fridge. The result is colour and texture indistinguishable from never-frozen product.

**Do This:**
1. Prepare a brine: dissolve 30 g fine sea salt per litre of water. Heat to 40°C (104°F). Use a probe thermometer — do not estimate. You need enough liquid to fully submerge the fish.
2. Remove the frozen fish from its vacuum pack. Submerge it in the 40°C brine for exactly 60 seconds. Start timing from full submersion. The exterior will begin to soften; the core remains frozen solid.
3. Remove the fish. Pat dry thoroughly with paper towels — moisture on the surface accelerates oxidation and colour loss. Wrap tightly in a clean, dry muslin cloth or paper towel, then in cling film. The cloth wicks residual surface moisture away from the flesh during the slow thaw.
4. Place in the coldest part of the fridge — 1-3°C (34-37°F). Allow a minimum of 12 hours, up to 18 hours for loins thicker than 8 cm. The fish thaws from the outside in, slowly and evenly.

**Why It Works:**
The 3% salt concentration matches the approximate salinity of ocean water and, critically, the intracellular fluid concentration of marine fish muscle. When frozen fish contacts fresh water, osmosis pulls water into the cells, rupturing them and producing the mushy texture that ruins thawed sashimi. A 3% brine equalises osmotic pressure across the cell membrane, preventing water influx and maintaining cellular integrity. The 40°C temperature softens the surface ice layer just enough to allow the salt solution to make contact with the flesh, initiating the protective osmotic equilibrium — but the 60-second window ensures the core stays well below 4°C (39°F), preventing any bacterial risk in the temperature danger zone. The slow fridge finish allows ice crystals to melt gradually rather than rupturing cells through rapid thermal shock.

The colour preservation is a separate mechanism. Myoglobin — the protein responsible for the deep red colour of tuna — oxidises to brown metmyoglobin when exposed to air and warmth. By keeping the surface exposure to warm water under 60 seconds and immediately wrapping to exclude air, the myoglobin remains in its reduced, red state. Slow thawing in a low-oxygen wrap extends this further.

**The Variables:**

| Factor | What Happens | Adjust By |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| Salt concentration below 2% | Osmotic imbalance — water enters cells, texture turns soft and waterlogged | Weigh salt precisely. 30 g per litre, not a pinch |
| Salt concentration above 4% | Surface begins to cure slightly, texture becomes firm, flavour turns salty | Weigh salt precisely. This is not the place for approximation |
| Brine temperature above 50°C | Surface begins to cook. Myoglobin denatures. Colour turns grey-brown at the edges | Use a probe thermometer. 40°C is the ceiling |
| Submersion time beyond 90 seconds | Core begins to warm unevenly, creating texture gradient between surface and centre | Set a timer. 60 seconds. Remove the fish |
| Thawing in fridge above 4°C | Bacterial growth accelerates; texture degrades faster; colour oxidises | Verify fridge temperature with a separate thermometer, not the built-in display |

**You Know It Worked When:**
The thawed fish is uniformly coloured — deep red for tuna, translucent for white fish — with no grey or brown patches at the surface. The flesh springs back when pressed lightly with a fingertip, returning to shape within 1 second. There is no pooled liquid in the wrapping. The texture when sliced is clean and firm, with the knife passing through without tearing or dragging. If you see milky liquid pooling around the fish, the osmotic balance was wrong or the thaw was too fast.

**At Sea:**
This method is built for yacht galleys. Sashimi-grade fish is almost always frozen aboard — fresh tuna landed that morning is a provisioning rarity outside of specific ports. The overnight fridge thaw means you prep this the day before service, not the morning of. Label and date the wrapped fish. If the fridge temperature fluctuates during a crossing, move the fish to the coldest zone and extend the thaw time rather than accelerating with warm water.

**Source:** The technique is widely practised in Japanese sushi preparation and documented in Kazuo Nagashima's protocols for handling frozen maguro.
