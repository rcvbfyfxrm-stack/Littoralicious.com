## Sucre
*Saccharum officinarum · New Guinea, ~8000 BCE · Year-round*

**$48,900.** That's what the sugar industry paid three Harvard scientists in 1967 — roughly $400,000 in today's money — to publish a review in the *New England Journal of Medicine* that shifted the blame for heart disease from sugar to dietary fat. The scientists were named. The Sugar Research Foundation selected the studies. The conclusion was predetermined. Fifty years of low-fat dietary guidelines, an obesity epidemic, and millions of deaths from metabolic disease followed. The paper was not retracted until the payments were uncovered in 2016 by researchers at UCSF who found the correspondence in archived industry documents.

**What It Actually Is:**
Sucrose — a disaccharide, two simple sugars (glucose and fructose) bonded together. Your body cannot absorb sucrose directly; the enzyme sucrase in the small intestine cleaves the bond, releasing glucose (which enters the bloodstream and fuels every cell) and fructose (which goes almost entirely to the liver for processing). This distinction matters. Glucose triggers insulin. Fructose triggers lipogenesis — fat production in the liver. They arrive in the same crystal, but their metabolic fates are entirely different. "Sugar" is not one thing. It is two molecules handcuffed together, doing different jobs the moment they separate.

**The Story That Sticks:**
Between 1501 and 1867, approximately 12.5 million Africans were shipped across the Atlantic in chains. Sugar was the reason for most of them.

The Caribbean sugar plantation was the most profitable enterprise in the 18th-century world — and the most lethal. Average life expectancy after arrival on a Barbados sugar estate: seven years. The work of cutting, hauling, and milling cane in tropical heat was so brutal that plantation owners calculated it was cheaper to work enslaved people to death and buy replacements than to improve conditions. Britain's per capita sugar consumption rose from near zero in 1700 to 10 kg by 1800 — sweetened tea, the new national drink, fueled by the blood of people whose names were never recorded.

The Haitian Revolution of 1791-1804 — the only successful large-scale slave revolt in history — was born in the cane fields. Sugar built the wealth of Bristol, Bordeaux, Lisbon, Amsterdam. The elegant Georgian townhouses tourists photograph today were paid for in molasses and human cargo. The sweetness in your cup has a history most prefer not to taste.

**The Tradition:**
Caramelization is sugar's oldest transformation and its most misunderstood. At 160°C, sucrose molecules begin breaking apart — not melting, decomposing. Over 100 new compounds form: diacetyl (buttery), furanones (caramel), maltol (toasty), hydroxymethylfurfural (bitter depth). Dry caramel is not sugar plus heat. It is a new substance with a new chemistry, as different from sucrose as bread crust is from flour.

In Persia, *nabat* — rock sugar crystallized slowly on threads suspended in syrup — has been made since the 9th century. Japanese *wagashi* confectionery requires *wasanbon*, a fine-grained sugar hand-kneaded for days from a specific sugarcane variety grown only on Shikoku. In France, the *canelé* of Bordeaux — caramelized crust, custardy center — was born from the egg whites used for fining wine in the Chartrons district; the yolks went to the nuns, who added sugar, rum, and vanilla. Every confectionery tradition is a record of trade routes, colonial access, and who could afford sweetness.

**The Compound That Matters:**

| Compound | Also Found In | What It Does |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| Sucrose (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁) | Maple sap, sugar beet, most fruits | The baseline. Activates T1R2/T1R3 sweet taste receptors on the tongue. Benchmark against which all other sweeteners are measured. Reference sweetness = 1.0. |
| Hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF) | Honey (increases with age/heat), roasted coffee, balsamic vinegar | Formed during caramelization and Maillard reactions. Responsible for the bitter, complex depth in dark caramel, aged honey, and reduction syrups. The compound that separates pale caramel from great caramel. |
| Diacetyl (2,3-butanedione) | Butter, Chardonnay, beer | Buttery, toffee note produced during caramelization above 170°C. The reason butterscotch tastes buttery without butter — this molecule fools the brain. Also present in brown butter, creating the bridge between caramel and beurre noisette. |

**Three Pairings You Haven't Tried:**
*Based on shared volatile compounds and cross-modal chemistry:*

1. **Dry caramel + soy sauce** — Shared furanones and Maillard products. Deglaze a dark caramel (180°C, amber, smoking slightly) with 20ml good soy sauce. The umami amplifies the caramel's depth while the salt suppresses bitterness. Glaze roasted pork belly or seared duck breast. Two ingredients. A sauce that tastes like it took hours.
2. **Muscovado sugar + raw ginger** — Shared gingerols and phenolic warmth compounds. The molasses in muscovado provides the same volatile overlap as fresh ginger's pungency. Grate raw ginger into muscovado simple syrup (1:1, barely warmed). Use in a Dark and Stormy or over grilled pineapple. The depth will not come from white sugar.
3. **Caramelized white chocolate + passion fruit** — Valrhona's Dulcey or any white chocolate roasted at 160°C for 20 minutes develops Maillard-driven lactones and furanones that share volatile profiles with passion fruit's sulfur compounds and tropical esters. Ganache the caramelized chocolate, pipe it, top with fresh passion fruit pulp. The acidity cuts the sweetness. The shared aromatics make them taste like they grew on the same vine.

**The Science That Surprises:**
Sugar does not cause hyperactivity in children. This has been tested repeatedly and definitively. Wolraich et al. (1995) conducted a meta-analysis of 23 controlled trials in *JAMA* and found zero evidence that sugar affects behavior or cognitive performance in children. In double-blind studies where parents were told their child had consumed sugar (regardless of whether they actually had), parents rated the child's behavior as more hyperactive. The "sugar high" is a parental expectation, not a physiological event.

What sugar does do — unambiguously — is drive insulin resistance, hepatic lipogenesis, and metabolic syndrome when consumed in chronic excess. Lustig et al. (2012) in *Nature* argued that fructose meets the criteria for regulation as a controlled substance: toxicity, ubiquity, potential for abuse, and negative societal impact. The dose makes the poison. At 5g in a dessert after dinner, sugar is a tool. At 75g per day in processed food — the current American average — it is a public health catastrophe.

**Where Most Go Wrong:**

1. **Stirring caramel.** Once sugar dissolves and reaches a boil, put the spoon down. Stirring introduces movement that encourages crystallization — one rogue crystal can seed the entire pan into a grainy mass. If color is uneven, swirl the pan gently. If crystals form on the sides, brush them down with a wet pastry brush. The caramel knows what it's doing. Let it work.
2. **Fearing dark caramel.** Most pastry stops at light amber — 170°C, sweet, one-dimensional. Push to 185-190°C — dark amber, faintly smoking, the scent shifting from candy to coffee — and the complexity multiplies. More HMF, more diacetyl, more furanones. The bitterness at this stage isn't a flaw. It's the flavor. The difference between caramel as decoration and caramel as an ingredient.
3. **Substituting sugars as if they're interchangeable.** White sugar is pure sucrose. Brown sugar is sucrose plus 3-6% molasses. Honey is 38% fructose, 31% glucose, and 200+ volatile compounds. Maple syrup is 67% sucrose with furanones and phenolics from the tree. They behave differently in every application — browning rate, moisture content, acidity, crystal structure. A recipe calling for 200g caster sugar will not work with 200g honey. The chemistry changes. The water content changes. The result changes.

**Sourcing:**
- **Look for:** Origin stated, minimal processing described. Caster sugar (fine crystal) for pastry — dissolves faster, aerates better. Demerara for crunch. Muscovado (dark or light) when you want molasses depth without buying molasses.
- **Avoid:** "Raw" or "natural" sugar marketed as healthy — turbinado, raw cane, coconut sugar are all sucrose with trace minerals at nutritionally irrelevant quantities. Your body cannot distinguish their fructose from white sugar's fructose. Buy them for flavor, not for virtue.
- **Good vs. great:** Standard caster and granulated — the workhorses, buy whatever is cheapest. For finishing and flavor: **Muscovado** (Billington's or Mauritius) — deep, complex, toffee-forward, irreplaceable in sticky toffee pudding and rum cocktails. **Panela/piloncillo** — unrefined Latin American cane sugar, smoky and round, extraordinary in sauces and braises. **Wasanbon** — Japanese hand-kneaded sugar, dissolves instantly, ethereal sweetness, for wagashi or any preparation where sugar should disappear into silk.

**On Board:**
- **Storage:** Airtight, always. White sugar is hygroscopic — it pulls moisture from marine air and clumps. Brown sugar does the opposite — it dries out, hardens into a brick. A small terracotta disc (soaked in water, sealed in the container) keeps brown sugar soft for months. White sugar in a sealed tub with a silica gel packet. Both away from heat sources.
- **Shelf life:** White sugar does not expire. It is a pure chemical compound in crystal form — no moisture, no protein, nothing to spoil. Brown sugar keeps 18-24 months sealed. Honey never expires — the 3,000-year-old honey found in Egyptian tombs was still edible. Maple syrup, once opened, refrigerate — it can grow mold on the surface.
- **The move:** Dry caramel. 150g caster sugar in a clean, dry, heavy-bottomed pan over medium heat. Do not touch it. When the edges begin to liquefy, swirl gently. Wait. Let it go past amber to deep mahogany — 185°C, the scent of roasted coffee, thin smoke rising. Kill the heat. Add 100ml warm cream in a steady stream (it will erupt — stand back). Whisk. Add 30g cold butter, a pinch of flaky salt. Pour into a jar. You now have salted caramel better than anything with a label on it. Five minutes. Four ingredients. No recipe needed once you've done it twice.
