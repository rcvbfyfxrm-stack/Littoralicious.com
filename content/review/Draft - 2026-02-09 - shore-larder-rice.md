## Riz
*Oryza sativa · Pearl River Delta, China, ~9,000 BCE · Year-round*

**3.5 billion.** That is the number of people who depend on rice as their primary calorie source. Half the planet. No other single food comes close. Wheat feeds more countries, but rice feeds more mouths — and it does so on less arable land, in standing water, in conditions no other cereal grain tolerates. It is the most consumed food crop on earth, with over 520 million tonnes produced annually. And yet most professional kitchens in the West treat it as filler. A bed. A side. Something to soak up the sauce. This is a failure of understanding, not of the grain. The difference between mediocre rice and extraordinary rice comes down to a single molecular ratio — amylose to amylopectin — and almost nobody cooking it professionally can tell you what that ratio is, what it controls, or why it matters. It controls everything.

**What It Actually Is:**
A semi-aquatic grass — one of only a handful of cereal crops that grows submerged in water. The edible grain is the seed of *Oryza sativa*, which divides into two major subspecies: *indica* (long-grain, grown in tropical lowlands — basmati, jasmine, most of the rice eaten in South and Southeast Asia) and *japonica* (short-grain, grown in temperate climates — sushi rice, arborio, the rice of Japan, Korea, and northern China). The distinction is not cosmetic. *Indica* rices are typically high in amylose (20-30% of total starch), which means the cooked grains separate, stay firm, and do not cling. *Japonica* rices are lower in amylose (15-20%) and higher in amylopectin — the branched starch molecule that gelatinizes into a sticky, cohesive mass. Glutinous or "sticky" rice has virtually zero amylose — it is nearly pure amylopectin, which is why it compresses into a single, pliable sheet rather than individual grains. This one ratio — amylose to amylopectin — determines whether your rice is fluffy or sticky, whether it holds its shape in a pilaf or dissolves into congee. It is not technique. It is chemistry. Technique follows.

**The Story That Sticks:**
In 1966, at the International Rice Research Institute in Los Banos, Philippines, a team of plant breeders released a new variety designated IR8. It was squat, stiff-stemmed, and spectacularly ugly — nothing like the tall, elegant landraces that had fed Asia for millennia. It also yielded ten tonnes per hectare, nearly triple the output of traditional varieties. IR8 became the "Miracle Rice" of the Green Revolution. It spread across Asia within a decade. Combined with synthetic fertilizers and irrigation, it doubled or tripled production in India, the Philippines, and Indonesia between 1966 and 1980.

The math is not in dispute: IR8 and its descendants saved an estimated one billion people from famine (Khush, 2001, *Nature Reviews Genetics*). But the trade-off was real. The traditional varieties IR8 replaced carried 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline and dozens of other volatiles bred into them over thousands of years of selection for aroma and flavor. IR8 carried yield. The world got more rice. It got blander rice. The landraces that survived — basmati, jasmine, black rice, red rice — are the ones where farmers chose flavor over tonnage. They are not premium products. They are the remainders of what we almost lost.

**The Tradition:**
In Japan, rice washing is not preparation. It is ritual and engineering. *Togu* — the act of washing rice — removes surface starch (loose amylopectin released during milling) that would otherwise cook into a gummy, cloudy paste around each grain. The method: submerge, swirl gently with fingertips, drain the milky water, repeat until the water runs nearly clear — typically five to seven rinses. The first wash uses cold water and is drained immediately, before the dry grain absorbs the starchy runoff. Time matters. Soaking follows: 30 minutes in summer, 60 minutes in winter, allowing water to penetrate to the grain's core so it cooks evenly from the inside out. Japanese rice culture treats this as non-negotiable. It is also physically measurable — washed rice produces a cooked grain with a distinct, clean surface that reflects light differently than unwashed rice. The Japanese word for cooked rice — *gohan* — is the same word for "meal." The grain is not part of the meal. It is the meal itself.

In northern India, basmati is aged for 12 to 24 months before sale. The aging reduces moisture content, allows the grain to harden, and produces a rice that elongates dramatically when cooked — sometimes doubling in length — without breaking. Aged basmati smells different from fresh: deeper, more pronouncedly nutty, the 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline having concentrated as the grain dried. In Thailand, jasmine rice is prized in its new-crop form — *khao mai* — fragrant, soft, and slightly sticky. Two rice cultures, two opposite preferences for the same variable: age. Both are correct. Both know why.

**The Compound That Matters:**

| Compound | Also Found In | What It Does |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| 2-Acetyl-1-pyrroline (2-AP) | Pandan leaves, popcorn, bread crust, white bread crumb | The single molecule responsible for the aroma of basmati and jasmine rice. A heterocyclic compound formed via the Maillard reaction between proline and sugars. Aromatic rice varieties contain 0.04-0.09 ppm; non-aromatic varieties contain roughly one-tenth that amount. It is volatile and degrades over time, which is why freshly harvested jasmine rice smells more intense than year-old stock. Genetic control: a single recessive gene (*badh2*) that produces a non-functional enzyme, allowing 2-AP to accumulate instead of being metabolized. The fragrance is literally a broken enzyme. |
| Amylose | Potatoes, corn, wheat, all starch-bearing plants | The linear starch molecule. In rice, its concentration ranges from 0% (glutinous varieties) to 30% (long-grain indica). When cooked, amylose leaches into the cooking water and, upon cooling, retrogrades — reassembles into crystalline structures that resist digestion. This is resistant starch (see below). High-amylose rice cooks dry and firm. Low-amylose rice cooks soft and sticky. The amylose percentage printed nowhere on the bag is the single most important number determining how your rice will behave. |
| Inorganic arsenic (iAs) | Groundwater, soil in arsenic-rich regions, apple juice, seaweed | Rice accumulates arsenic at roughly ten times the rate of other cereal grains because it is grown in flooded paddies where anaerobic conditions mobilize arsenite from the soil into solution. The FDA's 2016 risk assessment found inorganic arsenic concentrations in US rice averaging 92 ppb (parts per billion), with brown rice consistently higher than white — the arsenic concentrates in the bran. The WHO recommends a limit of 200 ppb for polished white rice and 400 ppb for husked (brown) rice. Not a reason to stop eating rice. A reason to rinse it, cook it in excess water (6:1 ratio, drain), and vary your grains. Rinsing alone reduces inorganic arsenic by 10-30% (FDA, 2016). |

**Three Pairings You Haven't Tried:**
*Based on shared volatile compounds and complementary chemistry:*

1. **Jasmine rice + pandan + coconut milk** — All three share 2-acetyl-1-pyrroline as a dominant volatile. Cook 300g jasmine rice in 400ml coconut milk with two pandan leaves knotted and buried in the grain. The 2-AP concentration stacks from three independent sources, creating an aroma intensity none of the three achieves alone. Serve alongside anything grilled. The rice becomes the centrepiece, not the background.
2. **Cooled sushi rice + white miso + raw egg yolk** — Tamago kake gohan, reimagined. Freshly cooked short-grain rice seasoned with rice vinegar (30ml per 300g), a tablespoon of white miso dissolved into the warm grain, topped with a raw egg yolk and a few drops of soy. The glutamic acid in the miso and the egg yolk's emulsified fats amplify the rice's own subtle umami. Breakfast or midnight snack. Under three minutes.
3. **Toasted rice powder + lime + chili** — *Khao khua* — the backbone of Thai larb. Dry-toast 100g raw sticky rice in a pan over medium heat until deep gold and fragrant — the Maillard reaction generates pyrazines and furanones shared with roasted coffee and cocoa. Grind to a coarse powder. Fold into any salad or tartare with lime juice and bird's eye chili. The powder adds a toasted, nutty crunch and absorbs dressing so the salad never pools at the bottom. A Thai technique with universal application.

**The Science That Surprises:**
When cooked rice is cooled to room temperature or refrigerated, a portion of its starch undergoes retrogradation — the amylose chains reassemble into tight crystalline structures that human digestive enzymes cannot easily break down. This is resistant starch (RS3), and it behaves more like dietary fibre than like a carbohydrate. A 2015 study from the College of Chemical Sciences in Sri Lanka (Sudhair James et al., presented at the American Chemical Society National Meeting) demonstrated that cooking rice with a teaspoon of coconut oil and then cooling it for 12 hours increased resistant starch content by up to ten-fold compared to normally cooked rice. The lipid-amylose complex formed during cooking resisted enzymatic digestion even after reheating.

The practical consequence: day-old rice is not just different in texture from fresh rice. It is metabolically different. It produces a lower glycemic response. Fried rice, rice salads, onigiri, and any preparation that uses cooled rice delivers less available glucose per gram than the same rice served hot. The Chinese, Japanese, and Thai traditions of using leftover rice for fried rice and congee are not frugality. They are — inadvertently — nutritional optimization.

**Where Most Go Wrong:**

1. **Not rinsing.** Surface starch left on milled rice cooks into a gummy paste that glues grains together and turns the pot cloudy. This is not a seasoning. It is milling residue. Rinse in cold water until the water runs mostly clear — three to five changes for most white rice, more for short-grain japonica. The one exception: risotto. Arborio and carnaroli are not rinsed because the surface starch is the mechanism — it dissolves into the cooking liquid to create the creamy emulsion that defines the dish. Know the difference. Rinse everything else.
2. **Lifting the lid.** Rice steams. Once the water is absorbed and you reduce to the lowest heat, steam pressure inside the pot finishes the cooking. Every time you lift the lid, you release steam, drop the temperature, and disrupt the gradient that is cooking the top layer of grain. Japanese rice cookers are engineered to never open during the cook-steam cycle. Ten minutes cooking, ten minutes steaming, lid sealed. If you are cooking on a hob, resist the urge. Set a timer. Walk away. The rice does not need you watching it.
3. **Using the wrong water ratio for the wrong rice.** There is no universal ratio. Long-grain basmati: 1:1.5 (rice to water by volume), after soaking 30 minutes. Short-grain japonica: 1:1.2 — it needs less water because the grain absorbs more during the mandatory soak. Brown rice: 1:2.5 and 45 minutes of cooking. Sticky rice: no boiling at all — soak overnight and steam over water, never submerged in it. The ratio printed on the bag is an average. The ratio for your specific rice, at your altitude, with your burner, must be learned by cooking it five times and adjusting. There is no shortcut.

**Sourcing:**
- **Look for:** Crop year or harvest date on the bag — rice is seasonal even though it is sold year-round. Varietal name, not just "long grain" or "short grain." Country of origin. For basmati: "aged" or "extra-aged" on Indian brands. For Japanese rice: *shinmai* (new crop) if you want softer, stickier, more fragrant grain.
- **Avoid:** Blended rice — bags labeled "basmati blend" or "jasmine mix" combine cheap broken grain with whole kernels. Pre-seasoned or "ready" rice — the sodium content is unnecessary and the texture is compromised. Any rice stored in transparent bags under fluorescent light — UV degrades 2-AP and other volatiles.
- **Good vs. great:** Good is any single-variety rice from a reputable mill, stored properly, cooked correctly. Great is Koshihikari from Niigata prefecture, aged Dehradun basmati from India's Himalayan foothills, or Hom Mali jasmine from Thung Kula Rong Hai in Thailand's Isan region. The difference is terroir — the same concept that separates a Burgundy from a table wine. Soil, water, altitude, and post-harvest handling. For a yacht provisioning run: Tilda for basmati, Koda Farms for Kokuho Rose (US-grown japonica), and any Thai Hom Mali certified jasmine rice. Consistent, available, and genuinely good.

**On Board:**
- **Storage:** Airtight containers, always. Rice weevils (*Sitophilus oryzae*) are the primary threat — they bore into individual grains to lay eggs, and by the time you see adult insects, the infestation is weeks old. Bay leaves in the container deter but do not eliminate them. Freezing rice for 72 hours before transferring to storage kills eggs and larvae — do this with every new bag before it enters the dry store. Keep rice away from the hull where condensation forms. Humidity above 14% promotes mould and mycotoxin development. In tropical passages, check weekly.
- **Shelf life:** White rice, properly stored: 12 months minimum, functionally indefinite if sealed and dry. Brown rice: 3-6 months at room temperature — the bran oils oxidize and go rancid. In a warm galley, cut that to 8-12 weeks. If it smells musty or like old oil, it has turned. Wild rice (which is not rice — it is *Zizania*, an aquatic grass): 6 months sealed.
- **The move:** Persian *tahdig* — the crisp, golden rice crust that everyone fights over. Par-cook 400g basmati (soaked 1 hour, boiled 6 minutes in salted water, drained). Mix 100g of the par-cooked rice with 60ml yoghurt and a pinch of saffron bloomed in 2 tablespoons hot water. Spread this mixture across the bottom of a non-stick pot with 50ml neutral oil, heated to a shimmer. Layer the remaining rice on top, mounding it into a cone. Wrap the lid in a tea towel to absorb condensation. Cook 10 minutes on medium, then 40 minutes on the lowest flame. Invert onto a platter. The bottom is a shatteringly crisp, golden disc of saffron-stained rice. The interior is perfectly steamed, each grain separate. It is architecture disguised as a side dish, and the moment you crack through the crust at the table, every guest stops talking. Twenty minutes of active time. An hour of passive heat. The single most impressive rice dish you can produce on a yacht.
