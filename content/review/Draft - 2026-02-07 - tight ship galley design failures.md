# Galley Design: What Shipyards Get Wrong

*Category: Equipment / Galley Infrastructure*

**The Pain:**
"The beating heart of a boat is the galley — why are they so shit on below 50m?" This question, asked in the Yacht Chef WhatsApp Group, received no dissent. Zero. The galley is where the most demanding work on the vessel happens, yet it is consistently the space that receives the least intelligent design input. Shipyard designers consult naval architects, interior decorators, and owners. They rarely consult the person who will spend 16 hours a day in the space, under pressure, feeding the people who paid EUR 50 million for the boat.

This is a catalogue of the ten most common galley design failures, documented from working yacht chefs across two WhatsApp groups with over 1,300 members combined. Each failure has been reported independently by multiple chefs on different vessels built by different yards. These are not edge cases. These are patterns.

---

## Failure 1: Domestic Appliances on a Commercial Vessel

One chef described it bluntly: "One domestic sized oven, fridge and freezer — all half the size of ones in my home. Lack of storage and prep area can be an absolute joke."

A 50m yacht with 12 guests and 14 crew requires feeding 26 people, three meals a day, plus snacks, afternoon tea, and late-night requests. That is a small restaurant's output. Yet shipyards install household-grade appliances rated for a family of four.

**The fix:** Specify commercial-grade appliances at the build stage. A Rational iCombi Pro (polled at 33 votes vs. 0 for any competitor) takes the same footprint as two domestic ovens and does the work of four. The cost difference is negligible against a EUR 50m build budget. There is no rational argument for installing a Miele oven on a yacht — "fuck Miele" is not editorial commentary, it is professional consensus based on years of documented failures, shattered cooktops, and simultaneous dual-oven breakdowns.

---

## Failure 2: Inadequate Electrical Infrastructure

One burner tripping the entire galley is not an equipment failure. It is a design failure. The electrical infrastructure was undersized at build.

A professional galley on a 50m+ vessel needs dedicated circuits for:

| Appliance | Typical Draw | Circuit Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Combi oven (Rational iCombi Pro) | 18–40 kW (depending on model) | Dedicated 3-phase circuit |
| Induction hob (4-zone) | 7–11 kW | Dedicated circuit, not shared with oven |
| Dishwasher (Hobart commercial) | 6–9 kW | Dedicated circuit with hot water feed |
| Blast chiller | 2–4 kW | Dedicated circuit |
| Thermomixer/food processor | 1.5–2 kW | Shared circuit acceptable |
| Chest freezer | 0.3–0.5 kW | Dedicated or shared low-draw circuit |

**Total galley electrical requirement:** 35–70 kW depending on configuration, before lighting and ventilation.

When a shipyard allocates 20 kW to the galley because "it's just a kitchen," the chef cannot run the oven and the hob simultaneously. Service becomes a game of electrical Tetris.

**The fix:** During build or refit, demand a load calculation for the galley as a separate zone. Every appliance must be listed with its peak draw. The total must include 20% headroom for simultaneous operation. If the yard says "you won't run everything at once," fire the yard.

## Failure 3: Power Tripping During Generator Switching

Yachts switch between shore power, generators, and sometimes battery banks. Each transition creates a momentary power interruption. In a well-designed electrical system, this is managed by automatic transfer switches with seamless changeover. In a poorly designed system, the galley loses power for 2–15 seconds every time the vessel transitions from anchor generator to main generator, or from shore power to generator during departure.

2 seconds is enough to reset a combi oven mid-programme. 15 seconds is enough to interrupt a blast chiller cycle and compromise food safety.

**The fix:** Uninterruptible power supply (UPS) for critical galley equipment — specifically the combi oven and any blast chiller. This is standard in data centres and hospital kitchens. It costs EUR 2,000–5,000 per unit. On a EUR 40m vessel, this is a rounding error.

## Failure 4: Criminal Refrigeration

"3 upright Liebherrs for 14 crew world cruising — idiotic." This was not a budget build. This was an expensive yacht with a refrigeration specification written by someone who has never provisioned for a crossing.

### The Calculation

| Factor | Guideline |
|--------|-----------|
| Refrigerated storage per person (guest) | 40–50 litres minimum |
| Refrigerated storage per person (crew) | 25–30 litres minimum |
| Frozen storage per person (guest) | 20–30 litres |
| Frozen storage per person (crew) | 15–20 litres |
| Buffer for crossing/remote cruising | +50% of total |

**Example: 12 guests + 14 crew on a 50m**

| Storage Type | Calculation | Minimum Required |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Fridge (guest) | 12 × 45L | 540 litres |
| Fridge (crew) | 14 × 27L | 378 litres |
| Freezer (guest) | 12 × 25L | 300 litres |
| Freezer (crew) | 14 × 17L | 238 litres |
| **Total fridge** | | **918 litres + 50% buffer = 1,377 litres** |
| **Total freezer** | | **538 litres + 50% buffer = 807 litres** |

Three domestic Liebherr upright refrigerators provide roughly 1,050 litres of combined fridge space and no dedicated freezer volume. That is 24% below the minimum fridge requirement and 100% below the freezer requirement.

**The fix:** Walk-in fridge and walk-in freezer. On any vessel over 40m, this should be non-negotiable. Below 40m, under-counter commercial refrigeration (not domestic units) with chest freezers in the lazarette or crew area. Chest freezers maintain temperature 40% more efficiently than upright units because cold air does not fall out every time you open the door.

## Failure 5: No Chef's Workstation

The founder of Superyacht Galley Designs argues that chefs need a proper office space for paperwork — menu planning, provisioning orders, allergen documentation, guest preference management, budgets. He is right.

A yacht chef manages a food operation with a budget of EUR 50,000–200,000+ per year. That requires a desk, a screen, and a space separated from the cooking environment where documents stay dry, clean, and organised. Most galleys offer none of this. The chef does admin on a cutting board, on a phone, or crouched in a crew cabin after a 16-hour shift.

**The fix:** A 1m x 0.6m fold-down desk with a waterproof laptop shelf, integrated power outlets (one 220V, two USB-C), and a clip-mounted tablet holder. This can be integrated into any galley during a refit for under EUR 3,000.

## Failure 6: No Dumbwaiter (or a Useless One)

A poll across the WhatsApp groups asked whether 50–70m boats should have dumbwaiters: 29 said No, 12 said Yes. But the "No" votes came with a caveat — they do not want the dumbwaiters that currently exist. A heated dumbwaiter, tall enough for a full tray with plate covers, would change the answer.

The standard yacht dumbwaiter is too small for plates with cloches, unheated (food arrives cold to the upper deck), and mechanically unreliable. Chefs bypass it and carry plates up ladders. A dumbwaiter that creates more problems than it solves is worse than no dumbwaiter at all.

**The fix:** If installing a dumbwaiter, specify: interior height of 500mm minimum (to clear cloches), heated to 65°C holding temperature, interior width for a standard service tray (530mm x 325mm GN), and marine-grade stainless mechanism rated for 10+ years. If you cannot build it to this specification, do not build it.

## Failure 7: Inadequate Ventilation

A combi oven at full steam produces litres of vapour per hour. An induction hob at full power heats the ambient air. A dishwasher on a hot cycle adds humidity. In a galley with ceiling heights of 2.0–2.2m and floor area under 12 square metres, inadequate ventilation turns the space into a sauna within 30 minutes of service.

Poor ventilation is not just a comfort problem. It is a food safety problem (ambient temperature above 25°C accelerates bacterial growth on exposed prep) and a health problem (heat exhaustion during a 14-hour shift in a 38°C galley).

**The fix:** Extraction rated at minimum 30 air changes per hour for the galley space, with make-up air to prevent negative pressure (which pulls engine room fumes into the galley through gaps in bulkheads). The extraction hood must cover the combi oven and the hob — not one or the other.

---

## Failure 8: Galley Floor That Becomes a Skating Rink

Shipyard designers select galley flooring for aesthetics. The chef works on it in conditions that include rendered fat, fish liquid, dishwasher overflow, condensation from refrigeration units, and spilled stock. A polished stone or smooth composite floor in these conditions is a personal injury lawsuit waiting for a jurisdiction.

**The fix:** Anti-slip rating of R12 minimum (DIN 51130 standard) for any galley floor surface. Teak gratings or commercial anti-fatigue mats over the base floor. Drainage — at least one floor drain with a grate, positioned at the low point of the galley. Water on the galley floor is not an exception. It is the daily condition.

## Failure 9: Faucets and Plumbing Afterthoughts

The galley faucet is used hundreds of times per day. It must be operable with one hand (often an elbow or wrist), deliver both cold rinse water and hot cleaning water, and survive salt air for years.

The recommendation from the groups is specific: **Echtermann SmartClean or ComfortClean** — "the best I've seen." These are commercial galley faucets designed for marine environments, with lever operation, pre-rinse spray, and marine-grade stainless construction.

Domestic kitchen faucets with ceramic cartridges fail within 12–18 months in a marine environment. The salt corrodes the cartridge housing. Replacement requires a plumber, dry dock time, and the inevitable delay of parts shipping to wherever the boat happens to be.

**The fix:** Specify commercial marine-grade faucets at build. Echtermann is the benchmark. Budget EUR 800–1,500 per faucet versus EUR 200–400 for domestic. The domestic faucet will be replaced three times in the life of one Echtermann.

---

## Failure 10: No Latching System for Rough Weather

Drawers fly open. Doors swing. Equipment slides off countertops. The first beam swell of the season reveals every unsecured surface in the galley.

**The fix:** SVB twist locks on every drawer and cabinet door. Fiddle rails (50mm minimum height) on all counter surfaces. Non-slip matting under all countertop appliances. Anti-vibration feet on the combi oven. These are aftermarket solutions to a problem that should have been designed out at build. Total retrofit cost: EUR 500–2,000 depending on galley size. Every yacht chef has done this themselves because the shipyard did not.

## The Systemic Problem

As one chef put it: "Galley size and quality is not discussed enough."

The root cause is structural. Shipyard design processes do not include the chef. Interior designers specify the galley based on aesthetic continuity with the rest of the vessel. Naval architects allocate space based on structural requirements. The owner approves based on renderings that show gleaming surfaces and invisible appliances. Nobody asks: "Can a chef actually produce 78 meals a day in this room?"

Superyacht Galley Designs (a consultancy and YouTube channel dedicated to galley specification) exists specifically to address this gap. If you are joining a new build or a refit, advocate for a galley design consultation with someone who has cooked professionally at sea. The cost of a consultant is EUR 5,000–15,000. The cost of a galley refit after the first season when the chef quits is EUR 100,000+.

**The Catch:**
Most of these failures cannot be fixed without a refit. If you are on an existing vessel with a bad galley, your options are limited to aftermarket improvements (latches, mats, portable equipment, UPS units). The real audience for this article is the chef who has the captain's ear during a new build or refit — and the captain willing to listen.

**Why It Works:**
A well-designed galley is a retention tool. The most common reason chefs leave yachts is not salary — it is working conditions. A vessel that invests EUR 50,000 in a proper galley specification will save EUR 200,000 in recruitment fees, training time, and the chaos of chef turnover across a 5-year ownership period.
