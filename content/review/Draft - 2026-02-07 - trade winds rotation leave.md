# Rotation, Leave, and the 5:1 Lie

*February 2026 · Global*

**The Development:**
The yacht industry's standard offer of "60 days leave per year" has become the most common contract provision that means almost nothing. Marketed as rotation, it is not rotation. Rotation means a fixed schedule where you leave the vessel on a predictable date and return on a predictable date, with a replacement covering your absence. What most contracts actually deliver is discretionary leave — taken when the captain decides it's convenient, often cancelled when a last-minute charter appears, and rarely totalling the promised 60 days by year's end. One widely shared post in the yacht chef community — "5:1 is NOT rotation" — crystallised what the industry already knows but refuses to say plainly: five months on and one month off is an endurance test dressed up as a benefit.

**Who's Affected:**
Every yacht chef on a permanent contract, particularly sole chefs without a rotation partner. Chefs on charter vessels are hit hardest — leave windows collapse during peak season, and by the time summer ends, half the promised days have evaporated.

**What It Means:**
The distinction between "rotation" and "leave" is not semantic. It is structural. True rotation — 2:2 (two months on, two months off) or the increasingly sought 10:10 (ten weeks on, ten weeks off) — requires the owner to fund a second chef. That costs money. Discretionary leave requires only a promise. The result: chefs burn out, retention collapses, and captains post in WhatsApp groups asking "why can't we find a chef that will stay?" while offering the same unsustainable terms that drove the last three chefs away.

**The Real Numbers:**

| Rotation Model | Industry Perception | Reality |
|----------------|--------------------|---------|
| 5:1 (five months on, one off) | "Rotation" | Not rotation. This is a leave allowance with no replacement coverage. You return to chaos |
| 3:3 (three months on, three off) | Acceptable compromise | Workable if contractually fixed. Breaks down when "three on" stretches to four |
| 2:2 (two months on, two off) | Professional standard | The minimum that functions as true rotation. Requires a rotation partner |
| 10:10 (ten weeks on, ten weeks off) | Ideal | Growing in demand. Delivers genuine recovery time. Rare below 60 m |
| "60 days leave" (unscheduled) | Generous | Means nothing without fixed dates. Average chef reports taking 40–50 actual days |

**How to Negotiate:**
The chefs who get real rotation share a consistent approach. The leverage point is not the initial offer — it is the moment after you have proven your value.

One chef in the WhatsApp group described the tactic directly: he put in his notice. Not as a bluff — he had another position lined up. The captain, faced with losing a chef the owner liked, offered a 2:2 rotation within 48 hours. The lesson is uncomfortable but clear: rotation is not given. It is negotiated from a position where walking away is a real option, not a threat.

Practical steps:

- **Get it in writing before you sign.** "We'll discuss rotation after the season" means there is no rotation. If the dates are not in the contract, they do not exist.
- **Define the replacement.** Ask who covers when you leave. If there is no answer, there is no rotation — only leave that depends on the boat being idle.
- **Separate leave from rotation in the contract language.** Leave is days owed. Rotation is a fixed schedule with a named or agency-sourced replacement. They are different provisions and should be listed separately.
- **Use the retention argument.** Captains and management companies understand cost. Recruiting and onboarding a new chef costs EUR 5,000–15,000 when agency fees, travel, trial periods, and lost productivity are factored. A rotation partner costs less per year than annual chef turnover.
- **Talk to other chefs on similar vessels.** Salary and rotation data shared in professional groups has made the 5:1 model increasingly difficult to defend. If three chefs on comparable programmes are getting 2:2, your captain knows it.

## The Contract Checklist

Before you sign, confirm these five items are in writing:

| Item | What It Must Say | Red Flag |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| Rotation schedule | Fixed dates (e.g., "2 months on / 2 months off") | "60 days leave per year" with no fixed dates |
| Rotation partner | Named relief chef or agency commitment | "We'll sort cover when you need it" |
| Leave dates | Specific months or a selection process you control | "Leave at captain's discretion" |
| Cancellation terms | What happens if leave is cancelled (compensation, rescheduling) | No mention of cancellation |
| Handover protocol | Minimum 2-day overlap with relief chef | "Just leave a menu" |

**Your Move:**
If your contract says "60 days leave" with no fixed schedule and no rotation partner, you do not have rotation. You have a promise. Before your next contract renewal or your next position, decide what rotation model you need to sustain a career beyond three years — then negotiate for it specifically, in writing, with dates. The leverage exists. Use it before you burn out.
