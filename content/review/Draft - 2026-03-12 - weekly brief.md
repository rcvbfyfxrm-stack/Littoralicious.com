# Weekly Brief — Week of March 12, 2026

## THE THREAD

Noma opens in Silver Lake, Los Angeles on March 11. Forty-two seats. $1,500 per person. Completely sold out through June 26. René Redzepi's team spent months researching ingredients, producers, and traditions within a 300-mile radius of LA — the Pacific, the desert, the mountains — building a menu that is "quintessentially Noma but also completely new."

This is, structurally, exactly what a yacht chef does at every port call: arrive somewhere unfamiliar, research local sourcing, build a menu from what the coastline and the season give you, and serve it that night. The difference is the price tag and the press coverage. The principle is identical.

The week also brings three stories that reshape what's available to cook with: ICCAT raised the bluefin tuna quota 19%, CITES pulled 70+ shark species off restaurant menus, and US tariffs on European food imports are about to make olive oil, pasta, and wine significantly more expensive in American waters. What you serve, what you can serve, and what it costs — all shifting this week.

## SHORE LARDER — Bluefin Tuna

*Thunnus thynnus · Eastern Atlantic/Mediterranean · Purse seine season: May–July*

**48,403 tonnes.** That's the Eastern Atlantic and Mediterranean bluefin tuna TAC for 2026–2028, set by ICCAT at their November 2025 meeting in Seville. A 19.3% increase over the 2023–2025 period. The EU secured 25,164 tonnes per year — over half the total allocation, an increase of 3,661 tonnes annually.

This is the most commercially valuable fish in the Mediterranean. A single premium bluefin sold for $3.1 million at Tokyo's Toyosu Market auction in January 2024. On yacht menus, it appears as otoro, chutoro, akami, tataki, and — when the chef knows what they're doing — tartare with nothing but knife work and restraint.

The quota increase reflects genuine stock recovery. Mediterranean bluefin fisheries earned MSC certification in 2020 — the first for any bluefin population globally. Science-based management through ICCAT's management procedure (Recommendation 25-04) is working. This is a sustainability success story, and it's worth knowing when guests ask.

| Cut | Fat Content | Best Application | Price Tier |
|-----|------------|------------------|------------|
| Otoro (belly) | 25–40% | Sashimi, nigiri — raw only | Ultra-premium |
| Chutoro (medium belly) | 15–25% | Sashimi, light sear | Premium |
| Akami (loin) | 1–5% | Tataki, tartare, seared | Standard |

**Sourcing:** Peak fresh availability May–July during purse seine season. For February–April, frozen saku blocks from Japanese or Spanish processors are the standard. Balfegó Group (Tarragona, Spain) and Grupo Ricardo Fuentes (Cartagena) are the dominant Mediterranean suppliers — both invest heavily in MSC-compliant operations. When provisioning, request ICCAT catch documentation (BCD) — this is the chain-of-custody proof that the fish is quota-legal.

## THE METHOD — Searing Tuna: The Cold-Centre Technique

**The Move:** Start with partially frozen tuna. Sear at maximum heat for exactly 30–45 seconds per side. The frozen core acts as a thermal buffer, keeping the interior raw while the exterior develops a Maillard crust. No resting. Slice immediately.

1. Remove saku block from freezer 20–30 minutes before service. Target: firm exterior, still frozen at core (internal temp -5 to 0°C).
2. Pat bone-dry with paper towels. Season with flaky salt only. No oil on the fish.
3. Heat a carbon steel or cast iron pan to smoking — 260°C+ surface temperature. Add a thin film of high-smoke-point oil (grapeseed, rice bran).
4. Sear 30–45 seconds per side. Four sides if the block is square-cut. Do not touch it while searing — movement prevents crust formation.
5. Remove. Slice immediately with a sharp yanagiba or slicing knife into 8mm slices. The cross-section should show a 2–3mm seared ring around a raw, jewel-red centre.

**Why it works:** The Maillard reaction requires surface temperatures above 140°C. At 260°C+ pan temperature, the amino acids and reducing sugars on the fish surface react in under 30 seconds, creating pyrazines (nutty, roasted) and furanones (caramelised). Meanwhile, the frozen core absorbs the inward-moving heat, preventing the interior from exceeding 10°C. The temperature gradient is steep: 260°C at the surface, -5°C at the centre, 3cm apart. That gradient is the technique.

| Factor | What Happens | Adjust By |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Fish too thawed | Centre overcooks; grey band widens | Start from semi-frozen; 20 min max at room temp |
| Pan not hot enough | Steaming instead of searing; no crust | Wait until oil shimmers and first wisp of smoke |
| Fish wet | Steam barrier prevents Maillard | Triple-pat with paper towels; salt draws surface moisture |

**You know it worked when:** The seared face is dark bronze, almost charred. The slice reveals a razor-thin brown ring around raw crimson. The centre is cool to the tongue. The guests stop talking.

## THE EVIDENCE — CITES CoP20: 70+ Shark and Ray Species Protected

CITES CoP20, Samarkand, Uzbekistan. November 24 – December 5, 2025. Multiple proposals adopted by consensus.

The most significant wildlife trade decision in a generation for marine species. Every single shark and ray proposal at CoP20 was adopted.

| Protection Level | Species | Trade Impact |
|-----------------|---------|--------------|
| Appendix I (full trade ban) | Oceanic whitetip shark, whale shark, all manta and devil rays | No international commercial trade permitted |
| Appendix II (regulated trade) | 20 species of gulper sharks, 30 species of smoothhound/tope sharks, school sharks | Export permits required; sustainability proof mandatory |
| Zero export quotas | Wedgefish, giant guitarfish | Already Appendix II; now effectively banned from trade |

**The numbers:** approximately 70% of global shark and ray meat trade and 96% of the fin trade are now under CITES controls.

**What this means in the galley:** Smoothhound shark — known as *palombo* or *vitello di mare* in Italian markets, commonly sold as boneless steaks throughout the Mediterranean — is now CITES Appendix II. It requires export permits and sustainability certification. If your fishmonger can't provide documentation, the product is potentially illegal. Skate wings (rajiformes) are increasingly restricted. The menu pivot is straightforward: monkfish, swordfish, and cobia fill the same textural niche — firm, meaty, forgiving on the grill.

**What this does NOT mean:** That all shark products disappeared overnight. CITES Appendix II allows regulated trade, not a ban. But the documentation burden makes casual sourcing impractical. For yacht chefs, the simplest path is to remove shark from the repertoire entirely and replace with sustainable alternatives.

**Notable rejection:** Proposals to list European eels and sea cucumbers were voted down. Both remain tradeable — for now.

**Source:** CITES CoP20 Decisions, December 2025; SeafoodSource; Inside Climate News.

## LITTORAL HERITAGE — Tonno di Favignana

*Favignana, Egadi Islands, Sicily*

The *mattanza* — the annual bluefin tuna harvest off Favignana — was one of the oldest continuous fishing traditions in the Mediterranean, documented since the 13th century under Arab-Norman rule, with roots likely extending to Phoenician times. A *rais* (fishing master, from the Arabic *ra'is*) directed the operation: a labyrinth of nets funnelling migrating bluefin into a final chamber called the *camera della morte*, where fishermen hauled them by hand.

The last commercial mattanza on Favignana took place in 2007. Overfishing had reduced bluefin stocks to the point where the ritual could no longer function — the fish simply weren't there. The rais, Gioacchino Cataldo, was the last. The tradition died with the fishery's collapse.

The irony is precise: the same ICCAT management that killed the mattanza by allowing industrial purse seiners to intercept the migration routes has now, through quota controls and MSC certification, rebuilt the stock. The fish are recovering. The tradition is not.

What survives is the cuisine. Tonno di Favignana — tuna preserved in olive oil, tuna bottarga, *lattume* (tuna milt), *buzzonaglia* (the dark, blood-rich belly meat cured in salt). Every part of the fish was used. The tradition was total utilisation born from a brief annual abundance.

| Element | Traditional | Why It Matters |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Whole-animal use | Every organ, every cut preserved | Zero waste before the term existed |
| Bottarga (roe sac) | Salt-cured, pressed, air-dried 2–4 months | Concentrated umami; grated over pasta like Parmesan |
| Lattume (milt) | Poached, sliced, dressed with lemon and oil | Creamy, delicate — the most perishable cut |
| Buzzonaglia (belly) | Salt-cured dark meat | Intense, iron-rich, served as antipasto |

**On board:** You won't witness a mattanza — the last one was 2007. But you can provision bottarga di tonno in any Sicilian market (Trapani, Palermo, Favignana harbour), and the canned tonno di Favignana preserved in olive oil is among the finest tuna conserves in the world. Grate bottarga over spaghetti aglio e olio. Tell the guests the fish this came from was caught in a net system designed by Arabs in the 9th century. The tradition ended, but the flavour didn't.

**Pairing:** Grillo from Trapani — Marco De Bartoli or Ferrandes. Saline, almond, same island.

## THE HORIZON — Noma Opens in Los Angeles

*March 11, 2026 · Silver Lake, Los Angeles*

Noma's LA residency begins March 11 and runs through June 26 — 16 weeks, 42 seats per night, Tuesday through Friday. Price: $1,500 per person including menu, beverage pairing, and hospitality. Completely sold out.

René Redzepi's team is not importing Copenhagen dishes. They've spent months researching ingredients, producers, and traditions within a 300-mile radius of Los Angeles — the Pacific coastline, the Central Valley, the Mojave Desert, the San Gabriel Mountains. Redzepi calls it "quintessentially Noma but also completely new."

The details that matter: a Noma Projects Shop opens in Silver Lake (the brand's first retail space outside Copenhagen, selling fermentation-lab products and test-kitchen output). One table is reserved nightly for hospitality professionals aged 25 and under to dine free — an investment in the next generation that costs Redzepi $1,500 per seat per night.

This residency is the most significant restaurant event of 2026. But for yacht chefs, the underlying method is more interesting than the headline. Building a world-class menu from local terroir within a defined radius, using fermentation and preservation to extend seasonal windows, and treating each location as a blank canvas rather than importing a fixed repertoire — this is the job description of every yacht chef who provisions in Palma one week and Sardinia the next.

## TRADE WINDS — US Tariffs on European Food Imports: The Provisioning Crisis

*January–February 2026 · United States*

US tariffs on European food imports are reshaping provisioning costs for any vessel operating in American waters.

| Product | Current Tariff | Proposed/Pending | Impact |
|---------|---------------|------------------|--------|
| EU agri-food (base) | 15% | — | Across all EU food imports including EVOO, wine, pasta, cheese |
| European wine, champagne, spirits | 15% (current) | **200% (proposed)** | Trump proposed in response to EU's planned 50% tariff on US whiskey |
| Italian pasta (13 largest exporters) | 15% + 91.74% anti-dumping | **~107% combined** | Cost could more than double for US importers |
| Olive oil | 15% | — | Compounds existing supply-driven price pressure from Andalusia drought |

Analysts note that tariff-driven cost increases typically lag 12–18 months from implementation. 2026 is the inflection point — prices haven't fully hit shelves yet, but they will.

**Your move:** If provisioning in US ports (Fort Lauderdale, Miami, Newport, San Francisco), budget for 15–20% increases on European staples immediately. For Italian pasta: identify US-milled alternatives (Sfoglini, Rustichella is imported but pre-tariff stock may still be available). For EVOO: California producers (McEvoy Ranch, California Olive Ranch) offer legitimate quality at non-tariff prices. For wine: domestic alternatives exist at every price point, but if the 200% proposal passes, European wine service on US-based charters becomes economically brutal. Build a domestic wine programme now as insurance.

**One bright spot:** Cocoa prices crashed 28.5% in the past month (down 56.7% year-over-year from record highs) as West African harvest prospects improved. Pastry programmes get relief.

**Source:** USDA ERS Food Price Outlook 2026; Food Navigator; GOBankingRates; NPR.
