# The Galley Equipment Verdict

*What yacht chefs actually use, actually recommend, and actually despise — from 24,000 messages of unfiltered opinion.*

Equipment debates in the yacht chef community are not polite. Nobody is protecting a brand relationship. Nobody is angling for a sponsorship. These are professionals who use the same machines 14 hours a day, six or seven days a week, in a salt-air environment that destroys anything not built to survive it. Their opinions are earned.

What follows is a category-by-category verdict, drawn from two major yacht chef WhatsApp groups (combined 1,300+ members). Where polls exist, the numbers are cited. Where quotes are used, they are verbatim. Where consensus exists, it is stated plainly. Where opinion is divided, that is stated too.

No affiliate links. No sponsored positions. No "best for the money" hedging. The professional choice, and the reasoning behind it.

---

## Ovens

### The Verdict: Rational iCombi Pro

**Poll result: 33 votes Rational, 0 votes Convotherm.** Not 33 to 5. Not 33 to 12. Thirty-three to zero.

The Rational iCombi Pro is the universal recommendation for yacht galleys where space and power allow a professional combi oven. The reasons are consistent across every discussion: intuitive controls, reliable probe readings, consistent results across steam, convection, and combination modes, and a build quality that survives the marine environment.

One independent service engineer, who has maintained every Rational generation since 1996, identifies the CM/CCC era as the best ever built, but notes the current iCombi Pro maintains the core engineering philosophy that makes Rational the standard.

The marine version includes "idiot locks" — physical controls that prevent accidental mode changes. On a vessel in any kind of sea state, this is not a luxury feature. It is a necessity.

**Runner-up: MKN SpaceCombi** — a solid alternative specifically for built-in galley installations where the Rational footprint does not fit. Less universally loved, but nobody complains about it either.

### What to Avoid

**Convotherm** — "dogshit." "Non-intuitive touchscreen." "Should come with drywall to punch." The touchscreen interface is the primary complaint. Professional chefs working under pressure need tactile controls they can operate with wet hands without looking. Convotherm's touchscreen fails this test.

**Miele** — "fuck Miele" is a recurring refrain in both groups. Domestic ovens marketed as suitable for yacht installation. They are not. Repeated reports of oven failures, cooktops shattering, and both ovens dying simultaneously. Miele designs for a kitchen that gets used twice a day by someone cooking for four. A yacht galley is a professional kitchen that moves. The engineering gap is structural, not fixable with a service call.

**My Chef (compact ovens)** — "How much do we all hate these?" posed as a question and answered with universal agreement. Underpowered, unreliable, and a false economy that costs more in frustration than it saves in purchase price.

**Unox** — "quite horrible." Limited discussion, which itself is telling — chefs who have used them move on quickly.

## Blenders & Food Processors

### The Verdict: Heinzelmann Chef-X

16,000 units sold across the EU. No touchscreen. Described as having "zero safety features" — which, in the context of professional kitchens, means no paternalistic interlocks that slow down experienced operators. The defining characteristic: "you cannot destroy them."

The Heinzelmann Chef-X is 220V only. This matters for vessels wired for 110V or dual voltage. Verify your galley power before ordering.

What the Chef-X does: it blends, it heats, it processes. It does these things with industrial durability in a form factor designed for professional use, not for a domestic countertop. The absence of a touchscreen is a feature — physical controls work when your hands are covered in stock.

### The Professional Alternative: Robot Coupe Cook

1800W, 4L capacity, built-in scraping spatula. Industrial heritage. Robot Coupe has been the restaurant standard for food processing for decades, and the Cook model adds heating capability. Less discussed than the Heinzelmann in yacht circles, but respected by chefs who have used both.

### What to Avoid

**Thermomix** — "built for Karens." "Got worse with every version for chefs." The most telling quote comes from the founder of Superyacht Galley Designs: "Thermomix is dead to me."

The Thermomix is designed for home cooks who want one appliance to do everything. In a professional context, its limitations are immediate: small capacity, fragile components under heavy use, software updates that change functionality without warning, and a proprietary ecosystem that creates dependency. It does many things adequately. It does nothing exceptionally. In a galley where every piece of equipment must earn its space, that is a disqualifying characteristic.

### The Budget Pacojet Alternative

**Ninja Creami** — described as "a temu Paco." Not a professional recommendation, but an acknowledgement that for frozen dessert applications where a Pacojet (EUR 5,000+) is not in the budget, the Ninja Creami (under EUR 250) produces acceptable results. It is a domestic appliance. It will not last like a Pacojet. But for a boat that does not justify the Pacojet investment, it exists.

## Mixers

### The Verdict: Hobart 10L

"The winner." Hobart planetary mixers are the standard in professional kitchens ashore and afloat. The 10L capacity suits most yacht galley applications — bread, pastry, general mixing. Build quality is industrial. Parts are globally available. Service technicians exist in every major yachting port.

### Alternatives

**Ankarsrum** — specifically recommended for bread. The Ankarsrum's arm-and-roller design handles high-hydration doughs better than a planetary mixer. If bread is a significant part of your output (and on most yachts, it is), the Ankarsrum earns its counter space alongside the Hobart, not instead of it.

**Buffalo 10L** — the budget option. Functional, but the bowl lock mechanism has a known failure point. Multiple reports of the lock breaking under heavy dough loads. For light pastry work it is adequate. For daily bread production, spend the money on the Hobart.

**KitchenAid** — mocked. It is a domestic mixer. The gears are zinc alloy, not steel. Under professional daily use, KitchenAid mixers fail within months. The yacht galley equivalent of putting a dinghy engine on a tender and expecting it to run all season.

## Dishwashers

### The Verdict: Hobart

Hobart dominates the commercial dishwasher market for the same reasons it dominates mixers: industrial engineering, global service network, reliable performance under heavy use. Yacht galleys running 3-4 full cycles per meal service need a machine that will do this for years without failure.

### What to Avoid

**Miele** — ongoing complaints. The same pattern as their ovens: domestic engineering marketed for professional use. A Miele dishwasher in a home running one cycle per day is fine. In a galley running 10-15 cycles per day, it is not built for the duty cycle. Breakdowns are frequent and parts availability in remote locations is poor.

## Sous Vide

### The Verdict: Polyscience

The professional choice with documented longevity of 10+ years in galley environments. Polyscience immersion circulators are built for continuous professional use — running a 72-hour short rib cook is not an edge case, it is the intended application.

### Alternatives

**Anova Pro** — the professional-tier Anova (not the consumer model). Reliable, good app integration if you want remote monitoring, more affordable than Polyscience. A legitimate professional option.

**Breville Joule / Bath models** — consumer-grade but functional. Acceptable for boats where sous vide is occasional rather than daily.

**Inkbird** — budget option. Functional for basic applications. Do not expect Polyscience longevity.

**Fusion Chef** — commercial grade, less discussed but respected by chefs who use them. Worth investigating if Polyscience is out of budget.

### The Practical Note

Salt air corrodes electronics. Whatever immersion circulator you choose, store it dry between uses, keep the control head away from steam, and budget for replacement every 3-5 years for consumer-grade models. Polyscience units routinely exceed 10 years. That durability premium is real.

## Cookware

### The Verdict: De Buyer Carbon Steel

"All day everyday." De Buyer carbon steel pans are the consensus professional choice for yacht galleys. The reasoning is straightforward: they are lighter than cast iron (matters on a moving vessel), they develop a non-stick patina with use, they handle extreme heat without damage, and they are effectively indestructible.

Carbon steel requires seasoning and maintenance. It is not for the chef who wants to toss a pan in the dishwasher. But any chef reading this publication already knows how to maintain a carbon steel pan.

### Alternatives

**Cristel** — French-made stainless steel. Excellent for sauces, reductions, and applications where carbon steel's reactivity is a problem (acid-based sauces, wine reductions). Good weight, good balance, removable handles for storage.

**DeMeyere** — Belgian-made, similar quality tier to Cristel. Strong stainless steel option.

### What to Avoid

**HexClad** — "noisy and the black stuff comes off." The hexagonal pattern that is HexClad's selling point creates a textured surface that generates noise during cooking and, per multiple reports, degrades over time with the dark coating separating from the pan surface. Marketing does not survive daily professional use.

## Cutting Boards

### The Verdict: It Depends

**Hasegawa** — loved for its cutting feel and blade-friendliness. The synthetic rubber compound is gentle on knife edges. The downside: it stains and wears. Replacement is the cost of doing business, not a defect.

**Asahi rubber boards** — the alternative. Harder-wearing than Hasegawa. Maintenance tip from the groups: "get the deckies to sand them" when the surface becomes scored. A belt sander restores the cutting surface to near-new condition.

### The Practical Recommendation

Run both. Hasegawa for fine knife work (protein fabrication, vegetable brunoise), Asahi for heavy-duty prep (stocks, butchery, anything where the board takes impact). Replace Hasegawa boards when staining becomes a food safety concern, regardless of how much surface life remains.

## Knives & Maintenance

This guide does not cover knife selection — that is a separate article. But knife maintenance came up repeatedly with specific, actionable intelligence.

### The Game Changer

**Leather strop with diamond compound** — "changed everything." Multiple chefs reported that adding a stropping step to their maintenance routine dramatically extended the time between full sharpenings. A loaded leather strop realigns the edge at a microscopic level, maintaining sharpness through days of service without removing steel.

### Daily Maintenance

**Jewelstik diamond steel** — recommended as the daily honing tool. Diamond-coated steels remove a small amount of metal with each pass, which means they actually resharpen rather than just realigning. For a working chef doing 14-hour days, this means the edge holds from morning prep through evening service.

### Professional Sharpening

There is a knife sharpening shop in Antibes that multiple chefs recommend for the Riviera season. If you are based on the Cote d'Azur, have your knives professionally sharpened at the start of the season and maintain with strop and steel from there.

## Pizza Ovens

### The Verdict: Effeuno N4

Highly recommended by multiple chefs. The Effeuno N4 is a compact electric pizza oven that reaches temperatures needed for Neapolitan-style pizza (450°C+) in a form factor that can fit in a yacht galley. It is the reason several chefs have added pizza nights to their charter repertoire — the oven makes it possible where it previously was not.

### Alternatives

**Ooni Volt 2** — electric, portable, capable. Less powerful than the Effeuno but easier to store. Good for boats where pizza is occasional rather than a regular menu feature.

**Forni David** — commercial-grade, larger footprint. For yachts with the space and the power budget, this is a step up from the Effeuno. Rare in yacht galleys due to size constraints.

## The Meta-Lesson

Across every category, the same pattern repeats. The professional choice costs more upfront. It lasts longer. It performs better. It is repairable. It has a global service network. The cheap alternative fails under professional use, costs more in downtime and replacement than the price difference, and generates the kind of frustration that makes a chef consider leaving the industry.

Buy once, cry once. The galley on a EUR 30,000,000 yacht should not be equipped with domestic appliances. If the owner or management company pushes back on the cost of a Rational over a Miele, show them the maintenance records. Show them the replacement timeline. Show them this article.

The equipment earns the money. Or it costs the money. There is no middle ground.
