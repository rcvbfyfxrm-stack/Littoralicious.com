# Galley Order: The Free, Open Provisioning System

*The single most under-engineered part of yacht-chef work is the order list. We accept dietary changes by text at 18:00 for a tasting at 20:00, but we still source the saffron, the tahini and the dry-aged ribeye from a paper sheet last printed for the previous chef on the previous boat. That sheet survives a season, then dies with the next handover. The brand that made the difference walks off with the chef who wrote it down.*

**This is the fix.** A free, open, browser-based provisioning system, with the 2,500-item master catalog already inside it. No account. No subscription. No data leaves your browser. Print a clean A4 order. Archive every order you've ever sent. Hand it intact to the next chef.

**Where it lives:** [littoralicious.com/galleyorder](/galleyorder/)

## How it works

**Open the page.** The catalog loads. Everything stays in your browser.

**Fill the order.** Search, or open a category. Type a quantity. The row turns gold to show it's in the order. Add notes if there's a brand swap, supplier instruction, or a specific cut you want — "side cut, scored, pin-boned, vacuum-sealed at 1 kg per bag."

**Star what you order often.** Favourites pin to the top of their section. Items used across multiple archived orders sort up automatically. The list learns your kitchen.

**Three filters.**

| Filter | Use it when |
|---|---|
| **★+ / ★★+ / ★★★** | You want to scan only chef-picks or only luxury heroes. Useful when planning a guest-night menu. |
| **Only ordered** | You've finished filling. Cut the catalog to just what you're sending. |
| **Search** | Free text on item or brand. "Yuzu" finds the paste, the juice, the kosho, the salt. |

**Save before you finish.** The order autosaves every keystroke. Close the tab — your work is there when you come back. When the order is ready to send, click **Archive Order** to snapshot it. Up to sixty archives are kept.

**Load past orders.** "Archives" lists every order you've sent — date, supplier, vessel, item count. **Load** replaces the current order with that snapshot. **Duplicate** opens a new order pre-filled with last week's list — change three items, send, done.

**Print PDF.** Click Print. The browser print dialog opens. Choose "Save as PDF." The output is a clean A4 — masthead with chef, date, supplier, vessel — and **only the items you ordered**, organised by category. Sections with nothing in them disappear. The full catalog stays intact in the webapp for the next order.

**Export your data.** A single button saves a JSON file with everything — favourites, history, current order, unit overrides. Back it up to your drive. Move it to a new laptop. Hand it to the next chef on the boat.

## What it doesn't do

It is not a procurement platform. It does not contact suppliers, place orders, or send invoices. It is a list — a serious, complete, free list — and a clean print.

It does not run in the cloud. There is no account. No login. No ads. Your archive lives in your browser's local storage. Export the JSON regularly if you want a backup; that is the only backup that exists.

It does not work on the boat's WiFi. It works offline. The first time you load it, your browser caches the catalog. After that, the page works on a moored superyacht with the modem unplugged.

## Why it exists

Three pillars: the grandmother who taught you that the brand of olive oil is not a snobbery but the difference between a tomato salad you eat and one you remember; the scientist who tracks single-origin tahini provenance because the sesame seed cultivar changes the sauce; the chef who has done forty handovers and watched every paper sheet disappear with the chef who wrote it.

The order list is institutional memory for the galley. Treat it as institutional memory and the next chef walks on with a year of brand-tested knowledge already loaded. Treat it as a one-time printout and the kitchen starts from zero every season.

We chose to treat it as memory and to give the tool away. *Nutrire* — the mission of this publication — applies to the chef as much as the guest. A chef working from a sharper list serves better food. That's the whole argument.
