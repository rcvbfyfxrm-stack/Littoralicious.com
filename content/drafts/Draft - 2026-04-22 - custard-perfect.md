# Custard

**Cream and milk, warmed. Yolks and sugar, whisked pale. Temper. Back to the pan. Stir constantly to 82°C — not 83, not 85. Strain. Ice bath. This is the base recipe every serious dessert on a menu leans on, and the failure window is two degrees wide.**

Custard is the most fundamental, least forgiving preparation in the Western dessert kitchen. Done well, a crème anglaise is a silk sauce that glazes a spoon and tastes like the essence of dairy-and-vanilla; the same preparation five degrees hotter is scrambled eggs in sweet milk. This version sets the technique bulletproof — ratios, temperatures, the stir, the strain, the stop — and shows the three branches it supports: crème anglaise (stirred, pouring), pastry cream (stabilised with starch, pipeable), and the sous-vide method that removes the anxiety entirely.

**Source:** Harold McGee (*On Food and Cooking*, egg coagulation science), Jacques Pépin (*La Technique*, classical method), J. Kenji López-Alt (*The Food Lab*, temperature targets), Paula Figoni (*How Baking Works*, starch stabilisation), Thomas Keller (*The French Laundry Cookbook*, ratios).

**Key technique:** Temper-and-cook a 1:4 yolk-to-dairy custard to **82°C (180°F)** internal, hold briefly at nappe stage, strain immediately, shock in an ice bath. The target window for stirred custard is 80-84°C — below and it pours like milk, above and it curdles.

## METHOD — CRÈME ANGLAISE (classical stovetop)

### PHASE 1: Infuse the Dairy
*10 minutes — unattended once on heat*

**1.** Split the vanilla pod lengthwise. Scrape seeds into a heavy saucepan. Drop the pod in too — the pod carries most of the aroma.

**2.** Add milk and cream. Bring to a bare simmer over medium heat — **steaming, small bubbles at the edge, not rolling**. Pull off, cover, steep **10 minutes**. For coffee, tea, citrus zest, cardamom, saffron — infuse now in the warm dairy, strain before proceeding.

> **Why steep, not boil?** Vanilla aroma compounds are heat-sensitive — a rolling boil drives off the high notes. A gentle steep extracts maximum flavour without damage. The same rule applies to coffee (bitter above 90°C), tea (tannin extraction accelerates and turns astringent), and citrus zest (volatile oils boil off fast).

### PHASE 2: Build the Base
*5 minutes*

**3.** In a separate bowl, whisk egg yolks with sugar and salt until the mixture is pale yellow and ribbons off the whisk — **2-3 minutes vigorously**. This is not cosmetic. Whisking incorporates the sugar into the yolks, dissolving the granules and — more importantly — **coating the proteins in sugar molecules that raise their coagulation temperature by 10-15°C**. An under-whisked yolk is a pre-scrambled yolk.

**4.** Re-warm the infused dairy to a steam (80-85°C). Pour a third of it into the yolk mixture in a thin stream, **whisking constantly**. This is tempering — raising the temperature of the yolks gradually so they don't hit a thermal shock and coagulate on contact.

**5.** Pour the tempered yolks back into the pan with the remaining dairy. Whisk once to combine, then switch to a silicone spatula.

### PHASE 3: The Stir
*5-8 minutes — the only phase where you cannot look away*

**6.** Return the pan to medium-low heat. From this point, you **stir continuously** in a figure-eight pattern that sweeps the bottom and corners of the pan. Do not whisk — whisking aerates and prevents you from reading the texture.

> **Why figure-eight?** The coagulation danger zone is the pan bottom and corners, where direct heat concentrates. A straight back-and-forth stir leaves dead zones. The figure-eight with corner sweeps means every square centimetre of the pan is scraped every two or three seconds. This is the move that separates a home custard from a restaurant one.

**7.** As the custard approaches **75°C**, it will visibly thicken — the spatula leaves a clean trail on the pan bottom, and the surface loses its froth and becomes glossy. From here, two to three more minutes, still stirring.

**8.** The target is **82°C (180°F)**. Check with a thermometer held in the liquid, not touching the pan. The "nappe" test — coat the back of the spatula with custard, draw a line with your fingertip, the line should hold — happens at 82°C.

> **The window:** 80°C is thin and just-set. 82°C is the sweet spot. 84°C is the edge — you will feel the custard begin to grain. Above 85°C, the yolk proteins have contracted, squeezed out their water, and coagulated into visible curds. There is no recovery from a fully curdled custard. Sieve it and move on.

### PHASE 4: The Stop
*30 seconds — critical*

**9.** The moment the thermometer reads 82°C, **remove the pan from the heat immediately** and **pour the custard through the strainer into a bowl set over an ice bath.** Do not stop at the heat — the residual pan temperature will carry the custard over 84°C in seconds.

> **Why strain?** Even a perfect custard has a small amount of coagulated albumen (from the chalazae — the twisted white cords attached to the yolk) and tiny fragments from the bottom of the pan. Straining removes these. It is also the single best insurance against a slightly-overcooked batch — minor graininess passes through a chinois.

**10.** Stir the custard over the ice bath for **1-2 minutes** to drop the temperature below 30°C and stop all further cooking. Once cool, refrigerate.

### PHASE 5: Rest
*2 hours minimum*

**11.** Chill **at least 2 hours**, ideally overnight. The flavour develops significantly in the cold rest — vanilla deepens, the custard settles, and the texture firms from "pourable warm" to "glazes a spoon cold." This is not optional for ice cream base; for immediate use as a sauce, a 30-minute chill is acceptable.

**12.** Before serving, whisk briefly to restore the uniform texture, and thin with a splash of milk or cream if it has set too firm.

## METHOD — PASTRY CREAM (crème pâtissière)

*Same spine, starch added, different target.*

**1.** Infuse milk with vanilla as in Phase 1.

**2.** Whisk yolks, sugar, salt, **and cornstarch** together. The starch is whisked in now, raw.

**3.** Temper with one-third of hot milk, return to pan.

**4.** Over medium heat, **whisk constantly** (now you can whisk — starch prevents the yolks from seizing). The mixture thickens abruptly around 82-85°C as starch granules gelatinise. **Continue whisking and bring to a bare boil for 90 seconds** — this is critical: it deactivates an enzyme in the yolk (alpha-amylase) that would otherwise break down the starch and turn your pastry cream runny over 24 hours.

> **Why boil pastry cream but not anglaise?** Starch raises the coagulation ceiling of the egg proteins — the starch granules physically separate the protein chains so they cannot aggregate into curds. You can boil pastry cream; you cannot boil anglaise. The 90-second boil is the amylase kill step; without it, pastry cream held overnight turns into soup.

**5.** Off heat, whisk in cold butter cubes — richness and sheen.

**6.** Strain through a chinois, press plastic wrap **directly onto the surface** to prevent a skin, chill.

**7.** Before use, whisk hard for 30 seconds to restore smoothness — pastry cream stiffens dramatically in the fridge and needs re-homogenising.

## THE SCIENCE

*Why this recipe works*

### Egg Yolk Coagulation = A 15°C Window

Egg yolk proteins begin denaturing at 65°C and fully coagulate by 70°C in pure water. Custard protein coagulation is delayed because the proteins are diluted in dairy and shielded by sugar and fat — the practical window is 75-85°C. At 75°C, the custard is thin. At 82°C, the proteins have just cross-linked enough to thicken without aggregating into visible curds. At 85°C and above, the cross-links tighten and water is squeezed out, producing the grainy "sweet scrambled eggs" failure mode.

**What's happening:** Yolk proteins (primarily livetins and low-density lipoproteins) unfold and form a weak three-dimensional network that traps water. Sugar interferes with protein-protein bonding, widening the window. Starch, in pastry cream, extends the window further by physical separation.

**Why it matters:** Custard is one of the few preparations where a two-degree error is visible on the plate. The thermometer is not optional. Once you've made a hundred, you can read the texture — until then, verify.

*Source: Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 85-88, 97-98.*

### Tempering = Thermal Shock Prevention

Pouring hot dairy directly into cold yolks produces instant scrambled eggs on contact. Tempering — streaming a portion of hot dairy into the whisked yolks while whisking — raises the yolk temperature gradually, giving the proteins time to unfold rather than seize.

**What's happening:** Protein denaturation is irreversible once the tertiary structure collapses. Gradual heating gives proteins time to unfold and re-form into a weak matrix; sudden heating collapses them into aggregates.

**Why it matters:** Every curdled custard in the world is either an under-whisked yolk, an un-tempered addition, or a too-hot pan. Two of the three are fixed by tempering.

*Source: Jacques Pépin, La Technique, 1976; confirmed by Kenji López-Alt, "The Food Lab: How to Make the Best Custard," Serious Eats, 2015.*

### Sugar = Protein Protection

Sugar is not only sweetener — it is a coagulation delayer. Sucrose molecules bind to protein surfaces via hydrogen bonding and raise the temperature at which proteins can aggregate. A custard with 100 g sugar coagulates at 82°C; the same custard with 50 g coagulates at 78°C; sugar-free custard coagulates at 72°C.

**What's happening:** Sucrose hydrates the protein surface, competing with protein-protein bonds. More sugar = wider safety margin.

**Why it matters:** The ratio of sugar to yolk is structural, not stylistic. A reduced-sugar custard needs a lower target temperature. Do not pull sugar below 80 g per 6 yolks unless you move to sous-vide.

*Source: Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 97-98; Paula Figoni, How Baking Works, 2010, Chapter 10.*

### Starch = Permission to Boil (Pastry Cream Only)

In pastry cream, cornstarch or flour physically separates the egg proteins from each other, raising the effective coagulation ceiling above 100°C. This lets you bring pastry cream to a full boil — which is not just tolerated but required, because egg yolks contain alpha-amylase, an enzyme that slowly breaks down starch and would liquefy your pastry cream over 24 hours if not destroyed.

**What's happening:** Alpha-amylase is heat-stable up to ~82°C; it is destroyed by sustained boiling. Unboiled pastry cream (many home recipes stop at "thickened" around 82°C) is amylase-active and liquefies in the fridge.

**Why it matters:** If you have ever made pastry cream that was perfect in the evening and slumped into soup by morning, you stopped cooking too early. 90 seconds at a full boil, whisking constantly, is the kill step.

*Source: Paula Figoni, How Baking Works, 2010, Chapter 10; Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, p. 101.*

### Ice Bath Stop = Arresting the Carryover

A hot pan holds significant thermal mass. A custard pulled at 82°C and left in the pan will climb to 85-86°C from residual heat alone within 30 seconds. The ice bath plus the cold strainer mass absorb this residual energy and hold the custard at its target.

**What's happening:** Carryover cooking is thermal diffusion from the pan + liquid mass into the custard. Separating the custard from the hot pan and cooling it immediately stops the climb.

**Why it matters:** Skip the ice bath on a hot galley, and your perfectly-pulled custard overcooks in the bowl. The strainer and the ice bath together are the difference between 82°C final and 86°C final.

*Source: J. Kenji López-Alt, Serious Eats, 2015.*

### SUB-RECIPE: Sous-Vide Crème Anglaise (Tier 2 — the unfailable method)

| Ingredient | Same as Group A |
|------------|-----------------|

**Method:** Set immersion circulator to **82°C (180°F)**. Whisk yolks, sugar, salt in a bowl. Whisk in cold dairy and vanilla seeds (drop the pod in too). Transfer to a vacuum bag or silicone zip bag (water-displacement seal). Cook at 82°C for **45 minutes**, gently agitating or massaging the bag every 10-15 minutes to prevent settling. Pour out through a chinois, chill in an ice bath. Done.

**Why it works:** 82°C water holds the custard at exactly 82°C — no pan gradient, no carryover, no chance of curdle. The 45-minute hold is long enough to fully coagulate the yolks and pasteurise them without aggregating. The texture is slightly less silky than a perfectly-pulled stovetop version but the failure rate is zero. On a busy charter this is the right call.

*Source: ChefSteps, "The Best Crème Anglaise," 2016; Modernist Cuisine, 2011, Vol. 4.*

### SUB-RECIPE: Brown-Butter Vanilla Ice Cream (from this custard)

**To the base recipe, add:**

| Ingredient | Weight |
|------------|--------|
| Unsalted butter (browned dark) | 60 g |
| Additional yolks | 1 (total 7) |

**Method:** Brown 60 g butter deep and dark (8-10 min), scrape every brown bit into the cream during Phase 1. Use 7 yolks instead of 6. Proceed as base recipe. Chill overnight minimum. Churn per manufacturer. Hardens in 4 hours. The single best vanilla ice cream you will make.

### THE GALLEY HACK: The Morning Batch + Three Desserts

**For charter service:**

1. Morning: make 1 L of sous-vide crème anglaise (double the base recipe).
2. 250 ml → sauce for that night's plated dessert, held chilled.
3. 500 ml → churned into ice cream mid-afternoon, hardened for service.
4. 250 ml → into a blast-chilled pot de crème ramekin set for lunch day 2.

One technique, three desserts, one pot of ice cream, zero curdle risk.

### FREEZER STRATEGY

**Ice cream (from base):**
- Chill fully churned and hardened in a lidded container
- Up to 2 months
- Temper 5-10 min before scooping
- A splash of vodka in the base (20 ml per litre) prevents over-hardening without flavouring

**Custard sauce itself — do not freeze:**
- Anglaise freezes into a grainy, broken mess. The water crystallises out of the emulsion.
- Churn it into ice cream if you want long storage.

**Pastry cream — do not freeze:**
- Starch retrogrades in the freezer. Texture becomes powdery and weeping. 3 days fridge, maximum.

## TROUBLESHOOTING

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| **Curdled / grainy anglaise** | Exceeded 84°C / pan too hot / not stirred / no thermometer | Blitz with stick blender over ice bath (recovers 70% of batches); strain hard. Next time: thermometer, figure-eight stir. |
| **Custard too thin after chilling** | Under-cooked (stopped below 80°C) / too many yolks forgotten / too much milk | Can't recover anglaise; use it as a thin sauce. Pastry cream: re-cook briefly with 10 g more starch slurry. |
| **Custard too thick / pudding-like** | Over-reduced / too many yolks / ratio off | Thin with warm cream, a spoonful at a time |
| **Flavour flat** | No salt / under-infused vanilla / poor-quality eggs | Salt in the yolks. Full 10-min vanilla steep. Pastured eggs taste visibly brighter. |
| **Eggy smell / taste** | Under-cooked (still has raw albumen) OR over-cooked (sulphur off-notes) | 82°C target, non-negotiable. Strain through chinois. |
| **Skin on surface** | No plastic wrap directly on custard during chill | Press film directly onto surface. Lid over bowl is not enough. |
| **Pastry cream liquefied overnight** | Amylase enzyme in yolks not deactivated | Must boil pastry cream 90 seconds, whisking. Not "thicken and stop." |
| **Pastry cream stiff and broken** | Over-boiled / over-cooled / not whisked before use | Whisk hard before use. Add 1 tbsp milk and whisk if broken. |
| **Ice cream icy / crystalline** | Insufficient fat / insufficient churning / no alcohol / old freezer | 1:1 milk:cream minimum. 20 ml vodka in 1 L base. Serve at -14°C, not -20°C. |
| **Ice cream greasy / buttery** | Over-churned / over-stabilised | Churn to soft-serve, no further. Harden in freezer. |
| **Brûlée sugar layer thin / soft** | Too little sugar / torched damp custard | Sugar layer 2-3 mm, patted dry before torch. Pass 2-3 times, cooling between. |
