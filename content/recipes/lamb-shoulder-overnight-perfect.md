# Roast Lamb

**Bone-in shoulder, dry-rubbed the night before, set into a 100-110°C oven before bed and pulled from the rack at breakfast. Twelve hours at low temperature drives the connective tissue past 90°C internal — collagen melts, fat renders, the fibres separate under a fork without resistance. The skin lacquers under a final blast at 220°C. This is the answer to "is overnight best?" The answer is: yes, for shoulder. No, for leg.**

Two cuts of lamb, two physics problems. Shoulder is a worker — high collagen, high fat, dense connective tissue — and rewards long, low, wet-slow cooking that hydrolyses tough proteins into gelatin. Leg is lean and tender — almost no collagen — and ruins under the same treatment, going grey, dry, and cottony. The headline recipe here is overnight shoulder, the version that walks itself while you sleep. The leg variant — reverse-seared at 80°C to a 52°C medium-rare core — sits in **Elevation**. Both are correct. Both are different recipes for different muscles. Choose the cut to match the method, not the other way around.

## THE COMPARISON: Shoulder vs. Leg

*Read this before you choose the cut*

| | **Shoulder (this recipe)** | **Leg (see Elevation)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Connective tissue** | High — heavy collagen, intramuscular fat | Low — lean muscle, light silverskin |
| **Internal target** | 90-95°C — collagen fully hydrolysed, pull-apart | 52-54°C medium-rare core (carries to 56°C) |
| **Best method** | Slow-roast 100-110°C, 8-12 hr | Reverse-sear: 80°C oven to 50°C core, then 220°C blast |
| **Texture goal** | Pulled, jammy, fork-tender | Sliced, pink, juicy |
| **Overnight?** | **Yes — overnight is the optimum** | **No — overnight ruins it; leg above 60°C is dry** |
| **Fat** | Renders out and bastes the meat | Almost none to render; needs added fat |
| **Failure mode** | Under-cooked: still tough. Cure with more time. | Over-cooked: grey and dry. Irreversible. |
| **Yield from 2 kg** | ~1.4 kg pulled (heavy rendering) | ~1.7 kg sliced (light loss) |

> **The headline:** This recipe is for the shoulder version. The leg version is a different recipe, included as a TIER 2 elevation. Do not apply overnight method to a leg. Do not apply 52°C target to a shoulder.

## METHOD

### PHASE 1: Salt and Rub
*15 minutes active + 12-24 hours unattended*

**1.** Pat the shoulder dry with paper towels. With a sharp knife, score the fat cap in a 2 cm crosshatch — cut through the fat, not into the muscle. Scoring lets the fat render out across all 12 hours instead of only at the surface.

**2.** Combine 25 g coarse salt and 5 g cracked pepper. Massage all over the shoulder, into the score lines, into every crease. This is a **dry brine** — not just seasoning.

> **Why salt 12-24 hours ahead?** Salt on the surface draws moisture out of the meat (osmosis) within the first hour, dissolves into that moisture into a brine, then is reabsorbed deep into the muscle (diffusion) over the following 12-24 hours. The end result: seasoned through, not just on the outside, and surface proteins denatured for cleaner browning during the final blast. Salting 30 minutes ahead leaves you in the worst of both worlds — the meat is wet and salt is still on the surface. Either salt 24 hours ahead or salt right before cooking. The 30-minute window is the trap. *Source: Samin Nosrat, Salt Fat Acid Heat, 2017, pp. 105-115; J. Kenji López-Alt, The Food Lab, 2015, pp. 414-419.*

**3.** Place the salted shoulder on a rack over a tray, **uncovered**, in the fridge for **12-24 hours**. Surface drying is part of the goal — the drier the surface, the better the final crackle.

**4.** Make the rub: pound garlic, anchovy, rosemary, thyme, lemon zest into a paste in the mortar (or whisk in a bowl). Stir in olive oil, mustard, and the additional 3 g pepper. Hold in the fridge until 30 minutes before the oven.

> **Why anchovy?** Anchovy contributes free glutamates and inosinate — two umami compounds that synergise (perceived umami is roughly 8× the sum of either alone). At 20 g in a 2 kg shoulder, anchovy is undetectable as fish. What registers is a deeper, savourier, more roast-tasting lamb. The same trick that Italian grandmothers have used in slow-cooked sugo for four hundred years. *Source: Yotam Ottolenghi, Plenty More, 2014; mechanism in Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 187-189.*

### PHASE 2: Rub and Set the Bed
*15 minutes — before bed*

**5.** Pull the shoulder from the fridge. Rub the garlic-anchovy paste all over — into the score lines, under whatever skin folds catch it, across the meaty side and the fat side both. Do not rinse the salt off. The salt is in the meat now.

**6.** In the roasting tray, arrange the bed: onions cut-side down, carrots, celery, garlic head, bay, rosemary, peppercorns. Pour over the wine and stock — about 2 cm of liquid in the tray, no more. The rack sits above this; the shoulder above the rack. The liquid will not touch the meat.

**7.** Place the rack in the tray. Set the shoulder, fat-cap up, on the rack. Insert a probe thermometer into the thickest part of the muscle, away from the bone. Set the alarm to **90°C**.

### PHASE 3: The Overnight
*8-12 hours — fully unattended*

**8.** Heat the oven to **110°C (230°F)**. If your oven runs cool or has a fan that won't go below 130°C, see **Troubleshooting**. Slide the tray into the centre of the oven. Close the door. Go to bed.

> **Why 100-110°C?** This is the narrow window where collagen hydrolyses cleanly into gelatin (the process accelerates above 70°C, completes around 90-95°C internal) but the muscle proteins do not contract violently and squeeze out their water. Above 130°C the surface dries before the centre is done. Below 95°C the collagen never fully breaks down, no matter how long you cook. 110°C oven, 90-95°C internal target — that is the slow-roast envelope. *Source: Heston Blumenthal, In Search of Perfection, 2006, "Roast Lamb"; Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 152-155.*

**9.** Sleep. The shoulder will reach 90°C internal somewhere between **8 hours** (a 2 kg shoulder in a calibrated 110°C oven) and **12 hours** (a 2.4 kg shoulder in a 100°C oven, or one starting from fridge-cold straight to oven). The probe alarm tells you when. There is no checking it every two hours. There is no opening the oven door. **The oven door does the work.**

> **Why overnight, specifically?** Two reasons. First, the time required (8-12 hr) is exactly the length of a night's sleep — the oven is doing useful work while you do nothing. Second, the connective tissue continues to soften past the 90°C minimum: every additional hour at temperature pulls the texture from "tender" to "spoon-soft." A shoulder pulled at 8 hours is sliceable. The same shoulder at 12 hours falls apart under the weight of a fork. Overnight is structural, not aesthetic. *Source: Heston Blumenthal, BBC In Search of Perfection, episode "Roast Lamb," 2006.*

### PHASE 4: The Crackle
*30 minutes — morning*

**10.** When the probe reads 90°C, you have a choice. To eat now: skip to step 11. To eat later (e.g. cooked overnight, served at lunch): pull the shoulder from the oven, leave the probe in, tent loosely with foil, and rest at room temperature up to 2 hours, or refrigerate for up to 24 hours. The shoulder reheats spectacularly at 130°C until warm through, ~45 minutes from cold.

**11.** **The blast.** Crank the oven to **220°C (425°F)**. Lift the shoulder (still on the rack) and reset it on a clean tray to keep the bed from scorching. Return to the oven on the upper-middle rack. **15-20 minutes** until the fat cap is mahogany, blistered, and audibly crackling when tapped. Watch it the whole time. The line between caramelised and burned is two minutes wide.

**12.** Pull from the oven. Sprinkle Maldon over the surface — the hot fat catches it. Tent loosely with foil. Rest **30 minutes**. Resting matters less for shoulder than for medium-rare leg (the protein fibres are already relaxed) but it matters for the carving — a 30-minute rest means the meat slices and pulls cleanly without losing a tide of juice to the board.

### PHASE 5: The Jus
*10 minutes — during the rest*

**13.** Strain the bed liquids and rendered fat through a fine mesh strainer into a fat separator (or tall jug). Press the soft vegetables to release everything they have, then discard the spent solids — they have given. Let the jug stand 5 minutes. The fat rises in a clear amber layer; pour or skim it off. **Save it.** Lamb fat from a slow-roast is one of the great cooking fats, especially for potatoes.

**14.** Pour the de-fatted jus into a small saucepan. Bring to a brisk simmer over medium heat. Reduce by **a third to a half** — taste as you go. Adjust with a splash more wine, a squeeze of lemon, salt only if it needs it. The salt from the bird and the bed has already concentrated; salt at the end, not before.

### PHASE 6: The Service
*5 minutes*

**15.** Pull the shoulder apart with two forks at the table — let the guests see it come apart under the slightest pressure. The bone slides out. There is nothing more theatrical in a charter dining room.

**16.** Pile pulled lamb on warm plates or a platter. Spoon over the jus. Finish with chopped parsley, mint if using, lemon wedge on the side. Serve with whatever the season offers — see **Service Formats** below.

## THE SCIENCE

*Why this recipe works*

### Collagen Hydrolysis = The Whole Game

Connective tissue in mammalian muscle is built primarily of type I collagen — a triple-helix protein that is structurally inert at refrigerator temperature and tougher than steel at room temperature. Above 60-65°C it begins to denature; above 70°C it slowly hydrolyses into gelatin; the process accelerates exponentially as temperature rises and as time at temperature accumulates. At 90-95°C internal, given enough hours, all the collagen in a lamb shoulder is converted to gelatin, which lubricates the fibres and gives the cut its melt-on-the-fork character.

**What's happening:** The reaction is time-and-temperature dependent. At 60°C, full hydrolysis takes 24+ hours (the sous-vide pulled-lamb method). At 90°C, full hydrolysis takes 8-12 hours. At 100°C+, the muscle fibres begin contracting hard enough to squeeze out water faster than collagen can convert — you trade tenderness for dryness. The 100-110°C oven, 90-95°C internal corridor is the gentlest path that completes the conversion in a workable time.

**Why it matters:** Every other slow-roast lamb decision (rest, rub, blast) is cosmetic. Hit 90-95°C internal, hold for hours, and you have correct lamb. Miss this, nothing else rescues it.

*Source: Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 152-155, 599-605; J. Kenji López-Alt, The Food Lab, 2015, pp. 274-276.*

### Why Overnight Works for Shoulder

A lamb shoulder at 110°C ambient oven temperature climbs to 90°C internal at roughly **3.5-5 hours per kg** — a 2 kg shoulder is at temperature in 7-10 hours. Every additional hour at temperature past that minimum pulls more collagen into gelatin, softens the meat further, and renders more intramuscular fat. The fat is the bonus: a slow-roast shoulder loses 25-30% of its weight as rendered fat and water — fat that bastes the meat continuously and, separated, becomes one of the great cooking fats. The structural meat below the fat layer stays moist because the fat is leaving slowly, not flashed off.

**What's happening:** A high-temperature roast (180-220°C) shocks the surface, drives water off violently, and renders fat into the pan in a single dump. A 110°C roast lets fat render gradually across 10 hours, with the muscle staying near its juicy threshold throughout. The temperature gradient from outside-in is shallow — almost no overcooked outer band, almost no underdone core.

**Why it matters:** Overnight isn't a lifestyle convenience — it's the right cook time for the right cook temperature for this cut. A 4-hour shoulder at 160°C is recognisable but tougher and drier. The hours are doing structural work.

*Source: Heston Blumenthal, In Search of Perfection, 2006; Felicity Cloake, "How to make perfect roast lamb," The Guardian, 2013.*

### Why Overnight Ruins a Leg

Leg of lamb has perhaps a tenth of the connective tissue of shoulder and almost no intramuscular fat. The beauty of a leg is its lean, fine-grained muscle, which is at its peak between 50°C (rare) and 60°C (medium). Above 65°C the actomyosin complex contracts, water squeezes out, and the meat dries from the outside in — rapidly. A leg cooked overnight at 110°C arrives at 100°C internal in around 5 hours, then sits there for the remaining 6, dehydrating progressively. The result is grey, fibrous, dry — the opposite of what the cut wants to give.

**What's happening:** Without collagen to convert, there is nothing for the long, slow heat to *do* in a leg. The only thing happening at hour 6 is moisture leaving the muscle. The cut's character is in the medium-rare core, not in any conversion process.

**Why it matters:** This is the most common lamb mistake — applying shoulder logic to a leg, on the assumption that "longer = better." It's the opposite. The leg is a different recipe — see Elevation. *Reverse-sear at 80°C oven to a 50°C internal core, then 220°C blast.* That's the leg version.

*Source: J. Kenji López-Alt, The Food Lab, 2015, pp. 411-414, 432-436 (reverse-sear technique); Heston Blumenthal, In Search of Perfection, 2006 (leg-at-low-temperature method).*

### Dry Brine = Seasoned Through, Skin Dries

A 12-24 hour dry brine accomplishes three things in series. In the first hour, salt draws moisture out of the surface (osmosis is faster outward than inward at high concentration gradients). In hours 2-12, the brine that formed on the surface is reabsorbed into the muscle, carrying salt deep through the cut. By hour 12-24, the surface is dry to the touch — exposed, uncovered, in the fridge — because moisture has migrated inward and the air has wicked away what is left on the outside.

**What's happening:** The dry surface at the start of cooking is what allows the final blast to crackle. Wet skin at 220°C steams instead of crisping; dry skin at 220°C blisters and lacquers. The salt diffusion path also means the seasoning is internal, not just on the cut surfaces — every bite of pulled lamb is seasoned through.

**Why it matters:** Skip the dry brine and the meat tastes seasoned only on the edges, the surface stays soft under the final blast, and the crackle is patchy instead of edge-to-edge. The 12-24 hour wait is the cheapest upgrade in the recipe.

*Source: Samin Nosrat, Salt Fat Acid Heat, 2017, pp. 105-115; J. Kenji López-Alt, "The Food Lab: Why You Should Salt Your Meat At Least 24 Hours In Advance," Serious Eats, 2014.*

### The Bed = Aromatic Steam + Pan Jus in One Pot

Vegetables and wine in the tray are not roasting alongside the lamb — they are slowly steaming, releasing aromatic volatiles into the oven cavity, and capturing rendered fat and meat juices as they fall. Over 10 hours, the steam carries those aromatics back onto the surface of the lamb, and the liquid in the tray evolves from watered-down stock into a deeply flavoured base for the jus. By the end, the vegetables have surrendered everything; press them, strain, de-fat, reduce — and you have a sauce built with no extra effort.

**What's happening:** The closed oven environment is essentially a low-temperature combi steamer. Aromatics from rosemary, garlic, bay, and the wine's residual sugars and acids redeposit on the meat. The Maillard reactions on the bed's onions and the lamb's fat cap perfume the cavity with browned-meat compounds. The cook is doing four jobs at once — roasting the lamb, building the jus, perfuming the meat, and rendering the fat — in a single tray.

**Why it matters:** This is why a slow-roast lamb shoulder cooked on a rack with a bed tastes structurally different from one cooked on its own. Lose the bed and you lose the jus and the secondary aromatics.

*Source: Yotam Ottolenghi, Jerusalem, 2012 (shoulder method); Heston Blumenthal, In Search of Perfection, 2006 (rack-over-bed configuration).*

### The Final Blast = Maillard at the End, Not the Start

The 220°C, 15-20 minute final blast triggers the Maillard reaction on the now-dry, salt-tightened surface — caramelising amino acids and reducing sugars into the hundreds of brown, savoury, nutty volatile compounds that give roast lamb its distinctive aroma. Doing this at the end, not the beginning, means the surface arrives at the blast already dry (from the fridge stage) and already rendered (from the long cook), so it crisps fast before the interior overcooks.

**What's happening:** Maillard browning runs hardest above 140°C and accelerates rapidly above 180°C. The blast is short on purpose — long enough for the surface to colour and crackle, short enough that the 90°C interior creeps up by only 2-4°C. Salt applied at the end of the blast lands on hot fat and bonds.

**Why it matters:** Browning at the start of a long roast wastes the colour — the volatile aromatics evaporate over the next 10 hours. Browning at the end keeps them on the meat. The "sear at the start to seal the juices in" idea is folklore — it has been disproved in controlled tests since at least the 1930s. The crust seals nothing; it just tastes good. Time it for the end.

*Source: Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 778-783; J. Kenji López-Alt, "The Food Lab: The Reverse Sear," Serious Eats, 2013.*

### SUB-RECIPE: Leg of Lamb, Reverse-Seared (Tier 2)

*The opposite of overnight. Use only with leg, never with shoulder.*

| Ingredient | Weight | Volume |
|------------|--------|--------|
| Bone-in leg of lamb | 2.5-3 kg | 1 leg |
| Coarse sea salt | 30 g | 5 tsp |
| Cracked black pepper | 5 g | 2 tsp |
| Garlic, slivered | 30 g | 8 cloves |
| Fresh rosemary leaves | 5 g | 2 sprigs |
| Olive oil | 30 g | 2 tbsp |
| Dijon mustard | 20 g | 1 tbsp |

**Method:**

1. **24 hours ahead:** Pat dry, salt and pepper all over, rest uncovered on a rack in the fridge.
2. **3 hours before service:** Pull from fridge. Make incisions across the leg with the tip of a knife — slip a garlic sliver and a few rosemary leaves into each. Rub with mustard and oil.
3. **Reverse sear, stage 1:** Oven at **80°C (175°F)**. Leg on a rack over a tray, probe in the thickest part. Cook until internal reads **48°C** — about **2 hr** for a 2.5 kg leg, **2 hr 30 min** for 3 kg. **No higher.**
4. **Rest open:** Pull from oven, rest uncovered 20 minutes — internal carries to 50-52°C.
5. **Reverse sear, stage 2:** Oven cranked to **240°C (465°F)**. Return leg, **8-12 minutes** until the surface is deep mahogany and audibly crackling. Internal will rise to 54-56°C — medium rare, pink throughout, juicy.
6. **Rest 15 minutes** loosely tented. Slice against the grain in 5 mm slices. Serve with the same jus and bed approach — make the bed in the same tray, deglaze with wine after the leg comes out.

> **Internal temperature targets, leg only:**
> - **50°C** pulled — final 54°C — rare
> - **52°C** pulled — final 56°C — **medium rare** (the target)
> - **55°C** pulled — final 60°C — medium (some prefer)
> - **60°C+** — overcooked. Past 65°C, dry. No recovery.

*Source: Heston Blumenthal, In Search of Perfection, 2006 (low-temperature leg method); J. Kenji López-Alt, The Food Lab, 2015, pp. 432-436.*

### SUB-RECIPE: Rack of Lamb, French-Trimmed, Hot-and-Fast (Tier 2)

*30 minutes total. The fancy-dinner version.*

| Ingredient | Weight | Volume |
|------------|--------|--------|
| Rack of lamb, French-trimmed | 600-800 g | 1 rack, 7-8 ribs |
| Fine sea salt | 6 g | 1 tsp |
| Cracked black pepper | 2 g | ½ tsp |
| Dijon mustard | 15 g | 1 tbsp |
| Panko breadcrumbs | 60 g | 1 cup |
| Parmesan, finely grated | 20 g | 3 tbsp |
| Fresh parsley, finely chopped | 10 g | 3 tbsp |
| Fresh thyme, chopped | 5 g | 2 tsp |
| Garlic, grated | 5 g | 1 clove |
| Lemon zest | from ½ lemon | — |
| Butter, melted | 20 g | 1½ tbsp |
| Olive oil | 15 g | 1 tbsp |

**Method:**

1. **Salt the rack 1 hour ahead** (full 24 hr if you can). Bring to room temperature.
2. **Sear:** Hot heavy pan, olive oil, sear rack fat-cap down 2-3 minutes until browned, then 1 minute per other side. Rest off heat 5 minutes.
3. **Coat:** Brush with mustard. Combine panko, parmesan, herbs, garlic, zest, melted butter — press onto the mustard-coated fat side.
4. **Roast:** Oven at **200°C (400°F)**, on a rack, **12-15 min** until probe in the meat reads **52°C** (medium rare).
5. **Rest 5-8 minutes**, then slice between the bones into individual cutlets. Serve 2-3 per portion.

## MISE EN PLACE & PREP-AHEAD

*For professional service and galley-scale production*

### WHAT CAN BE PREPPED AHEAD

| Component | How Far Ahead | Storage | Notes |
|-----------|---------------|---------|-------|
| Salted shoulder (dry brine) | 12-24 hr | On rack, uncovered, fridge | Mandatory — minimum 12 hr |
| Garlic-anchovy rub | 3 days fridge | Sealed jar | Layer of olive oil on top to seal |
| Bed vegetables, prepped | 24 hr fridge | Airtight | Cut, hold under damp paper towel |
| Cooked shoulder (pre-blast) | Up to 24 hr fridge | Wrapped on rack | Reheat 130°C 45 min then blast |
| Pulled lamb leftovers | 3 days fridge / 2 mo frozen | In jus, sealed | Best second-day dish in the kitchen |
| Rendered lamb fat | 1 mo fridge / 6 mo frozen | Strained, jarred | Roast potato fat — exceptional |
| Pan jus | 5 days fridge / 3 mo frozen | Sealed | Reduce a touch when reheating |

### THE GALLEY PROTOCOL: Cook-Ahead, Reheat-to-Order

**For charter service when you cannot run the oven for 12 hours mid-day:**

1. **Day before charter:** Salt and rub the shoulder. Hold uncovered in the fridge.
2. **Night before service:** Slow-roast 8-10 hr at 110°C. Pull from oven at 90°C internal. Rest, refrigerate whole on its rack overnight (still on the bed-tray, juices held under).
3. **Day of service, 90 min before:** Lift shoulder from cold storage. 130°C oven, 45 min, until warm through.
4. **Final blast:** 220°C, 15 min, the regular crackle stage.
5. **Rest 30 min, pull, serve.**

This protects the technique under pressure. You are not running an overnight oven during a charter — you are reheating an already-perfect shoulder. The blast restores the crackle the fridge softened.

### YACHT GALLEY ADAPTATIONS

| Constraint | Adaptation |
|------------|-----------|
| **No leave-in probe thermometer** | Use a flash thermometer at hour 8, 9, 10. Check the thickest part. Stop fussing — the window is forgiving |
| **Underpowered oven (won't go below 130°C)** | Cook 6-7 hr instead of 10-12, watch the probe. The blast still works the same way |
| **Rough seas** | A cold roast is fine. Pre-cook in port. Reheat at sea on calm passages only — even then, a tray of liquid in a moving oven is a hazard |
| **Limited fridge space for dry brine** | Vac-pack the salted shoulder to dry-brine in a tighter footprint |
| **No bone-in available (charter butcher)** | Boneless rolled shoulder works. Add 1 hour to the cook time — the bone was helping |
| **No probe** | Default to 12 hours at 100°C for a 2-2.4 kg shoulder. Visual check: meat retracts from the bone, fat cap rendered, fork goes in unresisted |

### BATCH SCALING

| Component | ×1 (6-8 portions) | ×2 (12-16) | ×4 (24-32, charter scale) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|------------|---------------------------|-------|
| Shoulder | 2-2.4 kg | 4-4.8 kg (×2 separate) | 8-9.6 kg (×4 separate) | **Cook separately** — don't try one giant cut |
| Salt | 25 g | 50 g | 100 g | 12 g/kg always |
| Rub | base recipe | ×2 | ×4 | Make in one batch, divide |
| Bed (per shoulder) | base recipe | ×2 | ×4 | Each tray gets its own bed |
| Cook time | 8-12 hr | 8-12 hr | 8-12 hr | Time per cut does not scale — only batch count does |
| Final blast | 15-20 min | rotate trays mid-blast | rotate trays mid-blast | Watch every tray |

> **Charter rule:** Multiple shoulders, separate trays, separate racks. A doubled-up shoulder roast loses the airflow on the side facing the other piece — uneven crackle, partial collagen conversion. Always one shoulder per tray.

### FREEZER STRATEGY

**Raw lamb:**
- IQF / vac-packed shoulder freezes well — 6 months at -18°C
- Thaw 48 hours in the fridge for a 2 kg shoulder (longer than you think)
- Salt and rub after thawing, never before freezing — the salt accelerates oxidation in storage

**Cooked shoulder:**
- Pull, portion, freeze in 200 g bags with a splash of jus
- 2 months — beyond that, fat oxidises and the flavour fades
- Reheat in a covered pan with a splash of stock, gentle heat, 10 minutes — never microwave from frozen

**Lamb fat:**
- Strain through cheesecloth, jar in small portions
- Fridge 1 month / freezer 6 months
- Use for roast potatoes, confit garlic, vegetable braises

### PLATING IDEAS

**Family-style (the pull-apart):**
- Whole shoulder on a wood board, pulled apart with two forks at the table
- Jus in a small ladle pot
- Roast potatoes (in the lamb fat) in a separate dish
- Mint sauce or chimichurri in a tiny jug
- Lemon wedges on the saucer

**Plated restaurant:**
- 120 g pulled lamb, mounded on a warm plate
- Jus dragged through with a spoon — not pooled
- Polenta or white bean purée alongside, smear-style
- A confit garlic clove squeezed onto the meat
- Pickled red onion in a deliberate ribbon
- Microherbs only if they have flavour — otherwise nothing

**Charter buffet:**
- Shoulder on a warm board, pre-pulled, kept warm in jus
- Two flatbread baskets either side
- Three condiments: tzatziki, tomato salsa, pickled onion
- Lemon halves, mint sprigs, flaky salt within reach

## TROUBLESHOOTING

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| **Shoulder still tough after 8 hours** | Internal not yet at 90°C / oven runs cool | Keep cooking — every additional hour helps. Verify oven with a thermometer next time |
| **Meat dry, fibrous** | Cooked at too high a temperature / leg, not shoulder, used / past 95°C internal for too long with no liquid | Confirm cut next time; ensure 100-110°C; pull at 90-93°C internal not 100°C |
| **No crackle on the fat cap** | Surface still wet at the blast / oven too low at the blast / fat under-rendered | Dry-brine fully 24 hr; blast at 220°C minimum; pat surface dry just before the blast |
| **Burnt fat cap, raw inside** | Final blast at start, not end / oven too hot | Always blast at the END, after slow-roast hits 90°C. Drop blast to 200°C if oven runs hot |
| **Pan jus weak, watery** | Bed under-loaded with aromatics / liquid not reduced enough | Reduce by half; add a spoon of cold butter at the end for body; punch with anchovy or miso if flat |
| **Pan jus burnt** | Liquid evaporated mid-cook / tray too shallow | Top up bed with stock at hour 6 if it dries; use a deeper tray |
| **Meat tastes flat / under-seasoned** | Salted only at the surface, not 24 hr ahead | Dry-brine next time. For now, season the pulled meat with flaky salt and a squeeze of lemon |
| **Smells of mutton (off)** | Lamb beyond expiry / poor-quality grain-finished lamb | Source matters — Welsh / Pyrenean / NZ grass-fed are reliable; trust your nose at the unwrap stage |
| **Leg of lamb dry and grey (overnight applied)** | Wrong cut for this method | Cannot rescue. Use leg version at 80°C reverse-sear next time. Leftover dry leg salvages as ragù — braise in tomato 30 min |
| **Probe reads 90°C but meat still firm** | Probe placed near bone (reads bone temperature, not muscle) | Reposition probe into thickest muscle, away from bone. Continue cooking |
| **Tray smoking heavily during the blast** | Fat layer in pan ignites at 220°C | Pull bed contents before the blast — clean tray, just the rack |

 Bone-in shoulder 2.0-2.4 kg, fat cap intact
 Welsh / Pyrenean / NZ grass-fed
 Score fat cap in 2 cm crosshatch

 25 g coarse salt + 5 g cracked pepper
 Massage all over, rest UNCOVERED on rack in fridge

 30 g garlic + 20 g anchovy + 10 g rosemary + 5 g thyme

 Pound to paste, rub all over

 2 onions halved + 3 carrots + 4 celery + garlic head
 + bay + rosemary + 250 ml white wine + 500 ml stock
 In tray, rack on top, shoulder on rack fat-up

OVERNIGHT:
 110°C (230°F) oven, probe in thickest muscle
 8-12 hr until INTERNAL 90°C (target 90-95°C)
 DO NOT OPEN THE DOOR

THE BLAST:
 220°C (425°F), 15-20 min, watch closely
 Crackle the fat cap, sprinkle Maldon

REST:
 30 min loosely tented

JUS:
 Strain bed, de-fat (save the fat),
 reduce by 1/3 to 1/2, season last

SERVE:
 Pull at the table, two forks
 Jus, lemon wedge, parsley, mint

LEG VARIANT (different cut, different method):
 Reverse-sear: 80°C oven to 50°C internal,
 then 240°C blast 8-12 min to 56°C medium rare
 NEVER apply overnight method to a leg

STORE: pulled lamb 3 d / 2 mo frozen in jus
       lamb fat 1 mo fridge / 6 mo frozen
