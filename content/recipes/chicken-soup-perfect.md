# Chicken Soup

**Cold-start extraction. Roasted bones for depth. Fish sauce and a parmesan rind for umami. Chicken poached separately and pulled, never boiled into cotton. Every step exists for a reason — and the reason is why this tastes like the chicken soup you remember before you actually ever had it.**

Most chicken soup recipes waste the bird. A whole chicken boiled with vegetables gives you tired meat and pale broth — both compromised by the same pot. This version separates the stock build from the protein cook, roasts the bones for Maillard depth, uses cold-water extraction to pull collagen cleanly, and finishes with umami seasoning that stays invisible. The chicken goes in tender. The broth goes in clear. The bowl tastes like more than the sum of its parts.

## METHOD

### PHASE 1: Roast the Bones
*30 minutes — unattended*

**1.** Heat oven to 220°C (425°F). Arrange bones and wings in a single layer on a sheet pan. Roast until deep mahogany, **25-30 minutes**, turning once at the 15-minute mark. You want colour, not carbon.

> **Why roast?** Roasting triggers Maillard reactions on the surface proteins of the bones and skin, creating hundreds of new flavour compounds that cold-water extraction alone cannot produce. Raw-bone stock is cleaner and lighter. Roasted-bone stock is deeper and rounder. This soup wants round.

**2.** If using chicken feet, bring a small pot of water to a rolling boil, drop the feet in for **60 seconds**, then shock in ice water. Peel off the yellow outer membrane — it comes off in sheets — and trim the nails. The feet now go into the stock raw. They are 70% collagen and will transform the body of the broth.

### PHASE 2: Build the Stock
*3 hours — mostly unattended*

**3.** Transfer roasted bones to the 8 L stockpot. Deglaze the sheet pan with a cup of water, scraping up all the browned bits, and add that to the pot. Every brown fleck is flavour.

**4.** Add **cold** water to cover by 5 cm — about 4 L. Do not use hot water, do not shortcut. Cold-start extraction opens the bones slowly as the water heats, drawing out collagen and soluble proteins cleanly.

> **Why cold water?** Proteins and collagen dissolve across a temperature gradient. A slow climb from cold to simmer extracts the full spectrum. Dropping bones into already-hot water seizes surface proteins into a scum layer and locks gelatin inside the bone matrix. Every serious stock cook knows this.

**5.** Bring to a bare simmer over medium heat — **90 minutes** is ideal for the climb. You will see scum and foam rise as the water heats. Skim ruthlessly with a shallow skimmer for the first 20 minutes, then every 15 minutes for the first hour. This scum is coagulated albumen (egg-white-like protein) plus impurities. Skimming it is the difference between broth and consommé-clear.

**6.** Once the pot holds a bare simmer — bubbles breaking only occasionally on the surface, never a rolling boil — add the chicken feet, onions, carrots, leek tops, celery, garlic, parmesan rind, bay, peppercorns, thyme, and parsley stems.

> **Never boil.** A rolling boil emulsifies fat into the water — you will never get it out, and the broth will taste greasy and look cloudy. A bare simmer is 85-90°C (185-195°F). Use a thermometer the first few times until your eye knows.

**7.** Simmer uncovered, bare simmer, for **2 hours 30 minutes to 3 hours** from the moment the aromatics go in. Top up with hot water only if the bones become exposed. Do not stir. Skim any fat or scum as it rises.

**8.** At the end of the simmer, strain through a fine-mesh strainer lined with cheesecloth or a superbag set over a large container. Do not press the solids — press and you cloud the broth.

**9.** Cool rapidly by setting the container in an ice bath, stirring occasionally. Once under 20°C, refrigerate. Within 2 hours, a cap of pale-yellow fat (schmaltz) sets on top, firm enough to lift off. This is the cleanest stock you will ever make.

### PHASE 3: Poach the Chicken
*25 minutes — unattended*

**10.** This can happen in parallel with the stock's final hour. In the 4 L poaching pot, bring 2 L water + 10 g salt to a bare simmer. Add thigh (and breast if using) skin-side up. Maintain bare simmer — **never boiling** — for **18-22 minutes** for thighs, **14-16 minutes** for breast. Internal temperature target: **74°C (165°F)** for thigh, **63°C (145°F)** for breast pulled and carried.

> **Why separate?** Chicken muscle above 75°C squeezes out its juices — protein fibres contract and shed water. A bird simmered for 3 hours with a stock is leathery, washed out. A separately poached chicken, held at 85°C water, stays tender because the poaching liquid never rises above the protein's squeeze-out threshold.

**11.** Lift chicken out. Cool on a plate. Once cool enough to handle, pull the meat off the bones in hand-sized pieces — not shredded to threads, not cubed. Pieces with character. Discard skin and bones, or save skin for crisping as garnish. Hold meat in 100 ml of the poaching liquid to keep it moist.

**12.** The poaching liquid is now a light second broth — use it to stretch the main stock if needed, or save for rice cooking.

### PHASE 4: The Soup
*20 minutes*

**13.** Lift the schmaltz cap off the chilled stock and reserve in a jar — it is one of the best cooking fats in the kitchen. Warm the stock in the 4 L soup pot over medium heat.

**14.** In a separate pan, sweat the leek whites, carrot brunoise, celery, and fennel in 20 g of the schmaltz over medium heat until soft and sweet but without colour, **8-10 minutes**. Salt lightly as you go — **3-4 g**. Soft vegetables in clear broth, not raw crunch.

**15.** Add the sweated vegetables to the warming stock. Simmer **10 minutes** — enough for the vegetables to fully release into the broth but not so long they disintegrate.

**16.** Add the pulled chicken in the last **2 minutes** to warm through — do not simmer the meat further.

**17.** Off the heat, add grated garlic, fish sauce, lemon juice, parsley, and dill if using. Taste and adjust salt.

> **Seasoning protocol:** Salt the stock lightly during the simmer (about 4 g per litre — not final seasoning, just a baseline). Salt the sweating vegetables lightly (3-4 g). Final seasoning at the end of Phase 4, by taste. Salting early and lightly layers the flavour; salting at the end corrects. Do not salt the stock heavily at the start — as the stock reduces, an early-salted stock becomes inedibly salty.

### PHASE 5: The Bowl
*At service*

**18.** Cook the starch separately per Group E. Do not cook it in the soup pot.

**19.** Place a portion of starch in each warm bowl. Ladle hot soup over the top — broth, vegetables, chicken. Finish with a teaspoon of reserved schmaltz (it blooms the aroma), a crack of black pepper, a pinch of parsley, and an optional squeeze of lemon at the table.

> **Why assemble at the bowl?** Starch in broth absorbs and swells — 200 g of noodles held in the stock pot for 30 minutes will drink half the liquid and turn the broth starchy. Cooking and holding separately keeps the bowl clear, the noodles bouncy, and the reheating of leftovers honest.

## THE SCIENCE

*Why this recipe works*

### Cold-Start Extraction = Clean Gelatin

Collagen is a triple-helix protein that lives in connective tissue and bone matrix. Below 70°C, it is inert. Between 70-90°C, it slowly unwinds and hydrolyses into gelatin — the compound that gives stock its body, mouth-feel, and lip-stick glossiness. Above a rolling boil, the process continues but the agitation emulsifies fat into micro-droplets that suspend in the water permanently.

**What's happening:** The slow climb from cold lets soluble proteins (albumen) coagulate and rise as scum you can skim, while collagen unwinds gently into gelatin. The temperature gradient does the sorting for you.

**Why it matters:** A cold-start, bare-simmer stock is clear, glossy, and clean-tasting. A hot-start or boiled stock is cloudy, greasy, and muddy. The technique is the whole game.

*Source: Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 599-605.*

### Chicken Feet = The Gelatin Multiplier

Chicken feet are roughly 70% collagen by dry weight. A stock with feet sets to a firm jelly when chilled; a stock without feet sets to a soft wobble. That jelly is mouth-feel — the reason a restaurant chicken broth coats the lip and a home version does not.

**What's happening:** The feet's ratio of connective tissue to bone is the highest of any chicken part. Blanching first removes surface impurities and the yellow cuticle; simmering extracts a disproportionate gelatin yield per gram.

**Why it matters:** 300 g of feet added to a 4 L stock roughly doubles the gelatin content without changing flavour. It is the single cheapest upgrade in soup-making and most home cooks skip it.

*Source: Thomas Keller, Bouchon, 2004; corroborated by Kenji López-Alt, Serious Eats, "Pressure-Cooker Chicken Stock," 2017.*

### Roasted Bones = Maillard Depth

Roasting bones at 220°C triggers the Maillard reaction between amino acids and reducing sugars on the bone surface and residual skin. This creates hundreds of volatile aromatic compounds — nutty, toasted, meaty, caramelized — that do not exist in raw-bone stock.

**What's happening:** Dry heat above 140°C drives surface moisture off and lets proteins and sugars brown. When the bones then go into cold water, those compounds dissolve gradually and carry their aroma into the stock.

**Why it matters:** Raw-bone stock tastes of clean chicken. Roasted-bone stock tastes of roast chicken. For soup-as-comfort, the roasted version is what memory expects.

*Source: Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 778-782.*

### Fish Sauce + Parmesan Rind = Invisible Umami

Fish sauce and aged parmesan both deliver high concentrations of free glutamates — the amino acid responsible for the umami taste. At 1 tbsp of fish sauce in 2.5 L, the flavour is undetectable as fish or salt; at 30 g of parmesan rind simmered for 3 hours, the flavour is undetectable as cheese. Both contribute to a rounder, deeper, more savoury broth — the "how is this chicken soup so good?" dimension.

**What's happening:** Glutamate receptors on the tongue (T1R1/T1R3) fire in response to free L-glutamate and amplify the perceived intensity of other flavours. Fish sauce provides up to 1,300 mg of glutamate per 100 g; aged parmesan provides up to 1,200 mg per 100 g.

**Why it matters:** The two seasoning additions triple-underline the chicken flavour without announcing themselves. Skip them and the broth tastes thin; add them and it tastes restaurant.

*Source: Kenji López-Alt, "The Food Lab: The Best Chicken Stock," Serious Eats, 2014; Ikeda Kikunae's glutamate-receptor science, 1908, consolidated in McGee.*

### Separate Protein Cooking = Tender Meat

Chicken muscle protein (actomyosin complex) begins to squeeze water out above 65-70°C and contracts dramatically above 75°C. Meat simmered in stock for 3 hours loses nearly all its internal water and turns leathery. Meat poached gently to 74°C internal and pulled holds its juices and stays tender.

**What's happening:** Controlled-temperature poaching keeps the protein fibres below the squeeze-out threshold for most of the cook, letting collagen in the connective tissue (dark meat) dissolve without driving out the water in the muscle bundles.

**Why it matters:** The single most common failure of home chicken soup is cotton-dry meat. The fix is structural, not seasoning. Cook the chicken separately.

*Source: Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 148-152; Samin Nosrat, Salt Fat Acid Heat, 2017, pp. 45-51.*

### Acid at the Finish = Brightness Without Loss

Lemon juice added at the end of cooking brightens the bowl because the volatile aromatic compounds in citrus (limonene, citral) are driven off by prolonged heat. Acid added to hot broth after the heat is off preserves the volatiles and balances the fat and salt on the palate.

**What's happening:** Volatile citrus oils boil off between 170-180°C and degrade above 60°C over time. A lemon squeezed into the pot an hour into the simmer yields dull, tired citrus. The same lemon squeezed in at service yields sharp, lifted citrus.

**Why it matters:** The acid closes the loop. Salt + fat + umami + acid is the four-corner seasoning grid; skip the acid and the soup tastes one-note no matter how good the stock.

*Source: Samin Nosrat, Salt Fat Acid Heat, 2017, Chapter 3.*

### SUB-RECIPE: Matzo Balls (Tier 2)

| Ingredient | Weight | Volume |
|------------|--------|--------|
| Matzo meal | 120 g | 1 cup |
| Eggs, separated | 4 | — |
| Schmaltz, melted | 60 g | 4 tbsp |
| Chicken stock | 60 g | ¼ cup |
| Fine salt | 4 g | ¾ tsp |
| Baking powder (for floaters) | 2 g | ½ tsp |
| Fresh dill, chopped | 5 g | 1 tbsp |
| Freshly ground black pepper | pinch | — |

**Method:** Whisk yolks with schmaltz, stock, salt, dill, pepper. Whip whites to soft peaks; fold into yolk mixture. Fold in matzo meal and baking powder. Chill **30 minutes** (non-negotiable — the meal needs to hydrate). Wet hands; form 8 balls, golf-ball size. Drop into bare-simmering salted water (not the soup — matzo clouds stock). Cover, **30-40 minutes**, no peeking. Transfer to the soup at service.

> **Floater vs. sinker:** Baking powder + whipped whites + covered simmer = floater. No baking powder + yolks-only + uncovered = sinker. House style is floater.

### SUB-RECIPE: Consommé Clarification (Tier 3)

| Ingredient | Weight |
|------------|--------|
| Finished stock, cold | 2 L |
| Ground chicken (or minced chicken wings) | 200 g |
| Egg whites | 3 |
| Mirepoix (onion/carrot/celery) brunoise | 150 g total |
| Tomato paste | 15 g |

**Method:** Whisk all ingredients except stock in a pot. Whisk in **cold** stock. Place over medium heat, stirring gently every few minutes from the bottom. As the stock heats, the egg white + meat + vegetables form a raft that floats to the surface, capturing particulate and fat as it rises. Once the raft sets — around 75°C — stop stirring. Reduce to the barest simmer. Cut a small chimney in the raft with a ladle handle and simmer for **45 minutes**. Ladle the clarified broth out through the chimney, straining through a superbag. The result is a crystal-clear consommé with reinforced chicken flavour from the meat and aromatics added to the raft.

### THE GALLEY HACK: Stock Ice Cubes + Portioned Soup Bags

**For charter service:**

1. After straining, portion finished stock into 1 L vacuum bags and 500 ml ice-cube trays.
2. Freeze flat — thaw faster, stack efficiently.
3. Freeze cooked matzo balls on a tray, then bag them.
4. Freeze pulled chicken in 200 g portions with a splash of stock.
5. At service, thaw the needed portions in a warm-water bath. Soup ready in 15 minutes, not 4 hours.

**Yield math:** 2 kg bones + 4 L water + 300 g feet → 2.5 L finished stock + 80-100 g schmaltz + negligible evaporation with a proper lid schedule.

### FREEZER STRATEGY

**Stock (priority freezer item):**
- Chill completely with fat cap intact
- Portion into 1 L vacuum bags or sealed containers
- Freeze flat
- Up to 3 months; after that, flavour fades
- Thaw in warm water bath or overnight in fridge
- Never microwave from frozen — uneven thaw breaks the gelatin

**Finished soup:**
- Freeze without the starch and without the pulled chicken
- Add starch and chicken fresh at reheat
- Up to 2 months
- Reheat gently, never boil

**Chicken fat (schmaltz):**
- Strain through cheesecloth to remove water droplets
- Jar in small portions
- Fridge 2 months; freezer 6 months
- Use as you would duck fat

## TROUBLESHOOTING

| Problem | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| **Cloudy broth** | Boiled, not simmered / starchy vegetables added / pressed strainer | Skim more aggressively next time; never press solids; maintain 85-90°C |
| **Greasy broth** | Fat emulsified by boiling | Chill, lift fat cap; if not chillable, blot surface with paper towel; next time, bare simmer only |
| **Thin body / no gelatin** | No feet / no wings / raw not roasted / too much water | Add feet next time; reduce stock by 25% now to concentrate |
| **Bitter broth** | Over-simmered (beyond 4 hr) / too many liver pieces / scorched bones | Strain, dilute, add a spoon of honey or a peeled raw potato simmered 10 min to absorb bitterness |
| **Dry, stringy chicken** | Boiled with stock / cooked past 75°C internal | Cook separately next time; rescue now by shredding and stirring into schmaltz + broth to re-hydrate |
| **Flat flavour** | Under-salted / no umami anchor / no acid | Salt first, then fish sauce or parmesan rind, then lemon at the end |
| **Gluey / starchy bowl** | Starch cooked in the broth pot | Cook starch separately; for current service, thin with more stock |
| **Matzo balls dense** | Not chilled before forming / mix too dry / boiled hard | Chill 30 min minimum; add 1 tbsp stock to loosen; covered bare simmer |
| **Matzo balls falling apart** | Mix too wet / water boiling | Add 1 tbsp matzo meal; reduce heat to bare simmer |
| **Broth tastes of cardboard** | Bones under-roasted / vegetables over-cooked / oxidation from long fridge hold | Roast darker; vegetable simmer cap 2.5 hr; use stock within 5 days or freeze |
