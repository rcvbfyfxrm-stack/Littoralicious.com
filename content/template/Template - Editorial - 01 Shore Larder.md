# SHORE LARDER — Template

**Voice:** A love letter written by a scientist. Reverent but not romantic. You're introducing the reader to something they think they know — then proving they don't.

**Rhythm:** Open with a number that stops them. Ground it in tradition. Go deep on the science. Show them where they've been getting it wrong — gently, so they laugh and learn. End with something they'll try tonight.

**Word Count:** 700-900 · **Read Time:** 3-4 min

---

## [INGREDIENT NAME]
*[Latin name] · [Origin region] · [Peak season]*

**[NUMBER].** [One-sentence fact that reframes everything. A number, a historical detail, a chemical truth that earns the next paragraph.]

**What It Actually Is:**
[2-3 sentences. Botanical or zoological reality. What part of what organism. What process creates the flavor you know. Not Wikipedia — the details a chef should be embarrassed not to know.]

**The Story That Sticks:**
[One paragraph. A person, a place, a moment in time. Not "it was traded along the Silk Road" — who traded it, why, what happened when it arrived. A funeral, a war, a monopoly, a grandmother. Something they'll retell.]

**The Tradition:**
[One paragraph. How this ingredient has been used in its homeland for centuries — the preparation that defines it, the ritual, the context. Not museum-piece history — living practice. What a grandmother in [origin] would recognize. What the commercial version lost. What we can learn from the original use that improves how we handle it today.]

**The Compound That Matters:**

| Compound | Also Found In | What It Does |
|----------|---------------|--------------|
| [Primary aroma/flavor compound] | [2-3 surprising foods] | [Flavor effect + any bioactive role] |
| [Secondary compound] | [2-3 foods] | [Effect] |

**Three Pairings You Haven't Tried:**
*Based on shared volatile compounds (VCF database / Foodpairing analysis):*

1. **[Ingredient] + [Surprise]** — [Shared compound]. [One sentence on why it works and how to use it.]
2. **[Ingredient] + [Surprise]** — [Shared compound]. [Application.]
3. **[Ingredient] + [Surprise]** — [Shared compound]. [Application.]

**The Science That Surprises:**
[One genuinely surprising nutritional or physiological finding. Not superfood marketing — peer-reviewed, specific, verifiable. The kind of finding that makes a chef rethink how they use this ingredient or what they tell curious guests. Cite the study: institution, journal, year, key number. If the finding has a limitation or a "but," say it. Always name what commercial processing does to the science.]

**Where Most Go Wrong:**
[2-3 common mistakes. Not a lecture — a recognition scene. The reader should see themselves in at least one of these. Write it conspiratorial, not condescending: "We've all done it." Each mistake follows: what people do → why it fails (the science) → what to do instead. Keep it entertaining — the reader should smile while learning something they'll actually remember.]

1. **[Mistake name.]** [What people do. Why everyone does it — make it recognizable. One sentence on the science of why it fails. The fix.]
2. **[Mistake name.]** [Same structure. Make it specific enough that the reader thinks "that was last Tuesday."]
3. **[Mistake name.]** [Optional third. Only if genuinely useful — two strong beats better than three weak ones.]

**Sourcing:**
- [What to look for: color, texture, smell]
- [What to avoid: specific red flags]
- [The difference between good and great (and whether great is worth it)]

**On Board:**
- **Storage:** [Temperature, wrapping, container. Standard yacht AC applies — only flag unusual storage hacks when they genuinely extend shelf life or improve quality beyond the obvious.]
- **Shelf life:** [Honest, not packaging claims]
- **The move:** [One preparation in 3-4 sentences. Not a full recipe — an idea. Something unexpected that takes under 5 minutes and makes guests remember you.]

**Sources:** [Named references — book, database, researcher. For science claims: full citation with journal, year, DOI if available.]

---

## Quality Gates

- [ ] Opening fact genuinely surprises (not "did you know vanilla is expensive")?
- [ ] Story has a human name or specific place?
- [ ] Tradition section shows living practice, not textbook history?
- [ ] At least one pairing they've never seen?
- [ ] Compound data is verifiable?
- [ ] Science claim is peer-reviewed with named source?
- [ ] Common mistakes — reader recognizes themselves in at least one?
- [ ] Common mistakes are entertaining, not preachy?
- [ ] Storage advice is practical? (Skip the obvious — most yachts have AC. Only flag storage hacks when they're genuinely worth knowing.)
- [ ] Every claim sourced or clearly marked as professional experience?
