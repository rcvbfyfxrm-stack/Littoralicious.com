« The best way to predict the future is to invent it — but increasingly, the future is inventing itself. » — paraphrasing Alan Kay

L'Éditorial

Three weeks ago, Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.6, and within hours, a quarter-trillion dollars in software market capitalization evaporated. These two facts are connected, and the connection tells you everything you need to know about where artificial intelligence sits in February 2026: it is no longer a technology looking for a market. It is a market looking for survivors.

The era of the LLM as chatbot is over. What has replaced it — agents that plan, execute, debug, and iterate across multi-step workflows — is something the software industry has never faced before. Not disruption in the Clayton Christensen sense, where an inferior product eats from the bottom. This is disruption from above: the most capable tool arriving first for the most complex tasks.

Les Grands Récits

Anthropic's Opus 4.6 and the Billion-Dollar CLI

On February 5, Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.6 with a one-million-token context window and a feature that sounds technical but carries enormous implications: agent teams — the ability for multiple AI agents to coordinate on complex coding tasks, dividing labor, sharing context, and working in parallel.

The specification reads like a staffing plan. A lead agent decomposes a problem, creates tasks, and assigns them to specialist sub-agents — one for frontend, one for backend, one for testing. The sub-agents execute independently, report back, and the lead agent integrates their work. It is, in effect, a software development team that costs pennies per hour, never sleeps, and can be spun up for a single commit.

The commercial context makes the technical achievement concrete. Claude Code, Anthropic's command-line interface for AI-assisted development, has reached a $1 billion annualized revenue run rate — just six months after its launch. Anthropic's most recent funding round valued the company at $350 billion, with $10 billion raised. These are not startup numbers. These are infrastructure numbers.

What makes Claude Code's growth remarkable is its distribution model. It does not sell to enterprises through lengthy procurement cycles. It sells to individual developers through a terminal command. One developer tries it, commits code with it, and their team notices the velocity change. Adoption spreads laterally, from keyboard to keyboard, not top-down from a CTO's mandate. By the time procurement gets involved, the tool is already embedded in the codebase.

The $285 Billion Rout That Nobody Predicted (But Everyone Should Have)

On February 3, two days before the Opus 4.6 release (which had been pre-announced), $285 billion in software stock market capitalization vanished in a single trading session. The catalyst was not a rate hike, not an earnings miss, not a geopolitical shock. It was fear — specifically, the fear that Anthropic's AI coding tools could make significant portions of the enterprise software stack redundant.

The selloff hit hardest in categories where AI agents are already demonstrating capability: project management, customer support, code review, testing automation, and low-code development platforms. Investors looked at Claude Code's revenue trajectory, looked at the enterprise software companies charging $50-100 per seat per month for tools that an AI agent could increasingly replicate, and did the math.

This is not a bubble bursting. It is a repricing. The enterprise software industry was built on a model where humans needed software to be productive. What happens when software needs neither the humans nor the other software? The market is beginning to answer that question, and the answer is denominated in hundreds of billions.

The nuance that the selloff ignored: enterprise software companies are not passive targets. Salesforce, ServiceNow, Atlassian, and others are integrating AI agents into their own platforms at speed. The question is not whether AI replaces enterprise software but whether the incumbents capture the value or the AI-native companies do. History suggests some of both, but the transition period — which we are in — favors the attackers.

OpenAI Fires Back: GPT-5.3-Codex

OpenAI did not watch Anthropic's February quietly. The release of GPT-5.3-Codex — described as OpenAI's most capable agentic coding model — arrived with a distinction that made security researchers sit up: it is the first model to be classified as "High capability" for cybersecurity by OpenAI's own internal evaluation framework.

What this means in practice: GPT-5.3-Codex can identify vulnerabilities in code with a sophistication that approaches professional penetration testers. It can reason about attack surfaces, suggest exploitation paths, and — crucially — generate patches. The dual-use nature of this capability is obvious and uncomfortable. A model that can find and fix vulnerabilities can also find and exploit them.

OpenAI has implemented usage restrictions and monitoring, but the cat-and-mouse dynamic is now permanent. Every improvement in defensive AI capability is simultaneously an improvement in offensive capability. The cybersecurity industry, which spent 2025 talking about AI-augmented threats as a future concern, is now dealing with them as a present reality.

The competitive dynamic between Anthropic and OpenAI has shifted from "whose chatbot gives better answers" to "whose agent writes better code and operates more reliably in production." This is a fundamentally different race, with fundamentally different winners.

The Quiet Revolution: Agents That Actually Work

Behind the headlines about billion-dollar revenues and stock routs, a less dramatic but more consequential shift has occurred over the past twelve months. Agentic AI systems now reliably execute 10-to-15-step workflows. This sentence deserves to be read twice, because twelve months ago it was not true.

In early 2025, AI agents were impressive demos and unreliable tools. They could write a function but not integrate it. They could answer a question but not follow up on ambiguity. They could generate a plan but not adapt when the plan failed. The gap between "impressive in a demo" and "trustworthy in production" was a canyon.

That canyon has narrowed to a ditch. The combination of reasoning models with native chain-of-thought capabilities, improved function calling (models now reliably select the right tool, invoke it with correct parameters, and interpret the results), and better context management means that agents can now execute complex, multi-step operations with error rates low enough for production use.

What does this look like in practice? A developer describes a feature in natural language. The agent reads the existing codebase, identifies the relevant files, writes the implementation across multiple files, runs the test suite, interprets failures, fixes its own bugs, and submits a pull request — all without human intervention. Not every time. Not for every feature. But often enough that the developers who use these tools report 2-5x productivity gains on routine coding tasks.

The implications extend far beyond software development. Customer support agents that handle multi-turn conversations, resolve issues across multiple systems, and escalate intelligently. Financial analysis agents that pull data from multiple sources, run calculations, generate reports, and flag anomalies. Research agents that search literature, synthesize findings, and draft summaries. These are not hypotheticals. They are products shipping today.

Reasoning Models: The Architecture That Changed Everything

The shift from 2025 to 2026 has a technical explanation that is worth understanding, even in non-technical terms. The models that power today's agents are reasoning models — systems that think before they answer.

Earlier models generated text token by token, each word influenced by what came before but without any explicit planning or deliberation. Reasoning models introduce a step before generation: they produce an internal chain of thought — a sequence of reasoning steps that are not shown to the user but that guide the final output. The model considers multiple approaches, evaluates their likelihood of success, and selects the most promising path before committing to a response.

The practical effect is dramatic. Function calling — the ability of a model to decide when to use an external tool, construct the correct input, and interpret the output — went from unreliable to near-perfect. Multi-step planning — the ability to decompose a complex task into subtasks and execute them in the right order — went from fragile to robust. Error recovery — the ability to recognize when something has gone wrong and try a different approach — went from rare to routine.

This is why agents work now when they did not work eighteen months ago. The underlying models crossed a threshold of reliability that makes the difference between a toy and a tool.

Signaux Faibles

The $285 billion selloff was concentrated in mid-cap software companies ($5-50B market cap). The mega-caps — Microsoft, Google, Amazon — were comparatively unscathed, suggesting that investors believe the platform companies will capture AI value while pure-play software companies may lose it. This divergence is a signal worth watching.

Claude Code's revenue model — usage-based pricing through a CLI — is a return to the metered-computing economics of the mainframe era. Developers pay for what they compute, not for a seat license. If this model spreads beyond coding tools, the entire SaaS pricing model (per-seat, per-month) may be approaching obsolescence.

The "High capability" cybersecurity classification for GPT-5.3-Codex creates a regulatory question that nobody has answered: should AI models above a certain capability threshold require licensing for access? The EU is thinking about this. The US is not. The gap may matter.

Agent teams — multiple AI agents coordinating on a task — introduce a new failure mode: agents disagreeing with each other. Early reports from Claude Code's agent teams suggest that when sub-agents reach contradictory conclusions, the lead agent's resolution is not always optimal. The orchestration problem is the next frontier.

La Connexion

The through-line connecting every story this week is the collapse of a comfortable assumption. For twenty years, the technology industry assumed that AI would automate simple tasks first and complex tasks last. The evidence of February 2026 suggests the opposite: AI is automating complex, high-value cognitive work — software engineering, security analysis, financial modeling — while simple physical tasks remain stubbornly resistant.

This inversion has implications that extend beyond technology. If the most valuable white-collar work is the first to be augmented (or replaced), then the economic distribution of AI's benefits will look nothing like what policymakers assumed. The developer using Claude Code is more productive, but the developer competing against Claude Code is more vulnerable. The enterprise that adopts AI agents cuts costs, but the enterprise software company that sold to that enterprise loses revenue.

We are watching, in real time, the fastest repricing of human cognitive labor in history. The question is not whether this repricing will continue — the technology trajectory makes that certain. The question is whether institutions can adapt at the speed required. Based on the $285 billion that evaporated in a single afternoon, the market's answer is: probably not.
