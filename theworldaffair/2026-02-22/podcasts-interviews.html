Journal Liberté — Podcasts & Interviews

22 février 2026

« A good interview is not a transaction of information. It is a collision of curiosities. »

L'Éditorial

Podcasting in February 2026 sits at a peculiar crossroads. Joe Rogan still commands roughly eleven million listeners per episode — a number that dwarfs most television audiences and that nobody in the medium can approach. And yet the most interesting developments in audio this month are happening at the margins: a late-night host accusing his own network of censorship, a new history podcast designed to make its listeners uncomfortable, and a CBC series where the interviewer turns the microphone on herself.

The medium has matured past its adolescence. The question now is whether it can produce journalism, not just conversation.

Les Grands Récits

Colbert, CBS, and the Interview That Never Aired

On February 20, Stephen Colbert did something that late-night hosts almost never do: he accused his own network of killing an interview. Speaking on his show — with the careful phrasing of a man who has consulted lawyers but not been silenced by them — Colbert alleged that CBS had spiked a completed interview segment over fears of backlash from the Federal Communications Commission.

Colbert did not name the interview subject, which has generated intense speculation. What he did describe was a process: a segment was recorded, reviewed by the show's editorial team, approved for broadcast, and then pulled by network executives who cited "regulatory considerations." The implication — that CBS is pre-emptively censoring content to avoid conflict with a politically appointed regulator — is extraordinary, not because it is surprising but because a network employee said it on the network's own airwaves.

The context is the ongoing tension between broadcast networks and the current FCC, which has signaled a willingness to scrutinize content decisions more aggressively than its predecessors. Whether the FCC's posture constitutes genuine regulatory pressure or performative intimidation is debatable. What is not debatable is the effect: at least one network, by Colbert's account, is making editorial decisions based on what a regulator might think rather than what the audience deserves to see.

For podcasting, the episode is a recruiting advertisement. Every interview that a broadcast network declines to air is an interview that a podcast could run unedited, without regulatory oversight, to an audience that has already demonstrated its appetite for unfiltered conversation. The migration of serious long-form interviewing from broadcast to podcast — a trend that has been building for a decade — just received another push.

"History for the Reckoning": Uncomfortable by Design

Launched on February 19, History for the Reckoning is a new podcast with a premise that sounds simple and proves difficult: tell historical stories that most people would prefer not to hear, and extract from them lessons that might prevent repetition.

The first episode deals with a subject the show's creators describe only as "an event that shaped a nation's identity and that the nation has spent a century trying to forget." The production values are high — archival audio, original scoring, interviews with historians and descendants — but the editorial posture is what distinguishes it. The show does not adopt the neutral, both-sides-have-a-point tone that dominates history podcasting. It has a point of view: that historical discomfort is not a side effect of learning history but the point of it.

The show arrives in a media environment that is simultaneously oversaturated with history content (there are now more than 3,000 active history podcasts in English alone) and undersaturated with history content that challenges its audience. Most history podcasts are designed to make listeners feel clever — "here's a fascinating story you didn't know." History for the Reckoning is designed to make listeners feel implicated — "here's a story you didn't want to know, and here's why it matters that you do."

Whether the audience for discomfort is large enough to sustain a podcast remains to be seen. The early download numbers, reportedly strong, suggest that at least some listeners are tired of history as entertainment and ready for history as confrontation.

Helena de Groot Turns the Lens on Herself

On February 10, CBC's Personally series aired an episode titled "Creation Myth," in which host Helena de Groot — a Dutch-American poet, editor, and interviewer known for her work on the Poetry Foundation's podcast — does something that interviewers almost never do well: she becomes the subject.

De Groot is a skilled interviewer precisely because she disappears. Her questions are precise, her silences are generous, and her subjects routinely say things they did not intend to say — not because they were tricked but because they were heard. Turning that apparatus on herself is a risk. Self-interview can descend quickly into self-indulgence.

It does not, in this case. "Creation Myth" is a 40-minute examination of de Groot's own creative origin story — growing up between languages, between countries, between the desire to make art and the suspicion that making art is a selfish act. The episode works because de Groot brings the same editorial discipline to her own story that she brings to her subjects': she cuts what is merely interesting and keeps what is true.

The result is one of the most honest pieces of audio memoir produced this year — not because it reveals scandalous details but because it reveals the ordinary machinery of a creative life: doubt, persistence, accident, and the slow realization that the thing you are making might matter to someone other than yourself.

Black History Month: The Robesons, the Renaissance, and the Mess

Among the dozens of Black History Month podcast specials airing in February, one stands out for its refusal to sanitize its subjects. Our Ancestors Were Messy — the title is both a thesis statement and a warning — dedicates its February episode to Eslanda and Paul Robeson, set against the backdrop of the Harlem Renaissance.

Paul Robeson is one of the most extraordinary figures in American history — an All-American football player, Columbia Law graduate, internationally acclaimed singer and actor, and a political activist whose opposition to American racism led him to embrace the Soviet Union at the worst possible moment. Eslanda Goode Robeson, his wife, was an anthropologist, author, and political figure in her own right — and a woman who managed, navigated, and occasionally endured a marriage that was as politically complicated as it was personally turbulent.

The podcast does not lionize either Robeson. It does not condemn them. It presents them as they were: brilliant, flawed, courageous, contradictory, and embedded in a historical moment — the Harlem Renaissance — that was itself brilliant, flawed, courageous, and contradictory. The Harlem Renaissance was not a tidy cultural movement. It was a collision of art, politics, sexuality, class conflict, and racial identity that produced masterpieces and feuds in roughly equal measure.

By placing the Robesons inside this context rather than above it, the podcast achieves something that most biographical shows do not: it makes its subjects more interesting by making them less heroic. The messy ancestors of the title are not diminished by their messiness. They are enlarged by it.

Orson Welles' Radio Show, Rediscovered

A podcast episode that aired on February 18 explores a piece of broadcast history that has been largely forgotten: Orson Welles' "Commentaries," a series of radio editorials that Welles delivered on ABC in 1946.

Welles, who was 30 years old and already the director of Citizen Kane, used the ABC platform to deliver political commentary that was, by the standards of 1946 radio, incendiary. He spoke about racial violence, police brutality, the treatment of returning Black veterans, and the hypocrisy of a nation that had just defeated fascism abroad while practicing racial apartheid at home. The series lasted approximately four months before ABC cancelled it — under pressure, according to Welles, from sponsors who objected to his content.

The podcast episode — which uses archival recordings of Welles' original broadcasts, interviews with media historians, and readings from Welles' personal correspondence — draws an explicit line between 1946 and 2026. The parallels it identifies are uncomfortable and deliberate: a gifted communicator using a mass medium to address racial injustice, corporate sponsors exerting editorial pressure, and a network choosing commercial safety over editorial courage.

The Colbert-CBS story from earlier this week makes the parallel particularly pointed. The medium has changed — radio to podcast, FCC to streaming platform — but the dynamics of editorial suppression have proven remarkably durable.

Rogan's Gravity: 11 Million and the Problem of Scale

Joe Rogan continues to average approximately 11 million listeners per episode on The Joe Rogan Experience, a number that exists in a category of its own and that shapes the podcast industry in ways that are not always visible.

There is no second Rogan. The next largest interview podcasts — Call Her Daddy, SmartLess, Armchair Expert — operate at roughly one-fifth to one-tenth of his audience. This concentration of attention in a single show creates a gravitational effect: publicists route their biggest guests through Rogan first, which makes his show more attractive to listeners, which makes publicists route more guests through Rogan, and so on. The rich-get-richer dynamic that dominates every media industry has produced, in podcasting, a degree of market concentration that would attract antitrust scrutiny in any other sector.

The more interesting question is what Rogan's dominance means for the medium's journalistic potential. Rogan is not a journalist. He does not investigate, verify, or hold his subjects accountable in the way that journalism demands. He provides a platform — an enormously valuable platform — for long-form conversation. But conversation is not journalism, and the conflation of the two is the central confusion of the podcasting era.

The shows covered in this report — History for the Reckoning, Our Ancestors Were Messy, the Orson Welles episode — are journalistic in their ambitions: they research, they verify, they have a point of view grounded in evidence. They reach audiences measured in thousands, not millions. The gap between what podcasting can do at its best and what podcasting does do at its most popular is the medium's defining tension.

Signaux Faibles

Colbert's on-air accusation has produced, as of this writing, no official response from CBS. The network's silence is itself a signal — either the legal exposure is real and the network is consulting counsel, or the accusation is accurate and the network has no defense it is willing to articulate publicly.

History for the Reckoning is distributed independently, without a network or studio deal. Its creators chose independence specifically to avoid the editorial interference that Colbert described. The show's production costs are funded by a combination of grants and listener support — a model that works for quality but not for scale.

The Orson Welles episode has generated unusual interest from media studies academics, who see in the 1946 ABC cancellation a case study for contemporary debates about platform responsibility and editorial freedom. At least two universities have added the podcast episode to spring 2026 syllabi.

Rogan's 11 million figure has been stable for approximately 18 months. Growth has plateaued, not because listeners are leaving but because the addressable audience for a three-hour conversational podcast may have a natural ceiling. The question for Spotify, which pays Rogan approximately $250 million per contract cycle, is whether a flat audience justifies escalating costs.

La Connexion

The thread connecting Colbert's accusation, a new history podcast, a CBC memoir, the Robesons, Orson Welles, and Joe Rogan is a question that is as old as broadcasting itself: who decides what gets heard?

In 1946, ABC's sponsors decided that Orson Welles' commentary on racial violence should not be heard. In 2026, CBS executives decided — according to Colbert — that an interview should not be aired for fear of regulatory consequences. In both cases, the suppression was not ideological in the crude sense. It was commercial. The calculation was not "this content is wrong" but "this content is costly."

Podcasting was supposed to solve this problem. No gatekeepers, no sponsors with veto power, no FCC. And in some cases, it has. History for the Reckoning exists because its creators do not need CBS's permission. Helena de Groot's memoir exists because CBC's podcast division operates with editorial independence that its broadcast division may not enjoy.

But the Rogan paradox complicates the narrative. The most-heard voice in podcasting is not a journalist, not an investigator, not a provocateur in the Welles tradition. He is a conversationalist with extraordinary reach and limited accountability. The medium that was supposed to democratize speech has produced its own concentration of power — not through censorship but through attention.

The question is not whether podcasting is free. It is. The question is whether freedom, without the discipline of journalism, produces anything more than noise at scale and excellence in obscurity.
