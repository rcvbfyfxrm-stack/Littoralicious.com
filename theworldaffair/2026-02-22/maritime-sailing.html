« La mer ne récompense pas ceux qui sont trop anxieux, trop cupides ou trop impatients. » — Anne Morrow Lindbergh

L'Éditorial

Sailing has always been an industry that moves at the speed of wind. But this week, the winds are shifting in several directions at once. Miami just wrapped a boat show that quietly acknowledged what the industry has been whispering about for two years: the future of sailing might not look like its past. Meanwhile, in New Zealand, wooden boats are staging a comeback that nobody predicted, and somewhere in a naval architecture studio, engineers are designing a 226-foot vessel that needs neither diesel nor shore power. The sea is not dying. It is transforming.

Les Grands Récits

Miami Bets on Sailboats Again — And That Says Everything

The Miami International Boat Show (February 11-15) has long been a cathedral of fiberglass excess — a place where motor yachts with helicopter pads commanded the docks and the attention. This year, something different appeared at IGY Yacht Haven Grande Miami: Sailor's Cove, an entirely dedicated zone for sailboats, catamarans, and sailing gear.

On the surface, it sounds like a simple zoning decision. Beneath it lies an industry acknowledgment. The powerboat market, which drove margins for years, is cooling. Sailboat and catamaran interest — particularly among younger buyers drawn to the idea of sustainable, self-sufficient cruising — is climbing. Show organizers did not create Sailor's Cove out of sentimentality. They created it because the foot traffic data told them to.

The timing is deliberate. Chartering companies in the Caribbean and Mediterranean report that catamaran bookings now outpace monohull bookings by nearly 3 to 1 for first-time charterers. The liveaboard community, once a niche subculture on YouTube, has become a genuine demographic that boat manufacturers now design for. Miami noticed. Miami adapted.

Life Jackets and the Quiet Work of Saving Lives

The Sea Tow Foundation's 2026 Life Jacket Loaner Program opened applications this month, with the deadline of February 28 approaching. The program, which provides free life jacket loaner stations at marinas, boat ramps, and waterfront parks across the United States, is the kind of initiative that never makes headlines but consistently saves lives.

The numbers are unglamorous and devastating in equal measure. The U.S. Coast Guard's most recent data shows that in drowning deaths where the victim was aboard a vessel, roughly 85% were not wearing a life jacket. The Sea Tow program addresses this not through regulation or enforcement but through access — put the jackets where people launch, make them free, remove every excuse. It is public health dressed as boating infrastructure, and it works because it asks nothing of the people it protects except that they reach for the vest hanging on the rack.

Applications are open to any waterfront facility in the country. The bureaucracy is minimal. The impact, by the Coast Guard's own admission, is measurable.

Auckland's Wooden Boat Festival: 110 Vessels and a Philosophy

The Auckland Wooden Boat Festival has confirmed its dates — March 13-15, 2026 — and finalized a fleet of over 110 boats on the water. In an era of carbon fiber and foiling trimarans, a festival dedicated to wooden boats sounds like nostalgia tourism. It is not.

What Auckland has assembled is a floating argument about craftsmanship, sustainability, and the idea that not everything in sailing needs to go faster. The festival draws boatbuilders from across the Pacific — New Zealand, Australia, Fiji, Tonga — and pairs them with audiences who increasingly want to understand how things are made, not just how fast they go. Several of the vessels in this year's fleet are restorations spanning decades: a kauri-planked cutter from the 1930s, a Polynesian-inspired sailing canoe built using traditional lashing techniques, a gaff-rigged ketch that has crossed the Tasman four times.

The event has quietly become one of the southern hemisphere's most significant maritime heritage gatherings. Its growth — from 40 boats in its early editions to 110 this year — mirrors a broader cultural appetite for authenticity in a world saturated with composite hulls and autopilot.

The Industry Reckons with Itself

Step back from the events and the pattern becomes visible. The yachting industry is in a period of structural transformation, and the signals are converging.

Consolidation is accelerating. The major groups — Beneteau, Ferretti, Azimut-Benetti — are absorbing smaller yards and rationalizing production lines. Independent builders who thrived on bespoke commissions are finding that the post-pandemic order book surge has flattened, and the clients who remain are more demanding, more informed, and more likely to compare options across a digital marketplace that did not exist five years ago.

Refits are outperforming new builds. This is the number that keeps shipyard executives awake. Owners are choosing to refit existing vessels — new electronics, upgraded rigging, repowered engines — rather than commission new construction. The economics are straightforward: a well-executed refit delivers 70-80% of a new boat's capability at 40-50% of the cost, with a fraction of the delivery time. For the refit yards in Palma, La Ciotat, and Auckland, this is a boom. For new-build yards, it is an existential question.

Digital platforms are reshaping sales. The traditional brokerage model — a broker who knows a guy who knows a guy — is giving way to platforms that aggregate listings, provide condition reports, and connect buyers with surveyors and financing in a single interface. The broker is not dead, but the broker who adds no analytical value is endangered.

Project Zero: The Ketch That Needs Nothing

The most quietly radical development in sailing this month comes not from a race or a show but from a design studio. Project Zero, a 226-foot (69-meter) performance ketch, has been unveiled with a proposition that sounds impossible until you read the engineering: the vessel will use only solar and wind energy for both propulsion and all onboard electricity.

No generators. No diesel tanks. No shore power dependency.

The design relies on a combination of advanced solar panel arrays integrated into the deck and superstructure, high-capacity battery storage, regenerative propulsion from the sailing rig itself, and a hull form optimized for efficiency at low speeds. The ketch rig — two masts, with the mizzen forward of the rudder post — was chosen specifically because it offers the best balance of sail area and handling for a vessel of this size that must sail in all conditions, not just favorable ones.

Project Zero is not a concept rendering. It is in active development, with a naval architecture team that has built vessels before. Whether it achieves true energy independence in real-world ocean conditions remains to be proven, but the ambition is significant: a superyacht-scale sailing vessel with a carbon footprint that approaches zero during operation. If it works, it redefines what "performance" means in the context of large sailing yachts.

Signaux Faibles

The details that most people overlook but that often matter most:

The life jacket loaner program's February 28 deadline is not arbitrary — it aligns with marina preparation for spring commissioning season. Stations installed before April have historically achieved 3x the utilization of those installed mid-summer, because they are present when seasonal boaters are still forming their habits for the year.

Auckland's decision to include Polynesian traditional sailing vessels alongside European designs is a political statement disguised as curation. New Zealand's maritime identity is contested territory — British colonial sailing heritage versus Polynesian voyaging heritage — and the festival is choosing both.

The refit-over-new-build trend has a second-order effect that few are discussing: it is creating a skilled labor shortage in the refit yards. A refit demands more diverse craftsmanship than a new build (which benefits from production-line specialization). The yards that can attract and retain multi-skilled shipwrights will dominate the next decade.

La Connexion

What links Miami's Sailor's Cove, Auckland's wooden boats, the refit economy, and Project Zero is a single theme: the sailing world is rediscovering that its most valuable asset is not speed or size but self-sufficiency. The catamaran buyer in Miami wants to anchor out and not need a marina. The wooden boat builder in Auckland wants to repair with materials found locally. The refit owner wants to extend the life of what already exists rather than consume something new. And Project Zero wants to cross oceans without consuming anything at all.

In an industry that spent two decades chasing bigger, faster, and more luxurious, the pendulum is swinging toward something older and, perhaps, wiser: the idea that the best boat is the one that needs the least from shore.
