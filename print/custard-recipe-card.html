Custard — Crème Anglaise

Cream and milk, warmed. Yolks and sugar, whisked pale. Temper. Back to the pan. Stir constantly to 82°C — not 83, not 85. Strain. Ice bath. The base every serious dessert on a menu leans on.

Yield: 750 ml (8) Active: 25 min Total: 45 min + chill Target: 82°C (180°F) Window: 80–84°C

Kit: heavy 3 L saucepan (light interior) · silicone spatula · instant-read thermometer · chinois · ice-bath bowl · whisk (yolks only)

Method — Crème Anglaise

  1. Infuse dairy. Milk + cream + split vanilla (seeds + pod). Bare simmer, cover, steep 10 min. Coffee / tea / zest / saffron infuse now. Vanilla volatiles flee a rolling boil; a gentle steep extracts without damage.
  2. Yolks to ribbon. Yolks + sugar + salt. Whisk vigorously 2–3 min until pale and ribboning. Sugar coats the proteins and raises their coagulation temp by 10–15°C. Under-whisked = pre-scrambled. — McGee, pp. 97-98
  3. Temper. Re-warm dairy to steam. Pour 1/3 into yolks in a thin stream, whisking constantly. Return tempered yolks to the pan with remaining dairy. Switch from whisk to silicone spatula. Gradual climb lets proteins unfold rather than seize. Every curdled custard is either un-tempered or too hot.
  4. The stir. Medium-low heat. Figure-eight with corner sweeps, continuously. No whisking — it aerates and hides the texture. 5–8 min. Coagulation danger is at the pan bottom and corners where heat concentrates. Figure-8 means every cm² is scraped every 2–3 sec.
  5. Read the thickening at ~75°C: clean trail on the pan bottom, surface goes glossy. Two to three more minutes.
  6. Target 82°C (180°F). Nappe test: line held across the spatula back with a fingertip. Thermometer in the liquid, not touching pan. 80°C = thin. 82°C = perfect. 84°C = edge. 85°C = curdled. No recovery above the ceiling.
  7. THE STOP. At 82°C, off heat immediately, pour through chinois into the ice-bath bowl. Do not stop at the heat — carryover hits 85°C in seconds.
  8. Stir over ice 1–2 min to drop under 30°C. Press plastic wrap directly on the surface. Chill 2 hr minimum, ideally overnight — flavour develops.
Pastry cream variant: starch in yolks + whisk (not stir) + BOIL 90 sec (amylase kill — unboiled pastry cream liquefies overnight). Off heat, 30 g butter. Film on surface. Whisk before use.
Sous-vide variant (bulletproof): same ingredients → bag → 82°C / 45 min / agitate every 15 min → chinois → ice bath. Failure rate zero.

Elevation

Tier 1 — No Extra Time
  • Tahitian vanilla — floral/cherry-cola vs. Bourbon
  • Brown sugar swap — 30 g of total
  • Extra yolk — 7 for ice-cream base
  • Malt syrup — 10 g, Horlicks register
Tier 2 — Worth It
  • Sous-vide — 82°C / 45 min / unfailable
  • Caramelised cream — dry-caramel 40 g, deglaze off heat
  • Brown-butter base — replace 100 ml cream
  • Duck yolks — 4 ducks for 6 hens, richer
  • Crème brûlée — same base, ramekins, bain-marie 150°C 30–40 min, brûlée at service
Tier 3 — Restaurant
  • Ice cream — 7 yolks + 60 g brown butter, churn, harden 4 hr
  • Pot de crème — espresso-cup bake, bain 150°C 25–35 min
  • Custard espuma — iSi + 1 N₂O, chill 1 hr
  • Île flottante — anglaise + poached meringue + caramel
  • Sabayon — Marsala replaces 100 ml cream, bain-marie whisk

Troubleshoot

Curdled / grainyPast 84°C. Stick-blender over ice bath (saves 70%); strain hard.
Too thin chilledUnder-cooked (<80°C). Use as thin sauce; re-cook impossible.
Too thickOver-reduced. Thin with warm cream, spoonful at a time.
Eggy smell / sulphurOver-cooked. 82°C target, chinois, non-negotiable.
Skin on surfaceNo film contact. Press plastic directly on custard.
Pastry cream soup AMAmylase not killed. Must boil 90 sec whisking.
Ice cream icyNot enough fat / no alcohol. 20 ml vodka per L base.
Brûlée top softToo little sugar / damp top. 2–3 mm sugar, pat dry, 2–3 torch passes.
