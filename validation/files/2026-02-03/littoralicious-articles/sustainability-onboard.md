# Sustainability Onboard

**The regulations are tightening. The science is clear. Here is the working protocol for yacht crews who want to do it properly — not performatively.**

Sustainability on superyachts has an optics problem. Floating structures burning 500 liters of diesel per hour do not become environmentally virtuous because someone swapped the cling film for beeswax wraps. But that fact does not excuse inaction. The regulatory framework is closing in, the operational savings are real, and the galley — where the most controllable waste stream originates — is where crew have the most direct agency.

This is not a manifesto. It is a protocol guide.

## The Regulatory Reality

### What Applies to You Now

**MARPOL Annex V** governs garbage discharge from every vessel regardless of size. As of 2025, ships of 100 GT and above must maintain a Garbage Record Book (lowered from 400 GT via IMO Resolution MEPC.360(79)). Even below that threshold, the discharge prohibitions apply.

The **Red Sea and Gulf of Aden** became MARPOL Special Areas on 1 January 2025 — garbage discharge restricted to regulation 6 compliant procedures only. The **Mediterranean Sea SOx Emission Control Area** took effect 1 May 2025 — fuel sulphur content capped at 0.1% or equivalent scrubber system required.

**Norwegian Sea and Canadian Arctic NOx ECAs** activate 1 March 2026.

### What Is Coming

The **EU Emissions Trading System** now covers shipping for vessels over 5,000 GT in commercial operation. 40% of emissions subject to allowance surrender in 2025, rising to 100% by 2027. **FuelEU Maritime** mandates a 2% GHG intensity reduction versus 2020 baseline from 2025, tightening progressively toward 2050.

An **IMO carbon levy** is expected from 2027. The pricing framework remains under negotiation, but the direction is unambiguous.

Translation: running costs are going up. Waste reduction and efficiency are no longer ethical preferences — they are operational cost controls.

## Food Waste: The Galley's Controllable Variable

A superyacht generates food waste comparable to cruise ship data — approximately 1 kg per person per day when accounting for bespoke multi-course service, preference sheet overprovisioning, and the mismatch between what is ordered and what guests actually eat.

### The Provisioning Fix

The single highest-impact intervention is not recycling. It is not buying the waste in the first place.

**Menu-driven provisioning** means every item on every order ties to a specific dish on a specific day. Generic stock-up orders — "get some nice cheeses," "grab extra fruit" — are how you end up throwing away 30% of your fresh provisions.

Build a tentative menu before each charter based on the preference sheet. Create provisioning lists from that menu. Adjust quantities using your experience with actual consumption patterns, not aspirational ones. A 12-guest charter with a preference for light lunches does not need the same protein volume as a 12-guest charter that wants full silver service three times a day.

**FIFO is non-negotiable.** First In, First Out. Label everything with receipt date. Plan meals around what needs using first. This is basic kitchen management, but under charter pressure it is the first discipline to collapse.

**Storage protocol matters.** Never buy pre-packed salads — buy whole heads, wash, dry, store leaf-by-leaf between paper towels in sealed containers. Extends usable life from 2 days to 7. Never stack different produce types in the same container. Portion and freeze excess immediately rather than storing it in the fridge while hoping to use it tomorrow.

### Creative Waste Elimination

| Waste Stream | Protocol |
|---|---|
| Vegetable trim, peels, herb stems | Stock production — freeze in labeled bags until stock day |
| Fish heads, bones, shells | Fumet, bisque, shellfish butter |
| Bread past service standard | Breadcrumbs, croutons, bread pudding (crew mess) |
| Guest plate returns | Crew meal base (same day only, food safety applies) |
| Overripe fruit | Compotes, sorbets, smoothies, vinegar ferments |
| Citrus peels | Oleo saccharum, dried garnish, cleaning agent |

### Tracking

You cannot reduce what you do not measure. Implement a daily food waste audit: weigh what leaves the galley as waste. Log it. After two weeks you will know exactly where your waste concentrates. The data consistently shows the same three culprits: overprovisioned perishables, buffet overproduction, and crew meal neglect (cooking separately for crew when guest leftovers could feed them).

### Donation Infrastructure

**Yachting Gives Back** in Palma de Mallorca redirects surplus provisions to local food banks and soup kitchens. Clean out galley stores at season end and before crew changes. Every major Mediterranean port has food donation infrastructure — the barrier is not availability, it is crew awareness.

### Use-By vs. Best-Before

Use-by dates are safety measures. Respect them. Best-before dates are quality indicators. A tin of tomatoes three months past its best-before date is not a health risk. A yogurt two days past its use-by date might be. Know the difference. It eliminates a significant fraction of unnecessary disposal.

## Sustainable Seafood: The Mediterranean Problem

Approximately 85% of Mediterranean fisheries are overfished. Around 60% of seafood consumed in European Mediterranean nations is imported, frequently from fisheries with poor environmental standards. This is the baseline you are provisioning from.

### Buy This

| Category | Species | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Small pelagics | Sardines, anchovies, mackerel | Fast reproduction, low trophic level, abundant |
| Shellfish | Mussels, clams, oysters | Minimal environmental impact, often improves water quality |
| Responsible aquaculture | Arctic char, rainbow trout, domestic tilapia | Well-managed farming operations |
| Underutilized | Sea urchin, lesser-known rockfish | Reduces pressure on popular species |

### Never Buy This

- **Bluefin tuna** — critically overfished in the Mediterranean. No exceptions. No "sustainable bluefin" marketing.
- **European eel** — severely depleted, complex lifecycle makes recovery slow.
- **Orange roughy** — matures at 10-20 years, high mercury accumulation.
- **Wild sturgeon / beluga caviar** — vulnerable across all populations.
- **Shark** (any species) — apex predator removal destabilizes marine ecosystems.

### Certification Shorthand

**MSC** (blue label) = wild-caught, verified sustainable fishery. **ASC** = responsibly farmed. Specify these when ordering through provisioners. Download the **WWF Mediterranean Seafood Guide** (traffic-light system, updated regularly, available in multiple languages) and the **Monterey Bay Aquarium Seafood Watch** app. Carry them. Reference them before placing fish orders.

When a provisioner says "sustainable" without a certification, it means nothing.

## Water

Average superyacht consumption runs 115-380 liters per guest per day across personal use, laundry, cleaning, pools, and tenders. The galley contributes significantly through continuous rinsing, dishwashing, and food preparation.

### The Bottled Water Problem

The superyacht industry consumes an estimated 40 million liters of bottled water annually. Every 10,000 crew generate 3.2 million plastic bottles per year — 100 tonnes of single-use waste.

The solution is not complicated. Modern 5-stage filtration systems connected to onboard watermakers produce water that meets or exceeds bottled water quality. Provide branded refillable bottles to guests. Octo Marine's #ThinkOutsideTheBottle campaign claims 4 million bottles saved per year across its client base.

If the owner insists on branded bottled water for aesthetic reasons, that is a conversation for the captain. But the galley can eliminate crew bottled water immediately with zero guest impact.

### Galley Water Protocol

- Install spray taps if not already fitted — reduces water use by 30-50% versus continuous flow
- Never run water continuously for rinsing produce — fill a basin
- Reuse vegetable washing water for initial deck cleaning
- Run dishwashers only when full

## Single-Use Plastic Elimination

### The Galley Swap List

| Remove | Replace With |
|---|---|
| Cling film | Beeswax wraps, Stasher silicone bags, glass containers with lids |
| Plastic produce bags | Reusable mesh bags |
| Plastic-wrapped provisions | Request paper-only packaging from provisioners |
| Disposable gloves (where regulations allow) | Reusable food-safe gloves, proper hand hygiene protocols |
| Individual sauce packets | Bulk containers, ramekins |
| Plastic straws | Stainless steel (or none — most adults do not need straws) |

### Provisioner Protocol

Request paper-only packaging from all provisioners as default. Several yacht chefs report that provisioners comply without resistance when asked directly. The barrier is asking, not availability.

**Ecoworks Marine** produces cleaning products designed specifically for marine environments. **Greenspeed** is recommended for galley-specific cleaning (floor cleaner, washing-up liquid). Both are available through standard yacht chandleries.

## Composting at Sea: The Biodigester Option

The **Power Knot LFC** is the leading marine-rated biodigester. It performs aerobic decomposition of food waste within 24 hours — fruit, vegetables, meat, fish, cheese, bread, rice. Food is not ground; it decomposes to liquid that exits through a fine mesh screen and can be pumped overboard (in compliant waters) or into the sewage treatment system.

The superyacht *Just B* installed the world's smallest commercial-grade unit. ROI typically within 18 months through eliminated waste storage, reduced port disposal fees, and reclaimed deck space previously occupied by pulper tanks.

**Regulatory caution:** Some port states classify biodigester output differently under biosecurity laws. Verify local regulations before discharging in territorial waters. MARPOL Annex V still governs all food waste discharge regardless of processing method.

## Certifications Worth Knowing

**RINA Green Plus** — The Italian classification society's voluntary notation covering 12 pollution sources with scored design and operational solutions. Three tiers: Green Plus, Green Plus Gold, Green Plus Platinum. Goal-based, not prescriptive. Platinum Award presented annually at Monaco Yacht Show.

**SEA Index** — Founded by Yacht Club de Monaco. Ranks yachts 1-5 stars based on CO2 performance. Based on IMO's EEXI methodology, developed with Lloyd's Register. Expanded to 15 marinas in 2024.

**Water Revolution Foundation YETI** — Science-based lifecycle carbon intensity measurement using AIS data. Currently being translated into an ISO standard (submitted to ISO/TC 8/SC 12 Large Yachts).

**Environmental Crew Guidelines v1** — Free, open-source, 24 sections. Published by Water Revolution Foundation, MB92, and Danella Hopkins. Introduces the **Eco-Sustainability Ambassador (ESA)** crew role. Download it. Read it. Assign the role.

## Shore Power

Connect to it whenever available. Every hour on shore power is an hour your generators are not running. This eliminates local emissions, reduces noise, extends generator service intervals, and in most cases costs less than fuel.

More marinas are installing shore power connections. Ensure your vessel's electrical system is compatible with the **IEC/ISO/IEEE 80005** framework. The EU mandates shore power infrastructure at major ports by December 2025, with mandatory use for large commercial vessels from 2030. Superyacht-specific mandates are not yet in force but the trajectory is clear.

## The Galley Sustainability Checklist

1. Appoint or become the Eco-Sustainability Ambassador for your department
2. Maintain a Garbage Record Book regardless of vessel tonnage
3. Weigh and log food waste daily
4. Tie every provisioning order to a specific menu
5. Source locally at every port
6. Carry the WWF Seafood Guide and Seafood Watch app
7. Never order bluefin tuna, eel, orange roughy, wild sturgeon, or shark
8. Specify MSC/ASC certification when ordering seafood
9. Eliminate cling film — beeswax wraps, silicone bags, glass containers
10. Request paper-only packaging from all provisioners
11. Install or advocate for onboard water purification
12. Implement at least two meat-free days per week for crew
13. Donate surplus to food banks at every port of call
14. Investigate biodigester installation
15. Connect to shore power whenever available

None of this requires owner approval. None of it costs more than current practice. Most of it saves money.

The yacht industry will not become sustainable by switching to electric propulsion — the energy density problem alone makes that decades away for vessels over 30 meters. But the galley can become sustainable now, with protocols that already exist, using infrastructure that is already in place.

The question is not whether the regulations are coming. They are. The question is whether you are ahead of them or behind.
