BEACON

Every food business ever opened is one of two ideas. One sells an occasion. The other sells a thing. Learn the two equations — and what kills each — before you build your own.

The Spoon Lab's concept bench: the food business taken back to first principles, then rebuilt — one honest concept at a time.

The method

Understand first. Act later.

When Ferran Adrià's team sets out to understand something, they do not brainstorm. They define, classify, compare, map the system, trace its history — a methodology called Sapiens, built by Adrià and the elBullifoundation team after the restaurant closed, out of its old habit of questioning everything. Their volume answering “What is cooking?” runs five hundred pages. Their rule: understand first, act later. metodologiasapiens.com

Beacon points that discipline at one question: what is a restaurant, really? Strip away the formats, the trends, the franchise decks — what is irreducibly there?

The decomposition

Five questions. Only five.

Feeding people for money always answers the same five questions. Everything else — cuisine, décor, branding — is surface.

  • SellWhat do you sell — a thing, or an occasion?
  • RoomWhere is it eaten — in a room you pay for, or wherever the guest carries it?
  • HandsWho serves — a paid front-of-house layer, or your own hand across the counter?
  • RangeHow wide is the menu — forty dishes that bend to any table, or one thing repeated ten thousand times?
  • PriceWhat is the guest actually paying for — the two hours, or the thing in their hand?
Why there are only two shapes

The five answers are not independent — they cluster. Choose occasion and you are forced to buy the room, the crew and the wide menu. Choose the thing and you may delete all three.

That clustering is why food service has two stable shapes and not a smooth continuum: every cost you delete must be replaced by volume, and every point of volume you give up must be replaced by check-building.

The two archetypes

The classic restaurant, and the hole in the wall.

The stakes first: about 26% of independent restaurants fail in their first year, and ~60% are gone within three. Parsa et al., Cornell Quarterly, 2005 The two ideas fail differently — so know which one you are building.

Archetype A

The Classic Restaurant

You sell an occasion. The food is the pretext; hospitality is the product.

revenue = seats × turns × average check
What the guest rents
Time — a two-hour slot of seat-hours that perish the moment they go unsold. Space — the room, the light, the right neighbours. A repertoire — a menu wide enough that the table of six all get what they want. Delivered by a crew whose entire job is the transfer of care.
The cost anatomy
Food 28–35% of sales. Labour 28–35% — the waiter layer is most of the difference from fast food. Occupancy 6–10%, rent alone ideally under 6%. What's left over: a median 2.8% pre-tax, against a ~5% historical norm. NRA 2025 Operations Abstract The whole business lives in a three-point sliver between four giant cost blocks.
What kills it
Rent ratchets against a fixed room — Union Square Cafe, thirty years one of New York's most loved rooms, was pushed out of its home in 2015 by a reported triple-rent lease ask. Empty seats — costs are fixed and seat-hours perish; a slow Tuesday is inventory thrown away. And the summit is no shelter: Noma, five times the world's best, announced in 2023 it would close as a regular restaurant — in Redzepi's words, financially and emotionally “it just doesn't work.” Pushed to its extreme, the archetype snaps.
What makes it great
The room as theatre, a crew that remembers your name, an evening that bends to the table. Hospitality is a moat no better recipe can cross — and it scales: Balthazar turns as many as ~1,500 covers on a big day, two hundred of them steak frites.

Archetype B

The Hole in the Wall

You sell one perfected thing, handed over fast. The queue is the dining room.

revenue = units × price × hours
What the guest buys
The thing itself. The room barely exists — El Califa de León in Mexico City is ~100 square feet, half of it grill, no chairs, four tacos on the menu; in 2024 it became the first taco stand ever to earn a Michelin star. Repetition is the technology; the open counter is the trust mechanism.
The cost anatomy
Everything A pays for is deleted. Front-of-house: 0% — the cook is the service. One product means near-zero waste and surgical purchasing, so food cost can run far above restaurant norms: Jay Fai packs 300–400 g of hand-picked crab into one Bangkok omelette, Nation Thailand because nothing else is being paid for. A Singapore hawker stall's median rent: about S$1,250 a month. Singapore MSE, parliamentary data
What kills it
Inflation against a price promise — the New York dollar slice was a price, not a dish; it broke to $1.50 in 2023. Crain's New York The owner's body — the product is a person, and mastery does not delegate. Footfall — with no check to build, location is oxygen. And fame cuts both ways: Hawker Chan scaled his S$2 chicken rice to 30+ outlets; the original stall lost its star. Michelin Guide, 2016–21
What makes it great
Narrowness is mastery, and capacity — not demand — is the ceiling. Franklin Barbecue has sold out of brisket every single day since 2009; selling out is the model, not the failure. Michelin keeps ratifying the archetype: a S$2 plate of chicken rice, a nine-seat ramen counter (Tsuta, 2015), a taco stand with no chairs.

The map

Everything else is a mutation.

Test any format against the five questions and it lands on the axis between A and B. Formats come and go; none has yet survived a third answer to what is being sold.

  • Food truckB minus the fixed rent — the hole moves to the demand. Franklin Barbecue began as a roadside trailer in 2009 and graduated to brick within a year: the truck is B's larval stage.
  • Fast casualB wearing A's ingredients. Counter order, no waiters, one product in permutations; the deleted service layer is spent on inputs instead. Chipotle ran a 26.2% restaurant-level margin in 2023 — that is B's cost anatomy with corporate purchasing power. Chipotle IR
  • Diner · brasserieA run at B's tempo — full room and crew, but high turns, tight repertoire, counter seating.
  • Cafeteria · buffetA minus the crew — the guest self-serves a wide repertoire; the service layer is deleted but the room, the breadth and its waste remain.
  • Ghost kitchenB minus even the wall — and the dining room's cost reappears as the platform's 15–30% commission, published platform tiers paid to someone else's landlord. With no room and no visible cook, neither archetype's moat survives: see the 2022–24 collapse (Kitchen United, Reef, MrBeast Burger).
  • Automat · vendingB minus the human. Horn & Hardart's automats were at their peak the largest restaurant chain in the world; the last one closed in 1991, beaten by fast food — B with a human beat B without one. The format survives only where nothing else can exist: stations, airports, 3 a.m.
  • Chef's counter · omakaseThe poles, fused. One repertoire and ten seats (B's body) — but the guest rents a master's time by the minute, prepaid (A's bundle, compressed). Push both archetypes to their extremes and they curl around and meet at a ten-seat counter: proof that A and B are the two poles of one axis, not separate boxes.

Building from the core

The concept ladder.

Understanding first — now the acting. Five rungs, in order, each one a gate.

  1. Choose your equation.

    seats × turns × check, or units × price × hours. Not a mood — an arithmetic. Which one will feed you, in your town, at your prices? Answer with numbers, not adjectives.

  2. Choose the one thing.

    For B: the dish you could repeat ten thousand times and still love — repetition is the product. For A: the occasion you can promise every single table, every night. Either way it must fit in one hand or one sentence.

  3. Strip.

    Whatever does not serve the one thing is quietly eating your margin. Every menu line, seat, gadget and printed card must fight for its life. The archetypes are pure because deletion is what makes them profitable.

  4. Close the numbers.

    Rule-of-thumb walls: prime cost (food + labour) under ~65% of sales, occupancy under ~10% — and whatever survives is your salary. If the equation only closes at full capacity, seven days a week, it does not close.

  5. Say it in one sentence.

    “X, done perfectly, for Y, at Z.” If the sentence needs a second sentence, it is not a concept yet — it is two concepts, and each is subsidising the other's confusion.

Études

Three concepts, developed from the core.

Sketched with the ladder to show the method at work — études, not business plans. Numbers are illustrative; a real concept closes them against a real street.

Archetype B

La Plancha

A quayside hatch. Sardines off the plank, on bread, in your hand.

units × price × hours · capacity-capped
The one thing
Grilled-to-order sardines on toasted pan de coca, salt, lemon, one green sauce. Nothing else. The plancha faces the queue — the cooking is the marketing.
Why it stands
One SKU, near-zero waste, no front-of-house, fish bought by eye each morning. Sell-out by 15:00 is the design, not the risk.
Where it dies
Seasonality — the equation must close in eight months, not twelve. And a price promise it cannot keep if the catch spikes: price the promise, not the fish.
Archetype A

Slack Tide

Eighteen seats. One menu. No choices. Two sittings a night.

18 seats × 2 turns × fixed check
The one thing
The occasion, kept; the option value, deleted. One table-d'hôte menu that changes weekly — A's room and care with B's waste profile inside it.
Why it stands
A fixed check makes revenue a known number by Tuesday. One menu collapses the brigade, the waste and the repertoire cost that grind classic rooms down to a 2.8% median.
Where it dies
The rent ratchet, like every A. And monotony: if the weekly menu stops turning, the neighbourhood stops returning. The room must earn regulars, not tourists.
The poles, fused

Eight

Eight stools around the stove. One sitting. The cook serves.

8 seats × 1 sitting × prepaid
The one thing
Access to the cooking itself — A's time-and-care bundle compressed onto B's counter. Prepaid, like the omakase economy, so a no-show is not a catastrophe.
Why it stands
Zero front-of-house, zero menu breadth, total intimacy: the two archetypes' strengths without either's overhead.
Where it dies
The owner's body — eight seats of mastery do not delegate and never take a night off. And at this scale two empty stools is a 25% hole in the night.
