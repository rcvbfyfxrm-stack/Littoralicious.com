Traditional — how it was always used

A canonical preparation always has a keeper. Name them:

Modern — the best current technique

What precision tools changed: sous-vide window, controlled Maillard, low-temperature infusion, the exact °C/time that beats the traditional method on consistency. State the number. Example: the contemporary preparation a working galley should default to now, and why it wins.

Frontier — the edge worth exploring

The reinvention: fermentation, spherification, dehydration-to-powder, cold-smoke, fat-wash/oil infusion, enzymatic ageing — whatever the modernist lineage unlocked for this specific ingredient. Name the technique and the compound it manipulates. Examples (no limit where each is genuinely interesting): list the frontier moves that actually improve it, each with the reason it works.

Poetic — the gesture that makes them pause

The evocative use: the aroma released at the table, the single-bite memory, the way it carries a place or a season onto the plate. Sensory and emotional, but still true to the ingredient. Example only if it earns it: the one gesture worth the theatre.

The sentence that links all four registers — what stays constant about this ingredient whether a grandmother or a frontier kitchen is cooking it.

Quality: How to Tell a Good One from a Bad One

Trust your eyes, nose and hands first:

A Good One

  • Look: the colour/sheen/structure a fresh, well-handled specimen shows.

A Bad One

  • Look: the dullness/spotting/wilt/crystallisation that says past-it.
