The Preference Sheet: Reading the Document That Runs the Job

A dislike is a preference; an intolerance is a safety brief. The sheet rarely tells you which — so you learn to read it like a chart, not a wish list.

The Problem: one document, three risk levels, no labels

The same sheet that lists a guest's dislike of coriander can also, three lines down, hold the one word that decides whether they leave the boat alive.

The preference sheet is the single document that runs a charter galley: often one or two pages, filled in by a broker or a stew, sometimes by the guest, sometimes copied from last year. It carries likes, dislikes, diets and allergies in one flat list, at one visual weight, with no grading of what happens if you get each line wrong.

That flatness is the whole problem. A restaurant sees a guest once and can ask at the table. You are cooking three services a day for one party for up to ten weeks off a form that treats doesn't care for blue cheese and anaphylactic to shellfish as the same kind of instruction. One is taste. The other is a chain of custody that runs from your provisioning list to the plate, and the law treats a break in that chain as your failure.

A preference sheet is three documents wearing one cover: a taste map you can bend, a diet brief you must plan around, and an allergen record you cannot break. Your first job is not to cook from it — it is to take it apart.

The System: read it as a chart, not a wish list

Before a single item goes on an order, every line on the sheet gets sorted, sourced and given a failure cost.

The protocol is one pass with a pen: read the whole sheet once for tone, then go line by line and assign each entry to one of three columns — Preference, Diet, or Safety — writing next to it what happens if that line fails. Nothing gets provisioned until every line has a column and a consequence.

The reason the sorting matters is dose and mechanism. A dislike has no threshold — a trace does nothing but disappoint. An intolerance (lactose, high-FODMAP onion and garlic, histamine) is dose-dependent: a little is uncomfortable, a lot is a ruined night, but it is not immune-mediated. An IgE allergy is neither — it is an immune reaction with no safe dose, where milligrams of hidden protein can trigger airway swelling within minutes. Same sheet, three different biologies. You provision for the biology, not for the tone of the sentence.

Column What the line means Cost if you get it wrong
Preference Taste and texture: dislikes, "not keen on", wants it well done Disappointment; a plate sent back. Recoverable in the next course.
Diet A system to plan around: vegan, kosher-style, low-FODMAP, GLP-1 small portions, religious fasting A menu that collapses mid-charter; a guest who can eat nothing you prepped.
Safety Allergy and medical: nut, shellfish, gluten as coeliac, sulphites, a named anaphylaxis A medical emergency at sea, hours from a hospital. Your legal liability.

Sort every line into three columns

The sort is where you catch the line that was written as a whim but is really a hazard.

Go through the sheet and force every entry into exactly one column. The act of sorting surfaces the ambiguities the form hides — the entries that could sit in two columns until you check.

  • "No gluten" — Preference (trend diet) or Safety (coeliac)? These are opposite provisioning jobs. Coeliac means separate boards, separate toaster, no shared fryer oil; a trend means you skip the bread. You must know which.
  • "Doesn't like shellfish" — a dislike, or an allergy softened into polite language on the form? Shellfish is one of the most common severe adult allergies; assume Safety until told otherwise.
  • "Dairy-free" — lactose intolerance (Diet, dose-dependent, butter often fine) or milk-protein allergy (Safety, no casein anywhere)? Butter in the mash decides nothing for one and everything for the other.

The failure mode is the softened allergy. Guests and brokers routinely write a life-threatening allergy as a preference — "prefers to avoid nuts" — because the form invites a casual tone and nobody wants to seem difficult. Under the UK's Natasha's Law (in force since October 2021) and the FSA's list of 14 named allergens, "prefers to avoid" is not a defence if you plate a hidden almond. When a line could be Safety, treat it as Safety until a human confirms otherwise in writing.

The sheet does not tell you which lines can kill. That is the one thing you have to work out before you buy a single ingredient.

Cross-check the sheet against the person

A sheet is a snapshot of an intention; the person on board is the live document, and the two disagree more often than anyone admits.

The second component is verification. A preference sheet is frequently second-hand, out of date, or contradicted by the guest's own behaviour once aboard. You reconcile the paper against reality before you trust it.

The three cross-checks

Each one closes a common gap between the form and the galley.

  • Contradiction check: read the whole sheet for lines that cannot both be true — a demand for daily seafood on a boat where another named guest is severely shellfish-allergic. Someone has to resolve that before departure; it is not yours to quietly cook around.
  • Provenance check: ask who filled it in and when. A sheet copied from a previous season carries a diet the guest has since dropped and misses one they have since developed.
  • Behaviour check: once aboard, watch what is actually eaten. The stated vegetarian who orders the carpaccio, the "no sugar" guest who wants dessert every night — the plate tells you more than the paper.

Before you provision, put every Safety-column line back to a named human — the broker, the chief stew, or the guest — and get the confirmation in writing. One message: "Sheet lists nut allergy for Guest 2 — is this a medical allergy requiring full separation, or a dislike? Please confirm." The written answer is both your protocol and your record.

The daily reconciliation

A charter is not a single event you prep for once; it is a document that drifts, and five minutes a day keeps it from drifting into a hazard.

The sheet you read at the dock is not the sheet you are cooking from in week six. Guests arrive that the form never mentioned, a child comes aboard mid-leg, a preference hardens into a demand. The discipline is a short daily reconciliation so the galley never runs on stale paper.

  1. Morning cross-reference: before prep, match the day's covers and any new guests against the current allergen list. Thirty seconds. Non-negotiable on a new leg.
  2. Label the Safety items: anything prepped for or around an allergic guest gets marked and physically separated in the fridge — not remembered, marked.
  3. Log the changes: when a preference shifts or a guest is added, write it on the working sheet the same hour. The version in your head is the one that fails at 11 PM.

Every morning, and every time the guest list changes: re-read the Safety column against who is actually on board. Total cost, under a minute. It is the cheapest insurance on the boat.

Proof it holds under a season

The system earns its place on the leg where the paper and the people came apart — and nobody got hurt.

The three-column read is not theory; it is what stands between a competent galley and the anecdotes every chef in the groups can recite. The preference sheet that demanded seafood daily while a guest was fatally allergic to it — sorted properly, that contradiction surfaces at the dock, not at the table. The "prefers to avoid nuts" that was an EpiPen-in-the-cabin allergy — verified in writing, it becomes a separation protocol instead of a near miss. The system does not make the sheet honest. It makes you the one who checked.

Over a season, the reconciliation habit compounds: the galley that never once ran on a stale sheet is the galley that never has the story. The chefs who get caught are not careless — they are the ones who trusted a document written by someone who was not going to cook from it.

Setup Checklist

Do this once at the dock and the whole charter runs off a brief you can trust — twenty minutes, before the first order goes in.

The once-per-charter setup that turns the raw sheet into a working document.

  1. Read once for tone, then sort. Assign every line to Preference, Diet, or Safety, and write the failure cost beside it.
  2. Flag the ambiguous lines. Any "no gluten / dairy-free / doesn't like X" that could be Safety gets a query, not an assumption.
  3. Confirm every Safety line in writing with a named human before provisioning.
  4. Screen for contradictions — one guest's daily-must against another's fatal allergy — and escalate, do not cook around.
  5. Build the working sheet you will actually reconcile against each morning, allergens at the top.
The One Thing

Sort every line into Preference, Diet, or Safety before you buy anything. The sheet will not grade the risk for you — and the one line that can send a guest to a hospital hours away is written in exactly the same ink as the one about coriander.

Cook from the person, not the paper — but read the paper as if a life depends on the lines you sort wrong, because on the worst charter, one does. Tested at sea.
