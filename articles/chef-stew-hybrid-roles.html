Two Jobs, One Salary: The Chef-Stew Hybrid, Decoded

'80 percent cook, 20 percent stew' is not a splash of extra duties. It is two positions paid as one, and the rest-hours ceiling is where it breaks.

What you'll get
  • How to read a "chef/stew" ad for what it actually is: two positions costed as one salary.
  • The rule that decides whether the split is workable — the MLC 2006 rest-hours ceiling, not the job title.
  • The one move before you sign: get the split in writing and price the second role, or walk.

Before the detail, the ground rules — because a role split you accept verbally becomes the contract you are held to.

The Situation

The friendliest words in a yacht-chef ad — "help with service" — are where a second unpaid job hides.

A run of ads now advertises a single hire to do two things: cook every meal and work the interior. "80% cook, 20% stew." "Chef/deckhand." "Solo chef, happy to help with service and turnarounds." On paper it reads as a cook's job with a splash of extra duties. In practice it is two positions — galley and interior — folded onto one person and paid at, or barely above, a single cook's rate.

It persists because it saves an owner a berth and a salary, and because the market is soft enough that someone will always take it. On a small crew the maths looks tidy from the bridge: one wage, two jobs covered. The cost lands entirely on the one body doing both — and, downstream, on the rest the convention says that body is owed.

"80% cook, 20% stew" is not 80% of a job. It is a full galley plus a fifth of an interior role — closer to 120% of one person, priced as less than one.

The Rules and Reality

Learn two things — the rest-hours ceiling and the role split — and every hybrid ad reads clearly.

Two structures govern whether a dual role is survivable or a trap. The first is a hard legal ceiling on how much you can be worked. The second is how honestly the split is written down. Start with the SEA — the Seafarer Employment Agreement, the contract every crew member signs, and the only document that binds what you are actually paid to do.

The rest-hours ceiling

Every seafarer, chef included, is covered by the Maritime Labour Convention 2006 — the international treaty setting minimum conditions at sea. Its rest-hours rule is not guidance; it is the wall a two-job workload runs into.

MLC 2006, Standard A2.3, sets a minimum of 10 hours of rest in any 24-hour period and 77 hours in any 7-day period. Rest may be split into no more than two blocks, one of them at least six hours, with no more than 14 hours between them. A full galley day already presses this ceiling; add breakfast service and evening turndowns and the honest hours stop fitting inside it.

What that means for the chef reading this is simple: if the two jobs cannot both be done inside the rest-hours wall, one of them is being done off the books — usually your rest.

If the workload only fits by under-recording your hours, the job is not "busy". It is non-compliant, and the record with your signature on it is the falsified one.

Capacity, not willingness

The second concept is capacity — not whether you are willing, but whether the hours physically exist. A sole chef on a 30–40 m yacht is already at the edge cooking three services plus crew food. Interior duties do not slot into gaps; there are no gaps. Watch the phrase "cross-over" — it means the two roles overlap in time, which is exactly when they cannot both be done.

Reading the Ad — The Verbs Decoded

  1. "Help with service" — you plate and you carry, clear and polish. A second role, named gently.
  2. "Chef/stew" or "cook/stew" — two titles, one wage. The slash is the whole story.
  3. "Flexible", "hands-on", "team player" — duties not yet defined, which means defined later by whoever asks.

Those are the rules on paper. Here is what the split means the day it bites.

"You cannot serve two masters" — the galley and the interior each think they have first call on you, and both are right until the contract says otherwise.

By the Split

The hybrid is not one deal — here is the version that matches the ad in front of you.

Case One — the "80/20" cook-stew

The most common hybrid, and the most defensible: mostly galley, interior help concentrated on turnaround and charter days. It can work — if the 20% is scheduled into days you are not also cooking three services, and if it is paid as a second responsibility, not assumed.

  • Qualifying test — is the interior share timetabled to specific days, or left as "when needed"? Only the first is real.
  • Document required — the split written into the SEA, with hours, not the ad's rounded percentage.
  • The threshold that voids it — the moment "20%" starts landing on cooking days, you are doing two full jobs and the rest-hours record cannot stay honest.

You take "80/20" as a cook's job, then find the interior 20% falls on the same days as full guest service — and your hours-of-rest log gets quietly rounded to stay legal.

The Ad Verbs, Side by Side

"Chef/stew"

Two jobs, one salary, stated plainly. Price it as one-and-a-fraction roles or pass.

"Chef/deckhand"

Cooking plus deck work — lines, tenders, wash-downs. Physical load on top of galley hours.

"Solo chef, help with service"

The soft version. Fine if the help is scheduled and paid; a trap if it is assumed and open-ended.

Which split you meet depends heavily on the size of boat, because that is what sets whether a second dedicated crew member exists at all.

Where the Hybrid Lives

Under 30 m

Where hybrids are the norm — small crew, no room for a dedicated stew, so the chef absorbs service.

30–40 m

The pressure zone: a sole chef already at capacity, now asked to "cross over" without a matching wage.

Day-boats / charter

Chalet-style, all-hands turnarounds; the split is real but the days are shorter and clearer.

The Practical Path

The rule is substance over slash marks — make the split real on paper before you sign.

You do not fix a hybrid by working harder inside it. You fix it before you sign, by turning the ad's vague split into defined hours and defined pay.

  1. Get the split in writing. The percentage and which days it falls on, written into the SEA — not the recruiter's message.
  2. Count it against the rest-hours ceiling. Add a realistic cooking day to the interior share; if it breaches 10 hours' rest in 24 or 77 in 7, the role does not fit the law.
  3. Price the second role. If it is two jobs, name a number for the second one; a hybrid at a sole-chef wage is a discount you are funding.
  4. Keep your own hours-of-rest log. A private record, daily, so the official one cannot be rounded without you knowing.
  5. Do not accept "flexible" as a job description. Undefined duties are defined later by whoever needs a hand — always upward, never in your favour.

Before you reply to a "chef/stew" ad, write the two job descriptions out in full and add the hours. If they do not fit inside 14 waking hours with rest to spare, the ad is under-priced — say so, or walk.

The Cost of Getting It Wrong

The bill is not money first — it is a season, and a falsified record with your name on it.

Take the hybrid unpriced and the cost compounds. You carry two jobs' fatigue at the sharpest tools on the boat. When the honest hours will not fit, the hours-of-rest record gets adjusted — and a falsified rest-hours log is a detainable deficiency under Port State Control, signed by you. Many hybrids end the same way: the chef leaves mid-season, and the boat re-advertises the same impossible split.

Worst case: you burn out by mid-charter, sign off owed leave you never took, and leave behind a rest-hours record that would not survive an inspection — all to save the boat one salary.

That figure is never really about the wage. It is about who pays it.

The hybrid is the chef plating a guest dinner on four hours' sleep, then still making the crew a proper hot meal because they, too, worked the same long day. The job costs a person; the people fed never see the bill.

The One Thing

A slash in a job title is a second salary someone hopes you will not charge for. Get the split and the rest hours into the SEA before you sign — or price both jobs and let them say no.
