Eggs: What Every Dish Asks of Them

When to stop whisking, when to salt, what heat does to the white before the yolk — the egg, decoded dish by dish.

History

Origin: a bird kept for its eggs before its meat

Why this matters: the egg was a daily, renewable food long before chicken was a cheap meat — which is why almost every coastal cuisine built a binder, a custard and a sauce around it.

The domestic hen descends from the red junglefowl (Gallus gallus) of Southeast Asia. Archaeological and genetic work places the bird's association with people across a long window — recent finds at Ban Non Wat in Thailand push reliable evidence of egg-laying domestic fowl back several millennia — but the constant across every account is that the bird earned its keep through the egg, the one protein a household could harvest without killing the animal.

By the Roman table the egg was structural to how a meal was even described: ab ovo usque ad mala, "from the egg to the apples," meant from the first course to the last. Romans already exploited what we still exploit — the white as a clarifier and binder, the yolk as an enricher — and kept incubation as a minor industry.

One hen converts feed into roughly 300 eggs a year. No livestock comes close to that yield-without-slaughter — the reason the egg sits at the centre of cooking on every coast, in every class.

The Journey: from binder to the sauce that took a port's name

The arc in one line: the egg's history is the history of discovering that one ingredient could thicken, set, lift and bind — each function found and named separately, over centuries.

The white's gift for trapping air made it the pastry kitchen's leavening before chemical raisers existed: beaten to a foam, it lifts a soufflé or a sponge on protein and steam alone. The yolk's gift is different — it is a natural emulsifier, able to hold fat and water in a single stable suspension.

That second gift carries a littoral origin story worth telling straight. Mayonnaise is most credibly named for Maó (Port Mahon), the harbour capital of Menorca: the French account has the Duc de Richelieu's cook adapting a local oil-and-egg sauce, salsa mahonesa, after the 1756 siege. Whatever the exact truth, the sauce is a Mediterranean port's: olive oil, egg yolk, a squeeze of acid, beaten until it turns.

The modern arc is precision. Where the old kitchen judged a custard by the spoon, the contemporary galley judges it by a thermometer — because every egg preparation is, underneath, a single controlled act of protein coagulation, and we can now name the exact temperature it happens at.

Cooking an egg is denaturation: heat unfolds the coiled proteins, and the exposed strands bond into a mesh that traps water. Too little and it runs; too much and the mesh tightens, squeezes the water out, and the egg turns rubbery and weeping. Every technique below is the management of that one window.

The Science

The Science: two foods, three set points

The payoff: the white and the yolk are different foods that coagulate at different temperatures — master the three numbers and every egg dish becomes one dial.

Key Compounds

  • Ovotransferrin (conalbumin) — ~12% of the white's protein and the first to set, denaturing from about 61–65°C. It is why the white begins to turn opaque before the yolk has moved. Evidence: strong (Belitz et al., Food Chemistry).
  • Ovalbumin — the dominant white protein (~54%), and the last to set, not fully coagulating until ~80°C. The gap between it and ovotransferrin is the entire textural range of a cooked white. Strong.
  • Ovomucin — the structural glycoprotein that makes the thick white thick. It holds a poached egg's shape and stabilises a foam; it degrades as an egg ages, which is why old whites go runny. Strong.
  • Yolk lipoproteins (LDL, livetins) — begin thickening ~65°C and set to a paste by ~70°C. This is the lower, narrower window that makes the yolk the harder of the two to hold at "just set." Strong.
  • Lecithin (phosphatidylcholine) — the yolk's emulsifier. One molecule has a water-loving head and a fat-loving tail, so it coats oil droplets and suspends them in water: the whole basis of mayonnaise, hollandaise and a silky scramble. Strong.
  • Lysozyme — an antimicrobial enzyme in the white that digests bacterial cell walls; part of why a fresh, intact egg keeps for weeks. Strong.

The single number to carry: the yolk sets (~70°C) before the white fully does (~80°C). Hold an egg at 63–65°C and you exploit the gap — the yolk thickens to custard while the white stays barely set. That is the entire trick of the onsen and sous-vide egg.

Salt and acid both lower the temperature at which egg proteins coagulate. Their ions shield the proteins' electrical charges, letting the strands bond sooner and — counter-intuitively — into a looser, more tender mesh that holds its water. Salting beaten eggs ~15 minutes before cooking gives a measurably more tender, less weepy scramble or custard (López-Alt, The Food Lab). Salt added to whipping whites does the opposite favour: it slows the foam and can weaken it, which is why meringues take acid, not salt.

Every Dish, Decoded

Every Dish, Decoded: target, salt, stop

The reference you actually cook from: the three decisions that change an egg dish — the temperature you are aiming for, when to add salt, and the exact moment to stop — for the eight preparations a galley runs most.

Preparation Target / doneness Salt timing Stop when…
Soft scramble Low heat, ~63–65°C curds, constant stir 15 min before — for tenderness still glossy and loose; carry-over finishes them off heat
French omelette Medium, fast; baveuse (just-runny centre) Into the beaten egg, just before surface barely wet; roll while the centre still shines
Poached Water ~82°C (bare shiver), splash of vinegar After, on the plate — salt in water toughens white set, yolk soft to the touch: ~3 min
Sous-vide / onsen 63–65°C, 45–60 min After, at service timer — yolk custardy, white a just-set veil
Hard-boiled Simmer ~9–11 min, then ice immediately After, peeled 11 min max — past it gives the grey-green ring
Crème anglaise Stir to 82°C, never past 85°C Pinch with the sugar it coats the spoon (nappe); pull and cool at once
Meringue Whites to stiff, glossy peaks No salt — use acid (cream of tartar) peaks hold and shine; stop before dull/grainy
Mayonnaise Cold yolk, oil added in a thin stream Into the yolk base, at the start thick and holding; stop adding oil if it thins/splits

Three of these dishes live or die in a 10°C window (63–72°C). Buy a probe thermometer before you buy anything else — the eye lies about egg, the number does not.

Four Ways to Use It

Four Ways to Use It: Traditional, Modern, Frontier, Poetic

One ingredient, four registers: the egg has been cooked the same way for two thousand years and reinvented in the last forty — the through-line is always the same coagulation window, only the precision changes.

Traditional — how it was always used

The boiled and the folded. Every coast has its version — the Provençal omelette, the Spanish tortilla de patatas set slow and turned, the soft-boiled egg with its soldier of bread. The one rule the old cooks never broke: gentle heat, and off before you think it's done, trusting carry-over to finish it.

Modern — the best current technique

The water bath. Holding eggs at a fixed 63°C for an hour gives a result the pan cannot: the yolk thickened to a warm custard, the white a barely-set veil, identical egg after egg. For a galley feeding a table on swell, it removes the one variable you can't watch — the pan. Cook a dozen, hold them, crack to order.

Frontier — the edge worth exploring

The cured and the spherified. A yolk buried in salt and sugar for a week firms to a translucent, umami-dense disc you grate like bottarga — controlled dehydration concentrating the lipoproteins. The elBulli lineage went further, using the yolk's own lecithin to build airs and the white's albumin as a clarifying raft for crystal-clear consommé. Each move manipulates a protein you already met above — no gimmick, just the same chemistry pushed.

Poetic — the gesture that makes them pause

The truffled egg. Sit whole eggs in a sealed jar with a fresh truffle overnight: the porous shell breathes the aroma straight into the white and yolk, so the egg tastes of truffle before a shaving ever touches it. A €0.30 egg carrying a €3,000/kg perfume — the cheapest luxury in the galley, and the one a guest remembers.

Grandmother's soft-boiled egg and a water-bath 63°C egg are the same dish — both chase the moment the yolk thickens but the white hasn't toughened. The thermometer just made the target visible.

Pairing

Traditional Pairing: what the coast already knew

What the old kitchens already knew: the egg is rich, fatty and faintly sulphurous — it asks for fat to carry it, acid to cut it, and an allium or a cured fish to give it edge.

Egg + brown butter (France)
Maillard pyrazines in the browned butter meet the egg's sulphur notes — the oeuf en cocotte logic.
Egg + smoked fish (North Atlantic)
Kedgeree, omelette Arnold Bennett: smoke and fat ride the yolk's richness without fighting it.
Egg + chive / spring onion
Allium sulphur compounds rhyme with the egg's own — same family of aroma, different intensity.
Egg + olive oil + lemon (Mediterranean)
The mayonnaise triangle: fat to emulsify, acid to brighten and to firm the proteins.
Egg + cured pork (Italy)
Carbonara: rendered fat and glutamate-rich guanciale, the yolk as the only sauce.
Egg + black pepper
Piperine lifts the egg's flatness; the one seasoning that belongs on nearly every egg.

The egg is the only ingredient that is both the sauce and the thing the sauce is for.

Poetic Pairing: the unexpected, still obeying the chemistry

The bold move: the yolk is fat-soluble flavour's perfect vehicle — anything aromatic and fatty bonds to it, which is why these surprising matches still hold.

  • Yolk + sea urchin — two rich, faintly briny, lipid-heavy foods; the urchin's marine sweetness reads as an extension of the yolk, not a contrast.
  • Egg + brown butter + capers — nutty Maillard plus a sharp pickled hit; the egg holds both and the caper stops it cloying.
  • Yolk + miso — cure a yolk in miso for two days; the koji enzymes break protein into glutamate and the yolk turns to amber, savoury jam.
  • Meringue + olive oil — a few drops folded through Italian meringue; the oil's fruitiness against the sugar-set white, a Mediterranean dessert in one bite.
  • Egg + coffee — Vietnamese cà phê trúng beats yolk and condensed milk to a warm foam; lecithin emulsifies the fat, the bitterness cuts the richness.

Fat carries aroma and off-notes. A yolk left open in the fridge will drink the smell of last night's fish within hours — cover it, or use that porosity on purpose with truffle, never by accident.

Flavour-Compounding: the shared-molecule bridges

The chemistry under it all: cooked egg flavour is built from sulphur compounds and Maillard products, while the yolk's fat dissolves and carries everything else — so eggs bridge to foods that share either trait.

Compound Shared with The pairing it explains
Hydrogen sulphide / thiols Alliums, brassicas, cooked seafood Why chives, leeks and crab sit so naturally with egg
Maillard pyrazines Brown butter, toast, roast coffee, seared meat The browned-butter fried egg; egg on toast; egg-coffee foam
Lecithin (carrier, not a flavour) Any aromatic fat — truffle, olive oil, cured fish Why the yolk absorbs and broadcasts truffle and smoke
Free glutamate (when cured/aged) Miso, parmesan, cured pork, tomato Why a miso-cured yolk or a carbonara tastes so deep

The yolk is not just food — it is a delivery system. Its lecithin and fat dissolve aromatic molecules and release them slowly on the palate, which is why one porous egg can carry the most expensive flavour in your fridge.

The Galley

Quality: How to Tell a Good One from a Bad One

Trust your eyes first: crack one onto a flat plate — freshness is written in how the egg stands up.

A Good One

  • Look: the yolk stands tall and domed; a tight inner white sits high, with a looser outer ring.
  • Float test: sinks and lies flat in water — the air cell is still small.
  • Smell: clean, almost nothing. Any sulphur off the shell is a warning.

A Bad One

  • Look: a flat yolk that breaks at a touch; a thin white that spreads to the plate edge — ovomucin has degraded.
  • Float test: stands on end or floats — the air cell has grown; floaters go in the bin.
  • Smell: any hydrogen-sulphide whiff = spoiled. Trust it absolutely.

Counter-intuitive but useful at sea: fresh eggs poach best (tight white, holds shape) but old eggs peel best when hard-boiled (the white's rising pH loosens it from the membrane). Date your trays and assign them — newest to the poaching pan, oldest to the boil.

Yacht Galley Intel

At sea, this is what counts: eggs are cheap, dense protein with a long fridge life — but they are also the dish most likely to fail on swell, because the pan is the one variable you can't hold steady.

  • Storage: 4°C, point-down, in the carton — the shell is porous, and the carton blocks both odour-uptake and moisture loss. Unwashed (cuticle-intact) eggs keep weeks at cool room temperature, which matters on a long passage with fridge pressure.
  • Provisioning maths: a 50 g egg is ~6 g protein for a few cents — the cheapest galley protein that also binds, sets and emulsifies. Carry more than you think; they cross every service.
  • The swell fix: on a moving boat, move egg cookery off the open pan. Water-bath the breakfast eggs at 63°C and hold — no flame to chase, no curds to over-set, crack to order. It is the single biggest reliability gain in a charter-morning galley.
  • Carry-over is real: in a warm galley an egg keeps cooking off the heat. Pull scrambles and omelettes while visibly underdone — by the plate they are right.

Cure yolks for service: bury 6 yolks in a bed of 500 g salt + 250 g sugar, cover with more of the mix, 5–7 days at 4°C, then rinse and dry. Grate over pasta, risotto or crew eggs like bottarga. Keeps 3–4 weeks wrapped — turns the cheapest part of the egg into a finishing luxury.

The Stories

The warmth: the egg is the ingredient cooks measure themselves against — which is why it carries so much lore.

The hundred folds

The pleats in a chef's toque are said to once have numbered a hundred — one for each way the wearer could cook an egg. Apocryphal, but the point holds: the omelette was the test, because it hides nothing.

A sauce named for a siege

When the French took Maó in 1756, the story goes, the Duc de Richelieu's cook found no cream and improvised with the island's oil and egg. Mayonnaise — mahonesa — carried a Menorcan harbour's name back to Paris.

The egg that proved the science

Hervé This used the egg to launch molecular gastronomy: holding one at a fixed low temperature, he showed coagulation was a precise, repeatable physics — not a cook's intuition. The 63°C egg on tasting menus worldwide is his footnote.

Storage

Chef's note: cook every egg to a temperature, not a time — the time changes with the pan, the swell and the heat, but the protein always sets at the same degree.

Stop early. Every egg failure in a galley is overcooking — the rubber scramble, the green-ringed yolk, the curdled custard are all the same mistake. Pull it while it looks underdone and let carry-over land it. Tested at sea.
