Miso From Scratch: Koji, Salt, and Time

Buy a paste and you buy someone else's decisions. Build it from koji and you control the salt, the sweetness, and how much glutamate the mould sets free.

What you'll get
  • Build your own miso and you set the salt, the sweetness and how much umami the mould sets free — one paste, tuned to your galley.
  • The control point is the koji: grow the mould near 30 °C, then leave it to work at cellar temperature for weeks or years.
  • The finished paste keeps for a season without a working fridge — the most reliable umami you can carry.

The Problem

A bought tub is someone else's recipe — one salt, one age, one note, in a container you cannot adjust.

The usual move is to buy a tub of miso and treat it as fixed: reach for it, spoon it into soup, put it back. You inherit whoever made it — their salt, their grain, their ripening time — and every dish that leans on it carries the same single note. On a boat that has stocked one red miso for the season, that note tires fast, and there is no dial to turn.

Boil that bought paste — stir it into a stock and let it come up to a rolling heat — and you denature the enzymes and drive off the aromatics you paid for. What is left is salt and a flat, meaty background. The umami you wanted has cooked itself away.

The Technique

The Move

Grow the mould, mix three things, wait. The koji does the work you were paying a factory to do.

Miso is not cooked into being; it is fermented into being. You inoculate a steamed grain with Aspergillus oryzae — the mould called koji — and let it flood the grain with enzymes. Mash that koji with cooked soybeans and salt, pack it down, and walk away. Over weeks to years the koji proteases cleave soy protein into free amino acids, and one of them, glutamate, is umami itself. You are not adding flavour; you are unlocking it from the bean.

The whole craft reduces to one lever: koji is an enzyme delivery system. More koji, warmer and younger, gives a sweet pale miso; less koji, cooler and older, gives a dark savoury one. Everything between is salt and patience.

None of this is new — it is one of the oldest kitchen technologies on earth, and it travelled a long way before it reached your bench.

This is the Japanese craft of koji — Aspergillus oryzae, named the national fungus of Japan — carried from Chinese fermented pastes over a thousand years ago and made into miso in temple and farmhouse kitchens. The long-aged dark paste of Aichi (Hatchō) and the sweet white miso of Kyoto (Saikyō) are the two poles every cook still works between. Lineage, not invented here.

The Steps

Four moves. Get the salt and the koji right and time does the rest.

  1. Grow or buy the koji. Steam short-grain rice, cool to blood heat, dust with A. oryzae spores, and hold at 30 °C (86 °F) for around 45 hours until the grains fur white and smell of chestnut. Or buy dried koji and skip this — no shame in it at sea.
  2. Cook and mash the soybeans. Simmer soaked soybeans until one crushes between finger and thumb (around 4 hours, or 45 minutes in a pressure cooker), then mash while warm. Keep a ladle of the cooking water to loosen the mix.
  3. Weigh the salt; do not guess it. Combine beans, koji and salt by weight. For a sweet, fast white miso use koji equal to the beans and salt at 5–6% of the total; for a dark, keeping red miso use less koji and salt at 11–13%. Salt is the whole safety margin — where the proteolysis is kept honest and spoilage kept out.
  4. Pack, weight, and wait. Press the mix into a crock with no air pockets, lay a sheet against the surface, weight it, and salt-cap the rim. Keep it cool and dark: a few weeks for white, six months to two years for red. Skim any surface bloom; the paste beneath is sound.

Write the make-date and the salt percentage on the crock in marker. Miso gives you no clock and no timer — that label is the only record of what you built and when it is due.

Critical Factors

Salt, koji ratio, temperature, air. Four dials; the rest is noise.

Factor What Happens Adjust By
Salt ratio Under ~5% and spoilage microbes move in before the koji finishes; over ~13% and the enzymes stall and the paste never ripens 5–6% for a sweet young white; 11–13% for a long-aged red
Koji ratio (kōji-buai) Less koji means slower ripening and a sharper, saltier paste; more koji means faster, sweeter, more glutamate Koji equal to the beans for sweet and fast; roughly half for dark and slow
Temperature Warm speeds ripening but flattens the aroma and can push it sour; cold slows it but builds depth Ripen at cellar temperature, roughly 20–25 °C (68–77 °F)
Surface air Exposed paste oxidises and grows surface mould Press a sheet to the surface, weight it, cap the rim with salt

A moving galley adds a fifth variable the recipe books never mention: temperature that will not sit still.

Engine-room heat and a summer crossing can hold a locker at 35 °C for days, and warm miso ripens fast and turns sour and boozy. Stow the crock low and forward against the hull, where it stays coolest, and for a summer make lift the salt to 12–13% to slow the enzymes and buy months of margin.

Once the paste is built, it feeds both ends of the boat off one jar.

Same jar, two tiers off one pass: for guests, a sweet white miso whisked into butter and brushed over grilled fish — the Saikyō glaze; for crew, a spoon of the dark red stirred through the braise or the noodle broth. One ferment, the plated version and the family version.

Why It Works

The koji mould floods the grain with proteases — endopeptidases and aminopeptidases such as leucine aminopeptidase — that snip soy protein down to free amino acids, and the one that matters is glutamic acid, the molecule behind umami. Its amylases meanwhile turn grain starch into the sugars that make a young white miso taste sweet. Matured miso is measurably rich in free glutamate, which is why a spoonful deepens a stock the way a Parmesan rind does — the same free-glutamate mechanism. The numbers the paste turns on:

Compound / Reaction Threshold °C What It Does
A. oryzae koji growth 30 Mould secretes the proteases and amylases into the grain — the enzyme dose for everything after
Proteolysis (ripening) 20–25 Peptide bonds cleaved slowly; free glutamate accumulates over months
Enzyme & aroma ceiling 70 Above this the enzymes denature and the volatiles boil off — whisk miso in off the heat
The Proof

You know it worked when: the paste has gone from pale and bean-smelling to dark, glossy and savoury, and a fingertip taste reads salty-sweet-meaty with no raw-bean edge and no boozy sourness. A young white is ready in weeks; a red is ready when it stops changing.

What that jar really buys you is not a condiment but a kind of insurance.

A jar of miso is the one umami aboard that does not depend on a working fridge, a good port, or a delivery that arrived. On the third grey day of a crossing, when the fresh is gone, it is what turns a thin crew soup into something that tastes like someone cared.

The one thing: build the paste around the koji and the salt — koji for the umami and the sweetness, salt for the safety and the pace — and you own a stable, tunable umami that keeps for a season without power.
