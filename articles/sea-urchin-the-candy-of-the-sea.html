Sea Urchin: The Candy of the Sea

Uni is a reproductive organ that tastes of sugar — and the reason why is two amino acids, glycine and alanine. The full profile: the candy chemistry, the alum trick that hides age, the four ways to use it, and how to keep something this perishable alive on charter.

History

Origin: Five Tongues Inside a Spine-Ball

Why this matters: once you know what you are eating, every quality rule and every season makes sense.

What we call uni is not a fish, not an egg, and not really one thing. Prise open the spiny test — the shell — and inside, arranged in the urchin's five-fold symmetry, hang five gonads: the reproductive organs, usually called "tongues" or "lobes." Both males and females are eaten and they taste near-identical, so the trade does not sort them by sex. That single anatomical fact governs everything downstream: a gonad swells and sweetens to feed the spawn, then empties and turns bitter once it does. You are eating the animal at the peak of its richest moment.

Humans worked this out a very long time ago. The Greeks and Romans prized the urchin — Aristotle dissected it, and the five-toothed chewing apparatus on its underside still carries the name Aristotle's lantern to this day. A surviving Roman recipe dressed the raw roe with vinegar, honeyed wine, parsley and mint. The Mediterranean has scooped it straight from the shell with bread, on the rocks, for two thousand years.

The grand delicacy of sushi counters and three-star tasting menus is, biologically, sea-urchin gonad eaten raw — and the people who named it after a lantern were doing so before the birth of Christ.

The Journey: From Rock-Snack to the World's Costliest Roe

The arc in one line: a fisherman's free lunch became a luxury Japan now buys by the planeload — while the same animal quietly eats the seafloor bare.

For most of its history uni was coastal-poverty food: cracked open at the waterline, eaten with bread, never shipped. Japan changed that. Today Japan consumes roughly 80–90% of the world's uni, far beyond what its own waters land, so it pulls in supply from Chile (the largest single producer), California, Maine and Russia. A morsel once eaten for free is now graded, packed and air-freighted like a jewel.

There is a darker, stranger chapter. Unchecked purple-urchin populations have stripped kelp forests into bare "urchin barrens" — Northern California lost over 95% of its kelp in about seven years. The cruel twist: barren urchins are starving, so their gonads are shrivelled and worthless. You cannot simply harvest the problem away.

Which is where the hopeful turn comes in. Ventures such as Urchinomics gather these worthless barren urchins and ranch them — fattening them in land tanks on controlled feed until the gonads fill to market grade, restoring kelp and diver livelihoods at once. Everyone honest about it admits it is, so far, a small dent. But it is a rare story where eating the luxury and healing the reef point the same way.

Colour comes entirely from diet. Urchins cannot build carotenoids themselves, so the gold-to-orange of the roe is dietary pigment — chiefly 9′-cis-echinenone, converted from the β-carotene in kelp. Bright colour signals a kelp-rich diet; it does not guarantee flavour, because tank-fed urchins can colour well yet taste flat.

The Science

The Science: Why It Tastes of Candy

The payoff: the sweetness is real chemistry — free amino acids on the tongue, not sugar.

Uni is unusually loaded with free amino acids — single amino acids floating loose in the tissue, landing directly on your taste receptors. Their balance is the whole flavour, and it is the balance that the season, the diet and the handling all push around.

The Taste Chemistry

  • Glycine & alanine — the sweetness. Glycine is the single most abundant free amino acid in the roe (30–60% of the free-amino-acid pool in green urchin), and both taste frankly sweet. This is the "candy." Evidence: strong, repeatedly quantified.
  • Glutamate (and aspartate) — the umami, the savoury depth under the sweetness. Reinforced by trace 5′-nucleotides.
  • Methionine & valine — the bitter "uni funk." In a good specimen they are a faint backnote; mishandled or post-spawn, they take over. (Note: valine is a bitter amino acid, not a sweet one — a common mix-up.)
  • Arginine — the bitterness of decline. As the gonad spawns or degrades, glutamate collapses and arginine surges — in one study from ~205 to over 700 mg/100 g. The sweet-savoury balance simply inverts.
  • 9′-cis-echinenone — the orange carotenoid; colour, not flavour.

The classic "omission test" (Komata, 1964) pinned it down: take the roe's amino acids out one by one and taste what's missing. Glutamate carries the umami; glycine and alanine carry the sweetness; methionine carries the unmistakable "uni" character. Remove the sweet pair and the candy is gone.

The Aroma — and a Myth to Drop

  • The "sea" smell: often blamed on bromophenols (the briny-iodine compounds of seafood generally). Honest caveat — the one direct aroma study of uni gonad found no bromophenols; its marine character came from a mix of alcohols, benzaldehyde and brominated methanes. So "bromophenols give uni its sea aroma" is a plausible analogy, not a proven fact for uni. Evidence: unconfirmed for uni specifically.
  • The spoilage smell: sulphur volatiles, chiefly dimethyl sulphide, plus rising ammonia. These, not the natural roe, are what "off" uni smells of. Evidence: strong.
Four Ways to Use It

Four Ways to Use It: Traditional, Modern, Frontier, Poetic

One ingredient, four registers: the through-line is restraint — heat and clutter are the enemies in every one of them.

Traditional — how it was always used

Raw, from the shell, with bread and a squeeze of lemon — the Mediterranean oursinade. The one cooked classic that earns its place is spaghetti ai ricci di mare (Sicily, Puglia, Sardinia), where the roe is stirred through hot pasta off the heat so it warms but never cooks. In Japan, the answer to a fragile, loose roe was the gunkan-maki "battleship" — a rice oval belted with nori to hold the tongues. Three cultures, one rule: do almost nothing to it.

Modern — the best current technique

The contemporary default is uni butter (beurre d'oursin): raw roe blitzed into room-temperature butter, a stable, freezable building block that melts into a sauce or onto toast. From it come the modern plates — uni butter folded off-heat through pasta or risotto, a uni hollandaise, or uni chawanmushi, a savoury egg custard baked in a water bath at ~95 °C until barely set. The egg and dairy here are not decoration: their fat and lecithin carry uni's volatile marine notes and round its edges.

Frontier — the edge worth exploring

Sea urchin is not at the edge of modernist cooking — it is at its origin. elBulli's first culinary foam, in 1994, was espuma de judías blancas con erizos: white-bean foam with sea urchin. The technique that launched a thousand foams was built on uni. From there: spherified uni "yolks," and the centuries-old Japanese preserved forms that are their own frontier — shio uni (salted), neri uni (a kneaded paste), and alcohol-cured tsubu-uni (gonads salted, then aged in ~20% alcohol; a Yamaguchi specialty from around 1887). Each concentrates the glutamate into a seasoning you can keep.

Poetic — the gesture that makes them pause

Serve it in its own opened shell over crushed ice, spines still moving, and the table goes quiet. Or the purest version: one warm tongue laid on hot buttered toast, eaten in a single bite while the sea-smell is still rising off it. The gesture is the point — uni carries a whole coastline onto the plate, and the best thing you can do is get out of its way.

Whether it is a Roman with vinegar, a nonna with spaghetti, or Adrià with a siphon, the discipline never changes: protect the raw sweetness, and never let heat turn it to rubber.

Pairing

Traditional Pairing: What Every Coast Already Knew

What the old kitchens already knew: uni is rich, sweet and saline, so it wants acid, fat, starch and a little salt to frame it — nothing that fights.

Uni + lemon (Mediterranean)
Acid lifts the sweetness and cuts the richness; the oldest pairing there is.
Uni + butter & bread (France)
Fat carries the volatile marine aromas; starch is a neutral stage. The oursinade in two ingredients.
Uni + egg yolk (Japan/France)
Two rich, sulphur-touched custards reinforcing each other — the logic behind uni carbonara and chawanmushi.
Uni + rice (Japan)
Vinegared rice adds gentle acid and a base for a roe too soft to stand alone — the gunkan.
Uni + Champagne or sake
Glutamate meets glutamate; the wine's own savoury depth and acid scrub the palate between bites.
Uni + pasta, off the heat (Italy)
Residual warmth only — enough to loosen the roe into a sauce, never enough to set it.

Everything uni pairs with is doing one of two jobs: carrying its aroma, or cleaning up after it.

Poetic Pairing: The Unexpected

The bold move: the surprises still obey the chemistry — they either share a savoury compound or supply a contrast uni is missing.

  • Uni + caviar — two glutamate-rich marine luxuries; the salt and pop of the roe punctuate uni's soft sweetness. A study of the oyster–Champagne match notes caviar is high in glutamate but low in nucleotides, so it leans on its partner for synergy — uni obliges.
  • Uni + pork fat — lardo or crisp pancetta. Animal fat carries the marine aroma even better than butter, and the umami layers; a recurring move in modern Japanese-Italian cooking.
  • Uni + brown butter — the nutty Maillard notes of beurre noisette echo uni's own faint hazelnut-benzaldehyde aromatic. Warm the butter, cool it to barely-warm, then add the roe.
  • Uni + dashi — the roe's glutamate plus katsuobushi's inosinate is textbook umami synergy: two savoury compounds that multiply rather than add. A few drops of dashi makes a little uni taste of much more.
  • Uni + sweetcorn — both run on glycine-led sweetness; a chilled corn velouté under cold uni is sweet-on-sweet that somehow reads as savoury.

Every one of these fails the instant you apply real heat. Bring the fat or stock to barely-warm, pull it off the flame, then introduce the roe. Above roughly 60 °C the proteins set and the candy turns to grain.

Flavour-Compounding: The Shared-Molecule Bridges

The chemistry under it all: uni's currency is free glutamate. Bridge it to a partner carrying a 5′-nucleotide and the savouriness multiplies — the same synergy that powers dashi and the miso trick in our umami piece.

Compound in uni Bridges to Why it works
Glutamate Katsuobushi, anchovy, cured ham (inosinate) Glutamate × inosinate = multiplicative umami, not additive. The single most powerful lever.
Glutamate Dried shiitake, tomato (guanylate) Same synergy via a different nucleotide; a sun-dried-tomato or shiitake note deepens uni without seafood-on-seafood.
Glycine / alanine Scallop, sweet prawn, sweetcorn Shared sweet-amino-acid backbone — these all taste sweet for the same reason, so they layer seamlessly.
Marine volatiles Oyster, caviar, nori A common briny-mineral register; stack them for an intensely "of-the-sea" plate.
Lipids / lecithin affinity Egg yolk, butter, pork fat Fat dissolves and carries uni's aroma compounds, spreading the flavour and softening the funk.

The reason a thimble of dashi or a sliver of cured ham makes uni taste richer is not seasoning — it is nucleotide synergy. Uni brings the glutamate; the partner brings the inosinate or guanylate; perceived umami jumps several-fold.

The Galley

Quality: How to Tell a Good One from a Bad One

Trust your eyes, nose and hands first: the single most reliable tell is whether the tongues hold their shape — and whether the smell is tide or ammonia.

A Good One

  • Look: firm, plump tongues with a defined granular surface, holding their shape; bright, uniform gold to orange.
  • Smell: clean, sweet, like a tide pool — barely "fishy" at all.
  • Feel: slightly bouncy and dry-surfaced, not weeping liquid.

A Bad One

  • Look: slumping, melting or collapsing into liquid; dull, grey, milky or browning.
  • Smell: sharp ammonia, or a strong sulphurous fishiness — the decisive sign. Walk away.
  • Feel: mushy, sticky, watery — structure already gone.

The "melt" is real biochemistry: uni has no structural skeleton holding the lobe, so once the animal dies its own enzymes (autolysis) digest the proteins that give the tongue its firmness, while cold-tolerant marine bacteria break amino acids down into ammonia and amines. That is why a tired tongue both slumps and smells of ammonia — same process, two senses.

Firmness can be faked. Trade uni is often dipped in alum (potassium aluminium sulphate) to stiffen tongues that would otherwise sag — so a suspiciously rigid, slightly bitter, chemically-clean specimen may be old roe propped up by aluminium. The smell test still wins: alum cannot hide ammonia.

Conservation: The Alum Trick and the Seawater Answer

Why ordinary storage fails it: uni is among the most perishable things in any galley — it begins digesting itself the moment it dies, and it cannot be frozen without turning to mush.

The trade's old answer is alum (myoban, 明礬 — potassium aluminium sulphate). Aluminium ions cross-link and denature the gonad's surface proteins, firming the tongues so they survive transport laid dry on wooden trays ("ita uni"). It works — but overdosed, it leaves a bitter, astringent, faintly metallic edge, and the finest roe is ruined by it. That is why the premium product is additive-free (mutenka), packed instead in ensui — seawater matched to the reef's own salinity, which cushions the tongues in transit without touching the flavour.

Buy ensui (seawater-packed), alum-free uni whenever you can. Hold it at 0–2 °C — the coldest part of the fridge, over (not in) ice — and never let fresh water touch it: uni is a marine osmoconformer, so fresh water floods and bursts the cells, turning tongues to slush. Eat within 24–48 hours for peak, three days absolute outside. Do not freeze. The one preservation that genuinely works is turning it into uni butter and freezing that.

Yacht Galley Intel

At sea, this is what counts: uni's storage window is measured in hours, so provisioning and timing are the entire game.

  • Source it last, serve it first. Buy ensui trays at the final provisioning stop before the guests' first dinner; it is a day-one ingredient, never a hold-it item.
  • Cold chain, no fresh water. 0–2 °C, over ice in a sealed tray so meltwater never reaches the roe. A breach of either ruins it silently.
  • Make uni butter on arrival. Blitz any roe you won't plate raw within a day into soft butter (roughly 1 part roe to 2 parts butter), roll, chill or freeze. It becomes your sauce-finisher for the whole charter.
  • Plate cold or barely-warm, always off the heat. Loosen uni butter into a finished risotto or pasta away from the flame; the residual heat is the maximum it should ever see.
  • Lead with the story. Tongues in the half-shell over ice, or one bite on warm toast, sells itself — let the guests watch you open it.

Uni butter: 50 g fresh roe + 100 g soft unsalted butter + a pinch of salt and a few drops of lemon, blitzed smooth. Roll in parchment, chill 1 hour or freeze up to 1 month. A 1 cm coin melted into pasta, scrambled eggs or a pan sauce off-heat carries one portion.

The Stories

The warmth: uni is worth caring about because every culture that found it built a small ritual around it.

Aristotle's Lantern

The urchin's five-toothed jaw still carries Aristotle's name, from a line in his Historia Animalium likening it to a horn lantern. Scholars now argue about what he actually meant — jaw or whole shell — but 2,400 years on, we still open uni through the structure he described.

The Foam That Started Everything

The first dish of the modernist era — elBulli's 1994 culinary foam, the technique that reshaped fine dining for two decades — was a white-bean espuma with sea urchin. Of every ingredient on earth, the one chosen to ride the very first foam was uni.

The Oursinades of the Côte Bleue

Every February since 1952, the village of Carry-le-Rouet near Marseille throws oursinades — open-air feasts where you sit at the quay, crack urchins, and scoop them with bread and white wine. No kitchen, no sauce. The luxury and the rock-snack are the same act.

Eating the Problem

When purple urchins razed California's kelp, the starving survivors were too empty to sell. Ranchers now collect these worthless barren urchins and fatten them in tanks — turning an ecological wrecking-ball back into dinner, and giving the kelp a chance. A small dent, honestly told, but the right direction.
