History

Origin: Five Tongues Inside a Spine-Ball

Why this matters: once you know what you are eating, every quality rule and every season makes sense.

What we call uni is not a fish, not an egg, and not really one thing. Prise open the spiny test — the shell — and inside, arranged in the urchin's five-fold symmetry, hang five gonads: the reproductive organs, usually called "tongues" or "lobes." Both males and females are eaten and they taste near-identical, so the trade does not sort them by sex. That single anatomical fact governs everything downstream: a gonad swells and sweetens to feed the spawn, then empties and turns bitter once it does. You are eating the animal at the peak of its richest moment.

It was first eaten for the simplest reason: it was free, and it was there. The urchin sits in the shallows within arm's reach of anyone wading a rocky shore, needs no boat, no net and no cooking, and it is at its fattest in the cold months when little else is going. So it entered the human diet the way limpets and mussels did — as the forager's reward, cracked on the rocks and scooped with bread. Humans worked it out a very long time ago: the Greeks and Romans prized it, and a surviving Roman recipe dressed the raw roe with vinegar, honeyed wine, parsley and mint. Aristotle dissected it, and the five-toothed chewing apparatus on its underside still carries the name Aristotle's lantern — a line from his Historia Animalium likening it to a horn lantern. Scholars still argue whether he meant the jaw or the whole shell; 2,400 years on, we open uni through the structure he described, and the delicacy of three-star tasting menus is, biologically, the same rock-snack a Greek ate before the birth of Christ.

The urchin is one of the rare foods whose luxury and poverty are the same act: crack the shell, scoop the roe, eat it where it was pulled. Every refinement that follows is only about protecting that first, free mouthful.

The Journey: From Rock-Snack to the World's Costliest Roe

The arc in one line: a fisherman's free lunch became a luxury Japan now buys by the planeload — while the same animal quietly eats the seafloor bare.

For most of its history uni stayed exactly what it began as: coastal-poverty food, cracked open at the waterline, never shipped. Japan changed that. As Japanese demand outgrew home waters, uni turned from a shoreline scrap into a graded, packed, air-freighted jewel — Japan now consumes roughly 80–90% of the world's uni, pulling supply from Chile (the largest single producer), California, Maine and Russia. The cultural rise was not the food changing; it was a whole country deciding this particular organ, at this particular moment of its life, was worth more than almost anything else in the sea.

The Mediterranean kept its own version of the ritual the whole time. Every February since 1952 the village of Carry-le-Rouet near Marseille throws its oursinades — open-air feasts where you sit on the quay, crack urchins and scoop them with bread and cold white wine. No kitchen, no sauce: the rock-snack and the luxury sitting at the same trestle table.

And on this coast, it became art. Salvador Dalí, who lived and painted at Portlligat on Cap de Creus, was fixated on the sea urchin's geometry — one sits in the foreground of his Madonna of Port Lligat (1949). As the guides at his house tell it, that fixation ran into the architecture: the domed Oval Room he built for Gala was shaped to the urchin's radial form so that its acoustics gather a whisper and send it circling the room. Whether the shell truly drew the plan or the story grew around it, the room is real and a murmur really does reverberate in it — proof that this animal has fed the coast's imagination as much as its tables.

There is a darker chapter, and it is worth telling straight. Unchecked purple-urchin populations have stripped kelp forests into bare "urchin barrens" — Northern California lost over 95% of its kelp in about seven years. The cruel twist: barren urchins are starving, so their gonads are shrivelled and worthless, and you cannot simply harvest the problem away. Ventures such as Urchinomics gather these worthless barren urchins and ranch them — fattening them in land tanks on controlled feed until the gonads fill to grade, restoring kelp and diver livelihoods at once. Everyone honest about it calls it a small dent so far. But it is a rare case where eating the luxury and healing the reef point the same way.

Colour comes entirely from diet. Urchins cannot build carotenoids themselves, so the gold-to-orange of the roe is dietary pigment — chiefly 9′-cis-echinenone, converted from the β-carotene in kelp. Bright colour signals a kelp-rich diet; it does not guarantee flavour, because tank-fed urchins can colour well yet taste flat.

The Science

The Science: Why It Tastes of Candy

The payoff: the sweetness is real chemistry — free amino acids on the tongue, not sugar.

Uni is unusually loaded with free amino acids — single amino acids floating loose in the tissue, landing directly on your taste receptors. Their balance is the whole flavour, and it is the balance that the season, the diet and the handling all push around.

The Taste Chemistry

  • Glycine & alanine — the sweetness. Glycine is the single most abundant free amino acid in the roe (30–60% of the free-amino-acid pool in green urchin), and both taste frankly sweet. This is the "candy." Evidence: strong, repeatedly quantified.
  • Glutamate (and aspartate) — the umami, the savoury depth under the sweetness. Reinforced by trace 5′-nucleotides.
  • Methionine & valine — the bitter "uni funk." In a good specimen they are a faint backnote; mishandled or post-spawn, they take over. (Note: valine is a bitter amino acid, not a sweet one — a common mix-up.)
  • Arginine — the bitterness of decline. As the gonad spawns or degrades, glutamate collapses and arginine surges — in one study from ~205 to over 700 mg/100 g. The sweet-savoury balance simply inverts.
  • 9′-cis-echinenone — the orange carotenoid; colour, not flavour.

The classic "omission test" (Komata, 1964) pinned it down: take the roe's amino acids out one by one and taste what's missing. Glutamate carries the umami; glycine and alanine carry the sweetness; methionine carries the unmistakable "uni" character. Remove the sweet pair and the candy is gone.

The Aroma — and a Myth to Drop

  • The "sea" smell: often blamed on bromophenols (the briny-iodine compounds of seafood generally). Honest caveat — the one direct aroma study of uni gonad found no bromophenols; its marine character came from a mix of alcohols, benzaldehyde and brominated methanes. So "bromophenols give uni its sea aroma" is a plausible analogy, not a proven fact for uni. Evidence: unconfirmed for uni specifically.
  • The spoilage smell: sulphur volatiles, chiefly dimethyl sulphide, plus rising ammonia. These, not the natural roe, are what "off" uni smells of. Evidence: strong.
Four Ways to Use It

Four Ways to Use It: Traditional, Modern, Frontier, Poetic

One ingredient, four registers: the through-line is restraint — heat and clutter are the enemies in every one of them.

Traditional — how it was always used

Raw, from the shell, with bread and a squeeze of lemon — the Mediterranean oursinade. The one cooked classic that earns its place is spaghetti ai ricci di mare (Sicily, Puglia, Sardinia), where the roe is stirred through hot pasta off the heat so it warms but never cooks. In Japan, the answer to a fragile, loose roe was the gunkan-maki "battleship" — a rice oval belted with nori to hold the tongues. Three cultures, one rule: do almost nothing to it.

Modern — the best current technique

The contemporary default is uni butter (beurre d'oursin): raw roe blitzed into room-temperature butter, a stable, freezable building block that melts into a sauce or onto toast. From it come the modern plates — uni butter folded off-heat through pasta or risotto, a uni hollandaise, or uni chawanmushi, a savoury egg custard baked in a water bath at ~95 °C until barely set. The egg and dairy here are not decoration: their fat and lecithin carry uni's volatile marine notes and round its edges.

Frontier — the edge worth exploring

Sea urchin is not at the edge of modernist cooking — it is at its origin. The first dish of the modernist era, elBulli's 1994 culinary foam — the technique that reshaped fine dining for two decades — was espuma de judías blancas con erizos, white-bean foam with sea urchin. Of every ingredient on earth, the one chosen to ride the very first foam was uni. From there: spherified uni "yolks," and the centuries-old Japanese preserved forms that are their own frontier — shio uni (salted), neri uni (a kneaded paste), and alcohol-cured tsubu-uni (gonads salted, then aged in ~20% alcohol; a Yamaguchi specialty from around 1887). Each concentrates the glutamate into a seasoning you can keep.

Poetic — the gesture that makes them pause

Serve it in its own opened shell over crushed ice, spines still moving, and the table goes quiet. Or the purest version: one warm tongue laid on hot buttered toast, eaten in a single bite while the sea-smell is still rising off it. The gesture is the point — uni carries a whole coastline onto the plate, and the best thing you can do is get out of its way.

Whether it is a Roman with vinegar, a nonna with spaghetti, or Adrià with a siphon, the discipline never changes: protect the raw sweetness, and never let heat turn it to rubber.

Pairing

Pairing: Three Ways to Frame the Sweetness

The one rule under all of it: uni is rich, sweet and saline, so a partner is only ever doing one of two jobs — carrying its aroma (fat, starch) or cleaning up after it (acid, a savoury foil). Read the three columns as a ladder: what every coast already knew, why the chemistry says it works, and where you take it next.

Traditional — iconic

  • + lemon — acid lifts sweetness, cuts richness; the oldest pairing there is.
  • + butter & bread — fat carries the marine aroma, starch is a neutral stage: the oursinade in two ingredients.
  • + vinegared rice — gentle acid and a base for a roe too soft to stand alone (the gunkan).
  • + egg yolk — two rich, sulphur-touched custards reinforcing each other (uni carbonara, chawanmushi).
  • + Champagne or sake — the wine's savour and acid scrub the palate between bites.

Flavour-compound — why it works

  • Glutamate → inosinate (katsuobushi, anchovy, cured ham) — multiplicative umami, not additive: the single most powerful lever.
  • Glutamate → guanylate (dried shiitake, tomato) — same synergy, a deeper note without seafood-on-seafood.
  • Glycine/alanine bridge (scallop, sweet prawn, sweetcorn) — all sweet for the same reason, so they layer seamlessly.
  • Lipid affinity (egg yolk, butter, pork fat) — fat dissolves and spreads uni's aroma, softening the funk.

Poetic — littoralicious

  • + brown butter — beurre noisette's nutty Maillard echoes uni's own faint hazelnut note; warm, then cool to barely-warm before the roe.
  • + a few drops of dashi — glutamate meets inosinate; a thimbleful makes a little uni taste of much more.
  • + chilled sweetcorn velouté — sweet-on-sweet that somehow reads savoury; cold under cold.
  • + lardo or crisp pancetta — animal fat carries the marine aroma even better than butter; the umami layers.
  • + caviar — two glutamate-rich luxuries; the roe's salt-pop punctuates uni's soft sweetness.

Every pairing here fails the instant you apply real heat. Bring the fat or stock to barely-warm, pull it off the flame, then introduce the roe. Above roughly 60 °C the proteins set and the candy turns to grain.

The reason a thimble of dashi or a sliver of cured ham makes uni taste richer is not seasoning — it is nucleotide synergy. Uni brings the glutamate; the partner brings the inosinate or guanylate; perceived umami jumps several-fold.

The Galley

Quality & Keeping: The Whole Test in One Look

The single most reliable tell is whether the tongues hold their shape — and whether the smell is tide or ammonia. Everything else follows from one fact: uni has no skeleton holding the lobe, so the moment it dies its own enzymes (autolysis) digest the proteins that give it firmness, while cold-tolerant bacteria turn amino acids to ammonia. A tired tongue slumps and smells of ammonia at once — same process, two senses.

Buy it — a good one

  • Look: firm, plump, granular tongues holding their shape; bright, even gold-to-orange.
  • Smell: clean and sweet, like a tide pool — barely fishy.
  • Feel: slightly bouncy, dry-surfaced, not weeping.

Refuse it — a bad one

  • Look: slumping, melting, collapsing to liquid; dull, grey, milky or browning.
  • Smell: sharp ammonia or heavy sulphur — the decisive sign. Walk away.
  • Feel: mushy, sticky, watery — structure already gone.

Keep it — the method

  • Buy ensui (seawater-packed), alum-free (mutenka) whenever you can.
  • Hold at 0–2 °C, over (never in) ice; no fresh water — it bursts the cells to slush.
  • Eat within 24–48h. Don't freeze the roe — freeze uni butter instead.

Firmness can be faked. Trade uni is often dipped in alum (potassium aluminium sulphate) to stiffen tongues that would otherwise sag: aluminium ions cross-link the surface proteins so old roe travels dry on wooden trays ("ita uni"). Overdosed, it leaves a bitter, astringent, metallic edge — and the finest roe is ruined by it. A suspiciously rigid, chemically-clean, faintly bitter specimen may be old roe propped up by aluminium. The smell test still wins: alum cannot hide ammonia.

Yacht Galley Intel

At sea, this is the whole game: uni's storage window is measured in hours, so provisioning and timing are everything.

  • Source it last, serve it first. Buy ensui trays at the final provisioning stop before the guests' first dinner; it is a day-one ingredient, never a hold-it item.
  • Cold chain, no fresh water. 0–2 °C, over ice in a sealed tray so meltwater never reaches the roe. A breach of either ruins it silently.
  • Make uni butter on arrival. Blitz any roe you won't plate raw within a day into soft butter (roughly 1 part roe to 2 parts butter), roll, chill or freeze. It becomes your sauce-finisher for the whole charter.
  • Plate cold or barely-warm, always off the heat. Loosen uni butter into a finished risotto or pasta away from the flame; residual heat is the maximum it should ever see.
  • Lead with the story. Tongues in the half-shell over ice, or one bite on warm toast — and tell them the arc: Aristotle's lantern, Dalí's Oval Room, the first elBulli foam. Uni sells itself twice, once on the plate and once in the telling.

Uni butter: 50 g fresh roe + 100 g soft unsalted butter + a pinch of salt and a few drops of lemon, blitzed smooth. Roll in parchment, chill 1 hour or freeze up to 1 month. A 1 cm coin melted into pasta, scrambled eggs or a pan sauce off-heat carries one portion.

Quick Reference

Best Season
Before spawning — Med autumn–early spring; Hokkaido bafun summer.
Best With
Lemon, butter, bread, egg yolk, rice, dashi, Champagne.
Avoid
Heat above ~60 °C, fresh water, freezing, anything that competes.
Storage
Ensui, 0–2 °C over ice, eat within 24–48h.

Chef's note: uni rewards the cook who does almost nothing — your only real jobs are to buy it fresh, keep it cold and dry, and serve it before the candy turns.

The sweetness is a window, not a fixture — it is highest just before the animal spawns and gone the moment heat or time takes hold. Buy it at its peak, keep it near freezing and out of fresh water, and serve it raw or barely warmed. Everything else is interference. Tested at sea.
