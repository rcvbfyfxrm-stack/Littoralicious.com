Prawn Shells, Fermented Into Umami

A 2026 Singapore study fed prawn by-products to six microbes. One mould turned shell-and-head waste into a glutamate load worth six grams of MSG per hundred.

What you'll get
  • One peer-reviewed finding, stated plainly — no hype.
  • The mechanism that makes it true, and the one galley move it earns you.
  • The honest edge of the claim — where it stops working.

The Claim

The finding, in one line you can act on tonight.

Prawn heads and shells are not waste. Fed to the right mould for three days, they turn into a seasoning as glutamate-rich as a spoon of monosodium glutamate — the umami you were throwing over the side.

The Paper

One named study — graded, so you know how much weight it holds.

The study

Lim, Yeo, Chin & Chen (2026). Microbial-dependent variations in umami compounds during fermentation of prawn by-products. Sustainable Food Technology (RSC). DOI: 10.1039/D5FB00939A

6 microbial strains · bench fermentation · in-vitro umami indices · Strength: preliminary

The Nanyang Technological University group ground prawn by-product and fermented it separately with six food microbes — among them koji mould, the tempeh mould, brewer's yeast and two lactobacilli — then measured how much umami each one built. It is a university-lab result, not a galley one.

The Mechanism

The molecule, the °C, the reaction — why the result holds.

The winner was Rhizopus oligosporus, the mould behind tempeh. In three days it lifted the ferment's umami from an EUC — equivalent umami concentration, umami expressed as its equal in grams of MSG — of 0.23 to 6.36 g MSG per 100 g, close to a thirtyfold rise.

The mould's enzymes do the work. Proteases cut prawn protein into free amino acids, freeing glutamic acid — the dominant umami compound here. Its taste activity value climbed past 1, the point at which the tongue actually registers it, while nucleotides from the same breakdown multiply the effect through umami synergy.

None of this is a new idea — only a new measurement of an old one.

Every coast that ever landed shrimp learned to ferment the trimmings into seasoning: Southeast Asia's belacan, kapi and terasi shrimp pastes, the fish sauces of Vietnam and Thailand, and Rome's own garum, pressed from the parts no one would plate. The grandmother salted the by-catch and waited; the paper says why the waiting works.

For a chef standing over a bin of prawn heads, that history is a standing instruction.

The umami was never in the meat you served. It was in the heads you binned.

Galley Implications

The science, turned into one move you make in a 4 m² galley.

The call it settles is a simple one: prawn by-product is a seasoning, not rubbish. What you do with it depends on your time.

Tonight, the fast route: roast the shells and heads hard, then simmer for a shellfish stock, bisque or an XO-style paste — heat and time free the same glutamate the mould does, just faster and safer. The slow route, if you run a fermentation programme aboard, is a salted shell garum or a koji cure — days of work for a seasoning you dose by the drop.

The economics only sharpen on a long charter.

A single prawn service already leaves the by-product aboard, weightless in your cost sheet. One bag of heads turned to bisque base seasons a week of crew and guest plates from an ingredient you paid for once.

What This Does NOT Mean

The overreach to refuse before it costs you a service.

It does not mean you can leave shellfish scraps to sit in a warm galley and call it fermentation.

This was a controlled ferment with chosen cultures, not spoilage. Prawn by-product left uninoculated in the heat grows histamine and pathogens fast, and scombroid-style poisoning is the real risk. A true ferment needs a known culture, salt and a controlled temperature — and the 6.36 figure is the umami of the ferment mass, not a licence to over-season the plate.

Limitations

Exactly how far the evidence reaches — and no further.

One study, at bench scale, behind a journal paywall. The umami was scored by EUC and TAV — calculated indices from the chemistry, not a trained panel eating the result — on one prawn by-product over a three-day window. Rhizopus beat five rivals; that is not the same as beating every method a kitchen already owns.

The one thing

Say it to the crew: the deepest umami on the boat is in the part we throw away — the prawn heads, not the tails. One Singapore lab just put a number on it: close to thirtyfold, in three days.
