The Brief: Five Forces Rewriting the Galley

Five shifts are changing the yacht-chef job this season — and not one of them started in the galley. Pay finally going transparent, a drug at the table, a border counting days, a culture you can't buy, and a machine learning to cook. The thread that ties them, in one read.

The thread
  • The job is being rewritten from the outside in — by money, medicine, law, culture and machines.
  • Not one of these five forces is something a chef controls — they arrive whether you read them or not.
  • The edge is no longer just the cooking. It is reading the change early and adapting first.

The recipes haven't changed much this season. The job around them has. Every force pulling on the galley right now started somewhere else — a salary survey, a pharmacy, a border control desk, a crew chat group, a server farm — and none of them is waiting for the chef's permission.

One thread runs through all five: the yacht-chef job is being rewritten from the outside in. Money, medicine, law, culture and technology are each reshaping the work — and none of them is in your control. What is in your control is how fast you read it.

The Five

1 · Pay goes transparent

The yacht-chef salary was a number the boat kept and the chef guessed at. That is ending: the band is now being mapped and tooled, so you can walk into a negotiation with data instead of a hunch. The number stops being a secret the boat keeps — see the 2026 pay map for the ranges and the Pay Check tool to find your own band.

Do this: before your next contract talk, pull your band from the Pay Check tool and name it first. The party with the figure sets the floor — make sure it's you.

2 · The Ozempic table

More guests are on GLP-1 drugs — Ozempic, Mounjaro — and they eat far less than the brief assumes. The old charter reflex of abundance now lands as waste and discomfort; medicine is quietly rewriting the menu. The flex this season is precision and restraint, not volume — the playbook is in cooking for Ozempic guests.

GLP-1 agonists slow gastric emptying and blunt appetite, so portions that read as generous can read as overwhelming. Smaller, more frequent, protein-forward plates are the adaptation — the science, not a fashion.

3 · The 90-day border

Europe's new EES biometric system turns the Schengen 90/180 rule from a thing you fudged into a hard clock the machine counts for you. For non-EU crew, those days now decide whether you can cook a full Med season at all. The law decides who's in the galley — the full count is in the 90-day clock.

The trap: treating the 90 days as a guideline. EES logs every entry and exit automatically — overstay and the next season's visa, not just this one's, is the price.

4 · The culture you can't buy

One thing no provisioning budget can order is a living sourdough starter with years in it. So crews trade them — dock to dock, jar to jar, no invoice — alongside the leads and the warnings that keep each other afloat. Some value is shared, not bought: under an industry that can run cut-throat, this mutual aid quietly persists. (Anonymised, from the crew groups.)

"Lost my starter on a yard period. Three messages later a stew two boats down had split hers and left it in our cockpit. You can't buy that — and you don't forget it."

5 · The machine in the galley

AI has arrived as a galley tool: provisioning apps that build the order, contract-readers that flag the clauses, menu help, photo cleanup — and job ads now written by it, with the tells if you know them. The upside is real and so is the slop; the trade is now arguing over what "authentic" even means. Technology is a galley tool now — for better and worse.

Do this: use AI for the grind — the provisioning maths, the first menu draft, the contract scan — and keep the cooking and the judgement human. Learn the tells in an AI-written job ad before you reply to one.

The Thread, Again

The thread

Five forces, one direction: the job is being rewritten from the outside in. Money, medicine, law, culture and machines are each reshaping the galley, and the chef commands none of them. The edge that's left isn't the knife work alone — it's reading the change early and adapting before it's forced on you. The pay number, the smaller plate, the day count, the shared jar, the new tool: each one is already on its way. The chefs who win this season are the ones already moving. From the dock.
