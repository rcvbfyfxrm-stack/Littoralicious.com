Focaccia: The Science-First Version

The recipe you actually want to read

Eighty-percent hydration for an open, custardy crumb. A 24-to-72-hour cold ferment for flavour you cannot fake with more yeast. A salamoia brine poured into the dimples for the crisp-tender crust of a Ligurian bakery. Three modifications. Five sources. One sheet pan.

Most focaccia fails in one of two directions. Either it is a thick, bready slab — under-hydrated, under-fermented, dense as a hotel roll — or it is a flat, greasy disc with no internal structure, oil pooling on a pale crust. The first is mixed too dry and baked the same day. The second is dimpled with plain oil and rushed through a single warm proof. The bread between those two failures is not a matter of a better brand of flour. It is a matter of water, time, and what you pour into the dimples. This version fixes all three structurally, not by adding more oil to cover the flaws.

Three modifications separate this from every other recipe: 80% hydration worked by coil folds rather than kneading (open, custardy crumb), a 24–72 hour cold ferment (flavour and extensibility no same-day dough can match), and a salamoia brine — water, oil, salt emulsified — spooned into the dimples (the crisp-tender Ligurian crust). All three are grounded in food science with named sources.

Method

Phase 1: Mix & Hydrate — 10 min, then 20 min rest

  1. Bloom nothing — just combine. In a large bowl, whisk 3g instant yeast into 500g flour. Add 400g cool water and the 25g dough oil. Mix with a wet hand until no dry flour remains and you have a shaggy, sticky mass. Hold the 11g salt back.
  2. Autolyse 20 minutes. Cover and walk away. The flour drinks the water and gluten begins to organise on its own — no work required.
Why hold the salt, and why autolyse?
Flour proteins glutenin and gliadin need water to link into the gluten network — and they hydrate faster without salt competing for that water. The 20-minute rest lets hydration and limited enzymatic activity begin to align the protein strands before you ever apply force, so the same dough strength costs far less mixing. Salt added afterward then tightens the developed network and brings fermentation under control. At 80% hydration this passive head start is the difference between a slack puddle and a workable, extensible dough.
— Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004 (gluten hydration); J. Kenji López-Alt, Serious Eats, no-knead mechanics (2020)
  1. Add the salt. Sprinkle 11g fine salt over the dough, pinch and fold it through for 30 seconds until fully worked in. The dough will feel slightly firmer immediately.

Phase 2: Coil Folds — 4 folds over 2 hours, ~1 min each

  1. First fold (t = 0). Wet both hands. Slide them under the centre of the dough, lift until the mass stretches and the bottom releases, then let it tuck under itself as you lower it. Rotate the bowl 90° and repeat until the dough resists — usually four lifts. Cover.
  2. Repeat at 30, 60, and 90 minutes. Four folds total. With each round the dough goes from soupy to smooth, domed, and noticeably stronger. After the final fold it should hold a soft mound that slowly relaxes.
Why fold instead of knead?
Kneading an 80%-hydration dough is futile — it is too wet to hold a ball, and machine kneading drives in heat that accelerates fermentation before flavour develops. Coil folds build the same aligned gluten network through repeated gentle stretching, while the rest between folds lets the strands relax and the dough hydrate further. Each fold also redistributes the gas the yeast is producing and pulls in fresh oxygen for the surface yeast. The result is a strong, extensible dough that traps large, irregular bubbles — the open, custardy crumb dense same-day focaccia never achieves.
— Ken Forkish, Flour Water Salt Yeast, 2012 (folds and dough strength); Modernist Bread vol. 3, high-hydration crumb structure

Phase 3: Cold Ferment — the modification that matters most, 24–72 hr, hands-off

  1. Refrigerate, covered, for 24 to 72 hours. Straight after the last fold. The dough will rise slowly and develop a webbed, bubbled surface. Any time inside this window works — 48 hours is the sweet spot.
What 48 hours in the fridge actually buys you
At 4°C yeast activity slows to a crawl but does not stop, while bacterial and enzymatic processes keep working. Amylase enzymes break starch into sugars that later caramelise into a deeper crust; protease enzymes nibble the gluten just enough to improve extensibility; and slow fermentation accumulates organic acids and esters — lactic, acetic, and aromatic byproducts that read on the palate as nutty, faintly tangy, complex. None of this can be rushed with more yeast at room temperature, which only produces gas and a yeasty, one-note flavour. Time, not quantity, is the active ingredient.
— Nathan Myhrvold, Modernist Bread, 2017 (cold retardation and flavour development); Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004 (fermentation acids)

This is the charter-prep lever. Mix on a provisioning day, fold through the afternoon, and the dough sits in the fridge doing nothing but improving for up to three days. You bake on the morning you need it. The longest part of the recipe demands none of your attention.

Phase 4: Pan & Room-Temperature Proof — 2–4 hr

  1. Oil the pan heavily. Pour 30g olive oil into the 23×33 cm pan and spread it up the sides with your hand.
  2. Turn the dough out. Scrape the cold dough into the pan in one piece. Drizzle a little oil on top, then gently stretch it toward the corners with flat hands. It will resist and spring back — that is the cold gluten. Do not fight it.
  3. Rest 15 minutes, then stretch again. The gluten relaxes as the dough warms; the second stretch reaches the corners easily.
  4. Proof at room temperature until pillowy. Cover loosely. Leave 2–4 hours at 20–24°C, until the dough has risen to roughly double, fills the pan, and wobbles like a set custard when you shake it. Cold dough straight from the fridge will take the full time.

Do not skip the warm-up proof. Baking cold dough straight from the fridge gives you a dense, gummy slab — the yeast needs to wake and inflate the crumb at room temperature first. The dough is ready when a fingertip pressed into it leaves a dent that springs back slowly and only partway.

Phase 5: Salamoia, Dimple, Top — 5 min

  1. Make the salamoia. Dissolve 4g salt in 30g warm water, then whisk in 40g olive oil hard for 15 seconds until it turns cloudy and emulsified. It separates fast — whisk again right before pouring.
  2. Pour it over. Spoon the salamoia evenly across the proofed dough. It will look like too much liquid. It is not.
  3. Dimple. Oil your fingertips. Press all ten fingers straight down to the pan bottom, all over the surface, spaced about 3 cm apart. The brine pools into the wells you create.
  4. Finish. Scatter oil-soaked rosemary leaves and 3g flaky salt across the top. Preheat the oven now to 230°C (450°F).
Why a brine instead of just oil?
Plain oil dimpled into focaccia fries the surface and leaves the seasoning sitting on top, uneven and easily knocked off. A salamoia — oil and salt-water whisked into a temporary emulsion — does three things at once. The water carries dissolved salt into the dough for even seasoning all the way down; it flashes to steam in the oven, lifting the dimpled craters and keeping the interior moist; and the oil left behind crisps and browns the crust. The emulsion is the whole trick of the Ligurian original: crisp where it should be crisp, tender everywhere else.
— Samin Nosrat, Salt Fat Acid Heat, 2017 (Ligurian focaccia and salamoia); Modernist Bread vol. 4, steam and crust formation

Phase 6: Bake — 20–25 min

  1. Bake at 230°C (450°F) on a middle-lower rack. Rotate the pan at the 15-minute mark for even colour.

Done when:

  • Top is deep golden-brown, not pale — pale means under-baked and gummy
  • Internal temperature reaches 96°C (205°F) at the centre
  • The base, lifted with a spatula, is crisp and browned from the pan oil
  • The whole sheet slides freely in the oiled pan when shaken
  1. Lift out immediately. Slide the focaccia onto a wire rack within a minute of leaving the oven. Left in the hot pan it steams against the base and the crust you built goes soft.
  2. Brush with a little raw oil while hot if you want extra gloss, then cool at least 15 minutes before cutting. Serve warm, never fridge-cold.

Colour is the doneness cue here, backed by temperature. A focaccia pulled at the first hint of gold is raw in the middle no matter how good it smells. Bake to deep brown and 96°C (205°F) internal — the crust is supposed to be assertive.

Charter Prep & Storage

The cold ferment is the make-ahead. Do not try to freeze the proofed dough — the ice crystals shred the gluten you built and the crumb collapses. Freeze the baked focaccia instead, and revive it with heat, not the microwave.

ComponentHow Far AheadMethod
Mixed, folded dough3 daysCold ferment in the fridge, covered. This is the recipe, not a shortcut.
Salamoia2 daysWhisk fresh is best; if made ahead, re-emulsify hard before pouring.
Baked focaccia (whole)1 dayRoom temp, loosely wrapped. Refresh 4 min at 180°C to re-crisp.
Baked focaccia (frozen)1 monthCool fully, wrap in cling + foil. Thaw, then 6–8 min at 200°C from cold.

Shelf Life

Best within 6 hr of baking · 1 day room temp (re-crisp to serve) · 1 month freezer — never refrigerate baked focaccia: the crumb stales fast at fridge temperature
