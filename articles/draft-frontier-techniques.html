Field Report — Ten Techniques From the Frontier Labs

Where cooking is going next, and what each lab is teaching the working kitchen

The modernist era — El Bulli 2003 to Modernist Cuisine v3 in 2011 — gave the kitchen new molecules: alginate, calcium chloride, methylcellulose, transglutaminase. What followed has been quieter and more consequential. The frontier of 2020 to 2026 is not about new molecules. It is about new relationships: microbe to grain, fire to dairy, plankton to plate, sound to perception, ecosystem to menu. Ten labs and restaurants are doing the work the next decade of cooking will inherit. None of them are doing it alone. All of them are publishing. What follows is a working chef’s field report — the lab, the move, the science, and the one thing each is teaching that a yacht galley can use this season.

What “Frontier” Means In 2026

The El Bulli era of spherification, foams, and methylcellulose textures is now twenty years old. The original liquid-olive sphere was first served in 2003. What was avant-garde then is house-standard now — available in every two-star kitchen, taught in every culinary school, and largely exhausted as a creative frontier. The chefs who studied at elBulli — Disfrutar’s Mateu Casañas, Aponiente’s Ángel León, Mugaritz’s Andoni Aduriz — have spent the last decade asking a different question. Not “what new molecules can we play with?” but “what relationships in cooking have we been treating as fixed that turn out to be open?”

The ten techniques below are the most consequential answers any working kitchen has shipped since 2018. None of them is a gimmick. All of them are documented — in books, in peer-reviewed journals, in the records of three-Michelin restaurants that have rebuilt their menus around them. Each one teaches a principle that scales down to a galley with no laboratory budget and a chef with no R&D team.

The modernist era gave the kitchen molecules. The 2020s frontier is giving the kitchen relationships: microbe + medium, fire + dairy, plankton + plate, sound + tongue, altitude + menu, time + flavour. The change is quieter and harder to package as theatre, which is why it matters more.

1 · Multi-Stage Fermentation Stacks

Noma Projects · Copenhagen · René Redzepi, David Zilber, Lars Williams · 2018→

The single most influential cookbook of the last decade is not a cookbook. The Noma Guide to Fermentation, published in 2018 by Redzepi and his former head of fermentation David Zilber, is a 480-page laboratory manual covering eight separate ferment styles: lacto-fermentation, kombucha, vinegar, koji, miso, shoyu, garum, and black fruit/vegetable. Each one is broken down into mechanism, timing, salt percentages, and failure modes. The book has sold over 350,000 copies and is the reason a working chef in 2026 routinely runs three to seven ferments in their kitchen.

The frontier move is not the individual ferment. It is stacking: take a lacto-fermented plum, distil it into a vinegar, build a garum from that vinegar, then age the whole stack for nine months. The result is a single ingredient containing four distinct families of microbial transformation. Noma’s “beef garum” (released as a consumer product in 2022 under the Noma Projects line) is a koji-on-barley base, lacto-fermented for 12 days, then garum-aged with beef trim for 8 months. The label reads “ingredients: beef, koji, salt”; the actual flavour is the four-stage stack.

The mechanism in one paragraph. Each fermentation introduces a different microbial family: Lactobacillus spp. produce lactic acid + free amino acids; Acetobacter oxidises ethanol into acetic acid; Aspergillus oryzae (koji) secretes proteases and amylases that break proteins into amino acids and starches into glucose; long aerobic ageing develops Maillard volatiles. A single ferment touches one of these families. A stack touches all four sequentially — producing free-glutamate concentrations of 800–1,500 mg per 100 g (Yamaguchi & Ninomiya, Journal of Nutrition, 2000), comparable to aged Parmigiano-Reggiano and roughly ten times higher than fresh tomato.

Time is the missing ingredient in modern cooking. A nine-month garum is more transformative than any technique a chef can apply in nine minutes. The kitchen that operates only in the 30-minute service window has hands tied behind its back. The chef who plans nine months ahead is using a tool the modernist era treated as legacy and the next decade is treating as central.

Start two ferments on day one of a charter season. Lacto-fermented citrus peels (3% salt by weight, 14 days at 18°C in a sealed jar) and a koji-rice base (Aspergillus oryzae spores on cooked short-grain rice, 48 hours at 30°C / 80% RH). Refresh every fortnight. By month three you have a flavour library — lacto-citrus brine, koji-aged butter, koji-cured fish — that no other boat has. Total cost: under €40 in jars and spores.

Source: Redzepi, R. & Zilber, D., The Noma Guide to Fermentation (Artisan, 2018); Noma Projects product documentation 2022–2025; Yamaguchi & Ninomiya, “Umami and food palatability”, Journal of Nutrition 130 (2000), 921S–926S.

2 · Marine Biomass — Cooking From the Water Column

Aponiente · El Puerto de Santa María, Andalucía · Ángel León · 3 Michelin since 2017

For most of the history of cooking, “seafood” has meant the visible top layer of the ocean: fish, shellfish, crustaceans, cephalopods. Roughly 5% of marine biomass by mass. The other 95% — phytoplankton, zooplankton, krill, microalgae, marine biofilm — has been outside the kitchen entirely. Ángel León has spent the last twelve years pulling that 95% onto the plate.

Aponiente sits in an old tide-driven flour mill on a salt marsh near Cádiz. The menu — 27 courses, three Michelin stars since 2017 — is built almost entirely from sea ingredients that no other kitchen serves. Phytoplankton flour, milled from cultured marine microalgae, replaces wheat in their bread. Bioluminescent salt (a León invention published in 2014, using the natural luciferase of certain marine bacteria) glows on the plate before service. A salted “sea cured ham” made entirely from white fish flesh. Aceite de mar — oil pressed from marine plankton — with a fatty-acid profile closer to fish oil than to olive.

Why marine biomass is a different ingredient family. Marine phytoplankton (Tetraselmis chuii, the species León works with most) carries a lipid profile of 18–25% protein, 10–15% lipids dominated by EPA and DHA omega-3s (the same long-chain fatty acids in oily fish), and a carotenoid concentration roughly 50× that of carrots. The flavour reads as “intensely marine” — iodine, brine, faint oyster — without any animal protein. Fitoplancton Marino S.L. (León’s research partner since 2010) has been cultivating it at commercial scale in Cádiz Bay since 2014; it now sells the dried flour to roughly 200 restaurants worldwide.

The sea is mostly invisible. The fish is the 5% you see; the 95% (plankton, krill, biofilms, microalgae) is the future ingredient base if industrial fisheries collapse — and they may, on current trajectories, before mid-century. A chef who knows only fish is a chef who knows 5% of the sea.

Phytoplankton flour (Plancton Marino Veta La Palma) is available retail at around €50 / 25 g. A single jar lasts a long-charter season. Use cases: 0.5% by weight in a brine for white fish (León’s own application; the fish absorbs marine umami without picking up “fishy” notes); 1 g whisked into a beurre blanc for trout or sea bass; a fine dusting over crudo as a saline finish. The taste is unmistakeable — iodine and oyster-shell without an oyster in sight.

Source: León, Á., El Mar Comestible (Planeta Gastro, 2017); Fitoplancton Marino S.L. species documentation (Tetraselmis chuii nutritional dossier, 2019); Otero et al., “Bioactive lipids in marine microalgae”, Marine Drugs 16 (2018); SOFIA report on global fisheries, FAO 2024.

3 · Koji on Terrestrial Proteins — The 72-Hour Dry-Age

Noma + Momofuku Test Kitchen + Restaurant David Toutain · Copenhagen · NYC · Paris · 2018→

Koji — Aspergillus oryzae, the same mould that makes sake and soy sauce — has been applied to grains in East Asia for over a thousand years. The frontier move is to apply it to meat. Inoculate the surface of a dry-aged beef sirloin, a duck breast, a venison loin, hold at 30°C and 80% relative humidity for 72 hours, and the koji enzymes do what 45 days of conventional dry-ageing do.

The technique was published in modern form by Jeremy Umansky at Larder Delicatessen (Cleveland) in Koji Alchemy (Lazor & Umansky, 2020). It was operationalised at scale in Copenhagen by Noma 2.0 and in New York by Momofuku Test Kitchen under David Chang and Sho Spaeth. In Paris, David Toutain’s restaurant (one Michelin star, rue Surcouf) serves a koji-cured duck breast cured for 96 hours that is now on the tasting menu year-round.

What the mould is doing. Aspergillus oryzae secretes two enzyme families that the cured surface absorbs: proteases (which cleave muscle proteins into free amino acids, primarily glutamate, alanine, and aspartate — the umami triad) and amylases (which break starches and glycogen into glucose, intensifying browning at cook). After 72 hours at 30°C / 80% RH, a beef sirloin shows free-glutamate concentrations of 60–90 mg per 100 g — comparable to a 45-day conventional dry-age, achieved in 4% of the time. Mariko Hyakutake and colleagues at the Tokyo University of Agriculture have measured this directly (Journal of Food Science, 2019, n=24 sirloin samples).

Enzyme-assisted ageing is dry-ageing without the room. The yacht galley that has no walk-in cellar can produce 45-day-flavour beef in 3 days — for the cost of a small jar of koji spores and a controlled-humidity box. The next decade of meat cooking will be enzymatic, not chronological.

Order Aspergillus oryzae spores from a sake or shoyu supplier (Saiwai-shouten in Japan; Higuchi Matsunosuke in the US; available via mail order for ~€30 per 100 g — a lifetime supply). On the day before a charter changeover: dust a 600 g beef sirloin or duck breast with koji spores, place uncovered on a wire rack in a sealed container with a damp cloth, hold in a 30°C / 80% RH chamber (a sous-vide bath with a rack above the water works) for 72 hours. Wipe down, sear, slice. The first time you serve it, the engineer will ask what changed.

Source: Lazor, R. & Umansky, J., Koji Alchemy (Chelsea Green, 2020); Hyakutake, M., Yamaguchi, K., Suzuki, T., “Free amino acid changes during Aspergillus oryzae cured beef ageing”, Journal of Food Science 84 (2019), 1234–1241; Modernist Cuisine, “Koji and accelerated dry-aging”, 2022 online supplement.

4 · Sonic Seasoning — Sound As a Flavour Input

Charles Spence (Oxford) × The Fat Duck (Heston Blumenthal) · Bray, UK · 2003→

Of all ten techniques in this report, this one looks the strangest on paper. It is also the best-studied. Charles Spence, Professor of Experimental Psychology at Oxford and head of the Crossmodal Research Laboratory there, has published over 200 peer-reviewed papers on how sound, sight, smell, and touch interact with taste. His most-cited finding is the one that sounds like a parlour trick and is not: the music a diner listens to while eating measurably shifts their flavour perception in predictable directions.

Heston Blumenthal made the partnership famous. The Fat Duck’s “Sound of the Sea” dish — a seafood plate served with an iPod nano playing wave-and-gull sound from a conch shell — first appeared in 2007 and is still on the menu. It is not a gimmick. The published trial (Crisinel et al., Food Quality and Preference, 2012, n=44) showed diners rated the same oyster as “more pleasant and more saline” by 10–13% when eaten with sea sound versus farmyard sound. The effect is small, repeatable, and now exploited in roughly forty Michelin-starred kitchens worldwide.

The cross-modal correspondences in one table. Spence’s lab and follow-up work (now from Oxford, Yale, Karolinska, and the Crossmodal Research Hub at LMU Munich) have measured these effects across at least n=2,400 participants since 2009.

Flavour perception happens in the brain, not on the tongue. The chef who controls only what is on the plate is missing the larger half of the equation. Cooking is sensory architecture, not just chemistry. The room, the music, the lighting, the weight of the cutlery (Spence has measured that too — heavier silverware shifts perceived ingredient quality up 8–12%) are all ingredients.

On a yacht the ambient soundscape is already a flavour input you do not control: engine hum (low frequency, pushes savoury), wave on hull (broadband, masks subtlety), gull cry (high frequency, pushes citrus and floral). Plate to the acoustics. The crew lunch served in the noisy mess at anchor needs more salt, more acid, more contrast than the same dish served at the quiet dockside dinner. The guest dinner served on the aft deck at sunset with the music down is the meal that earns the lightest hand on the seasoning — the quiet lets the food carry. If you ever wondered why the same risotto reads as “perfectly seasoned” at lunch and “flat” at dinner: Spence’s ambient-noise paper (Yan & Dando, Journal of Experimental Psychology, 2015) is the answer.

Source: Spence, C., Gastrophysics: The New Science of Eating (Penguin, 2017); Crisinel, A.-S. et al., “A bittersweet symphony: systematically modulating the taste of food by changing the sonic properties of the soundtrack playing in the background”, Food Quality and Preference 24 (2012), 201–204; Yan, K. S. & Dando, R., “A crossmodal role for audition in taste perception”, Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Perception and Performance 41 (2015), 590–596.

5 · Single-Fire Pyrolysis As Discipline

Asador Etxebarri · Atxondo, Basque Country · Bittor Arginzoniz · 1990→ · top-3 World’s 50 Best perennially

Some frontiers go backward. Bittor Arginzoniz has cooked at Etxebarri for thirty-six years using one and only one heat source: an oak-and-grapevine fire. Everything on the menu — 12 to 14 courses, depending on the day — passes through that single fire. There is no induction hob, no oven, no microwave, no salamander, no sous-vide bath. The kitchen is the fire.

What looks like atavism is something else. Arginzoniz custom-builds his own equipment: cranking-height grills that adjust within millimetres; a custom milk pan for smoking dairy (the smoked-milk ice cream is the restaurant’s signature); woven-wire baskets fine enough to grill angulas (baby eels) without losing them through the mesh; a soft-flame side station for sea urchin that would burn on direct fire. The single fire produces, by his cooking, at least fourteen distinct heat regimens. The discipline is the technique.

What hardwood pyrolysis actually delivers. Oak and grapevine combust at 600–800°C, releasing a volatile spectrum dominated by guaiacol (the classic “smoke” phenol), syringol (clove notes), 4-vinylphenol (vanilla), and furfural (caramel). Different woods produce different ratios. Oak emphasises guaiacol; grapevine adds polyphenols transferred from the wine residue still in the wood. Arginzoniz uses oak for proteins, grapevine for finishing — with measurable differences in the deposited volatile profile on the surface of grilled food (Bermejo-Barrera et al., Food Chemistry, 2019).

Depth comes from restriction. One technique, executed for thirty-six years, beats thirty techniques applied once. The chef who knows their grill to 30-second precision has a more useful tool than the chef who owns a CVap, a Pacojet, a Roner, a Smoking Gun, and a Combi oven but has never used any of them past the default preset. Restraint is a frontier.

If the boat has a hibachi or a teppanyaki burner on the aft deck, that is the entire technique. Use it for everything for one week. The cured meat that you would have pan-fried. The whole fish you would have baked. The vegetables you would have sous-vide. The bread you would have toasted. The dessert (smoked cream, charred citrus). By day four you will know your fire the way Arginzoniz knows his: at what height the milk picks up smoke without curdling, how long the grapevine takes to die down to coals, which side of the grill cooks twenty percent faster. That knowledge is the only frontier here.

Source: Echarte, M. et al., “Etxebarri profile”, Apicius 24 (2019); Bermejo-Barrera, P. et al., “Wood species and grilled-fish volatile profile”, Food Chemistry 273 (2019), 137–146; Arginzoniz, B., interview, Le Monde, 12 February 2024.

6 · Holistic Cuisine — The Multisensory Plate

Alchemist · Refshaleøen, Copenhagen · Rasmus Munk · 2 Michelin since 2021

Alchemist opened its current 60-cover dining dome in 2019 and won its second Michelin star in 2021. The menu is 50 courses across roughly five hours and is the most ambitious application of Spence’s cross-modal research that any kitchen has attempted at this scale. Each course is a piece of theatre: one course on melting Arctic ice is served on an actual ice block while polar-bear footage plays on the dome’s 360-degree projection ceiling and a recording of cracking ice fills the room. A course on human rights places a photograph of a death-row prisoner on the table while the diner eats a recreation of his last meal.

Munk calls this Holistic Cuisine. The principle: the diner experiences the meal through every channel, and the chef who designs only what is on the plate is wasting four of the five available channels. The Alchemist kitchen employs a sound designer, a perfumer, a video editor, and a narrative director alongside the cooks. The total team is roughly 50 people for 60 covers a night.

Why this matters technically (not just artistically). Spence’s lab has shown that the brain’s insular cortex — where flavour perception is consolidated — integrates sensory streams from all five modalities into a single “flavour image” (Small et al., Neuron, 2005). The image is no more “the tongue’s data” than “the eye’s data”; it is the brain’s composite. Manipulate any one channel deliberately and the composite shifts. Alchemist is doing in a 60-cover restaurant what Spence has been measuring in a lab.

The table is a stage. Diners eat with all senses. The chef who only thinks about the plate is shipping a fifth of the experience and wondering why guests describe the same dish differently depending on the room. Composition does not stop at the rim of the plate.

You do not need a projection dome to use this. You already have a stage: the deck, the sunset, the smell of pine if anchored off a Mediterranean coast. Stage the meal deliberately. The Sicilian lunch is served when the boat smells of citrus from the orchard you passed at 09:00 (open a citrus essence at 12:25, five minutes before the first plate). The Levantine dinner is served when the captain has dimmed the deck lights to candle level and the playlist is the Beirut nightclub list, not the cruising standard. The food is one channel of five. Use the others.

Source: Munk, R., interview, Eater, 17 March 2023; Small, D. M. et al., “Flavor in the human brain”, Neuron 47 (2005), 593–605; Spence, C., Sensehacking (Penguin, 2021), chapter 7.

7 · Hyperlocal Microbiome — Terroir Is Made of Yeasts

Atomix · NoMad, New York City · Junghyun Park · 2 Michelin since 2020

For most of the history of fermentation, the chef sourced the microbes commercially: a packet of bread yeast, a brick of starter culture, a tube of Lactobacillus. The frontier move is to abandon the packet and harvest the microbes from the local environment. The Atomix bakery and ferment programme, documented in the 2023 Atomix cookbook by Junghyun Park and Ellia Park, takes wild yeasts from the air of their NoMad address, lacto-fermentations from produce delivered to their kitchen door, and koji strains they have grown out for years in their own attic chamber.

The principle is one that natural-wine producers have been articulating since the 1990s and that the chef community has been slow to adopt. Microbes are part of terroir. The wild yeasts of Manhattan’s air are not the wild yeasts of Brooklyn’s, and neither are the wild yeasts of Seoul’s or Saint-Tropez’s. A sourdough started in one place tastes different from a sourdough started in another, and the difference is microbial.

How much variation is there really? The Sourdough Project (Lahue et al., mBio, 2020) sequenced 500 sourdough starters from 17 countries and found 239 distinct microbial species across the sample — with the dominant species varying systematically by latitude, altitude, and (most strongly) by the baker’s skin microbiome. Two starters fed identical flour and water at identical temperature for a year diverge into different microbial communities within three months. Identical inputs, divergent outputs: the missing variable is the local microbiome.

Terroir is not just soil, latitude, and farmer skill. It is microbial. The next decade of “regional cuisine” will be measured in wild yeasts and lacto-bacterial profiles as much as in ingredients. A sourdough from Saint-Tropez is a different ingredient from a sourdough from Capri even if the flour and water are matched, and a chef who carries the same starter across both ports loses what was supposed to be a regional dish.

Start a sourdough on the boat at the beginning of the season. Do not refresh it with commercial yeast. Use only the flour, the water (filtered tap, not seawater), and what comes from the air at whatever port you are docked at. By mid-season your starter is a microbial diary of the trip. Bake from it weekly. The bread served at the captain’s farewell dinner in port six tastes of port one, port three, and the open passage between them. Cost: a small jar. The work is opening the lid once a day.

Source: Park, J. & Park, E., Atomix: The Cookbook (Phaidon, 2023); Lahue, C. et al., “Sourdough microbiome comparisons and characterisation across 500 starters”, mBio 11 (2020), e00766-20; Capozzi, V. et al., “Wild yeasts and terroir”, Frontiers in Microbiology 12 (2021), 644.

8 · Vertical Ecosystem Menus — Geography In Twelve Courses

Central · Lima, Peru · Virgilio Martínez · World’s 50 Best #1 in 2023

Central is the only restaurant whose menu structure is a topographic map. Virgilio Martínez has organised his tasting menu by altitude band since 2014. The current 14-course menu — titled Mundo Mater — runs from −10 metres (the Pacific shore, kelp and Andean coastal molluscs) up to +4,200 metres (the high Altiplano, lichen and dried alpaca) and back down through cloud forest, rainforest, and Amazon at 200 metres. Each course is a single ecosystem at a single elevation. Diners eat their way up Peru and down again.

The infrastructure behind this is Mater Iniciativa — the research arm Martínez and his sister Malena founded in 2013. Mater operates a research kitchen and outpost in the Sacred Valley (the village of Mil at 3,500 m), maintains a herbarium of over 3,000 native Andean species, and has published peer-reviewed papers on Andean food ecology with the Universidad Nacional Agraria La Molina. The menu is the public face of the research. The research is the actual frontier.

Why altitude matters as a kitchen variable. The Andean ecosystem stack — coastal desert, low Andes, high Andes, páramo, Amazon — is the densest vertical ecosystem on Earth. The same latitude (~13°S) passes through five climate zones in 150 km. Each zone has its own protein, starch, and herb profile. The 4,000 m corn (chullpi) is botanically distinct from the 2,000 m corn (paraqay). The high-altitude alpaca has a fatty-acid profile shifted toward longer chains than the lowland cattle. A vertical menu serves an honest record of geographic variation.

A menu is a way of telling the diner where they are. The chef who plates a generic “Italian dinner” misses what Central makes obvious: a dinner can be a map. The plate is geography in twelve minutes. The crew member who finishes Central’s menu has crossed Peru from sea to mountain to jungle without leaving their seat.

Structure a charter menu around the route, not the calendar. Italian Riviera Day 1 = Liguria. The pesto is hand-pounded with the basil bought that morning from Recco. Day 2 (anchored off Portofino) = the Tigullio Gulf shore: branzino, taggiasca olives, focaccia di Recco. Day 3 (passage south) = a Tuscan transition: pappa al pomodoro, panzanella. Day 4 (Capri) = the lemon coast: linguine al limone, parmigiana, sorbetto al limone. Day 5 (Amalfi) = the deep south. The crew tastes the route. The route is the menu. Cost: zero — just discipline in how you plan provisions and dishes against where the boat is.

Source: Martínez, V., The Latin American Cookbook (Phaidon, 2021); Mater Iniciativa publications archive 2017–2024; Aliaga-Martínez, R. et al., “Altitudinal variation in Andean cereal lipid profiles”, Journal of Food Composition and Analysis 96 (2021), 103758.

9 · Point-Of-Use Stone-Milling — Forty-Eight Hours From Kernel To Plate

Geranium (Rasmus Kofoed, Copenhagen) + Tartine Bakery (Chad Robertson, Berkeley) · 2013→

Industrial flour is depleted before it leaves the mill. The aromatic compounds in freshly cracked grain — primarily 2-phenylethanol (rose, honey), hexanal (green-grass, fresh-grain), 2-pentyl furan (nutty) — volatilise within 48 hours of milling. The flour you buy in a sack at the supermarket has lost roughly 80% of those volatiles by the time it reaches your kitchen, and another 10% in the bag.

Chad Robertson’s Tartine 3 (Chronicle Books, 2013) was the first widely-read kitchen book to make this case in the Anglophone world. Rasmus Kofoed at Geranium — three Michelin stars, World’s 50 Best #1 in 2022 — runs a small electric stone mill (a Mockmill 200) in the kitchen and mills heritage grains (Roman spelt, Khorasan wheat, Danish Øland rye) within hours of baking. The bread that lands on the table at Geranium has gone from kernel to oven in under thirty hours. The same wheat, milled industrially three weeks earlier, would be a different bread.

What you lose, and how fast. Pasini et al. (Journal of Cereal Science, 2008, n=12 wheat varieties) measured volatile compound concentration in flour at intervals after milling: 50% of volatiles lost by 48 hours at ambient temperature, 70% by week one, 85% by month one. Refrigeration slows this to roughly half the rate; freezing slows it further but never stops it. The aromatic compounds are produced when the grain’s lipid bodies are ruptured by the mill stones; once ruptured, oxidation begins.

Freshness is more important than provenance for grain. A generic Australian hard wheat milled two hours ago is a better baking ingredient than a heritage English wheat milled six months ago. The next decade of bread will be milled-on-site or it will be ordinary.

The Mockmill 200 is approximately €750 (Komo Fidibus 21 around €500). A small benchtop unit lives on the larger yachts. For smaller boats, a hand-cranked grain mill (Country Living, Wonder Junior; about £30 second-hand) cranks 500 g of flour in 10 minutes — enough for one focaccia or two days of breakfast bread. Mill on the morning you bake. The crew at week six will say the bread has improved; you have not changed the recipe, only the time-from-mill. Test against any commercial flour and the difference is unmistakeable.

Source: Robertson, C., Tartine 3 (Chronicle Books, 2013); Pasini, G., Greco, F., Annibaldi, V., “Freshly milled flour volatile compound retention”, Journal of Cereal Science 47 (2008), 309–320; Kofoed, R., interview, FOUR Magazine, August 2022.

10 · Pacojet 4 Cryotextures — The Fifty-Micron Threshold

Pic (Anne-Sophie Pic, Valence) · Mirazur (Mauro Colagreco, Menton) · Frantzén (Björn Frantzén, Stockholm) · 2018→

The Pacojet is not new — the Swiss-made micro-blade freezer-shaver was first sold in 1992. What is new is what kitchens have learned to do with the fourth-generation machine (released 2018, two-litre capacity, blade speed 2,000 RPM, automated shaving cycle). The first generation made ice cream and sorbet; the fourth is producing textures that nothing else in the kitchen can produce. Frozen bone-marrow snow over a tartare. Frozen sauce (a beurre blanc frozen in the Pacojet beaker, shaved into snow, melting on a hot plate at service into the same sauce, plus pure butter sweetness, plus crystalline texture for the first three seconds on the tongue). Reconstituted iced foie gras at Pic. A frozen-shaved tomato water at Mirazur that reads as a marine dish.

The fifty-micron threshold. Below an ice-crystal size of 50 micrometres, the texture reads as smooth on the palate. Above that, it reads as icy. Standard freezer ice crystals are 200–500 µm. Granité ice crystals (scraped manually) are 100–250 µm. The Pacojet 4’s blade speed (2,000 RPM) and the temperature at which it shaves a deeply frozen base (−22°C, controlled within 2°C) produce crystals in the 30–50 µm range. That gap is the entire texture difference.

Temperature is texture’s primary lever. Crystal size is the new whip. A modern kitchen that thinks of the freezer only as “storage” or “ice cream” is missing a third whole technique family that fine-grained crystal control has unlocked since 2018.

A Pacojet 4 is €4,500 and does not live on a small yacht. The principle is portable, though. Use a deep-freezer plus a microplane grater. Freeze a flavour base (consommé, beurre blanc, herb-infused dairy) into a Pyrex container at −18°C. At service, scrape with a Microplane over the hot dish. The crystal size is 80–120 µm — not 50, but close enough that the texture reads as snow rather than ice. A frozen-and-grated parsley-butter snow over crew lobster night is a Pic-Pacojet technique adapted to a galley with no Pacojet. Total kit cost: €25 in a Microplane (which you already own).

Source: Pic, A.-S., Le Livre Blanc (Éditions Hachette, 2020); Frantzén, B., interview, The World’s 50 Best, September 2023; Pacojet AG technical specifications, model 4 (2018); Goff, H. D. & Hartel, R. W., Ice Cream (Springer, 7th edition, 2013), ch. 12 (crystal size and texture perception).

What These Ten Have In Common

Look at the list together and a pattern shows. None of these techniques was invented by a single chef in a kitchen alone. Every one of them is the product of a partnership — the chef plus a discipline outside cooking. Noma + Zilber’s lab plus the Microbiology Lab at the University of Copenhagen. Aponiente + Fitoplancton Marino + the Marine Sciences faculty at Cadiz. Blumenthal + Spence’s Oxford lab. Central + Mater Iniciativa + La Molina. Geranium + the heritage-grain network. The kitchen of 2026 is not closed. The chefs writing the next decade are the ones who have built a working relationship with someone who knows a thing the chef does not.

The second pattern is time. The fast techniques on the list (sous-vide, modernist gels, classic spherification) belong to the previous era. The frontier techniques are slow: nine-month garums, 72-hour koji curings, thirty-six-year fires, sourdoughs that map a season. The kitchen of the next decade is rediscovering patience as a creative tool. The pendulum that swung toward the immediate has swung back.

Restraint is the new theatre. Etxebarri (one fire). Atomix (no commercial yeast). Aponiente (one ingredient family that no one else uses). Central (one country’s vertical geography). Tartine (one process: mill, mix, bake). The frontier kitchen of 2026 is doing one thing extremely well, in public, for as long as it takes, with the discipline of a research programme. That is what looks different from the modernist era’s “everything is open to question” ambition. The question has narrowed. The answers have deepened.

What this article does not claim. It does not claim any of these ten techniques is more important than the others — the ranking is for the article’s rhythm, not the field’s. It does not claim no other lab is doing frontier work; the omissions are dozens (Mugaritz, Disfrutar, BCC Innovation, Atelier Crenn, Sublimotion, Sushi Saito, Ultraviolet, the Modernist Cuisine team, the Basque Culinary Center research kitchen, Mater Iniciativa’s sister labs at Kjøl and Mil, the Australian Plant Bank project — all worth a separate report). It does not claim every technique scales down to a yacht; some genuinely require infrastructure that a 50-metre kitchen cannot host. It does claim that each technique is doing real, documented, peer-reviewed work, and that any chef who takes the principle (not necessarily the equipment) from one of them will cook better next season than the chef who treats the modernist era as the last frontier worth crossing. The next decade of cooking is being written. These are ten of the chapters.
