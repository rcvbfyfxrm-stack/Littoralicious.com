The Burger Grind

Pre-ground mince is one muscle, one fat level, ground to mush. The galley fix is three cuts, two grinds and a thermometer — a patty with the bite, fat and beef depth a single-cut grind can't reach.

Bite a burger built from one supermarket tub and you get one note, held flat: lean and fat ground to the same paste, pressed to a dense puck. The fix is not a better cut — it is three cuts and a thermometer. Short rib for the fat and the deep beef, chuck for everyday beefiness, sirloin for the mineral bite; ground once, kept near-frozen, salted only at the pass. The whole trick lives in two numbers and a temperature.

The one idea: salt the crust, never the mix. Salt in the raw grind dissolves myosin — the protein that, once freed, cross-links into a springy, sausage-bound puck. Season the outside as it hits the pan and the inside stays loose and tender.

Where it comes from

The name is a place: Hamburg. German immigrants carried Hamburg-style minced beefsteak to New York in the 1800s — chopped, seasoned, cooked like a steak — and by the 1870s “Hamburg steak” sat on American menus a generation before anyone put it in a bun. It was, from the start, a way to make cheaper, tougher, more flavourful beef eat tender. That logic still picks the cuts: short rib and chuck, not a premium steak.

Two philosophies later split the same grind. The thin smash — a loose ball flattened on a screaming griddle for maximum crust — and the thick pub patty, seared and rested to a rosy centre. Same meat, opposite geometry. This blueprint grinds for the second; the close shows you how to send it the other way.

One pass through a 6 mm die multiplies the meat’s surface area many times over — which is the whole reason ground beef must reach 71 °C when a steak is safe seared rare. Bacteria live on surfaces; grinding folds the outside in. — USDA FSIS, Ground Beef and Food Safety

That surface area cuts the other way in the pan, too — all those ragged edges are crust waiting to happen, provided the fat behaves.

Beef fat stays solid below about 4 °C and starts to soften by 25 °C. Grind it warm and the fat smears, coating every lean strand in grease so it cooks dense and weeps. Grind it near-frozen and the fat shatters into discrete chips that survive into the patty as pockets — juice on the inside, char on the out.

Method

0/0 steps done

  1. The cold is not a precaution — it is the recipe.

    Chill everything first. Cut the three cuts into 3 cm dice, spread on a tray, and freeze 25 min until firm and the edges are crisp but the centre still yields. Put the grinder auger, die and bowl in the freezer too.

    Why
    Fat softens from about 25 °C; near-frozen, it stays brittle and cuts cleanly instead of smearing across the lean. A warm grinder undoes the chill in seconds. — J. Kenji López-Alt, The Food Lab, 2015

  2. One pass, coarse, and stop.

    Grind once through the large die. Run the chilled dice through a 6 mm (¼″) plate straight into the cold bowl set over ice. Let it fall in a loose pile — do not pack or stir.

    Why
    A single coarse grind keeps the strands long and open, so the patty stays craggy and holds juice. A second fine pass makes it pasty and dense. No grinder? Ask the butcher for a single coarse grind of your three cuts, kept cold.

  3. Here is where most home burgers are lost.

    Portion to 150 g, do not salt the mix. Weigh each patty, shape it a thumb wider than the bun, and press a shallow dimple in the centre. Handle as little as possible. Salt stays in the tin until the pass.

    Why
    Salt dissolves myosin, the muscle protein that then cross-links into a bouncy, sausage-like bind — the texture of a supermarket frozen patty. The dimple offsets the centre’s tendency to dome as it cooks. — The Food Lab, on salt and myosin extraction

  4. Crust is flavour you can hear.

    Sear hard, salt now. Get a heavy pan or plancha smoking with a film of dripping. Lay the patty down, salt and pepper the top. Leave it 2–3 min to build a dark crust, flip once, salt the second side. Never press — you only squeeze juice into the flame.

    Why
    The dark crust is the Maillard reaction — amino acids and sugars rearranging above ~140 °C into hundreds of new roast-and-savoury compounds. A loose, dry-surfaced patty browns faster than a packed wet one, which is why the cold loose grind pays off here.

  5. The honest step — read it before you serve a guest.

    Pull at temperature, then rest. The safe number for ground beef is 71 °C (USDA). Grinding your own from intact, well-trimmed cuts, kept cold and cooked promptly, lets many chefs serve 54–57 °C medium-rare — an informed risk you carry, never for the very young, pregnant, elderly or immunocompromised. Rest 2 min; toast the buns in the rendered fat; build.

    Why
    Surface bacteria are folded throughout a grind, so the centre must reach a kill temperature a seared steak never needs. The shorter the gap between grinding and cooking, the lower the risk — which is the real argument for grinding your own. — USDA FSIS, Ground Beef and Food Safety

Troubleshooting
Dense, bouncy biteSalt got into the mix, or it was over-worked → salt only the crust, handle less.
Greasy, grey pattyMeat or grinder was warm → chill everything near-frozen before grinding.
Domed, shrunken puckNo dimple, or pressed in the pan → dimple the centre, never press.
Pale, no crustPan not hot enough, or patty too wet → smoking pan, dry loose grind.
Charter prep & storage

Grind and portion at anchor, on a calm morning. Stacked between greaseproof discs, raw patties hold 24 hr in the coldest part of the fridge, or freeze flat for a month — cook from frozen on a lower heat if a tender opens late. The grind is the labour; once it is done, service is two minutes a patty. Crew tier: stretch the same blend with a little grated onion and breadcrumb into a thicker, heartier patty that feeds more from less prime beef.

Once you own the ratio — rib, chuck, sirloin; cold; salt the crust — the burger is yours: send it thick and rosy for guests, smashed and lacy for crew, or funked up with a little dry-aged trim. The Hamburg steak was always a way to make humble beef eat like a steak; grind it yourself and it finally does.
