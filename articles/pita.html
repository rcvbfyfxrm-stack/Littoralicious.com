Pita Bread: The Science-First Version

The pocket is not luck — it’s steam, trapped between two skins, on a surface hot enough to set them first. Reliable on a stone or a galley skillet. Twenty pitas for ten to twelve.

A flat, pocketless pita is a heat failure or a rolling failure — almost never the recipe. Bakeries hit a 300°C deck; you probably don’t, so this works two ways: hot stone, or the skillet that makes the same pocket on a stovetop.

Where it comes from

Pita belongs to the oldest family of breads on earth — the flatbreads of the Levant and eastern Mediterranean, baked on hot stone since the Neolithic, long before the closed oven existed. Even its name travels: the Greek pita shares roots with a word-family that may also have given us pizza. For millennia it has been plate, spoon and wrapper in one — bread you eat with, scooping hummus or cradling lamb. The pocket that makes that possible is pure physics: trapped steam, ballooning two thin skins apart.

The three moves: (1) 64% hydration — enough steam, dry enough to roll clean; (2) a double rest (bulk, then a relaxed rest after rolling) — even puff, no springback; (3) the skillet / plancha route — pocket heat with no deck oven.

The ratio — flour = 100%
Water 64% · Oil 3% · Salt 2% · Sugar 1.5% · Yeast 0.8% — below 60% water the pocket starves for steam; above ~68% it’s too slack to roll clean. 64% is the hand-rolled sweet spot.

Method

0/0 steps done

  1. Mix & knead. Whisk yeast + sugar into flour; add water + oil to a shaggy mass, rest 15 min, then add the 20 g salt and knead 8–10 min to smooth and elastic.

    Why
    64% water + developed gluten gives both the steam to inflate and skins strong and stretchy enough to hold it as one balloon. — McGee; Modernist Bread

  2. Bulk ferment 60–90 min until doubled. (Make-ahead: halve the yeast and cold-ferment 24–72 h.)

  3. Divide & first rest. Scale ~20 balls of 85 g. Rest 20–30 min, covered. Preheat the surface to maximum now (a stone needs 45 min).

    Why
    Balling tightens the gluten; the rest lets it relax so it rolls even and won’t spring back. An even round is the #1 predictor of a clean pocket. — Reinhart; López-Alt

  4. Roll & second rest. Roll each to ~18 cm, 4–5 mm even, turning a quarter per stroke. Rest the rounds 10 min, covered; brush off loose flour.

  5. Bake — pick your route, ~285°C surface. Stone/steel (oven): 275–290°C, top third — balloons in 2–3 min. Skillet/plancha (galley): dry, max heat, water skitters instantly — round down ~30 s, flip, balloons in 1–2 min.

    Why
    The skins must set faster than the steam can leak — only happens above ~230°C. Cast iron holds that heat, which is why the skillet works at sea. — McGee; Modernist Bread

  6. Keep soft. Stack each baked pita straight into a towel-lined bowl — trapped steam keeps them foldable. Pull at the dome, never at colour.

Charter prep & storage

Cold-ferment the dough and bake fresh on a hot skillet mid-charter — no deck oven. Dough keeps 3 days; baked pita 2 days airtight, or freeze 2 months (toast from frozen). Never refrigerate baked pita — it goes leathery.

Sources: McGee, On Food and Cooking; Myhrvold, Modernist Bread; Reinhart, The Bread Baker’s Apprentice; Roden, A Book of Middle Eastern Food; López-Alt, Serious Eats.
