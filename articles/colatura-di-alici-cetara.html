Colatura di Alici and the Town That Never Stopped

The town name itself is the evidence. Roman fish-processing facilities were called cetariae — the word that became Cetara. When Vesuvius buried Pompeii in 79 AD, it preserved the Garum Shop mid-production. After Rome fell, garum disappeared across most of Europe. Except here. This is the story of a 2,000-year fermentation that never stopped.

Cetara, Amalfi Coast, Campania

When Vesuvius buried Pompeii in 79 AD, it preserved the Garum Shop (Officina del Garum) mid-production: six sealed dolia containing anchovies and small fish being fermented whole, operated by Aulus Umbricius Scaurus, whose branded containers have been found in over 50 locations across Pompeii and Boscoreale. The finest garum sold for 1,000 sesterces per 12 pints — roughly a legionary’s monthly wages.

After Rome fell, garum disappeared across most of Europe. Except here. Cistercian monks at the monastery of San Pietro a Tuczolo near Cetara continued salting anchovies in wooden barrels through the medieval period. The tradition holds that they discovered colatura by accident — noticing a flavourful amber liquid collecting at the bottom of curing barrels, seeping through the wood. They recognised it as the successor to the garum described in classical texts.

In 900 AD, Saracen settlers further established Cetara’s identity as a fishing centre. For centuries afterward, colatura remained exclusively local — made by virtually every family for domestic use, never traded beyond the coast.

The Chemical Proof of Continuity

Analysis of amino acid profiles from archaeological garum residues at Pompeii shows a pattern dominated by glutamate, glycine, and alanine — the same amino acid profile found in modern colatura di alici. This is not cultural mythology. It is molecular evidence of an unbroken 2,000-year technique.

On Board

Transit the Amalfi Coast. Anchor off Cetara — it is between Vietri sul Mare and Erchie, small harbour, working fishing boats. Walk to one of the producers. Buy the colatura from the people who make it in the same town where the Romans named the process. Make the spaghetti that night. Tell the guests this liquid is older than Christianity and younger than the barrel it came from.

Pairing

Fiano di Avellino DOCG — Pietracupa or Ciro Picariello. Mineral, structured, Campanian granite.
