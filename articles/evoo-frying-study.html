EVOO Remains Stable at Frying Temperatures

The "save extra virgin for finishing" advice is based on outdated science. Here's what 36 studies actually show.

The Research

A 2024 systematic review from the University of Barcelona, published in Food Research International, analyzed 36 studies on olive oil behavior during cooking. Methods included frying, sautéing, and roasting across temperatures from 120°C to 240°C.

Sample size: 2,847 oil samples across all studies.
Funding: EU Horizon research grant (no industry funding disclosed).
Peer review: Yes, double-blind.

Key Finding

Extra virgin olive oil is more stable during cooking than previously believed, outperforming many seed oils due to its high antioxidant content and low polyunsaturated fat ratio.

The Numbers

Finding Data Context
Smoke point 190-215°C Higher than commonly cited 160°C
Polar compounds 23% lower than sunflower Polar compounds = degradation markers
Antioxidant retention 50-70% after 30 min at 180°C Polyphenols survive cooking
Trans fat formation Negligible below 200°C Lower than refined oils

Why EVOO Beats Seed Oils

Three factors protect EVOO during heating:

  1. Low polyunsaturated fat content. EVOO is ~10% polyunsaturated vs. sunflower's ~65%. Polyunsaturated fats oxidize faster. Simple chemistry.
  2. High oleic acid. Monounsaturated fats are heat-stable. EVOO is ~73% oleic acid.
  3. Polyphenols. Natural antioxidants in EVOO actively fight oxidation during cooking. Refined oils have had these stripped out.

What This Means

For sautéing and pan-frying: Use your good EVOO. The flavor compounds survive. The health benefits partially survive. The stability is better than you've been told.

For aromatics: Building a soffritto or mirepoix in EVOO transfers flavor compounds into the fat. This is why Mediterranean cooking tastes like it does. The science supports the tradition.

For searing: EVOO works up to ~200°C. For screaming-hot searing above that, refined avocado or grapeseed is still appropriate.

What This Doesn't Mean

This isn't permission to deep-fry in EVOO for extended periods. Studies focused on typical sauté/pan-fry durations (under 30 minutes). Prolonged deep-frying at high temperatures still favors refined oils.

It also doesn't mean all EVOOs are equal. Quality matters. Low-quality EVOO (often mislabeled, sometimes adulterated) showed faster degradation. Buy from reputable sources.

Limitations

  • Most studies used controlled laboratory conditions. Real kitchens have more variables—humidity, pan material, food moisture content.
  • Reusing oil (common in restaurants) wasn't thoroughly addressed. Fresh oil performs better than reused oil, regardless of type.
  • EVOO quality varies significantly. Studies used verified extra virgin; commercial products may not match.

Your Move

Stop reaching for "cooking olive oil" unless you're deep-frying. Use your good EVOO for sautéing aromatics and pan-frying fish. The flavor payoff is real, and the stability science supports it.

The Hard Truth

The "low smoke point" myth about EVOO came from outdated studies and got repeated until it became accepted wisdom. Smoke point alone doesn't determine stability—oxidative stability does. EVOO excels at both.

Sometimes what "everybody knows" is wrong. This is one of those times.
