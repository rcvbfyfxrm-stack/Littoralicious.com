Victoria Sponge: Reverse-Creamed, Equal-Weight, Cultured Butter

The recipe you actually want to read

The cake Queen Victoria took with her afternoon tea, rebuilt with fat-coats-flour mixing for the tightest crumb, equal-weight ratio for the historic British balance, cultured butter for diacetyl, hand-mashed raspberry jam, lightly whipped cream, and caster sugar — not icing — on top. Three pillars. Six sources. One level-topped sandwich cake the WI would still recognise.

The Victoria sponge has been miswritten for a century. The recipe you find in most books gives you the right ingredients in roughly the right ratio, then tells you to cream butter and sugar like a pound cake. That works, but it leaves you with a dome you have to trim, a crumb that runs slightly coarse, and a result that's good rather than precise. Reverse the order — paddle the soft butter into the dry mix first, then add the wet — and the same ingredients produce a flat-topped, velvet-crumbed sponge that cuts cleanly and reads exactly as the Women's Institute version always promised it would.

Three modifications separate this from every other recipe: the reverse-cream method (fat-coats-flour for the tightest crumb and a level top), the equal-weight 1:1:1:1 ratio (the Mrs Beeton / Constance Spry historic balance), and cultured butter with a touch of yogurt (diacetyl plus a fraction of lactic acid for finer aeration). Caster sugar on top, never icing.

Method

Phase 1: Raspberry Jam — 25 min, make first so it cools while sponges bake

  1. Macerate. Combine raspberries, 150g caster sugar, and lemon juice in a heavy small saucepan off-heat. Stand 5 minutes to draw the juice.
  2. Hand-mash with a fork. Leave half the berries broken and half whole. Texture target: rough conserve, not coulis.
Why hand-mash, not blend?
Blade work shears the seed coats and oxidises lycopene and the anthocyanin pigments that give raspberry its bright red. The result reads as brownish and bitter. Hand-mashing preserves fruit identity — you get a jam that still tastes of raspberry, not of reduced raspberry sugar. Raspberry already carries enough natural pectin (in the seed coats and cell walls) to set without commercial pectin added; the lemon juice simply drops the pH into the gel range.
— Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, on pectin gelation; Modernist Cuisine vol. 4 on preserves
  1. Simmer + reduce. Bring to a steady simmer over low heat, stirring every 30 sec. Reduce to 105°C / 220°F, 6–8 min. Test on a chilled plate: a finger drawn through the puddle should leave a clean trail in 10 sec.
  2. Cool to room temperature in a clean jar. Skin on the surface is normal. Hold fridge or RT until assembly.

Phase 2: Reverse-Cream the Sponge — 8 min hands-on

  1. Tin prep + oven. Butter two 20cm sandwich tins and line the bases with parchment circles. Preheat oven to 170°C / 340°F convection (or 180°C static), rack in the centre.
  2. Whisk dry into the mixer bowl. Plain flour, cornflour, baking powder, salt. Whisk 30 seconds to aerate and homogenise the leavener.

The butter must be at 18–20°C — soft enough that your fingertip leaves an indent, firm enough that it holds shape. Too warm and it greases the dry mix instead of coating it; too cold and the paddle can’t break it down before the eggs arrive.

  1. Add soft butter in chunks. Paddle on low for 2 minutes. The mixture should look like coarse damp sand with no visible flour pockets. This is the fat-coating step — do not rush it.
Why reverse-cream?
In the standard creaming method, butter and sugar are aerated together, eggs incorporated, and flour folded in at the end. Flour meets liquid, water hydrates the protein chains glutenin and gliadin, and gluten begins to develop — producing a slightly chewier, domed crumb. In reverse-creaming (also called the high-ratio or "paste" method), butter is paddled into the dry mix first, glazing each starch granule and protein chain with fat. When liquid arrives, water cannot penetrate the fat coating to reach the gluten precursors. Gluten development is suppressed almost entirely. The result is the tightest, most velvet crumb available to a creamed-butter cake, and — crucially — a level top. No dome to trim before assembly.
— Stella Parks, BraveTart, 2017, “High-Ratio Cakes”; Modernist Cuisine vol. 4 on gluten shortening via fat-coating
  1. Beat eggs + yogurt + vanilla in a jug. Whisk smooth, no streaks of white.
  2. Add half the egg mix to the bowl. Paddle on medium 30 seconds. The batter transforms from sandy to pale and fluffy in a single stage.
  3. Scrape down, add remaining egg mix in 2 additions. Paddle 20 seconds after each. Final batter ribbons off the paddle in a pale ivory drape.
Why cultured butter + yogurt?
Cultured butter is made from cream that has been fermented by Lactococcus lactis before churning. The bacteria produce diacetyl, the molecule the human nose reads as “buttery.” Sweet-cream butter carries less than 1 part per million of diacetyl; cultured butter, 2–4 ppm. The crumb tastes more of butter without using more butter. The 30g of yogurt does a second job: its lactic acid (pH ~4.4) reacts with the carbonate phase of a double-acting baking powder while the batter is still in the bowl, giving an early lift; the heat-triggered acid phosphate phase finishes the lift in the oven. Two staggered gas releases mean finer, more even alveoli than soda alone.
— Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 33–34 on cultured-dairy aroma; pp. 533–537 on chemical leaveners

The equal-weight ratio (butter : sugar : egg : flour = 1 : 1 : 1 : 1) is the historic British balance, traceable through Mrs Beeton (1861) and Constance Spry (1956). Eggs and flour bring the proteins that set the crumb; sugar and butter interrupt that set. Equal weight is the sweet spot — enough egg protein to lift, enough fat and sugar to keep it tender.

Phase 3: Bake — 22–26 min

  1. Divide batter by weight. Scale each tin to 410g ± 5g. Smooth the surface gently — do not tap the tins. Reverse-cream batter holds its air; tapping deflates it.
  2. Bake at 170°C / 340°F on the same rack, 4 cm apart. 22–26 minutes. Rotate at 18 min only if your oven runs uneven.

Done when:

  • Edges pull from the tin by 2 mm
  • Centre springs back from a fingertip press
  • Internal temperature reaches 96°C (205°F) at the centre
  • Skewer comes out dry, no moist crumbs
  1. Rest in tin 10 minutes. Gluten relaxes, structure sets.
  2. Turn out onto a wire rack, remove parchment, top-side up. Cool fully to room temperature, 45–60 min. Stack only when fully cool, or the second layer will weep on assembly.

Internal 96°C (205°F) is the only reliable doneness test. The visual cues (edge pull, fingertip spring) are reliable secondary checks — but the thermometer is the primary. A pale crumb can still be wet at the centre; a deep colour can hide a raw core.

Phase 4: Whip the Cream — 90 sec, at the last possible moment

  1. Cold equipment. Bowl and whisk into the fridge 15 min ahead. Fat stays solid in the foam.
  2. Whip cream + 15g caster + vanilla. Start low, climb to medium-high. 90 sec to soft peaks: a trail holds for 2 sec, the tip flops over.
Why soft peaks, why now?
Whipping cream introduces air bubbles into a fat-in-water emulsion. The fat globules clump partially around the air bubbles, stabilising them. At soft-peak, the structure is reversible — the foam holds 12–18 hours refrigerated before weeping. Past soft-peak, the fat globules clump irreversibly toward butter, and there is no path back. The 6% sugar (15g per 250g cream) stabilises the foam without pushing it into Chantilly sweetness that would fight the jam. Whipping at the last possible moment is non-negotiable: a foam built three hours ahead will weep onto the bottom sponge during service, no matter how well it was made.
— Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 32–33 on whipped-cream stability

If in doubt, under-whip and finish with 4–5 hand-whisk strokes. Once cream is over-whipped it is butter, and butter cannot be turned back into cream. There is no rescue.

Phase 5: Assemble + Finish — 3 min

  1. Choose the prettier sponge for the top, both flat-sides up on the work surface.
  2. Spread 200g jam on the bottom sponge. Leave a 1 cm jam-free border — the cream will push the jam outward when crowned.
  3. Pile cream on the jam. Spread to a slight dome in the centre. The jam border should still be visible.
  4. Crown with the second sponge, top-side up. Press down 2 mm — no more. Cream should kiss the edge of the cake without weeping.
  5. Dust caster sugar through a fine sieve. Even 1–2 mm layer, top only. The cake reads “Victoria” the second the sugar lands.
Why caster sugar, not icing sugar?
Caster sugar reads as visual sparkle — translucent crystals catching light against the matte gold of the crumb. Icing sugar reads as a domestic fondant-finish, dissolving into the surface within 30 minutes and pulling the top of the cake into a slightly tacky white sheen. Caster holds visually for 48 hours. It is the Mary Berry / Women’s Institute tell — the visual signature of a Victoria sponge made by someone who knows. Icing sugar dust belongs on a Genoise, on a Madeira, on a fairy cake. Not on this.
— Mary Berry, Baking Bible; Women’s Institute canon

Service window: Eat within 2 hours of assembly. Past 4 hours in the fridge the sponge dries; past 8 hours the cream weeps and the jam bleeds into the bottom layer. Charter rhythm: assemble at 16:00, plate at 16:30.

Charter Prep & Storage

The cream is the limiting factor. Cream cannot be made ahead, and it does not survive sea state 3 — the whisk loses its line and the foam clumps to butter. Whip cream at anchor only, in the 5 minutes before service. On a passage day, skip the cream and use 60g extra jam instead.
