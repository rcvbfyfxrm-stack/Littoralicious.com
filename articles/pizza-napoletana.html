Pizza Napoletana: Poolish, 48h Cold Ferment, Broiler Finish

The recipe you actually want to read

A 16-hour poolish for aroma compounds straight flour ferments will never give you. Forty-eight hours cold for protease-driven extensibility and a deeper crust. Sixty-five percent hydration for a home steel under the broiler, sixty for a wood-fired deck at 450°C. Three sourced modifications. Six panetti. Six pizzas at the pass.

Pizza Napoletana is the most legislated bread in Europe. The AVPN STG specification reads like a charter contract: Caputo 00, weight per panetto, hydration window, oven floor temperature, bake time, dome shape. Most of it is correct. What it doesn’t tell you is which decisions inside the box matter most when you’re working at home or on a boat, away from a 450°C wood-fired deck. This is that conversation, with the sources. The pizzaiolo’s slap of the dough is a hundred years old. The chemistry that makes it work is older. Both belong to the same evening.

Three modifications carry this version: a 16-hour poolish at 18°C for aroma compounds, a 24–48h cold bulk for protease-driven extensibility and crust colour, and a hydration that tracks the oven temperature inversely (65% for home steel + broiler, 60% for wood-fired). Caputo Cuoco 00 (W~310) takes all three without collapse.

Method

Phase 1: Poolish — 16h ahead, evening before bake day

  1. Dissolve the yeast. Whisk 0.3g instant dry yeast into 300g water at 18°C until the mix clouds, no clumps visible.
  2. Add the flour. Tip in 300g Caputo, whisk to a smooth batter, no dry lumps. Cover and stand 16 hours at 18°C.
Why a poolish, why 16 hours, why 18°C
A 100%-hydration pre-ferment is a wet sponge. Wet environments favour yeast metabolism over lactic acid bacteria, which means the aroma compounds that develop are clean fruity esters — ethyl acetate (pear), ethyl lactate, organic acids in low concentrations — rather than the sour profile of a stiff biga or sourdough. Sixteen hours at 18°C is the sweet spot: long enough for the yeast to complete its main reproductive cycle and pump out volatiles, short enough that aspartic proteases haven’t yet degraded the gluten in the pre-ferment. Past 18 hours, the dough turns slack and the bake suffers. This is why every serious Italian working dough — from Calvel-trained French boulangers to Naples pizzaioli — uses a poolish or a biga rather than a straight dough.
— Raymond Calvel, Le Goût du Pain, 1990; Modernist Bread vol. 2, “Pre-ferments”

Weigh the yeast on a 0.1g scale. Eyeballing 0.3g of IDY is the difference between a poolish at 16 hours and a poolish that’s collapsed by 10. There is no acceptable visual measure for sub-gram yeast.

Phase 2: Bulk Dough — 30 min mix + 2h bulk rise

  1. Combine ripe poolish + 285g cold water (12°C). Break the poolish into the liquid with your hand, dissolve to a milky suspension. Reserve 15g of water for the salt step.
  2. Add 600g flour. Fold by hand 3 minutes (or low speed on a mixer) until you have a shaggy mass, no dry flour visible. Cover. Autolyse 20 minutes.
Why autolyse, why Caputo Cuoco
Autolyse — flour and water alone, no salt, no yeast added yet — lets proteases and amylases begin work in a low-resistance environment. Glutenin and gliadin chains start linking; starches absorb water. After twenty minutes, kneading time drops by roughly a third and the final dough develops a thinner, more extensible windowpane. Caputo Cuoco is graded W~300–320 (work index): enough strength to survive 48 hours refrigerated without collapse, but with a P/L ratio (tenacity over extensibility) around 0.55, which means the disc opens by hand without snapping back. Bread flour at W~240 will tear; high-gluten 00 (W>360) will resist your fingertips.
— AVPN STG (EU Reg. 97/2010); Tony Gemignani, The Pizza Bible; Modernist Bread vol. 2 on W-index and autolyse
  1. Add 27g salt + 15g reserve water. Pinch the salt through the dough, fold-and-press 8 minutes by hand or 6 minutes on low speed. The dough should pull clean from the bowl and pass the windowpane test — pinch a small piece, stretch: translucent, no tearing.
  2. Final dough temperature check. Target 22–24°C. If hotter, rest the dough 10 minutes in the fridge before bulk rise. Hot kneaded dough overproofs in bulk.
  3. Bulk rise 2 hours at 22°C, covered in an oiled tub. Volume rises roughly 60%; surface goes taut; a finger-poke springs back slowly.
Why 3% salt, why added late
Sodium chloride above 2% inhibits yeast activity linearly — salt at the start of mixing slows fermentation throughout. By adding it after autolyse, you get the gluten-strengthening effect (chloride ions shield negatively-charged glutenin chains, allowing tighter cross-links) without paying the metabolic tax during the early dough development. Three percent is the AVPN floor and the perceptual minimum: below 2.5%, the cornicione reads flat on the palate even when everything else is right. This is the difference between a competent pizza and a memorable one.
— AVPN STG; Modernist Bread vol. 2, “Salt and Dough”

Phase 3: Divide, Shape, Cold Ferment — 24–48 hours

  1. Divide into 6 × 250g panetti. Pre-shape loose rounds, bench rest 15 minutes uncovered so a light skin (pellicle) forms.
  2. Final shape: tight rounds. Pinch the base, seam down, no flour on top. A tight skin holds the gas through the cold phase.
  3. Stack in an oiled box with 2–3 cm between panetti. Single layer only. Lid, fridge.
  4. Cold ferment 24–48 hours at 4°C. Twenty-four hours: clean wheat with yeast warmth. Forty-eight hours: pronounced butter, cream, light hazelnut.
Why 48 hours cold
Below 6°C, yeast cellular activity drops to roughly 5% of its room-temperature rate — the dough effectively stops rising. But aspartic proteases keep working at refrigerator temperatures, breaking peptide bonds in the gluten matrix and releasing free amino acids. Those amino acids are Maillard precursors: more of them means deeper crust colour and more developed flavour at the same bake temperature. The trade-off is structural — protease activity also degrades the gluten network, which is why you cap the cold phase at 48–72 hours. Past that window, panetti turn slack and tear on opening. AVPN technically allows 24h; working pizzaioli routinely push 48–72h for exactly this flavour gain.
— Modernist Bread vol. 2, “Long Cold Ferments”; AVPN STG specification

If your fridge runs colder than 4°C (most do, especially in the door), panetti can drop into a quasi-frozen state where proteases stall and you lose the flavour benefit. Test your fridge with a probe; the panetti box wants the warmest shelf, never the bottom or the door.

Phase 4: Final Proof + Open the Disc — 2 hours + 60 sec each

  1. Pull panetti from fridge 2 hours before bake. They go from cold-firm to slack; a finger-poke leaves a slow-return dimple. Underproofed = ball stays tight, tears on opening. Overproofed = pancake, no spring in the oven.
  2. Flour the bench heavily with semola rimacinata. Lay the panetto on the flour, never the reverse. Press centre-out with fingertips, leaving a 1.5–2 cm cornicione untouched.
  3. Lift, drape over your fists, gently stretch to 28–30 cm. Centre should be translucent against light; cornicione stays plump. Land on a semola-floured wood peel.

Checkpoint: Past the peel, you have about 30 seconds before the dough starts sticking. Sauce, cheese, oil, salt — one motion, no second-guessing. If it sticks, scrape and restart; a stuck pizza never recovers.

Phase 5: Bake — the 90 seconds that decide everything

  1. Preheat the steel. Top rack, 290°C / 555°F, minimum 45 minutes. Switch to broiler high 10 minutes before launch. Verify 280–290°C on the steel surface with an IR thermometer — the oven cavity reads hotter than the steel until it’s fully saturated.
  2. Build on the peel. 80g hand-crushed San Marzano spread in a spiral, leaving the 2 cm rim bare. 70g torn drained fior di latte scattered — not piled. 5g EVOO drizzled. Pinch of salt, dusting of grated pecorino on the tomato.
  3. Launch. A single confident peel jerk. The disc lands centred on the steel.
Why 65% hydration for home, 60% for wood-fired
Hydration and oven temperature trade off inversely. At 450°C in a wood-fired oven, the bake is over in 60–90 seconds — a wet dough at 65% will reach the surface temperature where moisture flashes off (~100°C) and cross into Maillard browning (~140°C) almost simultaneously, leaving the centre too damp. Drop to 60% and the moisture profile aligns with the bake clock: enough steam for oven-spring, dry enough to set a crust. At 280–290°C on a home steel under a broiler, the bake takes 90–120 seconds; the extra time lets a 65% dough finish drying without going leathery. Run the math wrong and you either get a cracker (too dry, oven too cool) or a soggy centre (too wet, oven too hot). The pairing is non-negotiable.
— Modernist Bread vol. 3, “Pizza”, hydration-vs-deck-temp tables; Tony Gemignani, The Pizza Bible
  1. Bake 90–120 seconds under broiler. Rotate once at the 45-second mark if the back of the oven runs hot. Cornicione blistered, top of the cheese pooled but not browned.

Done when:

  • Cornicione has risen 2–3 cm and shows uneven blister bubbles
  • Underside (lift with peel and peek) shows leopard spotting — charred dark spots on a golden field
  • Cheese has melted into pools, not browned into a sheet
  • Total bake time at the 90–120 sec mark for home steel, 60–90 sec for wood-fired
  1. Out: basil + finishing EVOO. Three fresh leaves on the hot disc, 5g raw oil drizzle. The residual heat releases the basil volatiles without burning them.
  2. Rest on a wire rack 45 seconds before plating. The underside dumps 30–40% of its residual steam — no soggy centre.
  3. Eat within 60 seconds. Neapolitan pizzas die at room temperature.

The leopard spots are pyrolysis, not Maillard. At 200°C+ surface temperature, the cornicione browns through Maillard chemistry; at the points where the dough touches the hot steel, surface compounds char (pyrolysis) into dark spots. Both reactions need a fast bake at high heat. At <280°C steel, neither happens fully — you get a tan crust instead of leopard skin.

Charter Prep & Storage

Baked Neapolitan pizza does not hold. The only acceptable boat compromise: par-bake 60 seconds, freeze flat, finish 4 minutes from frozen at 290°C. Anything else turns to leather within an hour.
