Sourdough at Sea: The Science-First Loaf

An open, blistered crumb and a deep mahogany crust — raised on a jar of flour and water you keep alive yourself. The wild loaf made reliable on a moving galley, where the longest step is the fridge doing the work, and the acidity that flavours it also makes it keep.

Cut into a good sourdough and the crust should crack like lacquer over an open, glossy, irregular crumb — not the tight, sour, doorstop most first loaves turn out. The fix is not more starter, and not more proof. It is reading the dough instead of the clock, and letting a long cold rest do the work no schedule can rush.

The one idea: sourdough is raised by a living culture you keep, not a packet you open — wild yeast for the lift, lactic bacteria for the flavour and the keeping. Master three things, in order, and the loaf is reliable anywhere: a ripe levain, a judged bulk ferment, and a long cold proof.

Where it comes from

Sourdough is simply the oldest way to raise bread. Wild yeast and bacteria living in nothing but flour and water were the only leaven there was until industrial baker’s yeast arrived in the 1860s — Egyptian bakers were lifting loaves on wild ferments well over three thousand years ago, and every culture with grain found its own version, from Ethiopian injera to German rye.

California gave it a name and a fame. The 1849 Gold Rush carried starters west, San Francisco’s cool damp fog suited the culture, and the bacterium that gives that city’s loaves their tang was later isolated and named for it. So a sourdough is less a recipe than a population you keep alive — and a working chef’s is a population that has travelled, passed hand to hand down the dock.

The Gold Rush prospectors were themselves nicknamed “sourdoughs.” Through the Klondike winter a frozen starter meant a winter without bread, so a seasoned miner guarded his culture by carrying the crock against his body — taking it to bed to keep it from freezing. The name came to mean a grizzled old-timer, against the green cheechako who hadn’t yet wintered over. A jar of flour and water, kept warm against the body, as the badge of someone who’d lasted. — Klondike & Alaska usage; Robert Service

The prospector and the fog are the romance. What makes the loaf reliable — and what keeps it edible days from the next shop — is one piece of chemistry you can taste.

A starter is a stable truce between wild yeast and lactic-acid bacteria — roughly a hundred bacteria for every yeast cell. The yeast makes the carbon dioxide that lifts the loaf; the bacteria make lactic and acetic acid that give it the tang and drop the crumb to about pH 4 — acidic enough to hold off the moulds and staling that take a yeasted loaf in a day or two. The sourness and the keeping quality are the same thing — which is exactly why this is the bread to bake when the next provisioning stop is a week away.

Shaping & baking

A starter is just flour and water gone wild — mix equal weights, leave it loosely covered in a warm corner, and feed it 1 part old to 5 flour to 5 water once a day; in five to seven days it doubles between feeds and smells of yoghurt and apples. The catch on a boat is the water. Chlorine and chloramine from shore mains, and the stripped water-maker product, can stall a young culture — feed it bottled spring water, or leave tap water uncovered overnight to off-gas. A warm galley (26–30°C) ferments fast, so feed more often or use cooler water; a cold provisioning fridge holds it dormant for a week or more between feeds. If yours dies on a crossing, the dock will have another — the culture is a commons. (To carry one through a long passage or a flight, see Charter prep below.)

Method

0/0 steps done

  1. Everything downstream depends on catching the levain at its peak — too young it won’t lift, too old it turns sharp and slack.

    Build the levain. The night before, mix 20 g mature starter, 100 g flour and 100 g water. Cover; leave 4–8 h at room temp until doubled, domed and a spoonful floats in water.

    Why
    A purpose-built levain gives you a known, vigorous population at a known time — far more reliable than feeding the mother and hoping. The float test reads dissolved gas: enough lift to raise the loaf. — Robertson, Tartine Bread; Modernist Bread

  2. Bakers call this rest the autolyse — flour and water getting acquainted before any work begins.

    Autolyse. Mix the 800 g bread flour and 100 g wholemeal with the 600 g water to a shaggy mass — no levain, no salt yet. Cover; rest 45 min.

    Why
    Flour hydrates and enzymes begin freeing sugars while the gluten knits itself with no work from you; salt and acid added now would tighten it too early. — McGee 2004; López-Alt

  3. Now the two held-back ingredients go in — the life, then the seasoning.

    Add levain, then salt. Pinch in the ~200 g levain until absorbed. Dissolve the 20 g salt in the reserved 50 g water and work that through too.

    Why
    Dissolving the salt first stops gritty hot-spots and distributes it evenly; salt tightens the gluten and reins in the yeast to a pace the long proof can ride. — Forkish, Flour Water Salt Yeast

  4. At this hydration you don’t knead — the dough is too slack to hold a shape. You fold.

    Bulk ferment, 4 folds. 24–26°C, ~4–5 h. In the first 2 h do four sets of stretch-and-folds (wet hands; lift, stretch, tuck, rotate) at 30-min spacing. Stop when the dough is risen ~40%, domed and jiggly, with bubbles at the edges.

    Why
    Folding builds aligned gluten that traps big irregular bubbles — the open crumb — without degassing it. Judge by rise and feel, never the clock: a warm galley can halve the time. — Robertson; Modernist Bread

  5. Two stages: a loose pre-shape to build skin, a rest, then the real shape with tension.

    Pre-shape & shape. Tip out, cut in two, pre-shape each into a loose round. Bench-rest 25 min, then shape into taut boules and set seam-up in rice-floured bannetons (or tea-towel-lined bowls).

    Why
    The rest relaxes gluten so the final shape doesn’t tear; a tight outer skin holds the loaf up through the cold proof and the bake. Rice flour has no gluten of its own, so it won’t glue the dough to the cloth. — Forkish; Robertson

  6. This is the step that does the most while you do nothing — flavour, and a galley schedule you can actually keep.

    Cold proof 12–36 h. Bag the bannetons and straight into the fridge at 4°C. Bake any time from the next morning to a day and a half later — straight from cold, no warm-up.

    Why
    At 4°C the yeast crawls while the bacteria keep laying down acid — deeper flavour, blistered crust, and a firm cold dough that scores cleanly. It also uncouples the bake from the proof, so you fire loaves to order across a charter. — Modernist Bread; McGee

  7. The home-oven secret is to trap the loaf’s own steam — a lidded pot is a steam-injected deck oven you already own.

    Bake covered, then bare. Preheat a Dutch oven (or combi) to 250°C (480°F). Turn the cold loaf out, score one confident slash, lid on 20 min; then lid off, drop to 230°C, ~20–25 min to a deep mahogany crust, 96–99°C internal.

    Why
    Trapped steam keeps the crust soft long enough for a full oven-spring, then gelatinises the surface starch into a glassy shell; uncovering it lets Maillard browning build colour and flavour. The crumb only truly sets near 96°C — pull it pale and it bakes gummy. — McGee 2004; Modernist Bread

  8. The hardest step on a hungry boat: walk away.

    Cool before cutting. Onto a rack, and leave it at least 2 h — the crumb is still cooking as it cools. Cut it hot and it gums to the knife.

The first loaf is best plain — torn warm with cold butter and flaky salt, or under a slick of good oil. After that it is the most useful thing in the galley: the raft under a crew brunch of eggs and avocado, the base for bruschetta with the day’s tomatoes, the toast for a guest’s smoked-salmon canapé. Three days on, when the crust has gone leathery, it is better than ever — griddled for panzanella, blitzed to crumbs, or cubed for a ribollita that hides a multitude of leftovers.

Take it further
Higher hydrationPush the water to 80–85% for a wilder, holier crumb — once your folds are confident and the swell is kind
Country loaf (pain de campagne)Take the wholemeal/rye up to 20–30% of the flour — earthier, faster-fermenting; add 3–4% more water
SeededSoak 150 g mixed seeds in water until plump, work in after the salt — a soaker, or they steal moisture from the crumb
Olive & rosemaryLaminate 150 g chopped Taggiasca olives and rosemary in during the folds — the Ligurian table loaf
Bâtard / báguetteShape long instead of round for more crust per slice and easier crew sandwiches
Tin loafSlack or unsure of your shaping? Proof in an oiled tin and bake covered with foil — sandwich bread for the week, no banneton needed
Stiffer levain for less sourBuild the levain at 60% hydration (60 g water to 100 g flour) for a milder, sweeter loaf — favours yeast over acid
One starter, more than bread — the discard

Every feed leaves a spoon of unfed starter behind. Don’t bin it — cold in a jar it keeps a week or two and turns into half the snacks on the boat, with the same tang for none of the wait.

CrackersSpread discard thin with oil, salt and seeds, bake 180°C until crisp, snap into shards — the canapé base that costs nothing
CrumpetsLoosen discard with a pinch of soda and bake in rings on a flat-top — holey, chewy, ready for butter
Pancakes / blinisFold discard into a batter and griddle — crew breakfast, or topped with crème fraîche and roe for guests
Pizza & focacciaSwap discard for some of the flour and water in a doughs and let it slow-rise — see the focaccia blueprint
Troubleshooting
Dense, tight crumbLevain not ripe, or under-proofed → build to a floating peak; let the bulk reach a ~40% domed rise before you shape
Flat, spreads on the peelOver-proofed or weak shaping → catch the bulk earlier, shape with real tension, and keep the cold proof under 36 h
Gummy, sticky centrePulled on colour, not doneness → bake to 96–99°C internal and cool at least 2 h before cutting
Too sourLevain too old or fridge too warm → use it younger, build it stiffer (60% water), and verify the fridge is at 4°C
No sour at allProof too short or too warm → give it the full cold days; acid builds in the fridge, not the heat
Pale, no oven-springPot or oven not hot enough → preheat to a true 250°C, score deeper, keep the lid on the first 20 min
Starter sluggishChlorinated or water-maker water, or fed too cold → switch to bottled water and a warm corner; it bounces back in a feed or two
Charter prep & storage

The cold proof is the make-ahead — it works for you while you run service. Mix on a provisioning day and you bake fresh loaves to order across a whole charter leg, no fresh dough each morning.

Bake to orderHold shaped loaves up to 36 h at 4°C; fire one or two straight from cold whenever the table needs bread
Par-bakeBake covered to pale (about 25 min), cool, then finish 10–12 min at 230°C at service — hot crust on demand
Freeze it bakedCool fully, wrap well, up to 1 month; refresh 10 min at 180°C from frozen — the acidity means it freezes and revives better than yeasted bread
Keeps without the fridgeCut-face down on a board, wrapped in cloth, 3–4 days at room temp — never the fridge, which stales bread fastest
Carry the culture — dehydrateSmear ripe starter paper-thin on parchment, dry below 40°C (dehydrator, low oven, or a shaded deck), flake and vacuum-seal. It survives months and flights in a pocket; revive with two or three feeds. The insurance policy against a crossing crew who bins your jar
On passageThe slack, high-hydration dough is forgiving of swell — it wants an even fold, not careful shaping; proof in the steadiest fridge and bake in port or a calm watch

Once the ratio is yours — flour 100, water 75, levain 20, salt 2 — the loaf is yours to drive: more wholemeal for an earthier crumb, a longer cold proof for more tang, a tin and a foil lid when the shaping or the sea won’t cooperate. And the part you can’t provision — the culture itself — you keep alive and you pass on. That is the whole tradition: every starter on every dock is a cutting of an older one, kept warm against the body through one more winter, one more crossing. When the sea kills yours, someone two boats down will spare you a spoonful. Bake the loaf, and pass the jar on.
