The Crew Quiche: The Science-First Version

Silky yet sliceable, never soggy, never weeping. Ten portions from one half gastronorm — cut clean and held across a watch.

A quiche fails in three places, none of them flavour: a scrambled, weeping custard, a wet base, a slice that collapses. Fix those and the garnish is almost incidental.

Where it comes from

For all its French reputation, quiche is a German word — from Kuchen, cake — a relic of Lorraine’s centuries on the shifting Franco-German border (the region is Lothringen in German). The original 16th-century quiche Lorraine had no cheese at all: just smoked bacon and a simple custard of egg and cream, the migaine, poured into bread dough rather than pastry. The Gruyère and the shortcrust came later. What never changed is the principle — a savoury custard, barely set.

The three moves: (1) a weighed appareil with extra yolks, strained — silky yet sliceable; (2) a blind-baked, egg-washed shell — a base that stays crisp; (3) a low 150°C bake pulled at a jiggle — no curdle, no weep.

The ratio — memorise, rescale anything
Per 1 L dairy → 8 whole eggs + 2 yolks, dairy 60% milk / 40% cream, salt ~1%. Push to 6 eggs/L for softer, 12 eggs/L for a firm transport quiche. The extra yolks are what keep it tender, not rubbery.
Scale 10 portions · 1/2 GN

Ingredients

Appareil (~2 L)
Garnish (cook & drain)
Pâte brisée (or sub frozen all-butter shortcrust)

Method

0/0 steps done

  1. Blind-bake the shell. Line the oiled 1/2 GN, dock, rest cold. Bake 180°C with beans 20 min, then 8–10 min till dry and pale gold. (Faster: thaw frozen all-butter shortcrust and skip the dough.)

  2. Seal it. Brush the hot inside with beaten egg, back in 2 min.

    Why
    The egg coagulates into a waterproof film over the dried base — the single fix for the soggy bottom under wet custard. — López-Alt; Keller

  3. Garnish — cook & drain hard. Render the bacon; sweat onions in a little of the fat until sweet and dry. Drain both.

    Why
    Every gram of water in the garnish leaches into the custard and stops it setting — a layer of weep. Drain harder than feels right. — McGee 2004

  4. Appareil. Whisk 12 eggs + 3 yolks + 800 g milk + 600 g cream + seasoning — blend, don’t aerate — then strain and skim.

    Why
    Yolks (lecithin + fat) set lower and softer than white → silk without rubber. Straining removes chalazae and air for an even set. — McGee; Keller

  5. Build & fill. Half the cheese on the base, then bacon + onion, then the rest. Set the pan on the rack first, then pour the appareil to ~5 mm below the rim.

  6. Bake low — 150°C (300°F), 45–55 min. Pull when the edges are set but the centre jiggles like soft jelly (73–75°C). Rest 20–30 min, then cut 2×5.

    Why
    Egg sets ~65→85°C; hotter and the proteins seize and squeeze out water — the grainy, weeping quiche. Low and slow is the only clean set; carryover finishes the middle as it rests. — McGee; Modernist Cuisine

Troubleshooting
Soggy bottomShell under-baked / unsealed → fully blind-bake + egg-wash + cheese on base
Grainy, weepingToo hot / too long → 150°C only, pull at the jiggle / 75°C
Won’t setToo much dairy or wet garnish → weigh to ratio, drain hard
Rubbery sliceToo many whole eggs, no yolks → hold 8+2 per litre
Charter prep & storage

Blind shell holds 1 day; cooked garnish 2 days; strained appareil 1 day. Excellent at room temp; reheat 150°C / 12–15 min — ideal for staggered crew meals. Baked quiche keeps 3 days fridge, freezes 1 month.

Sources: McGee, On Food and Cooking; Keller, Bouchon; López-Alt, Serious Eats; Modernist Cuisine; Larousse Gastronomique.
