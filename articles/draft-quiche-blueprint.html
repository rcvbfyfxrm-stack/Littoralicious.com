The Crew Quiche: The Science-First Version

The recipe you actually want to read

The appareil ratio that sets silky yet sliceable. A blind-baked, egg-washed shell that never goes soggy. A low, gentle bake pulled at a jiggle. Ten crew portions from one half gastronorm pan, cut clean and held across a watch. Three modifications. Five sources.

A quiche fails in three predictable places, and none of them is the flavour. The custard scrambles into a grainy, weeping sponge because it was baked too hot. The base turns to wet pastry because raw custard sat on raw dough. And the slice collapses because the egg-to-dairy ratio was guessed rather than weighed. Fix those three structurally and the garnish — bacon, onion, cheese — is almost incidental. This is the version built for a galley: scaled to a half gastronorm, sturdy enough to cut into ten clean squares, and good warm, at room temperature, or reheated on a staggered crew mealtime.

Three modifications separate this from every other quiche: a weighed appareil ratio with extra yolks then strained (silky yet sliceable), a blind-baked shell sealed with egg wash (a base that stays crisp under wet custard), and a low 150°C bake pulled at a jiggle (gentle coagulation, no curdle, no weep). All three are grounded in food science with named sources.

Key technique: Weigh the appareil to ratio and strain it; fully blind-bake and egg-wash-seal the shell; bake low and pull at a centre jiggle

The appareil formula — memorise this, rescale anything: per 1 L dairy, use 8 whole eggs + 2 extra yolks, dairy split 60% whole milk / 40% double cream, salt at ~1% of total weight. That sets sliceable but silky. Push to 6 eggs/L for a softer custard, 12 eggs/L for a firm transport quiche.

Method

Phase 1: Shell — Blind Bake & Seal — 45 min incl. rest

  1. Make the brisée. Rub or pulse cold butter into flour + salt to coarse crumbs. Add egg and just enough ice water to bring it together — do not overwork. Flatten, wrap, rest cold 30 minutes.
  2. Line the pan. Roll to ~3 mm, lift into the oiled 1/2 GN, press into the corners and up the sides leaving a slight overhang. Dock the base with a fork. Rest cold another 20 minutes — this sets the gluten and limits shrinkage.
  3. Blind bake. Line with paper and fill with beans. Bake 180°C (350°F) for 20 minutes; lift out the beans and bake 8–10 minutes more until the base is dry and pale gold.
  4. Seal it. Brush the hot inside with beaten egg and return for 2 minutes to set a waterproof film. Trim the overhang.
Why fully blind-bake, then egg-wash?
Pastry cooks by drying out; a raw custard poured onto raw dough keeps that dough below the temperature it needs to crisp, and the result is the classic soggy bottom. Blind-baking drives off the base moisture first. The egg wash then does what the custard would otherwise do badly — it coagulates into a thin sealed film that waterproofs the pastry against the wet appareil for the long, low second bake. This single step is the most reliable fix for the most common quiche failure.
— J. Kenji López-Alt, Serious Eats, blind-baking and soggy-bottom mechanics; Thomas Keller, Bouchon, 2004

Phase 2: Garnish — Cook & Drain — 20 min, runs during the blind bake

  1. Render the bacon. Cook lardons until crisp and the fat is released. Lift out, drain on paper. Keep a spoon of the fat.
  2. Sweat the onions. Soften the sliced onion or leek slowly in the reserved fat until sweet and dry — no liquid left in the pan. Drain, cool, fold through the herbs.

Drain harder than feels necessary. Every gram of water the bacon fat or the onions carry into the shell will leach into the custard and stop it setting cleanly — you get a layer of weep under the surface. The garnish should go in barely moist, never wet.

Phase 3: The Appareil — 10 min

  1. Whisk. Combine 12 eggs, 3 yolks, 800g milk, 600g cream, salt, nutmeg, white pepper, and Dijon. Whisk just until uniform — you want it blended, not aerated.
  2. Strain. Pass through a fine sieve into a jug and skim any foam off the top.
The ratio, the extra yolks, and why you strain
Texture is governed by the ratio of egg to dairy, not by the brand of cream. Too much egg and the custard sets firm and rubbery; too little and it never holds a slice. The working figure of 8 whole eggs plus 2 yolks per litre of dairy lands on sliceable-but-silky — and the extra yolks matter: yolk is rich in lecithin and fat, so it sets at a lower temperature and far more tenderly than white, buying silk without rubber. Straining removes the chalazae and the whisked-in air that would otherwise leave streaks and bubbles in an even set.
— Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004 (egg proteins and custards); Thomas Keller, Bouchon, 2004 (quiche ratio)

Phase 4: Build & Bake Low — 45–55 min

  1. Layer. Scatter half the cheese over the sealed base, then the bacon and onion, then the remaining cheese. The cheese on the bottom protects the seal a second time.
  2. Fill. Set the pan on the oven rack first, then pour the appareil to ~5 mm below the rim. Filling in place avoids carrying a brimful pan.
  3. Bake at 150°C (300°F). Low and slow — 45–55 minutes for this depth.
Why 150°C and not hotter?
Egg proteins begin to coagulate around 65°C and a custard sets fully near 80–85°C. Above that, the proteins contract violently and squeeze out the water they were holding — the curdled, grainy, weeping texture of an over-baked quiche. A 150°C oven keeps the custard climbing gently through the set window so the proteins form a smooth, water-holding gel instead of seizing. Hot ovens also soufflé the custard, which then deflates and cracks on cooling. Low and slow is not caution; it is the only way to a clean set.
— Harold McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004; Modernist Cuisine, egg coagulation temperatures

Done when:

  • Edges are set but the centre still jiggles like soft jelly when you nudge the pan
  • Internal temperature at the centre reads 73–75°C (163–167°F)
  • The top is barely coloured — a pale gold, not browned
  • A thin knife at the edge comes out clean; the middle will firm on resting

Phase 5: Rest & Cut — 20–30 min

  1. Rest 20–30 minutes. Carryover takes the centre to ~80°C and the custard firms to a clean, sliceable set. Cutting hot gives you a smeared, runny edge.
  2. Cut 2 × 5. Ten squares of roughly 13 cm. Serve warm, not oven-hot.

The centre jiggle is the doneness cue, backed by the thermometer. A quiche baked until the middle is firm in the oven is already over-baked — it sets the rest of the way as it rests. Pull it wobbly at 75°C (167°F).

Charter Prep & Storage

Crustless crew version: butter the GN, dust with Parmesan and fine breadcrumb, and build straight in — no pastry. Faster, lighter, easier to portion at sea, and it skips the only fiddly step. The appareil and bake are identical.
