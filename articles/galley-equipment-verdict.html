The Equipment Verdict: What Survives a Season at Sea

The galley runs on a handful of machines, and the fleet has hard opinions earned at sea. The gear that survives a season, the gear that dies on arrival day, and the one rule that keeps you cooking when a unit fails mid-charter.

What you'll get
  • The fleet's accumulated verdict on the machines that matter — what to buy and what to avoid.
  • The marine failure modes the marketing never mentions: voltage, vibration, salt and voided warranties.
  • The one rule that keeps you cooking when a unit dies mid-charter, with sorbet on the owner's list.

The superyacht fleet is the most brutal, unpaid test lab in the kitchen-equipment trade. A machine that lives a quiet decade in a shoreside restaurant gets a few months at sea — salt air, constant vibration, swell, a cramped install with no ventilation, and a charter schedule that does not forgive a failure. The showroom never tells you any of this. The chef groups do. What follows is the consensus they have argued their way to over a season of threads and polls.

Trust the field verdict over the spec sheet. The fleet does not care what a machine can do in a demo kitchen — it cares what still works after three months at sea, and whether a deckhand can swap a fuse when it doesn't. Buy for serviceability, not features.

The Combi

The combi oven is the heart of the galley

Nothing else earns this much argument, because nothing else does this much work. A good combi oven proofs, steams, roasts and holds — and a bad one poisons every service for a season. The consensus here is unusually hard. One informal group poll on a single head-to-head ran 33 to 0. When a fleet that agrees on nothing agrees on this, you listen.

The Induction Problem

The hob that cuts out mid-service

Here is a failure the brochure will never describe. An induction hob — often Miele — gets sealed into a worktop directly above an un-ventilated oven. Mid-service, with both running, the hob soaks up the oven's heat, hits its own thermal limit, and shuts itself down. Not broken. Just too hot to keep going, at the worst possible moment.

The cause is the install, not the appliance. Induction needs to shed heat from underneath. Trap it over a hot oven with no airflow and it will protect itself by switching off — every time the galley gets busy, which is exactly when you cannot afford it.

The group's field fixes, in ascending order of desperation: perforated risers to lift the hob and let air move; drilled vent holes through the surrounding joinery; and, when all else fails, an engineer's computer fan wedged beside the unit to blow the heat away. The deeper cause runs under all three — galleys designed by people who have never cooked a service in one.

Blenders & Processors

No machine to rule them all

This is where the fleet stops agreeing and starts feuding. The blender-and-processor question has no single answer, and the chefs who pretend it does tend to own the machine they are defending. The governing line, worth quoting in full:

"There is no such thing as one machine to rule them all."

Which is why most serious galleys run a Magimix and a Thermomix and a Vitamix — not one of them, all of them, each for the job it actually does well.

Ice & Paco

The frozen-purée question

The Pacojet inspires something close to worship — and then the Pacojet 4 turns up with failures reported in the field, which the maker is said to blame on "user error". The fleet's pragmatic fallback is the Ninja Creami, accepted as a budget stand-in and affectionately filed as "a temu Paco", on the strict understanding that you spin it once and do not push your luck.

The one-spin rule. The Creami earns its place only if you treat it as exactly what it is — a cheaper machine doing a single pass. Run it back for a second spin and you are gambling with the motor mid-charter.

The One Rule

Never buy the combo unit

If you take one buying rule from the whole fleet, take this one. Never buy the combined washer-dryer, the oven-and-cooktop-in-one, the single box that does two jobs. Buy two separate machines instead. The logic is not about performance — it is about what happens when something dies.

One machine, one job. When a standalone unit fails mid-charter, the other one is still standing — your backup is built in. A combo unit takes both functions down at once, and there is no shore to run to.

The stakes are not theoretical. The threads carry the scars:

  • A freezer that died on arrival day — with sorbet sitting on the owner's request list and no time left to source another.
  • An oven whose thermal fuse burned out during its own cleaning cycle, three days before a charter, taking the galley's main heat source with it.

Galley gear dies in ways home gear never does, and it is worth knowing why before you buy. Voltage: Europe runs 220–240V, the US 110V — a wrong-voltage unit is a paperweight or a fire, not a bargain. Install: tight, un-ventilated cabinetry that traps heat. Environment: salt air, constant vibration and the swell working every joint loose. Cover: warranties voided the moment a brand learns the machine is on a vessel. Buy for serviceability and spares, not for the feature list.

The showroom sells features. The sea tests survival. When the two disagree — and they will — the fleet's hard-won field verdict wins, every time.

The Takeaway

If you remember one thing

Pick machines a deckhand could swap a fuse on. Carry spares for the one unit that cannot fail. Never buy the combo — two boxes mean a built-in backup when one dies with the owner's order still on the list. And trust the fleet's field verdict over the showroom every single time, because the people who argued it out have already paid for the lesson you are trying to avoid. Tested at sea.
