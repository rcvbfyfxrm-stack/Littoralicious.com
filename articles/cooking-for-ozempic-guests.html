Cooking for the Ozempic Guest

More guests now board on a GLP-1 drug, and a rich tasting menu is exactly the wrong plan. The science of why appetite collapses on Ozempic — and the small, low-fat, protein-first service that keeps them comfortable, and fed.

What you'll get
  • Why a guest on Ozempic feels full after three bites — and it is not your cooking.
  • The galley protocol: small, protein-first, lower-fat plates served more often.
  • How to store the pen, mind the alcohol, and read a barely-touched plate for what it is.

A guest steps aboard who eats almost nothing. The tasting menu you planned for weeks comes back to the galley untouched, plate after plate. It is easy to take that personally. Increasingly, the explanation is pharmacological: the guest is on a GLP-1 drug — Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro — and their appetite has been turned down at the source. Plate the usual charter abundance and you will set beautiful food in front of someone who physically cannot eat it, and may make them unwell doing so.

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) is a gut hormone; semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) mimic it. Two effects reach the galley. First, they slow gastric emptying — food lingers in the stomach far longer, so the guest feels full fast and stays full. Second, they act on appetite centres in the brain to blunt hunger and quiet the constant "food noise". The net result is a much smaller appetite and early satiety. Large, fatty, rich meals then sit heavily and commonly trigger nausea, reflux and discomfort — and because fat itself slows gastric emptying, a buttery, cream-heavy course compounds the drug's own effect. Heavy sugar loads can feel worse too.

The galley protocol. Cook lighter, smaller, more often.

  • Plate small. Tasting-size, not charter-size — let them ask for more rather than face a full plate.
  • Protein-first, lower-fat. Lean proteins, vegetables, lighter sauces; ease off the butter-and-cream courses and the deep-fried.
  • Gentle, not greasy or fiery. Rich and very spicy food commonly provokes nausea.
  • More small plates beat one big service. Several light courses sit better than a single heavy one.
  • Mind the alcohol. It lands harder on a near-empty, slow-emptying stomach.
  • Keep dessert light. It is often skipped entirely — offer fruit or a sorbet rather than a rich plated finish.
At the Table

Never read a barely-touched plate as a verdict on the cooking. The guest who eats two forkfuls and stops is not being polite or difficult — their stomach is genuinely full. It is the drug, not the dish. Adjust the next course down in size and richness, and carry on as if it were the plan all along.

Grilled white fish, poached chicken, prawnsDeep-fried courses, fatty cuts, confit
Vegetables, broths, light saladsCream and butter-heavy sauces
Small, frequent platesOne large multi-course sitting
Fruit, sorbet, a few squares of chocolateRich plated desserts, sticky puddings

The pen. A guest may ask you to store their injector in the fridge — these are typically kept refrigerated at around 2–8°C. Honour the request: label it clearly, keep it separate from food, and treat it as the guest's private medication. This is culinary guidance, not medical advice. Anything about dose, timing, storage specifics or health belongs to the guest and their doctor, not the galley. Your job is comfort and nutrition.

The Reframe

The GLP-1 guest is quietly rewriting charter cooking. For decades the flex was abundance — more courses, more richness, the groaning table. For this guest the flex is the opposite: precision and restraint. Cook less, lighter, and more often, and read the plate honestly. That is not a lesser service. It is a more attentive one.

More guests will board this way each season. The chef who plans for it — smaller plates, leaner courses, a quiet shelf in the fridge for the pen — looks after them properly while everyone else is still wondering why the food keeps coming back. Cook for the appetite in front of you, not the menu you wrote. Tested at sea.
