The Tuna Bloodline

The dark stripe most cooks trim and bin is the most concentrated flavour on the fish. Treat it right and it becomes seasoning, not waste.

What you'll get
  • How to turn the dark bloodline you normally trim off a tuna loin into a concentrated seasoning instead of waste.
  • The control point: get it cold and cut it clean fast, because this is the fastest-spoiling muscle on the fish.
  • The proof — a dark, iron-rich powder or stock that reads as depth, and holds in a warm galley fridge.

The Problem

Trim the bloodline into the bin and you throw away the most flavour-dense muscle on the fish — and if you leave it on the loin, it drags the whole cut down.

Every tuna carries a wedge of dark red-brown muscle along its lateral line — the bloodline, chiai in Japanese. Most cooks do one of two wrong things with it: trim it off and throw it out, or leave it on a loin destined for raw service, where its strong, metallic, fast-oxidising flavour turns the plate. It is the single most concentrated tissue on the animal, and it is treated as offcut.

Leave the bloodline on a loin and it browns first, sours first, and goes fishy first — it is dense with myoglobin and lipid, both of which oxidise fast, and it pulls the surrounding white meat down with it.

The Technique

The Move

Stop treating the bloodline as a defect. Treat it as the fish's own stock cube.

The bloodline is dark because it is working muscle: continuous-swimming fish load it with myoglobin to store oxygen, which also loads it with iron, and with lipid for endurance. That same density is why it tastes strong. The move is to separate it clean, tame the oxidation and the trimethylamine that make it read "off," then concentrate it — cure and dry it to a powder, or simmer it into a dark stock. What replaces brute force here is timing and cold, not more knife.

The bloodline is not spoiled meat — it is fast meat. Everything that makes it taste strong (myoglobin, iron, lipid) also makes it spoil quickly, so the whole technique is a race against oxidation you win by keeping it cold and moving fast.

This is not a new idea — it is the logic behind the most famous dried fish in the world.

Japan built an entire craft on drying skipjack — a tuna relative — into katsuobushi, the smoked, dried, sometimes mould-fermented block shaved for dashi. The principle is identical: take strong, oxidation-prone red muscle and preserve its savour by driving out the water. We are borrowing that logic for the offcut.

The Steps

Follow these in order and the bloodline works for you instead of against you.

  1. Cut it cold and clean. Work the fish at or below 4 °C. Slide the knife along the natural seam between the dark bloodline and the pale loin and lift the whole wedge off in one piece — a clean separation, no smearing dark into white.
  2. Draw the blood. Soak the trimmed bloodline 10–15 minutes in iced 3% salt water (30 g salt per litre). This pulls residual blood and slows the browning; a splash of vinegar in the water sharpens the effect. Drain and pat bone-dry.
  3. Choose the road — cure or simmer. To powder: salt-cure the strips (2% salt by weight, 12–24 h refrigerated), rinse, then dry hard — a dehydrator at 50–60 °C, or a cold-smoke then dry — until brittle, and blitz to a coarse powder. To stock: sear the wedge hard for colour, then simmer gently at a bare 80–85 °C for 30–40 minutes with kombu and aromatics; do not boil.
  4. Season, don't dominate. The powder is a finishing seasoning — a pinch over grilled fish, into a noodle dough, folded through rice. The stock is a base for a dark, marine broth. In both, it adds iron-savoury depth; it is not the headline.

Keep a bowl of iced salt water at the board before you start breaking down the fish. The bloodline goes straight from knife to brine — every minute it sits warm and exposed is oxidation you cannot undo.

Critical Factors

These are the levers — get them right and the rest looks after itself.

Factor What Happens Adjust By
Temperature at the board Above ~4 °C the myoglobin browns and lipid oxidation races — metallic, rancid notes set in Work on ice; brine the trim immediately; hold at 0–2 °C
Freshness of the fish Old tuna already carries trimethylamine and free amino acids that green and sour on heating Only do this with fish fresh enough to eat raw — the bloodline ages first
Salt and drying time Under-dried strips spoil; over-salted ones are unusable as seasoning 2% cure, dry until brittle-snap, not leathery
Simmer temperature A rolling boil drives off aroma and can turn the stock green-grey and bitter Hold 80–85 °C; never let it boil

The board is one thing; a moving board is another.

On a beam sea the fridge fights you: every time the galley door opens the ambient heat drags the loin's surface temperature up, and the bloodline is the first tissue to brown. Break the fish down against a stable bulkhead, keep the iced brine wedged in a deep pan so it cannot slop, and if the reefer is running warm, drop your working temperature target to 0–2 °C rather than 4 °C.

Once it holds at sea, the same trim feeds both ends of the boat.

One tuna, two tiers off one pass: the clean loin plates raw for guests, while the reclaimed bloodline — powdered or simmered — seasons the crew's rice bowls and noodle broth. You bought one fish and fed the whole boat, and nothing went over the side.

Why It Works

Dark muscle is dark because of myoglobin — the iron-bearing oxygen-storage protein that also gives the tissue its strong, mineral flavour and its speed to spoil. On heating, tuna myoglobin can undergo a greening reaction: a study of skipjack tuna (Zhang et al., PeerJ, 2022, DOI 10.7717/peerj.13923) showed that during thermal treatment at 60 °C, an exposed cysteine on the myoglobin forms sulfmyoglobin in the presence of trimethylamine oxide — the pigment behind grey-green cooked tuna. Keeping the muscle cold, drawing the blood, and holding the simmer below a boil is how you stay ahead of that reaction and keep the savoury depth instead of the off-colour and off-smell.

The numbers it turns on:

Compound / Reaction Threshold °C What It Does
Myoglobin / metmyoglobin browning >4 Warm, exposed dark muscle oxidises red to brown within hours
Sulfmyoglobin greening (Cys + TMAO) 60 Heat exposes a myoglobin cysteine; forms the grey-green cooked-tuna pigment
Aromatic loss at the boil 100 A rolling boil strips volatile savour and pushes bitter, off notes
The Proof

You know it worked when: the powder is dry enough to snap and grinds to a fine, rust-coloured dust that smells savoury and clean, not fishy; the stock pours a clear, dark amber and coats a spoon with a deep, iron-mineral savour — not a grey, metallic tang.

That is the test. This is why it is worth doing.

A whole fish fed the guests their sashimi and the crew a proper hot bowl, and nothing was binned — that is the quiet respect a good galley shows the animal and the people it feeds.

The one thing: the bloodline is the fish's own stock cube — cut it cold, brine it fast, then concentrate it; the same density that makes it spoil quickly is exactly what makes it worth saving.
