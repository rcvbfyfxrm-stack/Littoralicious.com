What Yacht Chefs Actually Earn: A 2026 Pay Map

The numbers nobody publishes, read off five months of job ads and crew chatter: what a yacht chef earns from the bottom rung to a 50-metre charter, why the figure has barely moved since 2017, and how rotation, tips and the package decide the real hourly rate.

What you'll get
  • A clear 2026 pay map — from the €2,500 bottom rung to a $10,500-a-month head chef on rotation.
  • Why the package — leave, flights, medical, tips — can make a smaller salary the better-paid job.
  • The honest hourly rate behind a "$9,000 a month" headline, and the one line every chef in the group keeps repeating about rotation.

Ask a recruiter what a yacht chef earns and you'll get a shrug and a range a mile wide. Ask the chefs themselves, in private, and the numbers are exact — and so is the anger behind them. This map is read off five months of anonymised job ads and the chef-group threads that follow each one. It is the number the industry won't print honestly. Here it is.

The salary is the least useful figure on the contract. Two chefs on paper earning the same can take home wildly different money once tips, leave, flights and living costs are counted. Read the package, not the headline — and never the headline alone.

The Ladder

The 2026 pay map

The bands below are where the ads actually cluster, not where the brochures say they sit. The currency follows the market: smaller European-based boats quote euros, charter and worldwide programmes quote dollars. The thick middle is a sole chef on a 30-to-40-metre boat. Everything above it is rotation, charter, or both.

Role Boat / setup Monthly pay
Cook / chef-stew hybrid Sub-30m, private €2,500–3,500
Sole chef 30–40m €5,000–6,000
Sole / head chef 50m+ charter or worldwide $8,000–10,000+
Head chef 80m, 2:2 rotation ~$10,500
Sous chef 70m+ charter ~$7,000 + tips

Day rates run on a parallel track. Freelance and temp work pays by the day, and the spread is just as wide: $200 for crew relief, $350 cook-stew, $400 on a Caribbean charter — and $750–1,000 a day for the New York period on a 70m-plus programme, where a chef is worth what the city costs.

The comparisons that keep surfacing in the threads are the land jobs chefs left, or could leave for: an Alpine chalet chef on €300 a day; a private chef in Saudi on $10,000 a month plus car, flights and insurance. Those numbers are why the wage argument never quite dies.

The Package

The package behind the number

This is where the real money hides. A salary is half the story; the other half is everything the boat pays so you don't have to. A $6,500 rotational role can out-earn an $8,000 fixed one once you count what you keep and the life you get back.

What to add to the headline. Leave days (anywhere from 30 to 90), flights home, full medical cover, sometimes a 13th month, charter tips, and per-charter bonuses — one common pattern is roughly $1,000 a charter once the boat clears a threshold. None of it shows in the advertised salary. All of it spends.

Two chefs, same $8,000 line. One is fixed, season-long, no rotation, 30 days leave. The other is rotational at $6,500 with 90 days off, flights paid, and tips on top. On paper the first wins. In the bank, and in the marriage, the second usually does.

Frozen since 2017

Why the number hasn't moved

This is the heart of it, and the part the group returns to again and again. The recurring, heated claim is that chef pay has stalled while standards climbed — bigger ambitions, longer menus, harder guests, the same money. One member sarcastically welcomed yet "another wage conversation — it's been a few days." It comes round because nothing changes.

"Your wages haven't moved since 2017, when every other position on board has gone up fifty per cent or more."

The lines pile up the same way every time. "Ten grand a month — same pay rate for the past twenty-five years." A deckhand on a 40-metre boat scrubbing teak quietly out-earning a green chef who trained for years. The grievance isn't only the figure; it's the figure standing still while every other crew slot crept up around it.

The Tips Math

The real hourly rate

The counter-argument is always the same: forget the salary, it's the tips and the zero living costs that matter. Fair — so do the arithmetic properly. Take a $9,000-a-month chef and divide it down.

$9,000 a month ÷ 30 days ÷ 14-hour days ≈ $21.40 an hour. That's the base rate before a cent of tips — for a senior, sole-charge professional on call in someone else's kitchen, at sea.

Then add the tips — carefully. The often-quoted prize is "$80,000 tax-free in tips a year." It happens, on the right boat, in the right season. But two catches sit underneath it. First, tips are frequently held until the end of the season — an informal group poll ran roughly 54 in favour of paying "per charter" against about 1 for "per season," yet the cash still arrives weeks late on many boats. Second, the tax exemption isn't free.

The tax-free claim has a threshold. For UK seafarers the Seafarers' Earnings Deduction needs roughly 183 days outside the UK across the qualifying tax-year period. Miss the day count and the "tax-free" income isn't. The arithmetic only works if the calendar does.

Rotation

5:1 is not rotation

Rotation used to belong to captains and engineers. In 2026 it has finally reached chef ads — 2:2 at around $6,500, 10:10, and the dangled "earn rotation after a year." It is the single biggest lever on quality of life, and the group polices the language fiercely.

"5:1 is NOT rotation." Five months on for one off is a season with a long weekend attached, dressed up in the word everyone wants to hear.

The hierarchy is settled in the threads. 2:2 is the preferred deal; 10:10 the ideal. Watch for "60 days leave" marketed as rotation — it isn't, if the boat tells you when to take it. True rotation means a counterpart in the galley and a date you control. Anything else is leave with better branding.

The Drag

What pulls pay down

Three forces keep the number from rising, and they show up in the ads in plain sight once you know the shapes. The most common is two jobs sold as one.

The hybrid trap. A chef/stew role at €3,500–4,500 is two jobs on one salary — cook the food, then service and tidy the interior. These are usually private boats, so there are no tips to soften it. The "saving" is entirely the boat's.

Nationality-stratified hiring drags too. The ads aren't shy about it: a Filipino chef around $6,000 to cook for Filipino crew; a Russian chef in the UAE near $5,000. Pay is set to the passport as much as the plate, and it holds the whole band down.

And then the culture effect. A recurring theory in the group blames reality television for the squeeze — one chef called it "as damaging to yachting as Food Network was to restaurants." The argument: the shows inflate what owners expect on the plate while doing nothing for what they'll pay for it. Higher standards, same wage.

The Takeaway

How to negotiate it

The map is only useful if it changes what you sign. The chefs who do best treat the number as one line in a longer sum.

Price the whole package, not the headline. Hold your day rate — one freelancer turned down $350–500 lowballs until the boats that passed circled back agreeing terms. Get rotation and leave in writing, not promised. And know your MLC baseline: the Maritime Labour Convention and your flag state set the legal floor on leave, repatriation and contract minimums. Negotiate up from a floor you can name.

The Bottom Line

If you remember one thing

The headline salary is the one number everyone quotes and the one that tells you least. A €5,500 sole-chef job and a $6,500 rotational one and an $8,000 fixed grind are three different lives wearing similar figures. Count the leave, the flights, the medical, the tips and the hours, work out the real hourly rate, and pin the rotation in writing — then you'll know what you're actually being paid. The number won't move on its own. Move it yourself. From the dock.
