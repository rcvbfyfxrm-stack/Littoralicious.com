Technology in the Galley: AI, Apps, and the Tools That Actually Matter

Your phone is more powerful than the computer that landed Apollo 11. You're using it to scroll Instagram between services. Here's how to use it to become a better chef.

AI Is Not Coming for Your Job

An AI can't taste. Can't smell a fish and know it's two days past prime. Can't adjust a sauce because the humidity changed the reduction rate. Can't read a guest's face and know the course needs to land faster.

But an AI can do the things you hate: menu planning, cost calculations, dietary cross-referencing, provisioning lists, recipe scaling, and generating ideas at 2am when you're staring at a preference sheet that says "no gluten, no dairy, no nightshades, loves Italian food."

Use AI for the admin. Save your brain for the craft.

Claude & AI Assistants: Your Sous Chef That Never Sleeps

Menu Creation

Here's a real workflow:

  1. Receive guest preference sheet: "Mediterranean-inspired, pescatarian, one guest celiac, wine pairing required"
  2. Open Claude (or ChatGPT, or Gemini). Prompt:
    "Create a 5-course dinner menu. Mediterranean, pescatarian. One guest is celiac — all courses must be naturally gluten-free, no substitutions that taste like substitutions. Suggest wine pairings for each course. I have access to fresh local fish, excellent olive oil, and it's January so citrus is in season."
  3. Get 3 options in 30 seconds. Modify, combine, make it yours.
  4. Ask follow-up: "Scale this for 12 guests and generate a provisioning list with quantities."

Time saved: 45 minutes of staring at a blank page. You still make every creative decision. The AI handles the blank-page problem.

Recipe Development & Adaptation

Prompt: "I have a classic bouillabaisse recipe. Adapt it for a guest who can't eat shellfish but loves the flavor profile. Keep it authentic."

The AI will suggest alternatives based on flavor compound matching. You decide what works. It's a brainstorming partner that knows food chemistry and doesn't get offended when you reject its ideas.

Dietary Cross-Referencing

Guest preference sheet says: "keto, allergic to tree nuts, doesn't like cilantro, loves Asian food."

You could spend 20 minutes mentally filtering recipes. Or ask the AI to cross-reference restrictions against your planned menu and flag conflicts. It catches what you miss at midnight.

Cost Calculation

Paste your provision list. Ask: "Calculate per-head cost for this 7-day charter menu for 10 guests." Include your sourcing prices. Get instant cost-per-plate breakdowns. Present to management with data, not guesses.

Claude Code: Building Your Own Tools

This is where it gets interesting. Claude Code (Anthropic's CLI tool) lets you build custom tools using natural language. No coding background needed.

What You Can Build

  • Personal recipe database — Describe your filing system, let Claude Code create a searchable archive with tags for dietary restrictions, season, difficulty, and guest feedback.
  • Provision calculator — Input guest count, duration, meal plan. Output: exact quantities with waste factored in. Build it once, use it for every charter.
  • Menu rotation tracker — For repeat guests. The system flags if you've served the same dish to the same guest. No repeats.
  • Inventory manager — Track what's on board, what's expiring, what needs reordering. Simple spreadsheet connected to your provision list.
  • Preference sheet parser — Paste in a guest preference sheet, get a structured summary: restrictions, likes, dislikes, allergies, organized by course.

Real Example

"Build me a simple HTML page where I can input guest dietary restrictions and get a week of dinner suggestions that accommodate everyone. Include a shopping list output."

Claude Code builds this in minutes. You now have a custom tool that lives on your laptop, works offline, and does exactly what you need. No subscription. No app. Yours.

Smart Hardware

Three pieces of tech that earn their galley space:

  1. Bluetooth thermometer (ThermoWorks Signals/Smoke) — Monitor proteins from the bridge deck. Get alerts on your phone. No more standing over a roast while guests wonder where you are.
  2. Vacuum sealer with sous vide — Prep ahead, cook precisely, hold safely. The most important piece of tech in any yacht galley. If you don't have one, fight for the budget.
  3. Tablet (waterproof case) — Mounted in the galley. Recipe reference, provision lists, timer display, preference sheets. Replace the paper chaos.

Using AI for Flavor Pairing

This is where AI actually outperforms human intuition:

"I have fresh red snapper, blood oranges, and fennel. What unexpected ingredient would bridge these flavors based on shared volatile compounds?"

The AI draws from flavor compound databases (Foodpairing, VCF) and suggests connections you wouldn't make: saffron (shares safranal with fennel, limonene with blood orange), or black olive (oleic acid bridge, Mediterranean context).

You're not outsourcing creativity. You're expanding your palette of possibilities, then applying your judgment.

What AI Can't Do

Be honest about the limits:

  • Taste. An AI doesn't know your salt level is right. You do.
  • Read the room. The owner's mood, the guest's energy, the timing of the next course. That's human work.
  • Handle chaos. When the sea state changes, the guest count doubles, and the freezer dies—your experience solves that, not an algorithm.
  • Source quality. No AI picks a better fish than a chef who knows what to look for.
  • Replace relationships. Your fishmonger at Haulover knows your standards. No app replaces that.

The Workflow

Post-charter AI helps document what worked You learn, adapt, improve

The Hard Truth

Most yacht chefs resist technology because they think it diminishes the craft. It doesn't. A Bluetooth thermometer doesn't make you less of a cook. It makes you a cook who never overcooks a $200 piece of wagyu because you got pulled away to deal with a captain's request.

AI doesn't replace your palate. It replaces the three hours you spend on admin that contribute nothing to the plate. Use that time to prep better, rest more, or actually taste your food before service.

The best chefs in the world use every advantage available. The galley isn't a monastery. It's a workplace. Use the tools.
