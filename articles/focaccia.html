Focaccia: The Science-First Version

Open, custardy crumb. Crisp-tender Ligurian crust. Three moves make it reliable on any galley — and the longest step is the fridge doing nothing.

Most focaccia is a dense slab or a greasy disc. Both are fixable structurally — with water, time, and what you pour into the dimples, not more oil.

The three moves: (1) 80% hydration worked by coil folds, not kneading — the open crumb; (2) a 24–72 h cold ferment — flavour no same-day dough can fake; (3) a salamoia brine in the dimples — the crisp-tender crust.

The ratio — flour = 100%
Water 80% · Oil 5% · Salt 2.2% · Yeast 0.6% — high hydration is what gives the steam for a custardy, holey crumb; the low yeast lets the cold ferment carry the flavour.

Method

0/0 steps done

  1. Mix & autolyse. Whisk yeast into flour, add water + the 25 g oil to a shaggy mass — hold the salt. Rest 20 min.

    Why
    Gluten hydrates faster without salt competing for water; the rest aligns the strands before any work. Salt added after tightens the network. — McGee 2004; López-Alt

  2. Salt, then 4 coil folds over 2 h. Work in the 11 g salt. Wet hands, lift the centre till it stretches, tuck under, rotate; repeat at 30 / 60 / 90 min.

    Why
    At 80% hydration kneading fails; folds build the aligned gluten that traps big irregular bubbles — the open, custardy crumb. — Forkish 2012; Modernist Bread

  3. Cold ferment 24–72 h. Straight to the fridge, covered. 48 h is the sweet spot.

    Why
    At 4°C the yeast crawls while enzymes and slow fermentation build sugars, extensibility and organic acids — nutty, complex flavour no same-day dough matches. — Modernist Bread; McGee

  4. Pan & warm proof. 30 g oil up the pan. Scrape in, stretch to the corners (rest 15 min, stretch again). Proof 2–4 h at room temp until doubled and wobbling like set custard.

  5. Salamoia, dimple, finish. Whisk 4 g salt into 30 g warm water, whisk in 40 g oil till cloudy; spoon over. Dimple to the pan with oiled fingers. Scatter rosemary + flaky salt.

    Why
    The brine carries salt into the dough, flashes to steam (lifting the craters), and the oil left behind crisps the crust. The whole Ligurian trick. — Nosrat 2017

  6. Bake 230°C (450°F), 20–25 min to deep golden / 96°C internal. Lift onto a rack within a minute — left in the pan the crust steams soft.

Troubleshooting
Dense crumbUnder-proofed / baked cold → let it double and wobble first
Flat, no bubblesOver-proofed → catch it at double, not triple
Greasy pale crustNo brine / oven too cool → use the salamoia, verify a true 230°C
Gummy centrePulled on colour → bake to deep brown + 96°C, cool 15 min
Charter prep & storage

The cold ferment is the make-ahead: mix on a provisioning day, bake when needed (dough keeps 3 days). Best within hours of baking; re-crisp 4 min at 180°C. Freeze baked 1 month. Never refrigerate baked focaccia — it stales fast.
