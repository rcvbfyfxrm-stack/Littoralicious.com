Know What You Sign For: The Pay Check Tool

We built a free, anonymous tool that takes your role, your vessel and your pay, and tells you what the 2026 standard says you should earn — what the job should and should not ask of you for it, what belongs in the package, and how to defend your position. Here is what it does, and why it exists.

What you'll get
  • A free tool that turns “am I underpaid?” into a number — for sole, head, sous and crew chefs.
  • Not just a salary band: a cahier des charges of what the job should and shouldn't ask for that pay, the package that belongs with it, and how to negotiate.
  • Anonymous, saved only on your device, and it prints to an A4 checklist you can take to a review.

Pay is the most guarded number in yachting. Two chefs on the same 45-metre can sit four thousand a month apart, the salary guides quote a glossy ceiling while the offer in your inbox quietly sits below it, and almost no one tells a green chef — or a tired one — what the job is actually worth. So we built the thing we wished existed: Pay Check, a free salary tool for every galley role.

Pay Check answers one question honestly: for your role, your boat and your programme, what should you earn — and what should the job ask of you for it? It turns a feeling into a band, a verdict and a checklist you can defend. No sign-up, no data leaves your phone.

Why We Built It

The number nobody prints honestly

The recruiters' salary guides are real, but they are the ceiling — the number a strong chef lands on the right boat. The working floor, read off months of real job ads, sits lower, and the gap is where good chefs get lowballed. Meanwhile the loudest grievance in every crew chat is the same: pay that “hasn't moved since 2017 while every other position went up 50%.”

Both things are true at once: the guide bands are reachable, and the offer under them is also real. The only way to stop being lowballed is to know both numbers and walk in able to name them. That is the entire point of the tool.

What It Does

Four answers, not one band

Set your role, vessel length and programme, type in your base pay, and Pay Check returns four things — the last two are the ones no salary guide gives you.

1 · Your band & a verdict

The 2026 standard for your exact profile, and a plain read: underpaid, at market, or above — with the euro gap if you are short.

2 · The cahier des charges

What the job should ask of you for that price — and where it is over-asking, so you know what to push back on.

3 · The package

Cabin, medical, flights, leave, rotation, repatriation, tips — the lines that add a fifth to your real pay and belong in writing.

4 · How to negotiate

Exactly what to ask for, and say, when a line is missing from the offer or the payslip. Defend the package as hard as the number.

It covers the whole galley. Sole chef, head chef, sous chef and crew chef each get their own band and their own scope — because everyone should be able to see what they are signing for, not just the person at the pass.

How to Use It

Thirty seconds, then print it

There is nothing to learn. Three moves:

  • Set the boat. Role, vessel length, charter or private, rotation, experience — the levers that actually move the number.
  • Type your pay. Base only. The verdict updates live; tips and package are handled separately, as upside, never as base.
  • Print the checklist. One tap gives you an A4 cahier des charges — your band, your scope, your package and your negotiating lines — to keep, or to put on the table at a review.

Nothing you enter leaves your device — it saves to your own browser so it is there next time, and to no one else, ever. No account, no email, no tracking of your number. Pay is your business.

What It's Built On

Honest sources, stated plainly

The bands interpolate the 2026 superyacht crew salary guides by vessel length and role, then adjust for programme, rotation, experience and — for a sole chef — any second role. Tips follow the MYBA charter convention. The scope and rest-hour lines follow the Maritime Labour Convention and what chefs themselves report. It is cross-checked against the deeper read in our companion piece, What Yacht Chefs Actually Earn: A 2026 Pay Map.

It is a guide for negotiation, not financial advice. Your package, your itinerary and the boat in front of you move the real number, and anything to do with tax or a contract is for you and a professional to settle. The tool gives you the floor to argue from — the argument is still yours to make.

If you remember one thing

You cannot negotiate what you cannot name. For too long the number stayed guarded, and chefs — especially the green and the worn-out — signed for whatever was offered. Now you can name it: your band, your scope, your package, in thirty seconds, for free. Open Pay Check, run your number, and print the checklist. Then go and ask for what the boat already knows you are worth. From the dock.
