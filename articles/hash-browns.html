Oven Hash Browns: The Sheet-Pan Slab

Shatter-crisp on both faces, tender inside, and you barely touch it — one slab feeds the whole crew. The whole game is getting the water out before it ever hits the oven.

Gummy, pale, greasy hash browns are a water problem, not a skill problem. Grated potato is mostly water and surface starch; leave either in and you steam instead of crisp. Get them out and a preheated sheet pan crisps a whole slab while you cook everything else.

Where it comes from

Hash comes from the French hacher, to chop — but the dish is pure American diner: “hashed brown potatoes” appear on New York breakfast tables by the 1890s, the name soon worn down to hash browns. Europe had got there already, in the Swiss rösti, the farmhouse breakfast of grated, pan-crisped potato. Both chase the same prize the Maillard reaction promises — a deep-gold, shattering crust over a tender middle. Get the water out, and the chemistry does the rest.

The three moves: (1) rinse, salt, squeeze bone-dry — water is the enemy of crisp; (2) a thin cornstarch + fat coat — a crust that shatters; (3) a screaming-hot preheated pan, one flip — skillet crunch, oven hands-off.

The ratio — of the SQUEEZED potato
Cornstarch 2% · Fat 4% (toss), and squeeze out roughly 30% of the grated weight in water first. Everything good downstream depends on hitting that dryness — weigh it if you’re unsure.

Method

0/0 steps done

  1. Preheat hard. Oven to 230°C (450°F) with the sheet pan inside — a hot pan is half the crust.

    Why
    A preheated steel pan crisps the base by direct conduction, exactly like a skillet — the oven just frees your hands and browns the top. — McGee 2004

  2. Grate & rinse. Grate the 2000 g potato (food-processor disc — fast). Rinse in cold water until it runs clear, then drain.

    Why
    Rinsing washes off loose surface starch that otherwise turns gummy and gluey; the starch left clinging inside still binds. — López-Alt, Serious Eats

  3. Salt, rest, squeeze BONE-DRY. Toss with the 12 g salt, stand 10 min, then squeeze hard in a towel (in batches). Target ~30% lighter than grated.

    Why
    Salt pulls water out by osmosis; the squeeze removes the rest. Water in the pan flashes to steam and steam is the opposite of crisp — this single step makes or breaks the dish. — McGee; López-Alt

  4. Coat. Toss the dry potato with 30 g cornstarch, 60 g melted fat and plenty of pepper (and onion/paprika if using).

    Why
    A thin cornstarch film gelatinises then dehydrates into a glassy, shatter-crisp shell; the fat carries heat and drives browning. — López-Alt; Modernist Cuisine

  5. Press onto the hot pan. Pull the pan, add the 40 g oil and swirl, spread the potato in an even ~1.5 cm layer and press firm with a spatula. It should sizzle on contact.

  6. Bake, flip once. ~20 min until the base is deep golden, then flip the slab in sections (or slide onto a board and invert back) and bake ~12–15 min more to a crisp both sides.

  7. Season & cut. Salt the hot crust, cut into 12 squares, serve at once — crisp fades as it sits.

Troubleshooting
Gummy / glueySurface starch left in → rinse until the water runs clear
Pale, steamedToo wet / pan not hot → squeeze harder; preheat the pan fully
Sticks & tearsNot enough hot oil → swirl the 40 g into the hot pan first; let the base set before flipping
Crisp then softHeld too long → serve straight away; re-crisp in a hot oven, never covered
Charter prep & storage

Squeeze and par-bake the slab to pale-gold ahead, then finish-crisp to order in a hot oven — minutes to plate for service. Par-baked slab freezes 1 month; crisp from frozen at 230°C. Grated raw potato browns fast, so rinse-and-hold it under cold water only briefly.

Sources: McGee, On Food and Cooking (starch, browning); J. Kenji López-Alt, Serious Eats / The Food Lab (potato starch, hash-brown crisp); Modernist Cuisine (potato, crust formation).
