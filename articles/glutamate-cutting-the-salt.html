Glutamate, Honestly: Cutting a Third of the Salt Without Losing the Savour

Cooks have chased one molecule since before it had a name — the savour in a long stock, an aged cheese, a sun-dried tomato. It also happens to carry about a third of the sodium of the salt sitting next to it.

What you'll get
  • The one number that reframes the jar: monosodium glutamate is about one-third the sodium of table salt.
  • Why cutting salt need not cut savour — umami and saltiness reinforce each other on the tongue.
  • A two-item, low-sodium umami kit and a swap ratio for the guest who has been told to lay off salt.

Before the chemistry, the reason a cook keeps reaching for it.

Every grandmother's trick was a delivery system for one molecule: the parmesan rind in the bean pot, the anchovy melted into the sauce, the stock left on the stove all afternoon. None of them called it glutamate. They called it the taste of a table worth sitting at — and built it with time no galley has.

The ingredient is glutamate — an amino acid your food is full of. Monosodium glutamate is simply its sodium salt, the bottled form; kombu, parmesan, ripe tomato and anchovy carry the identical molecule the slow way. One compound, two guises: the tub on the shelf, and the larder that has always held it.

Where It Comes From

Origin: A Bowl of Kombu Broth, Tokyo, 1908

Know where it was first named and you stop treating it as a modern chemical.

In 1908 the chemist Kikunae Ikeda, at Tokyo Imperial University, set out to pin down the savour in kombu dashi — a taste that was plainly not sweet, sour, salty or bitter. He isolated it as the salt of glutamic acid and gave the taste a name: umami, roughly “pleasant savoury taste.” Ikeda published the finding in the Journal of the Chemical Society of Tokyo in 1909.

A year later he and Saburosuke Suzuki began selling the isolated seasoning. What Ikeda had done was not invent a flavour but name one cooks had chased for millennia — and hand them a way to add it without a four-hour stock.

The point the label buries:

Glutamate is not foreign to your cooking. It is the most abundant amino acid in a ripe tomato, a wedge of parmesan and a mother's milk. The bottled version differs from the parmesan version in exactly one way: it arrived without the cheese.

How It Travelled

The Journey: From Kelp to Feared to Reclaimed

The status flip explains why the jar still carries an apology on some shelves.

For half a century the seasoning spread quietly through professional and home cooking across Asia and beyond. Then, in April 1968, a physician named Robert Ho Man Kwok wrote a short letter to the New England Journal of Medicine describing numbness, weakness and palpitations after meals at Chinese restaurants, and floated glutamate as one suspect among several.

The letter was an anecdote. The label that grew from it — “Chinese Restaurant Syndrome” — was a scare, and a racialised one, that emptied the jar from a generation of Western pantries. It has taken decades of blind testing, and a wave of chefs putting the tub back on the pass, to unwind.

The panic that emptied a million pantries began as a single letter to a journal.

Why It Works

The Science: One-Third the Sodium, and Saltier For It

Get the mechanism and the sodium maths stops being a guess.

The Chemistry
Why a savoury salt carries less sodium
  • Monosodium glutamate — one sodium ion per molecule on a large glutamate backbone, so sodium is only about 12% of its mass. Table salt is nearly all sodium and chloride, so sodium is about 39%.
  • Glutamate with salt — umami raises the perceived saltiness of a solution, so less sodium chloride reads as equally salty (Hayabuchi et al., 2020).
  • Glutamate with inosinate — the two together multiply savoury intensity far beyond the sum of their parts (Yamaguchi, 1967).

The arithmetic is plain. Sodium chloride has a molar mass near 58 and one sodium at 23, so sodium is roughly 39% by weight. Monosodium glutamate, as the food-grade monohydrate, has a molar mass near 187 with that same single sodium, so sodium is roughly 12% by weight. Swap part of the salt for glutamate and, gram for gram on the portion you replace, you have taken sodium off the plate.

Replace part of a dish's salt with a pinch of glutamate and it still reads as salty — because umami and saltiness reinforce each other on the tongue, not because you have added back the sodium you removed.

The same molecule that carries the savour has been measured doing two useful things. Here is what holds up, and what does not.

  • Sodium reduction is real, on the plate: partial substitution of salt with glutamate cut sodium by up to roughly a third in several test foods while tasters still found them acceptable. Maluly, Arisseto-Bragotto & Reyes (2017), Food Science & Nutrition. DOI: 10.1002/fsn3.499. Review of sensory/substitution studies · Strength: moderate.
  • Umami makes low-salt taste saltier: adding 0.3% glutamate raised both saltiness and palatability ratings of low-salt solutions. Hayabuchi et al. (2020), Hypertension Research. DOI: 10.1038/s41440-020-0397-1. Randomised, double-blind, n = 584 · Strength: moderate.
  • The illness scare does not reproduce: under double-blind challenge, reported reactions were infrequent, inconsistent and not reproducible — and were not seen when glutamate was taken with food. Geha et al. (2000), Journal of Nutrition. PMID: 10736382. Multicentre, double-blind, placebo-controlled · Strength: moderate.

What none of this shows: that a pinch of glutamate lowers anyone's blood pressure or prevents disease. The measured win is narrower and honest — less sodium on the plate at the same savour. Whether that helps a given guest is a matter for their doctor, not the galley.

The tub is not a laboratory chemical. Commercial glutamate is made by bacterial fermentation of starch or molasses — the same broad process behind yoghurt, vinegar and citric acid — and the finished molecule is identical to the glutamate in a tomato. A yacht pays a few euros a kilo for it. The premium umami on the invoice is the parmesan and the colatura; the glutamate itself is the cheapest savour in the locker.

Poetic Pairing: The Unexpected

The moves that read like cheating and simply read as depth on the plate.

  • A pinch of glutamate in a thin tomato sauce — lifts a watery, out-of-season tin without another gram of salt.
  • Parmesan rind in the bean pot — the nonna's move, understood: free glutamate leaching into the broth for an hour.
  • Kombu in the rice water — a background savour under plain rice that guests taste but cannot place.
  • A few drops of fish sauce in a beef braise — inosinate and glutamate together, deepening the meat without reading as fish.
At the Dock

How to Buy: The Two-Item Kit

Most of the result is decided by what you carry, not what you cook.

  • Pure glutamate (a small tub) — the isolated tool for cutting sodium fast; a single tub lasts a whole season.
  • Kombu (dried kelp) — the natural glutamate slab; buy a named grade and keep it sealed and dry.
  • Parmigiano rinds — do not bin them; the drawer of savour that seasons a stock for free.
  • Colatura or fish sauce — bottled glutamate-and-inosinate; a few drops, not a splash.

And the one way to get it wrong:

Glutamate is not salt and does not scale like it. Past roughly 0.7–0.8% of a dish's weight the savour saturates and tips towards a flat, metallic note. Add a pinch, taste, stop. It is a seasoning, not a sauce.

Tested at Sea

Yacht Galley Intel

What survives a 4 m² galley, a swell, and a week without a market.

  • Storage: pure glutamate is dry and shelf-stable for years; kombu keeps a year sealed; parmesan rinds freeze indefinitely.
  • Batch prep: a no-salt umami stock — cold-steep kombu and dried mushroom overnight, strain, and let the savour carry dishes for a guest on a low-sodium regime.
  • Shortcut: for the crew mess, a pinch in the pan stands in for the splash of stock you ran out of two ports ago.
  • Form to keep aboard: one tub of glutamate and one bag of kombu is a complete low-sodium seasoning kit in two items and almost no space.

Of all that, one move is worth pinning above the prep bench:

To season a low-salt dish, take out about a third of the salt and put back an equal weight of glutamate. A soup you would have salted with 6 g becomes 4 g salt plus 2 g glutamate: sodium on the plate drops by roughly a quarter, and it still tastes seasoned. Taste and adjust — the figures are a starting point, not a recipe.

The Stories

The Stories

The tales worth telling at the pass — each one sourced, none invented.

The letter that started it. Kwok's 1968 note to the New England Journal of Medicine ran a few lines and named several suspects, glutamate among them. It was enough to attach a syndrome to a whole cuisine for fifty years — proof of how far one unverified anecdote travels when it flatters a prejudice.

An anecdote became a diagnosis, and a diagnosis became a stigma.

The blind test that could not find it. When Geha and colleagues challenged self-identified reactors under double-blind conditions in 2000, the symptoms scattered: infrequent, inconsistent, and gone the moment glutamate came with food rather than on an empty stomach.

The grandmother had it first. Long before Ikeda named it, the savour was coaxed from kelp, aged cheese and salted fish by cooks who had never heard of an amino acid. The science did not discover the taste. It caught up.

Stories told; here is the card to pin above the bench.

  • Best use — cutting sodium without losing savour.
  • Best with — inosinate and guanylate foods: bonito, cured meat, dried mushroom.
  • Avoid — over-dosing; past roughly 0.8% by weight it flattens.
  • Storage — dry and sealed; effectively indefinite.
  • Chef's note: a pinch is a seasoning, not a sauce.

And if the tub washes overboard, keep this much:

Glutamate lets you take about a third of the salt off the plate and still have it taste of something. That is a tool for the guest who has been told to watch their sodium — not a trick, and nothing to apologise for.
