How Many Senses You Have, and How Many Sit Down to Eat

The "five senses" is a 2,300-year-old shorthand, not the instrument panel your guest is eating with. Here is the honest count, and the eight or more senses that actually build a plate of flavour.

What you'll get
  • The honest count: why "five senses" is philosophy from 350 BC, and why neuroscience cannot agree on a number at all.
  • The eight-plus senses that actually build flavour, from retronasal smell to the crunch you hear and the colour you expect.
  • Galley levers: how to plate, season and time a dish for the full instrument panel, not just the tongue.

Flavour is not a sense. It is the brain fusing many of them.

Here is the payoff first. When a guest says a dish "tastes" of something, the tongue did only a fraction of the work. Flavour is the brain's integration of inputs from many senses at once — gustation, smell, the trigeminal burn of chilli, oral texture, temperature, sound and sight — fused into one percept (Spence, 2015, Cell). So before we count your senses honestly, hold this: the question "how many senses do you have?" and "how many sit down to eat?" have different, and surprising, answers.

Part One — The Honest Count

The schoolbook five are philosophy, not physiology

Where "five senses" came from, and why it was never a measurement.

The five senses are not a finding; they are a 2,300-year-old classification. Sight, hearing, smell, taste and touch trace to Aristotle, who first listed them in De Anima around 350 BC (Brandt, Dieterich & Huppert, 2024, Frontiers in Neurology). He individuated senses by their dedicated organs — eye, ear, nose, tongue, skin — and, tellingly for any cook, treated taste as a specialised form of touch. That was a philosopher sorting perception by its visible apparatus, not a physiologist measuring receptors.

B (secondary, on-point). The 2024 review by Brandt and colleagues is a verified, directly relevant historical source confirming the Aristotelian attribution and the taste-as-touch framing. For a strict primary bar, the originating source is Aristotle's De Anima, Book II ch. 6 and Book III ch. 1 (c. 350 BC).

"Five senses" is the simplified canon, not a single fixed doctrine — Aristotle's own works are inconsistent, sometimes tying senses to the four elements, elsewhere folding taste into touch. Treat the number as schoolbook shorthand, not a count of your machinery.

There is no agreed number of senses

Modern counts run from five to seventeen and beyond — and that is a definitional choice.

Ask how many senses a human has and the honest answer is: it depends how you cut. Modern sensory neuroscience individuates senses by distinct receptor classes plus dedicated neural pathways, so splitting or lumping submodalities changes the total. The OpenStax Anatomy and Physiology text (Betts et al., 2022) puts it plainly: most people name the major five, but the full list of sensory modalities "can number as many as 17" once you separate touch into pressure, vibration, pain, temperature and the rest. The count is a definitional choice, not a fixed biological fact.

Why does the number refuse to settle? Because no single criterion cleanly defines a sense. The philosopher Matthew Fulkerson (2014, Frontiers in Psychology) argues for sensory pluralism — the position that there is no one feature (receptor type, neural pathway, or distinct conscious quality) that classifies all senses, and that what counts as a distinct sense partly depends on your explanatory purpose. Touch alone spans several receptor types and pathways; modalities constantly interact. So the senses, Fulkerson argues, do not form a single natural kind.

B / B. OpenStax is a tertiary teaching text but an exact, on-point match for the "five up to seventeen" range; it caps at 17, so higher figures (22–33, often attributed in popular media to Charles Spence) should be reported as estimates, not facts. Fulkerson 2014 is a verified single-author philosophy position paper — the right source for the framing claim, not for experimental data.

Rather than promise a magic number, the honest move is to sell the reader on the criteria — which is also exactly why "flavour" is a fusion of taste, smell, touch and temperature rather than one sense.

Part Two — The Hidden Senses

The hidden senses: balance, body, internal state

Three senses Aristotle never listed — and every one of them matters at sea.

Three major senses sit entirely outside the schoolbook five, and a yacht chef feels all three daily.

Proprioception — the sense of where your body and limbs sit in space — was named and defined by Charles Sherrington in The Integrative Action of the Nervous System (1906), where he split sensation into exteroceptive, interoceptive and proprioceptive classes. Mechanoreceptors (sensors that report mechanical force) in muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs and joint capsules report muscle length, tension and joint angle, giving body-position awareness without vision. This is the sense you rely on to knife-work and plate steadily on a rolling deck without staring at every cut — and it degrades when you are fatigued or seasick.

A (primary). Sherrington's 1906 monograph is the originating work in which "proprioceptive" was coined and the three receptor classes defined. The per-receptor mechanism (spindle, Golgi, joint capsule) is modern physiology layered onto his definitional framing.

The vestibular sense (equilibrioception — balance and head motion) is a distinct system based in the inner-ear labyrinth, separate from hearing. Three semicircular canals transduce rotational head acceleration as endolymph (inner-ear fluid) deflects hair-cell cilia; two otolith organs (utricle and saccule) sense linear acceleration and head tilt against gravity via calcium-carbonate otoconia (tiny "ear stones") loading those hair cells (Khan & Chang, 2013, NeuroRehabilitation). This is the system that fails first at sea: vestibular conflict with vision is what drives seasickness, and both your balance and your nausea threshold in a pitching galley ride on it.

C (for the anatomy via Khan & Chang 2013). The vestibular system being a distinct sense beyond Aristotle's five is supported by Brandt et al. (2024); the detailed end-organ mechanism is carried by the peer-reviewed anatomical review of Khan & Chang. Whether it is "one" sense or several (rotation vs gravity) depends on the individuation criterion.

Interoception — the sensing of the body's internal state (hunger, fullness, thirst, heartbeat, breathlessness) — is a distinct sensory system with its own afferent pathway and cortical home. A.D. Craig (2002, Nature Reviews Neuroscience) showed that small-diameter lamina I afferents report the physiological condition of all body tissues up a dedicated pathway to the posterior, then anterior, insula, where this "material me" becomes conscious bodily feeling. For a cook this is not a footnote: hunger, fullness and thirst are sensory readouts you read in guests, not vague moods. Pacing courses and judging portion size is, literally, interoception management — and it closes the flavour loop, because fullness is the signal that ends the meal.

A (primary). Craig's 2002 review is the foundational source for interoception as a distinct sense with a dedicated pathway and insular representation. His strong further claim — that the anterior insula is the seat of all subjective feeling — is influential but contested within neuroscience.

Part Three — How Many Sit Down to Eat

Flavour is a fusion, not a sense

The single most useful fact in this article for a working cook.

What cooks call "taste" is really a multisensory construct. Charles Spence (2015, Cell) sets out the case directly: flavour is the brain's integration of gustatory, olfactory, trigeminal, oral-somatosensory, auditory and visual signals, converging — notably in the orbitofrontal cortex — into a unified percept. No single organ sums it. Plating, the weight of the bowl, the sound of the crust and the dining-room light all change how a dish reads, which means flavour work does not stop at seasoning.

Flavour is "perhaps the most multisensory of our everyday experiences." The same integration rules studied in vision and hearing govern how the flavour senses combine — so a stale-sounding crisp and a wrongly coloured sauce are not gimmicks, they are inputs the brain weighs alongside the tongue (Spence, 2015, Cell, grade A).

This is the frame for the rest of the article: each section below is one of the senses that actually sits down to eat.

Gustation: five tastes, maybe six

The tongue does less than you think — and one job superbly.

Gustation proper carries five well-established basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter and umami. Umami — the savoury taste of glutamate, identified by Kikunae Ikeda in 1908 — is detected by the T1R1+T1R3 heterodimeric receptor, and its response to glutamate is strongly potentiated by 5′-ribonucleotides (IMP and GMP) (Nelson et al., 2002, Nature). That synergy is the one chefs exploit with dashi: kombu brings glutamate, katsuobushi brings inosinate, and together they multiply savoury depth rather than just adding it.

Pair a glutamate source (parmesan, tomato, soy) with a nucleotide source (cured fish, dried shiitake, dashi) and the savoury depth multiplies. This is the most reliable umami lever in a galley — it is chemistry, not luck.

That savoury depth can also be built to linger on the palate — a separate receptor effect (kokumi, via the calcium-sensing receptor) covered in Flavours That Stay in the Mouth.

And possibly a sixth. Fat — named oleogustus — is a strong candidate for a sixth basic taste, distinct in quality from the established five (Running, Craig & Mattes, 2015, Chemical Senses). Medium- and long-chain free fatty acids produce an oral sensation that perceptual mapping separates from sweet, sour, salty and bitter: shorter chains read sour-like, but lengthening the carbon chain shifts the percept to a distinct, generally unpleasant quality. The galley use is a quality check — the fatty-acid taste of rancid or over-aged oil is detectable on the tongue, not just by smell.

A (umami receptor and IMP/GMP synergy, Nelson et al. 2002) · A (oleogustus, Running et al. 2015). Both are primary experimental sources and the origin of their respective concepts. Note the hedges: the 2002 paper itself frames T1R1+T1R3 as "a constituent of the umami response," with dedicated-receptor status being later consensus; and oleogustus is a candidate, not a settled sixth taste — the pleasant "fattiness" of food is mostly texture and aroma, while the pure free-fatty-acid taste is described as unpleasant.

Smell: the dominant partner, told honestly

Most of "taste" is smell — but not the percentage you have heard.

Smell is the dominant contributor to flavour, and it works largely through the back door. Paul Rozin (1982, Perception & Psychophysics) showed that olfaction is functionally dual: the same volatile molecules sniffed orthonasally (in through the nostrils) versus released retronasally (up the back of the nose from food in the mouth during chewing and swallowing) are referred to different locations and judged differently. Retronasal aroma is experienced as part of "taste" in the mouth — which is why a dish tastes flat to a guest with a head cold: the retronasal route is blocked. The chemistry of those cooking aromas — which seduce, which repel, and why they cling to a galley — is the companion to this piece, The Smell of Cooking.

Warm the plate and finish with fresh aromatics at the pass: heat and a final volatile hit drive aroma retronasally as the guest chews. A cold plate and pre-applied garnish lose that lift before the first bite.

Now the honesty. You have heard that "about 80% of flavour is smell." That figure is a useful illustration but not an empirically established constant. Spence (2015, Flavour) traces the popular 75–95% range chiefly to a single 1977 study by Murphy, Cain and Bartoshuk, in which occluding the nostrils removed as much as ~80% of rated "taste" for one stimulus — a lab solution of sodium saccharin plus a sweet-smelling odorant, ethyl butyrate, not a food. That single, stimulus-specific result was generalised into a universal law it does not support, and the real split depends on the food.

Do not quote a percentage at the pass. The principle — olfaction is the major contributor — is sound; the precise number is not, and the true contribution varies by food and by person. Say "most of what we call taste is smell." It is honest and still makes the point.

A (retronasal duality, Rozin 1982) · A (the 80% figure is unjustified, Spence 2015). Both are canonical primary sources making exactly these arguments. Rozin establishes the mechanism; Spence dismantles the number.

Chemesthesis: heat, cool and fizz you feel, not taste

Chilli, mint and carbonation never touch your taste buds.

Three of the strongest sensations on a plate are not tastes at all. Chemesthesis — the trigeminal chemical sense — adds chilli heat, mint cool and carbonation fizz through specific molecular receptors, not taste buds. Capsaicin opens TRPV1, a heat-activated ion channel, above roughly 43°C (109°F), so chilli is literally read as heat and pain (Caterina et al., 1997, Nature). Menthol opens the separate cold channel TRPM8, active below roughly 26°C (79°F), so mint reads as cold (McKemy, Neuhausser & Julius, 2002, Nature). And the prickle of carbonation is generated when carbonic anhydrase 4 on sour-sensing taste cells converts dissolved CO₂ to protons (Chandrashekar et al., 2009, Science).

Because capsaicin and menthol hijack the body's actual temperature-and-pain channels, chilli "heat" is a genuine thermal-pain signal and mint "cool" a genuine cold signal — felt, not tasted, at the same plate temperature. That is a lever: a dish can be engineered to read warmer or cooler than it physically is.

Capsaicin is fat-soluble, so dairy or oil quenches chilli heat where water only spreads it — serve yoghurt or a fatty sauce alongside heat, not a glass of water. Menthol and carbonation are felt, not tasted, and stack on top of the dish's actual taste, so use them to add lift without touching the seasoning.

Three pathways, three primary sources, each A; row graded B as a set. Caterina et al. 1997 carries capsaicin/TRPV1/heat directly. The menthol/TRPM8 leg (McKemy et al. 2002, with companion Peier et al. 2002, Cell) and the carbonation/CA4 leg (Chandrashekar et al. 2009) are each established by their own primary papers — no single citation can carry all three. Note one nuance: the CO₂ prickle is mediated by a taste-cell enzyme, so calling fizz purely "not taste buds" is a slight stretch.

Temperature is itself part of flavour

Warm the tongue and sweetness can appear from nothing.

Temperature does not just carry aroma — it changes taste directly. Cruz and Green (2000, Nature) showed that thermoception interacts with taste transduction: rapidly warming the front of the tongue from cold can evoke phantom sweetness, most often on the tip, with no tastant present at all. In these "thermal tasters," warming or cooling the tongue elicits a taste from temperature alone. The effect is strongest in a subset of people, but it proves the principle that temperature is woven into the taste signal itself.

Two robust, reproducible levers from this: serve ice cream slightly less cold and it reads sweeter; and a soup checked piping-hot will taste under-seasoned once it cools to eating temperature. Always season at serving temperature, not at the boil.

A (primary). Cruz & Green 2000 is the seminal source for thermally evoked phantom taste and the "thermal taster" subgroup. The thermal-taster effect is strongest in some people; the season-at-serving-temperature point is robust and widely reproduced.

The crunch you hear, the colour you expect

Two more senses are eating before the fork is even lifted.

Hearing and sight join the meal too. Zampini and Spence (2004, Journal of Sensory Studies) demonstrated sonic seasoning: amplifying the high-frequency crunch of a potato crisp made it read as fresher and crisper, even though nothing about the crisp itself had changed. The sound of a bite is texture information the brain folds straight into flavour.

And vision sets expectation so strongly it can override smell. Morrot, Brochet and Dubourdieu (2001, Brain and Language) gave 54 tasters a white wine dyed odourlessly red; the tasters described it using red-wine aroma terms. Colour rewrote what their noses reported. Sight does not merely decorate a dish — it primes the percept before tasting begins.

Protect the crunch: add crisp elements at the pass, not pre-plated, so the bite still sounds fresh. And use plate colour and garnish to set the right expectation — both change the eating before a fork is lifted.

A (auditory, Zampini & Spence 2004) · A (colour overrides smell, Morrot et al. 2001). Two separate primary sources, one per limb of the claim — the crisp study says nothing about colour, and the wine study nothing about sound, so each is cited to its own paper.

At the Pass

Plate for the whole instrument panel

One change to how you think about a dish.

Stop thinking of a plate as a delivery system for taste, and start treating it as a multisensory instrument you are tuning. The tongue contributes five tastes, maybe six. Everything else — the dominant retronasal aroma, the chilli burn and mint cool you feel rather than taste, the serving temperature, the crunch the guest hears, the colour they see, and finally the interoceptive fullness that ends the meal — is doing the heavy lifting. Aristotle's five was a 2,300-year-old shorthand; there is no agreed number of senses; and far more than five sit down to eat.

Engineer the whole panel deliberately: season at serving temperature, drive aroma retronasally with heat and fresh finishing aromatics, protect the crunch at the pass, set expectation with colour, and pace portions to the guest's fullness. Flavour is multisensory integration — so plate it that way.
