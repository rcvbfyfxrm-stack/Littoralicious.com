The Preservative Paradox

A 112,000-person study just linked eight common food preservatives — including added citric acid and ascorbic acid — to higher blood pressure and heart risk. What it means for a galley that runs on long-life provisioning, and what it doesn't.

What you'll get
  • One new, large study linking eight common food preservatives to higher blood pressure and heart risk.
  • Why added citric and ascorbic acid behaved nothing like the lemon they come from — and the galley move it earns.
  • The honest edge: this is an association, not a verdict, and where it stops.

The Claim

A finding that lands on the provisioning list, not the spice rack.

People eating the most of certain food-additive preservatives carried a measurably higher risk of hypertension and cardiovascular disease — and the additives flagged were not the lurid ones, but the everyday acids stamped on half the long-life shelf.

The Paper

112,000 people, eight years, a journal that does not print lightly.

Sellem, Touvier et al. “Preservative food additives, hypertension, and cardiovascular diseases: the NutriNet-Santé study.” European Heart Journal, 2026. DOI: 10.1093/eurheartj/ehag308.

French INSERM researchers followed 112,395 adults in the NutriNet-Santé cohort for up to eight years, matching detailed dietary records against 58 preservative additives and tracking who later developed high blood pressure or heart disease. It is observational — people, not a lab dish — but it is one of the largest of its kind.

The Mechanism

The same molecule does different work as a food additive than as fruit.

Eight preservatives stood out; among them, added citric acid (E330) and added ascorbic acid (vitamin C, E300) tracked with the higher risk — the top-consuming group showed roughly a 22% greater risk of hypertension.

The likely culprit is not the acid itself but the company it keeps: these additives mark ultra-processed foods, and they may also nudge the gut microbiome and the body’s sodium handling. The acid is the flag on the tin, not necessarily the hand on the blood pressure.

The vitamin C on the label is not the orange in the bowl. As an additive, it travels with everything we already suspect.

Galley Implications

The science, turned into one move on the next provisioning run.

“Contains vitamin C / citric acid” is not a health badge — on a long-life product it is a marker of how processed the thing is.

Read it as a processing tell, not a poison list. Where the galley already cooks from fresh — squeezing a real lemon over the fish, making the dressing aboard — you are clear. The shift is at the order stage: when two long-life products are equal, take the shorter additive line, and lean the crew table, where the same food repeats daily, toward what you cook rather than what you decant.

What This Does NOT Mean

The overreach to refuse before it scares a guest off a lemon.

This is about additive citric and ascorbic acid in processed food. The citric acid in a fresh lemon and the vitamin C in a pepper are not implicated — eat those freely. Squeezing a lemon is not the finding.

Treat an observational association as proof and you will chase phantoms. The study shows a link in eating patterns, not a dose that raises one person’s blood pressure on cue.

Limitations

Exactly how far the evidence reaches — and no further.

It is observational: it shows correlation, and heavy preservative eaters differ in many ways from light ones, so residual confounding is real even after adjustment. The cohort is French and skews health-conscious and female; intake came from self-reported diet records. It earns a change of habit at the margin, not a panic.

The one thing

Across 112,395 people over eight years, the highest intake of additive citric acid tracked with about a 22% higher hypertension risk — so on the long-life shelf, read the additive line as a processing score, and cook fresh where you can. — European Heart Journal, 2026
