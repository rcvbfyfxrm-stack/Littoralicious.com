The Cocoa Crash Is a Buying Window, Not a Bottom

Cocoa fell about 60% from its 2024 peak, but the crash is a rebound off a rising floor of disease and climate, not a return to cheap. The provisioning read: buy the couverture on the dip, not the confectionery, and hold it right.

What you'll get
  • The number that moved: cocoa is off roughly 60% from its 2024 peak — and why the chart is telling you the wrong thing.
  • Why it fell, and why the floor under it is now structurally higher than the price you paid for a decade.
  • The provisioning call — what couverture to buy on the dip, how to hold it, and the mistake that turns a saving into waste.

In early 2026 the cocoa price did something it had not done in two years: it fell, and it fell hard — from almost $12,900 a tonne at the 2024 peak to under $4,000, a drop the World Bank clocked at more than 30% in the first months of the year alone. Read that as the crisis ending, and you will buy chocolate at exactly the wrong moment.

The 2024 spike was not a bubble — it was the market pricing in a real, physical shortage: disease and a warming climate hollowing out the West African crop that grows 70% of the world's cocoa. One good harvest and a demand slump knocked the panic out of the price, but not the problem. Cocoa has already rebounded to about $4,900. The old $2,000–3,000 band is gone. This is a window to average down, not a bottom to wait for.

For a galley provisioning a season, that distinction is money. Chocolate on the shelf still costs what it did at the top of the market — retail lags the bean by a year or more — so the dip you can actually act on is in the raw couverture your supplier prices off the futures curve, and that window is closing.

The Signal

Read the arc, not the last candle — so you're acting on the supply balance, not on a headline that says "cocoa collapses."

The price didn't crash from a normal level; it crashed from an extraordinary one. Cocoa held the $2,000–3,000 a tonne band for most of a decade, spiked to almost $12,900 in 2024, then fell back under $4,000 by early 2026 before rebounding to about $4,900 by mid-year (a five-month high near $5,250 on 25 June). Evidence Grade: market price data (ICE futures, World Bank commodity report) · coverage = global benchmark · Strength: firm on the numbers, provisional on where it settles.

Behind the price sits the balance sheet. The 2023/24 season closed a brutal 489,000-tonne deficit — the shortfall that drove the spike. The grind, the tonnage of beans processors actually turn into chocolate, then fell as buyers recoiled from the high price, and 2024/25 tipped to a slim surplus of tens of thousands of tonnes. For 2025/26 the trader StoneX projects a surplus near 247,000 tonnes — but it has already revised that down, citing El Niño risk to the West African crop. A surplus that thin is one bad Harmattan from vanishing.

Why It's Happening

The crash is a weather-and-demand story. The floor beneath it is a disease-and-climate story — and that is the one that decides what you'll pay for a decade.

The near-term fall is the easy part. A more forgiving 2025/26 harvest met a market where high prices had already destroyed demand — Asian grindings in particular fell sharply as manufacturers shrank bars and reformulated. More beans, fewer buyers: the price gave way. None of that repairs the trees.

Cacao swollen shoot virus is quietly eating the world's biggest cocoa origin. A 2024/25 field survey of over 11,600 farms in Ivory Coast found more than 41% infected, up from 33% two seasons earlier; the virus cuts a tree's yield by roughly a third and has no cure. The surveyors put about 15% of Ivory Coast's entire supply at risk. Evidence Grade: field survey (Enveritas) · coverage = one country, one season · Strength: provisional but large sample. It is not a price blip — it is standing timber coming out of production.

Then there is the ceiling coming down to meet the trees. West and Central Africa grow more than 70% of the world's cocoa, and the belt is running out of the climate it needs. A 2025 mechanistic crop-model study projects that up to half of today's climatically suitable area could be lost by 2050, with the northern edge of the Ivory Coast and Ghana growing zones hit hardest — the same conclusion an independent projection reached back in 2013.

The mid-century number Asante, P.A., et al. “Climate change impacts on cocoa production in the major producing countries of West and Central Africa by mid-century.” Agricultural and Forest Meteorology, 2025. Grade B · mechanistic crop model, 2030–2060 projection

Stack them and the picture is plain: an ageing, virus-thinned tree stock on land the climate is slowly making unsuitable, supplying a demand that keeps growing. That is not a shortage that a single good harvest ends. It is a higher baseline the market is still learning to price.

The ceiling came down. The floor came up to meet it. What crashed was the panic, not the problem.

What It Means at the Provisioning End

Turn the signal into a buying decision you can make under real limits — space, humidity, and a shelf life that varies more than most chefs think.

In one line: the crash you can act on is in the couverture, not the confectionery, and the head of Lindt himself has said he doesn't expect cocoa to return to its old levels. Buy the raw material while it's soft.

The guest never sees the futures curve. They see whether the chocolate tart is still on the card in month two of the charter — at the same size, with the same bittersweet snap. Buy right on the dip and it stays; miss it, and the pastry section is the first place the squeeze shows.

Make that buy carelessly, though, and the saving turns straight into waste on a shelf.

Two traps. The first is reading −60% as “crisis over” and buying nothing, then paying the retail price that never fell. The second is over-stocking the wrong form: filled and milk chocolate carry milk solids and inclusions that cap their life at 6–12 months, and chocolate stored warm or damp blooms — fat or sugar migrating to a dull grey surface. Bloom is cosmetic, but a guest reads it as spoilage, and it ruins your temper.

The Move

One call, specific enough to forward to your supplier as it stands.

Order the season's dark couverture now, on the soft bean price — not when retail finally follows. Take plain dark couverture at 60–70% cocoa solids in sealed blocks or callets; store at 15–18°C (59–64°F), below 55% relative humidity, sealed and away from strong aromas. Held that way, couverture keeps a genuine 18–24 months; cocoa mass and butter keep longer still. Skip the filled and milk stock for the long hold.

No galley has unlimited dry, cool stowage, so carry a fallback before you over-commit.

The hedge: if you can't hold couverture cool and dry on board, buy the longest keepers — 100% cocoa mass and cocoa butter — and top up finished couverture per charter. And the honest uncertainty: the dip could deepen if demand keeps sliding, so this is a window to average down, not a bottom to time. But the floor under it is real. Averaging in beats waiting for a low that the disease and the climate have quietly retired.

Buy the couverture while the bean is soft, hold it cool and dry, and let the shelf price chase you instead of the other way round. Tested at sea.

The one thing

Cocoa's 60% fall is a rebound off a rising floor, not a return to cheap. Stock a season of dark couverture on the dip and hold it right — the old price isn't coming back.
