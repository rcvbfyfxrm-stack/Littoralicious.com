Yorkshire Pudding: The Perfect Version

Smoking fat, cold batter, sealed oven — the four-inch climb is physics

Beef dripping smoking-hot at 230°C. Cold batter into hot fat. Equal volumes flour, egg, milk — weighed properly, that means hydration north of 110%. Bread flour, not plain. No peeking until they have climbed. A steam balloon held up by a fragile gluten-and-egg shell — get the physics right and they rise three times the depth of the tin.

Most home Yorkshire puddings fail for one of four reasons: cold fat, lukewarm oven, the wrong flour, or the cook opens the door at minute fifteen to peek. Each failure produces the same compromised pudding — squat, dense, slightly greasy, structurally embarrassing. This recipe builds the pudding from the chemistry up: high-hydration batter for explosive steam, strong flour for a gluten cage that holds the climb, smoking-hot fat to set the base before the centre has time to settle, and a sealed oven that does not open until the structure has frozen in place. The Royal Society of Chemistry’s 2008 ruling — “a Yorkshire pudding isn’t a Yorkshire pudding if it is less than four inches tall” — is achievable in a domestic oven and a yacht galley alike.

The 1:1:1 ratio decoded: The classic English instruction — “equal volumes of flour, egg, milk” — is a 1700s rule of thumb that survived because it works. But equal volumes of three different densities means the actual weight ratio is roughly 1 part flour : 1.6 parts egg : 1.6 parts milk. That is hydration of about 115% — wetter than pancake batter, wetter than crêpe batter. Not optional. A drier batter cannot produce the steam volume needed for a four-inch climb.

Equipment: carbon-steel popover pan or non-stick 12-cup muffin tin (heavy black steel beats thin aluminium) · oven thermometer (most domestic ovens read 15–20°C off true) · digital scale · 1 L jug · whisk · sieve.

Method

Phase 1: Mix the Batter — 5 min

  1. Sieve the bread flour and salt into a large bowl or jug. Make a well in the centre.
  2. Crack the eggs into the well. Whisk the eggs into the flour from the centre outward — paste → thick batter → smooth. Whisk hard. The eggs need to be fully homogenised with the flour before the milk thins it. Once smooth and ribbon-thick, slowly stream in the cold milk while whisking, until the batter has the consistency of single cream — pourable, just-coating the back of a spoon.
  3. Pass through a fine sieve into a 1 L jug. Catches any unwhisked flour clumps.

Salt timing: Salt goes in at the start, with the flour. Do not salt a rested batter at the last minute — it will not dissolve evenly and you will taste salt grains in the crust.

Phase 2: Rest the Batter — or Don’t — 0, 30 min, or 12 hr

This is the only contested step. Heston rests the batter overnight. Kenji has tested this directly and found no measurable difference. Both can be right.

Why Heston rests
An overnight rest (a) lets the flour fully hydrate, so starches gelatinise more uniformly, (b) lets gluten relax and re-form into longer aligned chains that stretch farther under steam pressure, (c) lets entrapped air bubbles even out.
— Heston Blumenthal, In Search of Perfection, 2006
Why Kenji disagrees
Kenji ran a side-by-side: same batter, half rested 12 hours, half straight from mix. Both produced four-inch puddings. He concludes the rest is “tradition, not technique.”
— J. Kenji López-Alt, The Food Lab, 2015, pp. 522–526

House rule: Rest 30 minutes at room temperature if you have time. Rest overnight if prepping ahead. Use immediately if eggs are at room temp. None of these will give a bad pudding if the four non-negotiables are right.

  1. If resting overnight: cover, refrigerate. Pull from the fridge 30 minutes before frying so the batter is no warmer than 15°C — you want it cold when it hits the hot fat.

Phase 3: Heat the Tin and the Fat — 15–20 min

  1. Heat the oven to 230°C (450°F) fan-off, or 220°C with fan. Place the empty tin on the centre rack while the oven climbs.
  2. Add fat after 5 min. 1 tsp dripping per muffin cup, or 2½ tsp per popover. Return to the oven and heat 8–10 min more, until visibly smoking. Not faintly shimmering — smoking.
Why the fat must smoke
Beef dripping smokes around 200–210°C. Smoke means the fat has reached the temperature at which the surface of the batter will set within seconds of contact. Cold fat — even fat at 180°C — lets batter pool, soak in, and start to climb only after the centre has thickened. Smoking fat sets the base instantly and forces all the rising steam upward through a sealed perimeter.
— Heston, In Search of Perfection, 2006; mechanism in McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004, pp. 778–783

Phase 4: Pour and Seal — 60 sec

  1. Pull the smoking tin onto the open oven door. Pour the cold batter fast, filling each cup halfway to two-thirds. The batter will hiss and spit on contact — that is correct.
  2. Slide the tin back into the oven. Close the door cleanly. Do not open it again until the timer says so.
Why no peeking
Every door-open at 230°C drops the oven 25–40°C; recovery takes 90 seconds. In that window the unset puddings collapse. The puddings are at peak structural fragility during minutes 6–15 — fully climbed, not yet fully set. This is the failure mode that kills more home Yorkshires than every other cause combined.
— Felicity Cloake, The Guardian, 2012

Phase 5: Bake — Do Not Peek — 20–25 min

  1. Bake at 230°C for 15 minutes. Climb happens at minutes 6–12 — watch through the glass with the light on if you must.
  2. After 15 min, drop the temperature to 190°C (375°F) without opening the door. Continue 5–10 min until deep amber and the rims are crisp and dry. Total 20–25 min muffin / 25–30 min popover / 30–35 min single tray.

Done when:

  • Puddings are 8–12 cm (popover) or 5–7 cm (muffin) tall, with a deep crater
  • Rims are crisp, dry, deep amber
  • Domed and audibly hollow when tapped

Phase 6: Out and Eat — under 5 min, the window is short

  1. Pull the tin out. Lift each pudding straight out — they release if the tin was hot enough at the start.
  2. Eat immediately. Yorkshire puddings are at peak structural integrity for the first 3–5 minutes after the tin leaves the oven. After that, residual steam softens the crust from the inside, and the pudding deflates over the next 10 minutes. This is not a fault — it is physics. Plan service: pull the tin as the beef rests, plate, and serve directly.

If you must hold: Lay the puddings on a wire rack in a warm oven (90°C, door propped). They hold structure for 8–10 minutes. Never stack, never cover, never wrap in foil. Foil is the enemy.

The Science

Steam Pressure = the Climb

A Yorkshire pudding rises because of one mechanism: rapid water-to-steam expansion inside a setting batter shell. Liquid water at 100°C expands roughly 1,600 times its volume when it converts to steam. The high hydration of the batter (115% by weight) is not a stylistic choice — it is the fuel for the climb. As cold wet batter hits 230°C fat, surface water in the bottom millimetre flashes to steam in under a second, lifting the upper batter off the base. As heat penetrates inward, more water vaporises, and steam pressure inside the shell drives the climb upward. Egg proteins coagulate (60–70°C) and the gluten network sets (~80°C), freezing the structure at maximum extension. — McGee 2004, pp. 549–553; Heston 2006

Smoking Fat = Sealed Base

The single biggest difference between a tall pudding and a flat one is fat temperature at the moment the batter pours. Fat at 230°C sets the base into a sealed crust within 1–2 seconds. Fat at 180°C does not — the batter pools, soaks, and starts to climb only after the centre has begun thickening, by which point the steam pressure has dissipated upward through the still-liquid mass. The Royal Society of Chemistry’s 2008 ruling specifies “a hot tin and very hot fat” as a binding requirement. Smoke is the visual confirmation. — RSC 2008; Heston 2006

Bread Flour = Gluten Cage

Yorkshire pudding is a steam-leavened batter held up by a network of gluten and coagulated egg protein. Bread flour (12–14% protein) develops more gluten than plain flour (10–11%) under the same hydration. More gluten means a stronger, more elastic shell that stretches farther under steam pressure before tearing. Cloake’s 2012 comparative test found bread flour produced the tallest puddings every time. The “use plain flour” tradition predates supermarket bread flour and is no longer optimal. — Cloake 2012; McGee 2004, pp. 521–524

Egg Foam + Coagulation = Structural Lock

The four eggs are doing two jobs at once. They contribute water (eggs are 75% water) to the steam fuel, and proteins (albumen, ovotransferrin) that coagulate at 60–70°C and lock the climbing batter into a solid foam. Skimping on eggs (three instead of four) is the single most common Yorkshire pudding error. The rim climbs but the centre sags within minutes. Four large eggs to 140g flour is the empirical sweet spot — verified by Heston, Kenji, and Cloake independently. — McGee 2004, pp. 92–103

High Hydration = More Steam

The 115% hydration is what separates Yorkshire pudding from popover, from crêpe, from pancake. Yorkshire wants maximum steam volume per gram of solids. A drier batter (90% hydration, like American popover) produces a denser, more bread-like puff. Yorkshire’s 115% produces an open, hollow, almost paper-walled crust with a deep crater. If you weigh ingredients on a scale, you cannot get this wrong. If you measure by volume only, an over-packed cup of flour will drop hydration to 100% and the climb will be 30% shorter. Weigh. — Kenji 2014

Charter Prep & Storage

Bake to order. Always. Yorkshire deflates from peak in 10 minutes. Time the bake to land as the beef rests. Never hold longer than 8 min in a 90°C oven on a rack, uncovered.

Charter rule: Bake in the smallest batch that feeds the table. Two trays of 12 are better than one tray of 24 — the bigger tray takes longer, the climb is uneven, the early ones cool while the late ones finish. Yorkshire is a fresh-from-oven dish, full stop.
