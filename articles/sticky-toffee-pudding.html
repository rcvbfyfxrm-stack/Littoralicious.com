Sticky Toffee Pudding: Dark Muscovado & Whisky Caramel

The recipe you actually want to read

Dates bloomed with baking soda for a smooth crumb. Dark muscovado plus black treacle for caramel that’s deep, not flat. Whisky in the sauce because alcohol carries the volatiles sugar can’t. Three modifications. Five sources. One pudding that earns its place at the end of a charter dinner.

Skewer it warm, pour hot sauce over, and the sponge should drink the toffee until the surface goes dark and glossy — not sit there with the sauce pooling on top like a glaze it never asked for. Most recipes lean on a wall of sugar to fake that depth; this one leans on chemistry, three moves deep. The first happens before flour ever enters the bowl.

The one idea: a sticky toffee pudding is a sponge engineered to drink — a deliberately tight, low crumb built to soak hot sauce without collapsing. The dish is the absorption, not the cake; everything below serves the pour.

Where it comes from

Despite the very English name, this is a young dish with a contested passport. It was made famous in the 1970s by Francis Coulson and Robert Lee at Sharrow Bay, the country-house hotel on Ullswater in the Lake District — but Coulson himself admitted he hadn’t invented it. He had the recipe from Patricia Martin, who cooked at the Old Rectory in Claughton, Lancashire, and her son later told the food writer Simon Hopkinson that she got it from two Canadian airmen billeted at her hotel during the war.

That Canadian thread explains the crumb. This is not an English steamed sponge — it is closer to a North American date cake: chemically leavened, flat, and dense on purpose. The dates are the dish’s spine, not a flavouring; soften them right and the sauce has somewhere to go.

The “traditional English pudding” may not be English at all. The oldest written claim belongs to the Gait Inn at Millington, East Yorkshire, dated 1907 — but the line that actually reached the menus we know runs through Lancashire and, before that, two Canadian airmen in the Second World War. Coulson made it famous; he did not make it up. — Simon Hopkinson, via the Martin family; Wikipedia, “Sticky toffee pudding”

The romance is the country-house dining room. What separates a good one from a sad slab is the step you can’t taste yet — what the baking soda does to the dates before the bowl is even out.

Stir baking soda into the hot date soak and you push its pH from about 5 up to about 8. In that alkaline water the dates’ pectin and cell walls break down faster, the fibre dissolves, and the purée goes silky instead of stringy. The soda also spends the dates’ native acid early, so it doesn’t fight your chemical leaveners later — the sponge stays low and porous, exactly the texture that drinks sauce without falling apart.

Method

0/0 steps done

  1. This is the step the Canadians built the whole dish around — the dates, not the sponge, are the recipe.

    Bloom the dates. Pit and weigh 220 g. Stir 6 g soda into 280 g boiling water until clear, pour over, stand 15 min. Blend smooth, then cool to ≤40°C before it meets the batter.

    Why
    Alkaline water (pH ~8) dissolves pectin and fibre so the purée goes silky, and pre-reacts the dates’ acid so the soda doesn’t fight the leaveners later. Hot purée melts the butter-sugar emulsion — hence the cool-down. — McGee, On Food and Cooking, 2004

  2. Muscovado is the difference between “tastes caramel” and “tastes of burnt sugar and dark fruit”.

    Cream the sugars. Heat the oven to 170°C (340°F); butter a 20×20 cm pan and line it. Cream 100 g butter, 175 g muscovado and 25 g treacle 3–4 min until pale and aerated. A little grain is fine.

    Why
    Dark muscovado carries up to ~15% molasses by weight against light brown’s ~3.5% — deeper colour, smoke and dark-fruit notes, plus invert sugars that keep the crumb moist for days. — Stella Parks, BraveTart, 2017

  3. You are not building a fluffy cake — you are building a sponge that stays down to drink.

    Eggs, then flour and dates. Beat in 100 g egg one at a time with the 5 g vanilla. Whisk 175 g flour, 4 g baking powder and 3 g salt; fold in half the flour, all the dates, then the rest — stop at 80%, streaks are fine.

    Why
    Over-mixing develops gluten and traps too much air — you get a tall, springy cake that sheds sauce instead of holding it. Streaks of flour hydrate in the oven. — Rose Levy Beranbaum, The Cake Bible

  4. The thermometer settles every argument about a date sponge — colour lies, the centre doesn’t.

    Bake and rest. Bake 30–35 min to 94°C (200°F) at the centre — matte top, cracked edges, a skewer tacky not wet. Rest 10 min in the pan; do not unmould.

    Why
    At 94°C the starch has gelatinised and the crumb is set but still porous and warm — the state that drinks hot sauce. Unmoulded early it tears; left to cool it skins over and the sauce sits on top. — McGee, 2004

  5. Make this while the pudding bakes; build it in two stages so the emulsion never has a chance to break.

    Build the sauce. Melt 100 g butter with 150 g muscovado low until glossy, 3–4 min. Off heat, whisk in 200 g cream and 5 g vanilla; back on low, simmer 90 sec to coat a spoon — never a hard boil.

    Why
    A hard boil shears the fat out of the emulsion and the sauce splits into oil and syrup. A bare simmer holds it together and thickens it just enough. — Myhrvold et al., Modernist Cuisine, 2011

  6. The whisky goes in last and cold-side, or it leaves before the plate does.

    Finish off-heat, then drown the pudding. Off heat, whisk in 30 g whisky and 3 g flaky salt. Skewer the warm pudding 30+ times, pour over a third, let it vanish 60 sec, then cut and drench each plate, ~40 g a portion.

    Why
    Whisky’s esters and phenols boil off above ~78°C; added off-heat they survive onto the palate. Sauce at 70–80°C wicks 4–5 mm into warm crumb in a minute — cold sauce gels on top. — Myhrvold et al., 2011

Serve it hot, sponge soaked dark, with a cold counterweight on the side: clotted cream is the home-country move, crème fraîche if you want a little acid to cut the sugar, or vanilla ice cream when the dining room runs warm. Keep the reserved sauce hot in a jug and pour again at the table — nobody has ever refused the second pour.

One batter, two puddings — sticky ginger & walnut

The same date sponge is the base of the classic Lancashire variation — a small change at the bowl, a different pudding on the plate. The toffee sauce is unchanged and works on both.

SpiceAdd 4 g ground ginger to the dry mix and 30–40 g finely chopped stem ginger to the batter
NutFold in 60 g toasted, roughly chopped walnuts with the second half of the flour
BakeSame pan, same 170°C, same 94°C centre — the additions don’t change the timing
SauceIdentical whisky caramel; a maple-butterscotch version also suits the ginger

The toffee sauce is a standalone in its own right — it keeps 5 days in the fridge and reheats gently over ice cream, baked apples or a plain steamed sponge.

Dense, gummyDate purée too hot → cool to ≤40°C; hot purée melts the butter and kills the emulsion
Sunk middleUnderbaked or batter too wet → bake to a true 94°C internal, not on colour alone
Sauce splitBoiled too hard → off heat, splash in 30 g cold cream and whisk hard; it comes back

Charter prep & storage

The rule that saves the dish on a charter: pudding and sauce store apart, always. Frozen together the sauce fat-splits and the cake goes to mush; kept in two containers, both hold for months and reheat clean to order.

Baked pudding2 months frozen — cool, wrap in paper + foil (vacuum-seal best); reheat 120°C, 12 min, lightly pre-soaked
Toffee sauce2 wk fridge / 3 mo freezer; skim the fat layer and whisk back together in 30-sec microwave bursts
Date base5 days in a jar in the fridge; double the soak and next week’s pudding is half-made
Raw ramekinsPortion and freeze 1 month; bake from frozen +10 min at 165°C for à la carte service
Dry mix1 month airtight at room temp
NeverStore the two together, or pour the sauce cold — both undo the whole dish

For plated service at sea, ramekins or mini bundts win over one big pan: each comes out hot, holds its shape in swell, and takes its sauce at the table.

Once you own the ratio — flour and sugar level, butter and egg at half, dates again the same — the dish is yours: brown the butter for nutty depth, run it as sticky ginger & walnut for the Lancashire original, or, feeding crew, bake one big tray and pour generously rather than plating ramekins. The pudding that two Canadian airmen carried into a Lancashire kitchen was never about the cake; it was always about how much sauce the sponge could drink. Build it to drink.

Sources: McGee, On Food and Cooking; Parks, BraveTart; Myhrvold et al., Modernist Cuisine; Beranbaum, The Cake Bible; origin via Simon Hopkinson / the Martin family and the Gait Inn (1907) claim. Tested at sea.
