Cooking the Atlantic Crossing: The Galley Playbook

Three weeks of provisioning, twenty crew, two thousand miles and a galley that pitches. An ocean crossing is won before you leave the dock — the freezer strategy, the rough-weather meals, and the morale that quietly runs on baking and snacks.

What you'll get
  • A countdown that front-loads the whole job onto the calm days at the dock.
  • The freezer programme that lets you pull, heat, season and serve once the galley starts to pitch.
  • The rough-weather meals and the morale tricks — snacks and baking — that quietly carry the crew across.

Twenty crew, two thousand miles, three weeks at sea, and not a single shop between here and the far side. You cannot reprovision. The fridge holds what it holds. And on day four, when the wind comes round and the galley starts to roll, the chef may be the one with their head over a bucket. So the whole job moves to the dock. An Atlantic crossing is not cooked at sea — it is cooked before you slip the lines.

The crossing menu is built at the dock, not on the water. You do the heavy lifting while the boat sits flat — cook, portion, freeze, stow. At sea you are not a chef any more; you are a line cook pulling from your own past self: pull, heat, season, serve.

4 Weeks Out

Plan and build the dry stores

Start with the maths, not the menu. Crew count times days times a realistic per-head, then add a margin for the leg that runs long. For twenty crew over a three-week passage you are buying for roughly four hundred and twenty covers a meal — provision short and there is no second chance.

Build depth in the things that never spoil. Tinned tomatoes, beans and tinned vegetables; rice and pasta by the sack; duck confit in the fat; UHT and full-fat powdered milk for when the fresh runs out. Then the long-keeping veg: potatoes, carrots, roots and squash that hold for weeks in the dark.

Buy it unrefrigerated where you can. Eggs and many vegetables bought never chilled keep far longer than their fridge-cold cousins — an unwashed egg at cool room temperature holds for weeks. Source them ambient from the start and you free up cold space for the things that actually need it.

Walk the order in tiers: shelf-stable depth first, then long-keeping veg, then the cold and frozen last so it spends the least time off the boat. Label every case with its contents and a stow location before it leaves the dock — at sea you will not be reading handwriting by torchlight in a pitching dry store.

2 Weeks Out

The freezer programme

This is the core of the whole crossing, and it happens now, on dry land. You pre-cook and portion-freeze the meals that survive a swell — the ones you can serve one-handed with the boat heeled over. Kilos of browned mince. Proteins marinated and portioned. Lasagne, meatloaf, curries, braises, savoury pies, soups, vegetable purées. Cook once, divide, label, freeze flat so the portions stack and thaw fast.

"Do the heavy lifting when the seas are calm, so at sea you only pull, heat, season and serve."

Freeze flat in bags or shallow trays, not in deep tubs — a 30 mm slab thaws in a fraction of the time of a brick and gives you back freezer Tetris you will need. Write the dish, the cover count and the date on every parcel in marker, because in week three everything frozen looks identical.

Why freezing is the whole answer here. Chilling to −18°C halts microbial growth outright and slows the enzymatic and oxidative staling that would otherwise spoil food across a three-week passage. Cook, cool fast, freeze flat — and braises and sauces genuinely improve, because the held time lets the flavours marry. The one rule on the way back up: reheat to a safe core of ≥75°C / 165°F, every time.

1 Week Out

Storage and the last of the fresh

Now you front-load fresh produce for the first days at sea — the salads, the herbs, the soft fruit that will not last to the far side but make days one to five feel like shore. Eat the perishable early and let the frozen and tinned carry the back half of the leg.

The hard limit is volume. Know your fridge and freezer capacity to the litre, because that — not your imagination — is what sets the menu. When the built-in cold runs out, the veterans make more of it.

Two storage moves heard from across the fleet, anonymised: one chef runs a spare guest cabin cold as a walk-in, A/C cranked right down, and stows the overflow produce in there. Another straps a temporary chest freezer in the salon to a stabiliser strut so it cannot wander when the boat works. Improvised, yes — but on a crossing, capacity is the menu.

Departure → At Sea

Rough weather and the night watch

This is the day the plan earns its keep. The wind builds, the galley pitches, and people get seasick — and sometimes the chef is one of them. That is the entire reason everything is already made. You do not want to be browning mince at a heeling stove on day four; you want to pull a labelled parcel and slide it in the oven.

Never plan a dish that needs two hands in a swell. Rough-weather meals are one-handed and pop-in-the-oven: soups, braises, baked pasta. No knife work at a moving board, no pan you have to hold, no plating that slides. If a meal cannot be served while you grip a rail with the other hand, it is the wrong meal for the conditions.

Watches run through the dark, and they do not run on full meals — they run on hot drinks and snacks. Keep the kettle, the soup and the biscuit tin reachable without waking the galley. One chef's verdict from a hard leg: "dry, salty snacks saved most of the crew on their worst days." Crisps, crackers, pretzels, salted nuts — when nothing else will stay down, salt and crunch will.

And then there is morale, which is quieter and just as load-bearing. Mid-Atlantic, with the same horizon for the fifth day running, a tray of warm cookies or a cake does more than any plated dinner. As one chef put it, baking matters most "when morale gets low mid-Atlantic." Keep flour, butter, sugar and chocolate aboard for exactly that — the bake you make when the boat needs lifting, not feeding.

The owner who announces "I'll just eat crew food" for the passage — that lasted two days.

The Takeaway

If you remember one thing

The crossing is built at the dock, not at sea. Pre-cook, portion, freeze; front-load the fresh; keep the snacks and the baking coming for morale; and never plan a dish that needs two hands in a swell. Do the heavy lifting while the boat sits flat, and the three weeks of ocean become a job of pulling, heating, seasoning and serving — even on the day the chef is the one over the bucket.

An Atlantic crossing is not a feat of cooking under fire. It is a feat of planning under calm, eaten slowly over two thousand miles. Tested at sea.
