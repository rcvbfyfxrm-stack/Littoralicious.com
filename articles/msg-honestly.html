MSG, Honestly: Using Glutamate to Cut the Salt Without Losing the Flavour

Few ingredients carry a worse reputation on weaker evidence than monosodium glutamate. It is the most-studied way to cut a dish's sodium without flattening it — and the science that condemned it never held up. Here is what glutamate actually is, the salt it saves on a long charter, and the one honest caveat worth keeping.

Monosodium glutamate is the sodium salt of glutamate, the compound behind the savoury fifth taste. It carries about a third the sodium of table salt, and because it makes food taste more savoury it lets you cut total sodium by roughly 25–40% at the same palatability. The scare that condemned it traces to a single 1968 letter, not a study; double-blind trials never found a consistent effect. It is classified safe by every major regulator. Use it to feed people less salt — not to paper over thin cooking.

The fifth taste, isolated from kombu

In 1908 the chemist Kikunae Ikeda at Tokyo Imperial University set out to name the savour in a bowl of kombu dashi that salt and the other known tastes could not explain. He isolated glutamate — an amino acid — and called its taste umami, the savoury fifth taste alongside sweet, sour, salt and bitter. Monosodium glutamate is simply glutamate paired with a single sodium ion so it dissolves and keeps; the same compound sits naturally in parmesan, ripe tomato, anchovy, mushroom and soy. It is not a foreign chemical added to food — it is the molecule food already uses to taste of itself.

The sodium maths

Why a pinch saves a lot of salt

Two things make glutamate a sodium-reduction tool rather than just another seasoning. First, the obvious one: by weight it carries roughly a third the sodium of table salt — about 12% against 39%. Second, and more useful: glutamate makes a dish taste more savoury and, indirectly, saltier, so you can pull out a chunk of the actual salt and the palate doesn't notice the loss.

In controlled tasting work, replacing part of the salt in soups and savoury foods with a smaller dose of MSG held flavour acceptable while cutting total sodium by roughly 25 to 40 percent. A 2017 review of the technology put the practical reduction across foodstuffs in the same range; population modelling suggests a 7–8% cut in average sodium intake if the swap were used widely. The mechanism is sensory, not chemical trickery — umami and saltiness reinforce each other on the tongue.

The sodium-reduction evidence Jinap S, Hajeb P, et al. "Monosodium glutamate as a tool to reduce sodium in foodstuffs: technological and safety aspects." Appetite / Comprehensive Reviews in Food Science, 2017; with controlled-tasting confirmation in Foods, 2022. Grade A · sodium reduction at equal palatability

The galley swap: in a soup, brine, braise or dressing, cut the salt by about a third and add roughly half that weight back as MSG. Taste, then adjust. It works best where there's already savoury depth to amplify — stocks, tomato, alliums, cured fish — and does nothing for a dish that has no flavour to lift in the first place.

The scare

The myth that wouldn't die

The reputation comes from one place: a 1968 letter to the New England Journal of Medicine in which a doctor described numbness and palpitations after Chinese meals and floated MSG as one possible cause, among several. It was a letter, not a study — yet "Chinese Restaurant Syndrome" entered the language and hardened into fact. The same glutamate had been eaten for decades in parmesan and tinned tomato without complaint.

Since then, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials have failed to find a consistent, reproducible reaction to MSG in food-relevant amounts. Every major food-safety body — the FDA, the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee, the European Commission's Scientific Committee — classifies it as safe at normal dietary use. The honest reading is that the scare was built on a single anecdote and carried further by the era's suspicion of one cuisine than by any chemistry.

The one real caveat, kept in proportion: in a few studies, large doses — around 3 g or more of pure MSG taken on an empty stomach, without food — produced brief, mild symptoms in a small, sensitive minority. That is not the gram or two dissolved across a pot of soup eaten as a meal. Cook with it; don't take it neat by the spoonful.

At sea

Where it earns its locker space

On a long charter the sodium question is real: guests on blood-pressure medication, a crew eating the same hand's cooking for ten days, fresh herbs and acidity running thin by the back half of a passage. Glutamate is one bag that keeps indefinitely, weighs nothing against the flavour it returns, and lets you feed people noticeably less salt without anyone reaching for the cellar.

Hold the line on what it is: an amplifier, not a foundation. It makes savoury food taste more of itself, which is exactly why it can carry a low-salt dish — and exactly why it can't rescue a dull one. The grandmother reaching for parmesan rind, the cook melting an anchovy into the sofrito, and the chef adding a pinch of MSG are all doing the same thing, by different names.

The takeaway

Monosodium glutamate is a century-old, well-studied tool for putting less sodium on the plate without flattening it — weighed down by a reputation it never earned. Use it the honest way: to feed people less salt, not to fake flavour that isn't there. That is the whole of it.
