MSG, Honestly: Using Glutamate to Cut the Salt Without Losing the Flavour

Few ingredients carry a worse reputation on weaker evidence than monosodium glutamate. The fifth taste, isolated from kombu

The taste came first; the chemistry caught up. In 1908 the chemist Kikunae Ikeda at Tokyo Imperial University set out to name the savour in a bowl of kombu dashi that salt and the other known tastes could not explain. He isolated glutamate — an amino acid — and called its taste umami, the savoury fifth taste beside sweet, sour, salt and bitter.

The "mono-sodium" part is just delivery. Glutamate paired with a single sodium ion dissolves cleanly and keeps on a shelf. The very same compound already sits in parmesan, ripe tomato, anchovy, dried mushroom and soy. It is not a foreign chemical added to food — it is the molecule food already uses to taste of itself.

The sodium maths

Why a pinch saves a lot of salt

Two things make glutamate a sodium tool, not just a seasoning. First, the obvious one: by weight it carries roughly a third the sodium of table salt — about 12% against 39%. Second, and more useful: it makes a dish read as more savoury and, on the tongue, saltier, so you can pull out a chunk of the real salt and the palate never registers the loss.

In controlled tasting work, replacing part of the salt in soups and savoury foods with a smaller dose of MSG held flavour acceptable while cutting total sodium by roughly 25 to 40 percent. A 2017 review of the technology put the practical reduction across foodstuffs in the same range; population modelling suggests a 7–8% cut in average sodium intake if the swap were used widely. The mechanism is sensory, not a trick — umami and saltiness reinforce each other.

The sodium-reduction evidence Jinap S, Hajeb P, et al. "Monosodium glutamate as a tool to reduce sodium in foodstuffs: technological and safety aspects." Comprehensive Reviews in Food Science & Food Safety, 2017; controlled-tasting confirmation in Foods, 2022. Grade A · sodium reduction at equal palatability

The galley swap: in a soup, brine, braise or dressing, cut the salt by about a third and add roughly half that weight back as MSG. Taste, then adjust. It works where there's already savour to amplify — stocks, tomato, alliums, cured fish — and does nothing for a dish with no flavour to lift.

The scare

The myth that wouldn't die

The whole reputation rests on one letter. In 1968 a doctor wrote to the New England Journal of Medicine describing numbness and palpitations after Chinese meals, and floated MSG as one possible cause among several. It was a letter, not a study — yet "Chinese Restaurant Syndrome" entered the language and hardened into fact, while the identical glutamate in parmesan and tinned tomato drew no complaint at all.

A single letter, in 1968, built a fear that fifty years of trials could not unbuild.

The trials came, and they came up empty. Double-blind, placebo-controlled studies have failed to find a consistent, reproducible reaction to MSG in food-relevant amounts. The FDA, the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee and the European Commission's Scientific Committee all classify it as safe at normal use. The honest reading: the scare was carried further by the era's suspicion of one cuisine than by any chemistry.

The one real caveat, kept in proportion: in a few studies, large doses — around 3 g or more of pure MSG taken on an empty stomach, without food — produced brief, mild symptoms in a small, sensitive minority. That is not the gram or two dissolved across a pot of soup eaten as a meal. Cook with it; don't take it neat by the spoonful.

At sea

Where it earns its locker space

On a long charter the sodium question is real. Guests on blood-pressure medication; a crew eating the same hand's cooking for ten days; fresh herbs and acidity running thin by the back half of a passage. Glutamate is one bag that keeps indefinitely, weighs nothing against the flavour it returns, and lets you feed people noticeably less salt without anyone reaching for the cellar.

Hold the line on what it is: an amplifier, not a foundation. It makes savoury food taste more of itself — which is exactly why it can carry a low-salt dish, and exactly why it can't rescue a dull one. The grandmother reaching for a parmesan rind, the cook melting an anchovy into the sofrito, and the chef adding a pinch of MSG are all doing the same thing by different names.

The takeaway

Glutamate is a century-old, well-studied way to put less sodium on the plate without flattening it — weighed down by a reputation it never earned. Use it the honest way: to feed people less salt, not to fake flavour that isn't there. That is the whole of it.
